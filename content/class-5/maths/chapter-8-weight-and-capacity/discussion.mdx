## Choosing the Right Unit for Weight

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we need milligrams, grams, kilograms, quintals, AND tonnes? That is five different units just for weight!

**Ravi:** Imagine telling the doctor your paracetamol tablet is 0.0005 kilograms. Or telling the truck driver his load is 50,00,000 grams. Sounds silly, right?

**Kiran:** Ha ha, yes! The doctor would say 500 milligrams and the truck driver would say 5 tonnes.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Just like you use different spoons in the kitchen — a tiny one for medicine, a regular one for sugar, and a big ladle for dal — you use different units for different sizes of weight. Milligrams for medicine and gold, grams for small items, kilograms for groceries, quintals for grain sacks, and tonnes for trucks and elephants.

**Kiran:** An elephant! How heavy is an elephant?

**Ravi:** An Indian elephant weighs about 4 to 5 tonnes — that is 4,000 to 5,000 kilograms! Now imagine expressing that in milligrams.

**Kiran:** That would be... 5,000 times 1,000 times 1,000... five hundred crore milligrams! Definitely need the right unit!

## The Tricky Conversions

**Kiran:** Papa, I made a mistake on my test. I wrote 5 kg 50 g as 5,500 g instead of 5,050 g. Why did I get confused?

**Ravi:** This is a very common mistake. Think of it like money. If I say 5 rupees and 50 paise, how many paise is that?

**Kiran:** 5 rupees is 500 paise, plus 50 paise, so 550 paise.

**Ravi:** Now what about 5 rupees and 5 paise?

**Kiran:** That is 500 plus 5 equals 505 paise — not 55!

**Ravi:** Same thing with weight. 5 kg 50 g means 5,000 g plus 50 g equals 5,050 g. The 50 does not magically become 500 just because kg has three zeros. Always convert the kg part fully to grams first, then add.

**Kiran:** So the trick is to always write out the full conversion — 5 kg equals 5,000 g — before adding?

**Ravi:** That is the safest way. Write it out, add carefully, and you will never make that mistake again.

## Weight at the Sabzi Mandi

**Kiran:** Papa, today at the sabzi-wala, Mummy bought 2 kg 500 g of potatoes and 1 kg 750 g of onions. The shopkeeper said the total is 4 kg 250 g. Is that right?

**Ravi:** Let us check! Add the kg parts first: 2 plus 1 equals 3 kg. Now the grams: 500 plus 750 equals 1,250 g. But 1,250 g is more than 1,000 g, so convert — that is 1 kg and 250 g. Add the extra 1 kg to the 3 kg — final answer is 4 kg 250 g. The shopkeeper was correct!

**Kiran:** What if I need to find the difference? Like if we already had 750 g of tomatoes at home and Mummy bought 2 kg 200 g more?

**Ravi:** Convert everything to grams. 2 kg 200 g equals 2,200 g. Add the 750 g at home — total is 2,950 g, which is 2 kg 950 g. Or if you want the difference: 2,200 minus 750 equals 1,450 g equals 1 kg 450 g more than what was at home.

**Kiran:** Maths at the mandi is actually fun!

## Capacity — How Much Can It Hold?

**Kiran:** Papa, what exactly is the difference between weight and capacity?

**Ravi:** Weight tells you how heavy something is. Capacity tells you how much liquid a container can hold. Your water bottle weighs some grams when empty, but its capacity might be 500 ml — that is how much water it can hold.

**Kiran:** So my school water bottle holds 500 ml, and the big matka at Nani's house?

**Ravi:** A matka holds about 10 to 15 litres. A bucket holds about 15 to 20 litres. And the water tanker that comes to our colony? About 10,000 litres!

**Kiran:** 10,000 litres! That is 10,000 times 1,000 ml — one crore millilitres!

**Ravi:** Now you are thinking in conversions automatically. And remember the same tricky rule applies — 3 litres 8 ml is 3,008 ml, NOT 3,800 ml. The 8 ml stays as 8, not 800.

**Kiran:** Same trap as the weight conversion. I will be careful!

## Reading the Weighing Scale

**Kiran:** Papa, at the grocer's shop, the digital scale showed 1.750 kg for the dal. But the old balance scale at the village shop has those metal weights. How does that work?

**Ravi:** The balance scale is brilliant. You put the dal on one side and known metal weights on the other. When both sides balance perfectly — like a see-saw with equal weight on both ends — you know the dal weighs exactly as much as the metal weights.

**Kiran:** What if the dal is between two weights?

**Ravi:** Good question! That is why we have small weights too — 500 g, 200 g, 100 g, 50 g, 20 g, 10 g, even 5 g. You keep adding smaller weights until the scale balances. It is like making exact change with coins!

**Kiran:** And on a spring scale with a dial, what do the small marks mean?

**Ravi:** Always check two things first — the maximum it can measure, and what each small mark represents. If the scale goes up to 5 kg and has 10 marks between each kg, each mark is 100 g. But if it has 20 marks, each mark is 50 g. Read the scale carefully before you read the measurement!

## The King's Donation — A Multiplication Problem

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says a king donates wheat equal to 10 times his weight. If he donated 800 kg, his weight is 80 kg. But what if his weight was 85 kg?

**Ravi:** Then the donation would be 85 times 10 equals 850 kg. But here is a fun extension — 850 kg is 8 quintals and 50 kg. Or 0.85 tonnes. See how the same weight looks different in different units?

**Kiran:** What if every student in my class of 40 donated rice equal to their own weight? If the average weight is about 30 kg, that would be 40 times 30 equals 1,200 kg!

**Ravi:** That is 12 quintals or 1.2 tonnes of rice. Enough to feed many families for weeks. This is actually how community donations work during floods and festivals across India — small individual contributions add up to massive collective help.

**Kiran:** Maths helps me understand how even small efforts become big when people work together.

**Ravi:** That is a lesson worth more than any textbook, beta.
