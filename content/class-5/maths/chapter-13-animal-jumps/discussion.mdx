## Factors — Breaking Numbers into Pieces

**Kiran:** Papa, what exactly is a factor? I keep getting confused between factors and multiples.

**Ravi:** Think of factors as the building blocks of a number. If 12 sweets can be arranged into 3 rows of 4, then both 3 and 4 are factors of 12. Factors are numbers that divide evenly into a number with no remainder.

**Kiran:** So I need to find all the ways to split 12 into equal rows?

**Ravi:** Exactly! 1 times 12, 2 times 6, 3 times 4 — those give you all the factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12. Every number has 1 and itself as factors. And here is the key difference — factors are finite. You can list all of them. But multiples are infinite — they go on forever.

**Kiran:** Like the multiples of 3 are 3, 6, 9, 12, 15 and they never stop?

**Ravi:** Exactly! Think of it this way: factors divide INTO a number, multiples are what you GET when you multiply. Factors look backwards, multiples look forwards.

**Kiran:** Oh! That makes it clear. Factors are the ingredients, multiples are the products!

## Prime Numbers — The Atoms of Maths

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says some numbers have only two factors — 1 and themselves. Why are they special?

**Ravi:** They are called prime numbers, and they are the atoms of mathematics. Just like everything in the world is made of atoms, every number can be built from prime numbers. Take 12 — it is 2 times 2 times 3. Take 35 — it is 5 times 7. But 7 itself? You cannot break it further. 7 is 1 times 7, and that is the only way.

**Kiran:** So 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13 are all prime?

**Ravi:** Yes! And here is something interesting — 2 is the only even prime number. Every other even number can be divided by 2, so they all have 2 as a factor and cannot be prime.

**Kiran:** What about 1? It has only one factor — itself. Is it prime?

**Ravi:** Great question! By convention, 1 is NOT considered prime because it has only one factor, not two. A prime number must have exactly two factors. So 1 is neither prime nor composite — it is in a category of its own.

**Kiran:** Like how zero is neither positive nor negative!

**Ravi:** Exactly that kind of special exception.

## Common Multiples — When Animals Meet on the Number Line

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter shows a rabbit jumping by 4 and a frog jumping by 3 on a number line. They land on the same spot at 12, 24, 36. Why?

**Ravi:** Because 12 is a multiple of BOTH 3 and 4 — it is a common multiple. The rabbit reaches 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24... The frog reaches 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24... The positions where they both land are the common multiples: 12, 24, 36, and so on.

**Kiran:** And the smallest common multiple is 12 — the LCM?

**Ravi:** Right! LCM stands for Least Common Multiple. Here is a real-life example. The Rajdhani Express from Delhi departs every 3 days, and the Shatabdi departs every 4 days. If both left today, when will both depart on the same day again?

**Kiran:** In 12 days! Because LCM of 3 and 4 is 12.

**Ravi:** Now here is a twist. What if the two trains depart every 3 and every 6 days?

**Kiran:** Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12... Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18... They first meet at 6. So LCM of 3 and 6 is just 6!

**Ravi:** Because 6 is already a multiple of 3. When one number is a multiple of the other, the larger number IS the LCM.

## Common Factors and HCF

**Kiran:** Papa, what about common factors? The chapter finds common factors of 24 and 36.

**Ravi:** Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24. Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36. The numbers that appear in BOTH lists are common factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. The biggest one — 12 — is the HCF, the Highest Common Factor.

**Kiran:** When would I use HCF in real life?

**Ravi:** Suppose Mummy has 24 laddoos and 36 pedas and wants to make gift boxes where each box has the same number of laddoos and the same number of pedas, with nothing left over. The maximum number of boxes is the HCF of 24 and 36 — which is 12. So she can make 12 boxes, each with 2 laddoos and 3 pedas!

**Kiran:** Oh! HCF tells you the maximum number of equal groups you can make!

**Ravi:** Beautifully put. That is exactly what it means in practice.

## Divisibility Rules — Quick Checks

**Kiran:** Papa, is there a fast way to check if a number is divisible by 2 or 5 or 10 without actually dividing?

**Ravi:** Yes! These are called divisibility rules, and they are shortcuts. For 2 — check if the last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, or 8). For 5 — check if the last digit is 0 or 5. For 10 — check if the last digit is 0.

**Kiran:** So 3,478 is divisible by 2 because it ends in 8, but not by 5 because it does not end in 0 or 5?

**Ravi:** Perfect! Now think about cricket scores. India scores 275 runs. Is 275 divisible by 5?

**Kiran:** Ends in 5 — yes! 275 divided by 5 is 55.

**Ravi:** Is it divisible by 2?

**Kiran:** Ends in 5, which is odd — no!

**Ravi:** Is it divisible by 10?

**Kiran:** Does not end in 0 — no! These rules are so fast!

**Ravi:** When you learn divisibility rules for 3, 4, 6, 8, and 9 in higher classes, you will be able to check any number almost instantly.

## Sher Khan and Bagheera — The Jungle Problem

**Kiran:** Papa, the problem about Sher Khan hunting every 3 days and Bagheera every 5 days is really cool. They both hunt together every 15 days because LCM of 3 and 5 is 15.

**Ravi:** Right! And here is an extension. What if a monkey eats from a particular tree every 2 days, Sher Khan visits that tree every 3 days, and Bagheera every 5 days? When do all three arrive on the same day?

**Kiran:** I need the LCM of 2, 3, and 5. Multiples of 2: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10... Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12, 15... Multiples of 5: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30... The first number in all three lists is... 30!

**Ravi:** Excellent! LCM of 2, 3, and 5 is 30. All three animals meet at the tree every 30 days. This kind of problem appears in real life too — if three different buses pass your stop at intervals of 10, 15, and 20 minutes, the LCM tells you when all three arrive together.

**Kiran:** So LCM is the "meeting point" of cycles!

**Ravi:** That is a beautiful way to describe it, beta. I could not have said it better myself.
