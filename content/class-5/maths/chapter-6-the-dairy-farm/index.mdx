## Multiplication — Many Ways to Multiply

By now you know your multiplication tables. In this chapter, we'll learn to multiply **larger numbers** using different strategies — and see that there's more than one way to get the answer!

---

## Order Doesn't Matter in Multiplication

When you multiply two numbers, it doesn't matter which order you write them:

- **2 × 3 = 3 × 2 = 6**
- **5 × 8 = 8 × 5 = 40**
- **6 × 13 = 13 × 6 = 78**
- **9 × 0 = 0 × 9 = 0**

> This is called the **commutative property** of multiplication. The number of groups and the group size can be swapped, but the total stays the same.

---

## Multiplying by 10s, 100s, and 1,000s

### Multiplying by 10:
Each digit moves **one place to the left** — just add a zero at the end.
- 4 × 10 = **40**
- 20 × 10 = **200**
- 45 × 10 = **450**

### Multiplying by 100:
Each digit moves **two places to the left** — add two zeros.
- 3 × 100 = **300**
- 8 × 100 = **800**
- 10 × 100 = **1,000**

### Multiplying by 1,000:
Each digit moves **three places to the left** — add three zeros.
- 2 × 1,000 = **2,000**
- 5 × 1,000 = **5,000**
- 20 × 1,000 = **20,000**

### Multiplying two multiples of 10:
- 30 × 20 = 3 × 10 × 2 × 10 = **6 × 100 = 600**
- 60 × 50 = 6 × 5 × 100 = **3,000**
- 400 × 10 = **4,000**

> **Pattern:** Count the total zeros at the end of both numbers, multiply the non-zero parts, then add those zeros back.

## Strategy 1: Break and Multiply (Distributive Property)

Break one number into parts, multiply each part, then add the results.

### Example: 18 × 5
- Break 18 into 10 + 8
- 10 × 5 = 50
- 8 × 5 = 40
- 50 + 40 = **90**

### Example: 12 × 35
- Break 12 into 10 + 2, and 35 into 30 + 5
- 10 × 30 = 300
- 10 × 5 = 50
- 2 × 30 = 60
- 2 × 5 = 10
- 300 + 50 + 60 + 10 = **420**

This can be shown as a **grid** (area model):

```
        30      5
   ┌────────┬──────┐
10 │  300   │  50  │  350
   ├────────┼──────┤
 2 │   60   │  10  │   70
   └────────┴──────┘
      360      60    = 420
```

## Strategy 2: Doubling and Halving

If you **double** one number and **halve** the other, the product stays the same!

### Example: 3 × 18
- Double 3 → 6, Halve 18 → 9
- 6 × 9 = **54** (much easier!)

### Example: 22 × 5
- Halve 22 → 11, Double 5 → 10
- 11 × 10 = **110**

> **When is this useful?** When one number is 5, 25, or 50 — halving the other number and doubling 5 to 10 (or 25 to 50, or 50 to 100) makes the calculation much simpler.

More examples:
- 5 × 18 = 10 × 9 = **90**
- 25 × 12 = 50 × 6 = **300**
- 50 × 28 = 100 × 14 = **1,400**

---

## Strategy 3: Nearest Multiple

Round one number to a nearby multiple of 10, multiply, then adjust.

### Example: 4 × 19
- 19 is close to 20
- 4 × 20 = 80
- But we multiplied by 1 extra, so subtract: 80 − 4 = **76**

### Example: 14 × 21
- 21 is close to 20
- 14 × 20 = 280
- We multiplied by 1 less, so add: 280 + 14 = **294**

More examples:
- 99 × 15 = 100 × 15 − 15 = 1,500 − 15 = **1,485**
- 7 × 52 = 7 × 50 + 7 × 2 = 350 + 14 = **364**

## Multiplying 3-Digit by 1-Digit Numbers

### Example: 268 × 4

Break 268 into 200 + 60 + 8:
- 4 × 200 = 800
- 4 × 60 = 240
- 4 × 8 = 32
- Total: 800 + 240 + 32 = **1,072**

---

## Multiplying 3-Digit by 2-Digit Numbers

### Example: 453 × 13

Break into parts:
- 3 × 453: 3 × 400 = 1,200; 3 × 50 = 150; 3 × 3 = 9 → **1,359**
- 10 × 453: 10 × 400 = 4,000; 10 × 50 = 500; 10 × 3 = 30 → **4,530**
- Total: 1,359 + 4,530 = **5,889**

### Example: 574 × 125

- 5 × 574 = 2,870
- 20 × 574 = 11,480
- 100 × 574 = 57,400
- Total: 2,870 + 11,480 + 57,400 = **71,750**

## Waste and Composting — A Real-Life Problem

A family of 4 produces about **35 kg** of kitchen waste per month.

**In a year:** 12 × 35 = ?
- 10 × 35 = 350
- 2 × 35 = 70
- Total: **420 kg** of waste!

By composting, they get about 150 kg of compost, saving **₹3,600** per year (at ₹24 per kg).

> **Remember:** We must reduce waste and compost what we can. It saves money and helps the environment!

## Dairy Cooperative

A dairy cooperative collects milk from **268 villagers**, each having at least **4 milk-giving cows**.

Minimum cows: 268 × 4 = **1,072 cows**

Among these, **453 Gir cows** (a famous Indian breed from Gujarat) each produce **13 litres of milk** daily:
- 453 × 13 = **5,889 litres** of milk every day!

> **Jamanaben Maganbhai Naku** of village Tuked in Surat rears 27 Gir cows through low-cost farm investment — showing how women dairy entrepreneurs play an important role in India's dairy sector.

## Multiplication Patterns

### The 1-1-1 pattern:
```
    1 × 1     = 1
   11 × 11    = 121
  111 × 111   = 12,321
 1111 × 1111  = 1,234,321
```

### Multiplying by 11:
```
11 × 12 = 132    (1, 1+2, 2)
11 × 34 = 374    (3, 3+4, 4)
11 × 56 = 616    (5, 5+6, 6)
```

### The 9-pattern:
```
1 × 9 + 1 = 10
12 × 9 + 2 = 110
123 × 9 + 3 = 1,110
1234 × 9 + 4 = 11,110
```

## The King's Reward — A Puzzle

A king offers three ministers a choice:
1. Start with **5** coins, **double** every day for 7 days
2. Start with **3** coins, **triple** every day for 7 days
3. Start with **1** coin, **multiply by 5** every day for 7 days

| Day | Minister 1 (×2) | Minister 2 (×3) | Minister 3 (×5) |
|-----|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Start | 5 | 3 | 1 |
| Day 1 | 10 | 9 | 5 |
| Day 2 | 20 | 27 | 25 |
| Day 3 | 40 | 81 | 125 |
| Day 4 | 80 | 243 | 625 |
| Day 5 | 160 | 729 | 3,125 |
| Day 6 | 320 | 2,187 | 15,625 |
| Day 7 | 640 | 6,561 | 78,125 |

**Minister 3 wins by far!** Even though he started with just 1 coin, multiplying by 5 grows much faster than doubling or tripling.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Product** | The result of multiplying two numbers |
| **Commutative property** | Changing the order of numbers doesn't change the product (3 × 5 = 5 × 3) |
| **Distributive property** | Breaking a number into parts and multiplying each part separately |
| **Doubling** | Multiplying by 2 |
| **Halving** | Dividing by 2 |
| **Estimate** | Finding an approximate answer by rounding |
