## Why Learn So Many Ways to Multiply?

**Kiran:** Papa, I already know my tables. Why does this chapter show so many different ways to multiply?

**Ravi:** Tell me, Kiran — if you need to go from home to school, is there only one road?

**Kiran:** No, there are two or three different routes!

**Ravi:** Exactly! Some days one route has traffic, so you take the other. Similarly, some numbers are easy to multiply one way, but hard another way. For 22 times 5, you could do it the long way — or just halve 22 to get 11, double 5 to get 10, and say 11 times 10 equals 110. Done in your head!

**Kiran:** Oh! So different strategies are like different routes — I pick whichever is fastest?

**Ravi:** Exactly. A smart mathematician is not someone who always uses the same method — it is someone who picks the best method for each problem.

## The Magic of Multiplying by 10s

**Kiran:** Papa, I noticed that 30 times 20 is just 3 times 2 with two zeros added. Is that always true?

**Ravi:** Yes! Here is the trick. When you multiply two numbers that end in zeros, first count all the zeros, then multiply the non-zero parts, and stick the zeros back on. So 30 times 20 — the non-zero parts are 3 and 2, giving 6, and there are two zeros total, so the answer is 600.

**Kiran:** What about something bigger, like 400 times 50?

**Ravi:** Non-zero parts: 4 times 5 equals 20. Total zeros from both numbers: three. So 20 followed by three zeros — that is 20,000!

**Kiran:** That is so much faster than doing it the long way. It is like a shortcut on my cycle ride to school!

**Ravi:** And just like that shortcut, it only works when you know the road well — meaning you must know your basic tables first.

## Break and Multiply — The Grid Method

**Kiran:** Papa, our teacher drew a grid on the board for 12 times 35. Why not just multiply directly?

**Ravi:** Let me show you with a real example. Suppose Amul sells 12 crates of milk, each crate holding 35 packets. You need the total packets. Now, 12 times 35 is hard to do in one step. But break 12 into 10 plus 2, and 35 into 30 plus 5 — then you get four simple multiplications: 10 times 30 is 300, 10 times 5 is 50, 2 times 30 is 60, 2 times 5 is 10. Add them all — 420 packets!

**Kiran:** It is like cutting a big rectangle into smaller pieces and finding the area of each piece!

**Ravi:** Exactly right! The grid method is actually an area model. You are finding the area of a rectangle by adding up smaller rectangles. That is why maths is so beautiful — multiplication and area are really the same idea!

**Kiran:** So the dairy farm's 453 Gir cows giving 13 litres each — I could break 453 into 400 plus 50 plus 3 and multiply each by 13?

**Ravi:** Now you are thinking like a real mathematician!

## Doubling and Halving — The Mental Maths Trick

**Kiran:** Papa, I tried the doubling and halving trick at the sabzi-wala today. He said 25 rupees per kilo for potatoes, and Mummy wanted 12 kilos. I said the answer is 300 before he could even calculate!

**Ravi:** How did you do it?

**Kiran:** I halved 12 to get 6 and doubled 25 to get 50. Then 6 times 50 is 300!

**Ravi:** Shabash! That is the power of this trick. Whenever one number is 5, 25, or 50, doubling it gives you 10, 50, or 100 — and multiplying by those is very easy.

**Kiran:** What if I keep going? 6 times 50 — I could halve 6 to 3 and double 50 to 100. Then 3 times 100 is still 300!

**Ravi:** You can keep doubling and halving as many times as you want — the product never changes. It is like pouring water from a tall glass into a wide bowl. The shape of the glass changes, but the amount of water stays the same.

## The King's Reward — When Multiplication Grows Fast

**Kiran:** Papa, in the story about the king's reward, Minister 3 started with just 1 coin but ended up with 78,125 coins! How did he beat the others who started with more?

**Ravi:** Because his coins were multiplied by 5 every day, while the others multiplied by 2 and 3. Think of it like a cricket score. If Virat scores 2 runs every ball, after 7 balls he has 14 runs. But if a batsman could somehow score 5 times his previous total on each ball — starting from 1 — he would reach lakhs!

**Kiran:** So the multiplier matters more than the starting number?

**Ravi:** Much more! This is called exponential growth. Every time you multiply, the effect gets bigger and bigger. It is why a small tree sapling eventually becomes a giant banyan — each branch grows more branches, and those grow even more.

**Kiran:** And it is also why compound interest in the bank makes money grow faster over time, right? You told me about that!

**Ravi:** Very good connection, beta! You are already thinking across chapters.

## Real Life — Waste and the Dairy Farm

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says a family of 4 makes 35 kg of kitchen waste every month. That is 420 kg in a year! Where does all that waste go?

**Ravi:** Most of it goes to landfills — huge mountains of garbage. But here is the maths that matters. If that family composts their kitchen waste, they get about 150 kg of compost worth about 24 rupees per kg. That is 150 times 24 — can you calculate?

**Kiran:** Let me use the nearest multiple trick. 150 times 24 — that is close to 150 times 25, which is 3,750. But I multiplied by 1 extra, so I subtract 150. That gives me 3,600 rupees!

**Ravi:** Perfect! So a family saves 3,600 rupees AND reduces garbage. Now imagine if all 268 families in the dairy village do this — 268 times 3,600. That is over 9.5 lakh rupees saved by the whole village, and tonnes of waste kept out of landfills.

**Kiran:** Maths can actually help save the planet!

**Ravi:** Always remember — numbers are not just for exams. They help us understand the real world and make better decisions.
