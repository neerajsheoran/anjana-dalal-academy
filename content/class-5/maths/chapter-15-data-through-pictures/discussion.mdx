## What Is Data and Why Collect It?

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says data is information. But why do we need to collect information in maths?

**Ravi:** Let me ask you a question. How many hours of TV do you think children in your class watch every day — more than 2 hours or less?

**Kiran:** I think most of my friends watch about 1 hour. But I am not sure.

**Ravi:** That is exactly the point! Without data, you are guessing. But if you actually ask all 35 students in your class and record their answers, you have data. And data turns guesses into knowledge. Maybe 12 students watch 1 hour, 8 watch 1.5 hours, 7 watch 2 hours. Now you KNOW, instead of just thinking.

**Kiran:** So data is like evidence!

**Ravi:** Exactly. Scientists collect data before making conclusions. Doctors collect data about patients before prescribing medicine. Even cricket teams collect data about batsmen — which bowlers they struggle against — to plan their strategy.

**Kiran:** Virat Kohli's batting average is data!

**Ravi:** Perfect example.

## Pictographs — Pictures That Tell Stories

**Kiran:** Papa, what is a pictograph? The chapter shows little icons representing items.

**Ravi:** A pictograph uses small pictures to show data. Each picture represents a certain number of items — that is called the scale. For example, if one cricket bat icon represents 5 bats sold, then 4 icons mean 20 bats sold.

**Kiran:** Why not just draw 20 tiny bat icons?

**Ravi:** Because that would be too many! Imagine a shop sold 100 bats — you would need 100 icons. With a scale of 1 icon equals 5, you only need 20 icons. The scale makes the pictograph manageable.

**Kiran:** What if the number does not divide evenly by the scale? Like 23 bats with a scale of 5?

**Ravi:** Great question! 23 divided by 5 is 4 with a remainder of 3. So you draw 4 full icons and a partial icon — like a little more than half a bat icon. It is an approximation, but it works well enough to show the idea.

**Kiran:** So pictographs are not perfectly precise, but they are easy to understand at a glance?

**Ravi:** Exactly right. That is the trade-off — simplicity for a small loss in precision.

## Bar Graphs — Bars That Compare

**Kiran:** Papa, how is a bar graph different from a pictograph?

**Ravi:** A bar graph uses rectangular bars instead of pictures. The height of each bar shows the value. Let me give you an example from our school canteen. Monday — 3 kg food wasted. Tuesday — 5 kg. Wednesday — 2 kg. Thursday — 8 kg. Friday — 4 kg.

**Kiran:** So Thursday's bar would be the tallest?

**Ravi:** Yes! And Wednesday's would be the shortest. The beauty of a bar graph is you can SEE the comparison immediately — which day had the most waste, which had the least, and by how much.

**Kiran:** I can also see that Thursday's bar is 4 times the height of Wednesday's bar — so Thursday had 4 times the waste!

**Ravi:** Excellent observation! That kind of visual comparison is much harder with a table of numbers. That is why bar graphs are so popular in newspapers, weather reports, and business presentations.

**Kiran:** The IPL uses bar graphs to show run rates and scores, right?

**Ravi:** Exactly! The wagon wheel showing where a batsman scored runs is a type of visual data representation too.

## Reading a Bar Graph Carefully

**Kiran:** Papa, I saw a bar graph about Raman's daily routine in the chapter. Sleeping was 9 hours, school was 7 hours, studying was 2 hours, eating and playing was 3 hours, other activities was 3 hours. How do I check if this is correct?

**Ravi:** Add all the hours — they should total 24, because that is one full day. 9 plus 7 plus 2 plus 3 plus 3 equals 24. It checks out!

**Kiran:** What if someone showed me a bar graph where the total was only 20 hours?

**Ravi:** Then either some activities are missing, or the data is wrong. Always check the total. This is called data validation — making sure the data makes sense.

**Kiran:** Can I make a bar graph of my own daily routine?

**Ravi:** Absolutely! Track what you do for a whole day, round to the nearest half hour, and draw a bar graph. You might be surprised — many students discover they spend more time on screens than they thought, and less time playing outdoors.

**Kiran:** That sounds like a useful experiment!

**Ravi:** And that is the power of data — it shows you the truth about your own life, not just what you think is happening.

## Choosing the Right Scale

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I decide the scale for a pictograph or bar graph?

**Ravi:** Look at your data. If the numbers are small — like 2, 5, 8, 3 — you can use a scale of 1. Each icon or each grid line represents 1 item. But if the numbers are large — like 50, 120, 85, 200 — a scale of 1 would need 200 icons! So you choose a scale of 10 or 20.

**Kiran:** So the scale depends on the size of the numbers?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The goal is to keep the graph readable. Too many icons or bars squished together make it hard to read. Too few make it hard to see small differences. A good scale is one where the tallest bar is about 10 to 15 grid squares high.

**Kiran:** What about the food wastage example? The numbers are 3, 5, 2, 8, 4. Those are small, so scale of 1 works?

**Ravi:** Perfect — each grid square on the vertical axis represents 1 kg. The tallest bar (Thursday, 8 kg) would be 8 squares high. That is a clean, easy-to-read graph.

**Kiran:** I think I could make bar graphs for anything now — cricket scores, marks in tests, even the number of birds I see each day!

**Ravi:** And that is exactly what data scientists do — they collect information about something they care about and use graphs to find patterns and stories hidden in the numbers.

## Food Wastage — Data for a Better World

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter shows that our school canteen wasted 22 kg of food in one week. That could have fed 66 children! That makes me sad.

**Ravi:** And that is why collecting data matters, Kiran. Without measuring, no one would know how bad the problem is. But once you have data, you can act. The school could put up a chart every week showing food wastage. Students see the numbers going down — that motivates them to waste less.

**Kiran:** We could make a bar graph and put it on the canteen wall!

**Ravi:** Now you are using data for social change. This is exactly what the government does with campaigns like Swachh Bharat — they collect data about cleanliness and display it to motivate improvement. Data is not just maths — it is a tool for making the world better.

**Kiran:** One bar graph at a time!

**Ravi:** I could not be more proud of that attitude, beta.
