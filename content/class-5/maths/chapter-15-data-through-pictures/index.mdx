## What Is Data?

**Data** is information collected about something. It could be numbers, facts, or observations.

**Examples of data:**
- How many hours children watch TV each day
- Number of two-wheelers passing your house
- How much time you spend on different activities
- Amount of food wasted in a school canteen

> Before you can understand data, you need to **collect** it, **organise** it, and **present** it clearly.

---

## Collecting and Recording Data

### Using a Table

The simplest way to organise data is a **table** (also called a frequency table).

**Example:** Hours of TV watched by 35 children:

| Hours Watching TV | Number of Children |
|------------------|--------------------|
| Half an hour | 5 |
| 1 hour | 12 |
| 1½ hours | 8 |
| 2 hours | 7 |
| More than 2 hours | 3 |

From this table you can quickly answer questions like:
- How many children watch for more than 1 hour? → 8 + 7 + 3 = **18**
- How many watch for less than 2 hours? → 5 + 12 + 8 = **25**

## Pictographs

![Sample pictograph showing toy store data with icons representing 5 items each](pictograph-example.png)

A **pictograph** uses **pictures (icons)** to represent data. Each picture stands for a certain number of items.

### What is a Scale?

When there are too many items to draw one picture for each, we use a **scale** — one picture represents multiple items.

**Example:** Joseph Uncle's toy store:
- Scale: 1 icon = 5 items
- Toys: 6 icons = **30 toys**
- Board games: 4 icons = **20 board games**
- Sports items: 5 icons = **25 sports items**

### Reading a Pictograph:

**Example:** Two-wheelers passing Deepti's house (scale: 1 icon = 3 two-wheelers):

| Day | Icons | Count |
|-----|-------|-------|
| Monday | 8 icons | 8 × 3 = **24** |
| Wednesday | 5 icons | 5 × 3 = **15** |
| Friday | 7 icons | 7 × 3 = **21** |

- Most two-wheelers: **Monday** (24)
- Fewer on Wednesday than Monday: 24 − 15 = **9 fewer**
- Total over three days: 24 + 15 + 21 = **60**

> You can choose **any scale** that makes sense — the same data can be shown with different scales!

## Bar Graphs

![Bar graph showing daily routine with bars of different heights for sleeping, school, studying, eating, and other activities](bar-graph-daily-routine.png)

A **bar graph** uses **rectangular bars** to show data. The height (or length) of each bar represents the value.

### Parts of a Bar Graph:
- **Horizontal axis** (bottom) — shows the **categories** (what is being compared)
- **Vertical axis** (side) — shows the **numbers/values**
- **Bars** — one bar for each category, with height matching the value
- **Title** — tells what the graph is about

### Example: Raman's Daily Routine

| Activity | Hours |
|----------|-------|
| Sleeping | 9 |
| Time in school | 7 |
| Studying | 2 |
| Eating and playing | 3 |
| Other activities | 3 |

In a bar graph, you'd draw 5 bars of different heights — the "Sleeping" bar would be tallest (9), and "Studying" would be shortest (2).

### How Bar Graphs are Different from Pictographs:
- **Pictographs** use pictures/icons
- **Bar graphs** use rectangular bars
- Bar graphs are easier to read when comparing values, because you can see the differences in bar heights at a glance

## Reading a Bar Graph

![Bar graph showing food wastage in school canteen across weekdays with Thursday having the tallest bar](bar-graph-food-wastage.png)

### Example: Food Wastage in School Canteen

| Day | Food Item | Wastage (kg) |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| Monday | Sandwich | 3 |
| Tuesday | Paratha | 5 |
| Wednesday | Khichdi | 2 |
| Thursday | Puri-Sabzi | 8 |
| Friday | Idli-Sambar | 4 |

From the bar graph:
- **Highest wastage:** Thursday (Puri-Sabzi) — 8 kg
- **Lowest wastage:** Wednesday (Khichdi) — 2 kg
- **Total wastage:** 3 + 5 + 2 + 8 + 4 = **22 kg**
- If 1 kg feeds 3 children: 22 × 3 = **66 children** could have been fed!

> **Think about it:** How can we reduce food wastage? Serve smaller portions, take only what you can eat, and compost leftover food.

## Making Your Own Bar Graph

To make a bar graph:

1. **Collect data** — gather the numbers
2. **Choose a scale** — decide what each mark on the vertical axis represents
3. **Draw the axes** — horizontal for categories, vertical for values
4. **Draw the bars** — one for each category, with the correct height
5. **Label everything** — both axes, the bars, and give it a title

### A Fun Activity:
Cut paper strips the length of your index finger. Paste them as bars on a graph. Compare with your friends — who has the longest finger?

## Comparing Pictographs and Bar Graphs

| Feature | Pictograph | Bar Graph |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Uses | Pictures/icons | Rectangular bars |
| Scale | 1 icon = some number | Height of bar = value |
| Best for | Small, simple data | Larger data, easy comparison |
| Reading | Count icons × scale | Read bar height |

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Data** | Information collected about something — numbers, facts, observations |
| **Pictograph** | A graph that uses pictures to represent data |
| **Bar graph** | A graph that uses rectangular bars to represent data |
| **Scale** | How many items each picture or unit represents |
| **Horizontal axis** | The bottom line of a graph — usually shows categories |
| **Vertical axis** | The side line of a graph — usually shows numbers |
| **Frequency** | How many times something occurs |
