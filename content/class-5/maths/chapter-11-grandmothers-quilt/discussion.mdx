## Perimeter — Walking the Boundary

**Kiran:** Papa, what exactly is perimeter? I keep mixing it up with area.

**Ravi:** Imagine you are an ant walking along the edge of your notebook. The total distance you walk to go all the way around — that is the perimeter. It is the boundary length.

**Kiran:** So if my notebook is 25 cm long and 18 cm wide, the perimeter is 25 plus 18 plus 25 plus 18?

**Ravi:** Yes! That is 86 cm. Or you can use the formula — 2 times length plus 2 times breadth, which is 2 times 25 plus 2 times 18 equals 50 plus 36 equals 86 cm. For a square, it is even simpler — 4 times the side length.

**Kiran:** Like the carrom board at school. If one side is 74 cm, the perimeter is 4 times 74 equals 296 cm!

**Ravi:** Perfect. Now think about the cricket ground boundary rope. That is also a perimeter — the distance around the field. When commentators say the boundary is 65 metres, they mean the distance from the pitch to the rope. But the total perimeter of the ground is the full distance around!

**Kiran:** So perimeter is basically the length of the fence you would need to go around something?

**Ravi:** That is the best real-life way to think about it.

## Area — The Space Inside

**Kiran:** And area is the space inside the boundary?

**Ravi:** Yes. If perimeter is the fence around a garden, area is the amount of grass inside the garden. The formula for a rectangle is length times breadth. So if your notebook is 25 cm by 18 cm, the area is 25 times 18 equals 450 square centimetres.

**Kiran:** Square centimetres? Why square?

**Ravi:** Because area is measured in square units. Imagine covering your notebook with tiny 1 cm by 1 cm square stickers. You would need 450 stickers — that is 450 square cm. Each sticker is one square unit.

**Kiran:** And for a square floor tile that is 30 cm on each side?

**Ravi:** Area equals 30 times 30 equals 900 square cm. To tile Nani's 3 metre by 4 metre kitchen, you need the kitchen area — 3 times 4 equals 12 square metres, which is 1,20,000 square cm. Divided by 900 square cm per tile — about 134 tiles.

**Kiran:** Maths even helps us plan home renovation!

## The Surprising Discovery — Same Area, Different Perimeter

**Kiran:** Papa, something in the chapter confused me. It says two rectangles can have the SAME area but DIFFERENT perimeters. How?

**Ravi:** Let me show you with Nani's quilt. She has 12 square patches to arrange in a rectangle. She could arrange them as 6 rows of 2 — area is 12, perimeter is 2 times 6 plus 2 times 2 equals 16. Or she could arrange them as 4 rows of 3 — area is still 12, but perimeter is 2 times 4 plus 2 times 3 equals 14. Or even 12 rows of 1 — area is still 12, but perimeter is 2 times 12 plus 2 times 1 equals 26!

**Kiran:** Wow! All three have 12 square patches but the perimeters are 16, 14, and 26. That is a huge difference!

**Ravi:** And the reverse is also true. Rectangles with the same perimeter can have different areas. If you have 16 metres of fence for a garden, you could make a 7 by 1 rectangle with area 7, or a 6 by 2 with area 12, or a 4 by 4 square with area 16!

**Kiran:** The square gives the biggest area! Is that always true?

**Ravi:** Yes! For a given perimeter, a square always encloses the maximum area. This is why many storage containers are square-shaped — they hold the most for a given amount of material.

## Finding Missing Dimensions

**Kiran:** Papa, if I know the area is 64 square metres and the length is 16 metres, how do I find the breadth?

**Ravi:** You just reverse the area formula. Area equals length times breadth, so breadth equals area divided by length. That is 64 divided by 16 equals 4 metres.

**Kiran:** Like undoing a multiplication with division!

**Ravi:** Exactly — division is the reverse of multiplication, just like in the Coconut Farm chapter. Here is a practical example: Papa wants to paint a wall that is 5 metres long. The paint tin says it covers 30 square metres. If the wall is 3 metres high, the area is 5 times 3 equals 15 square metres. So one tin covers two walls of that size.

**Kiran:** What if the wall had a window in it? Do we subtract the window area?

**Ravi:** Smart thinking! If the window is 1 metre by 1.5 metres, its area is 1.5 square metres. So the actual area to paint is 15 minus 1.5 equals 13.5 square metres. In real life, builders always account for doors and windows when calculating paint or tile requirements.

## Estimating Area of Irregular Shapes

**Kiran:** Papa, not everything is a perfect rectangle. How do I find the area of my hand or a leaf?

**Ravi:** Place your hand on a centimetre grid paper — like the one in your maths notebook. Trace around your hand with a pencil. Now count the squares inside the outline.

**Kiran:** But some squares are only half covered!

**Ravi:** Here is the rule — if more than half the square is inside, count it as 1. If less than half is inside, count it as 0. Add up all the ones and you get an approximate area. It will not be exact, but it will be close.

**Kiran:** I tried it and my hand covers about 95 square centimetres!

**Ravi:** Great! Now try a mango leaf. Geographers use this exact method on maps — they place a grid over the map of a state and count squares to estimate the area. India's total area of about 32,87,000 square kilometres was calculated using advanced versions of this method.

## Why This Chapter Matters

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we learn both area and perimeter? In what job would I actually use these?

**Ravi:** Think about it — a farmer needs to know the area of her field to buy the right amount of seeds, and the perimeter to build a fence. An architect needs the area of each room to plan furniture, and the perimeter to calculate skirting tiles. A painter calculates wall area. A gardener measures the perimeter for border plants. Even you — when you cover your textbooks with brown paper, you are estimating area!

**Kiran:** And during Diwali, when we put lights around the balcony railing?

**Ravi:** That is perimeter! The length of the light string you need equals the perimeter of the balcony. See? You already use these concepts without even realising.

**Kiran:** Now I will realise, and that makes it even more interesting!

**Ravi:** That is the whole point of learning maths, beta. Not just to solve problems in a notebook, but to understand the world a little better.
