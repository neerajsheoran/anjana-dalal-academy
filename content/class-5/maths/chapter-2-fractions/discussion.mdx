## Why Do We Need Fractions?

**Kiran:** Papa, I hate fractions. Why can't we just use normal numbers?

**Ravi:** Okay, tell me — if I buy one pizza and we need to share it equally between you, me, Mummy, and Dadi, how much does each person get?

**Kiran:** Umm... less than one pizza each?

**Ravi:** Exactly! But "less than one" isn't precise enough. If I tell the pizza-wala "give each person less than one pizza," he'll be confused! We need a way to say **exactly** how much. That's what fractions do — they describe **parts of a whole**.

**Kiran:** So each of us gets... one-fourth? 1/4?

**Ravi:** Perfect! The pizza is cut into 4 equal pieces, and each person gets 1 piece. That's 1/4. Without fractions, you couldn't even share a roti properly!

---

## Numerator and Denominator — What's on Top and Bottom?

**Kiran:** But Papa, I get confused with the top number and bottom number.

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. Mummy makes a parantha and cuts it into **5 equal pieces**. You eat **2 pieces**. The fraction is 2/5.

**Kiran:** Okay...

**Ravi:** The **bottom number** (5) tells you how many **total equal pieces** the parantha was cut into. It's called the **denominator** — think of it as the "divider." The **top number** (2) tells you how many pieces **you took**. It's called the **numerator** — think of it as the "number of pieces you have."

**Kiran:** So denominator = total cuts, numerator = pieces I took?

**Ravi:** Exactly! If Mummy cuts the parantha into 8 pieces and you eat 3, that's 3/8. Denominator 8 (total pieces), numerator 3 (pieces eaten).

**Kiran:** That's actually easy!

---

## Equivalent Fractions — Same Amount, Different Names

**Kiran:** Papa, teacher said 1/2 and 2/4 are the same. But they look completely different!

**Ravi:** Let me show you with a real example. Take a chapati. Cut it in half — you have 2 pieces, and 1 piece is **1/2**. Now take the same size chapati and cut it into 4 equal pieces. Take 2 pieces — that's **2/4**. Put them side by side. Are they the same amount of chapati?

**Kiran:** Yes! Both are half the chapati!

**Ravi:** That's equivalent fractions! **Same amount, different way of writing it.** It's like calling our city "Delhi" or "Dilli" — same place, different name.

**Kiran:** Okay, but how do I find equivalent fractions without cutting chapatis every time?

**Ravi:** Multiply both top and bottom by the same number. 1/2 — multiply both by 2 → 2/4. Multiply both by 3 → 3/6. Multiply both by 5 → 5/10. All equal to half!

**Kiran:** So 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8 = 5/10... this can go on forever?

**Ravi:** Yes! Infinite equivalent fractions for every fraction. But they all represent the same amount.

---

## Comparing Fractions — Which Is Bigger?

**Kiran:** Papa, which is more — 3/4 of a pizza or 2/4 of a pizza?

**Ravi:** Easy one first! Both have the same denominator — 4. Same-sized pieces. So who has more pieces?

**Kiran:** 3 pieces vs 2 pieces. So 3/4 is more!

**Ravi:** Good. Now a tricky one — which is more, 1/3 or 1/5?

**Kiran:** Hmm... 5 is bigger than 3, so... 1/5?

**Ravi:** Careful! Think about it this way. You and your friend both get one roti each. You cut yours into **3 equal pieces** and eat one. Your friend cuts hers into **5 equal pieces** and eats one. Whose piece is bigger?

**Kiran:** Oh! If I cut into only 3 pieces, each piece is bigger. If she cuts into 5, each piece is smaller!

**Ravi:** Exactly! **More cuts = smaller pieces.** So 1/3 > 1/5. When the numerator is the same, the fraction with the **smaller denominator** is actually bigger. It's the opposite of what you'd expect!

**Kiran:** That's confusing but makes sense when I think of rotis!

**Ravi:** Always think of rotis. Maths becomes easy with food! 😄

## Fractions Greater Than 1 — More Than a Whole

**Kiran:** Papa, can a fraction be more than 1? Like 5/4? That doesn't make sense — how can you have 5 pieces out of 4?

**Ravi:** Great question! Think of it this way. Each paratha is cut into 4 pieces. You're very hungry and you eat 5 pieces. That means you ate one full paratha (4/4 = 1) plus one extra piece (1/4). So 5/4 = 1 and 1/4, which we write as **1¼**.

**Kiran:** Ohh! So I needed more than one paratha!

**Ravi:** Yes! When the top number is bigger than the bottom, you've crossed the "one whole" mark. We call this a **mixed number** because it mixes a whole number with a fraction. Like when Dadi says, "I drank **2½ glasses** of water" — that's 2 full glasses plus half a glass. In fraction form, that's 5/2.

**Kiran:** So 5/2 means 5 halves, which is 2 full things and 1 half left over?

**Ravi:** Exactly! You're getting it, beta.

---

## The Halfway Trick

**Kiran:** Papa, teacher gave us a problem — compare 5/8 and 3/7. They have different tops AND different bottoms. I'm stuck!

**Ravi:** Here's a clever trick. Check if each fraction is more than half or less than half.

**Kiran:** How do I check that?

**Ravi:** For 5/8 — what's half of 8? It's 4. Is 5 more than 4? Yes! So 5/8 is **more than half**. For 3/7 — what's half of 7? It's 3.5. Is 3 more than 3.5? No! So 3/7 is **less than half**.

**Kiran:** So 5/8 is more because it's above half and 3/7 is below half?

**Ravi:** Correct! It's like a cricket match — if one team scored more than half the target and the other scored less, you don't need to calculate further. The one above half is ahead!

**Kiran:** That's such a cool shortcut, Papa!

**Ravi:** Maths is full of shortcuts, beta. You just need to look for patterns.
