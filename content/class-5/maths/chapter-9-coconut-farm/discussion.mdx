## Division — Multiplication's Twin

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says division is the reverse of multiplication. What does that really mean?

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. If I tell you 8 friends share 56 laddoos equally, how many does each friend get? You need to find a number that, when multiplied by 8, gives 56. That number is 7, because 8 times 7 is 56. So 56 divided by 8 is 7.

**Kiran:** So every time I divide, I am actually asking "what number do I multiply by to get this?"

**Ravi:** Exactly! That is why knowing your multiplication tables makes division easy. If you know that 7 times 9 is 63, then you instantly know 63 divided by 7 is 9, and 63 divided by 9 is 7. One multiplication fact gives you two division facts for free!

**Kiran:** It is like a buy-one-get-two offer at the store!

**Ravi:** Ha ha! The best maths deal you will ever find.

## Mental Division Strategies

**Kiran:** Papa, I find it hard to divide big numbers in my head. Like 1,248 divided by 6 — how do I even start?

**Ravi:** Split the number into friendly parts. What is the biggest number close to 1,248 that divides easily by 6?

**Kiran:** 1,200! Because 1,200 divided by 6 is 200.

**Ravi:** Perfect. Now what is left? 1,248 minus 1,200 equals 48. And 48 divided by 6?

**Kiran:** That is 8! So the answer is 200 plus 8 equals 208!

**Ravi:** See? No long division needed. You just split the number into convenient parts. Another trick — for dividing by 4, just halve twice. Try 128 divided by 4.

**Kiran:** Half of 128 is 64, half of 64 is 32. So 128 divided by 4 is 32! That is so fast!

**Ravi:** And for dividing by 8, halve three times. These shortcuts are like express trains — they get you to the answer faster.

## The Remainder — What's Left Over

**Kiran:** Papa, at a birthday party, we had 23 gulab jamuns to share among 5 friends. Everyone got 4, but there were 3 left over. That is the remainder, right?

**Ravi:** Exactly! 23 equals 5 times 4 plus 3. The formula is Dividend equals Divisor times Quotient plus Remainder. Or simply, N equals D times Q plus R.

**Kiran:** But what happens to those 3 extra gulab jamuns?

**Ravi:** Ha ha, in real life you might eat them! But in maths, the remainder tells you that 23 is not perfectly divisible by 5. The remainder must always be less than the divisor. If the remainder were 5 or more, you could give one more to each friend.

**Kiran:** So the remainder is always smaller than the divisor?

**Ravi:** Always! If you ever get a remainder equal to or bigger than the divisor, you have made a mistake. That is a great way to check your work.

## Long Division — Susie's Way vs Sunitha's Way

**Kiran:** Papa, in the chapter, Susie and Sunitha both divided 582 by 6, but Susie took many small steps while Sunitha took just two big steps. Who did it right?

**Ravi:** Both! They both got 97. The difference is efficiency. Susie subtracted 6 times 20 again and again — safe but slow. Sunitha jumped straight to 6 times 90 — faster but requires more confidence.

**Kiran:** It is like going to school. I can walk, which takes longer, or Papa can drive, which is faster. Both get me there!

**Ravi:** Perfect comparison. When you are learning, it is totally fine to take smaller steps like Susie. As you get more confident, you will naturally start taking bigger steps like Sunitha. The important thing is getting the right answer.

**Kiran:** So there is no "wrong" way as long as the maths is correct?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Your teacher wants to see your thinking, not a race. Take the steps that make sense to you.

## The Coconut Farm — Real-Life Division

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says Susie got 547 litres of coconut oil from 4,376 coconuts. That is a LOT of coconuts!

**Ravi:** Kerala and Tamil Nadu are India's biggest coconut-producing states. Let us check that division — 4,376 divided by 8 coconuts per litre. Split it: 4,000 divided by 8 is 500, and 376 divided by 8 is 47. So 500 plus 47 equals 547 litres!

**Kiran:** And Ibrahim sold tender coconuts for 35 rupees each and earned 8,890 rupees. So he sold 8,890 divided by 35 coconuts. How do I do that?

**Ravi:** Use the nearest multiple trick. 35 times 200 is 7,000. Subtract: 8,890 minus 7,000 equals 1,890. Now 35 times 50 is 1,750. Subtract: 1,890 minus 1,750 equals 140. And 35 times 4 is 140. So 200 plus 50 plus 4 equals 254 coconuts!

**Kiran:** And his profit is the selling price minus the buying price — 8,890 minus 254 times 20, which is 8,890 minus 5,080, which equals 3,810 rupees!

**Ravi:** Beta, you just did a full business calculation. Ibrahim would be impressed!

## Checking Your Division — The Golden Formula

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I know if my division answer is correct?

**Ravi:** Use the formula: Dividend equals Divisor times Quotient plus Remainder. Always plug your answer back in and check. For example, if you divided 726 by 4 and got quotient 181 with remainder 2, verify: 4 times 181 is 724, plus 2 is 726. It matches the original number, so you are correct!

**Kiran:** What if it does not match?

**Ravi:** Then you have made an error somewhere — go back and check your steps. This verification is like a batsman checking the scoreboard after a big innings. The total should always add up.

**Kiran:** I will always check using the formula from now on. It is like having an answer key for every division problem!

**Ravi:** That is exactly the right attitude. In maths, checking your work is not a waste of time — it is the smartest thing you can do.
