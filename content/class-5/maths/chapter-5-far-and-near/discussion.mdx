## Why Do We Need Different Units?

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we have so many units — mm, cm, m, km? Can't we just use one?

**Ravi:** Imagine I ask you — how tall are you? And you say, "I am 1,350 millimetres tall."

**Kiran:** That sounds weird! I'd say "I'm about 4 feet 5 inches" or "135 cm."

**Ravi:** Exactly! Now imagine I ask, "How far is Nani's house?" And you say, "It's 2,80,000 metres."

**Kiran:** That's way too many digits! I'd just say 280 kilometres!

**Ravi:** See? Different units for different sizes. Millimetres for tiny things like the thickness of a coin. Centimetres for things you can hold — pencils, books. Metres for rooms, buildings. Kilometres for distances between places. It's like how we use different containers — a spoon for sugar, a glass for water, a bucket for mopping!

**Kiran:** Oh! Each unit fits a different "size" of measurement!

**Ravi:** Perfectly said!

---

## The Magic Number: 10

**Kiran:** Papa, what's the relationship between all these units?

**Ravi:** Everything is based on **10** (or powers of 10):
- 10 millimetres = 1 centimetre
- 100 centimetres = 1 metre (that's 10 × 10)
- 1,000 metres = 1 kilometre (that's 10 × 10 × 10)

**Kiran:** So it's always multiplying by 10?

**Ravi:** Yes! That's the beauty of the metric system. "Milli" means one-thousandth, "centi" means one-hundredth, "kilo" means thousand. These prefixes are used everywhere — not just in length! You'll see them in weight (grams, kilograms) and liquids (millilitres, litres) too.

**Kiran:** So "kilo" always means 1,000? Like 1 kilogram = 1,000 grams?

**Ravi:** Exactly! Learn the prefixes and you've understood all measurement units. It's like learning one master key that opens many doors!

## Millimetres — When Small Details Matter

**Kiran:** Papa, when would I ever need millimetres? They're so tiny!

**Ravi:** Look at your ruler. See those tiny lines between each centimetre mark? Each gap is 1 millimetre. Now, have you seen how Mummy measures her jewellery?

**Kiran:** She uses that small measuring thing at the jeweller's shop.

**Ravi:** Right! Gold bangles are measured in millimetres because even 1 mm difference changes the fit. A SIM card for your phone is about 15 mm long. The pencil lead you use — 0.5 mm or 0.7 mm. Medicine tablets are a few millimetres thick. Small but important things need small, precise units!

**Kiran:** Even the screws in my spectacles are probably millimetres!

**Ravi:** Exactly! Your optician uses millimetres to fit those tiny screws perfectly.

## Converting Between Units — The Golden Rule

**Kiran:** Papa, I get confused when converting. Like, is 5 m 34 cm equal to 534 cm or 5,034 cm?

**Ravi:** Let me give you a foolproof method. Think of it like money. ₹5 and 34 paise — how many paise in total?

**Kiran:** ₹5 = 500 paise, plus 34 paise = 534 paise!

**Ravi:** Same thing! 5 m = 500 cm, plus 34 cm = **534 cm**. The rule is always the same — convert the big unit into the small unit by multiplying, then add.

**Kiran:** What about the other way? Like 6,870 metres to kilometres?

**Ravi:** Divide! 6,870 ÷ 1,000. Since 1 km = 1,000 m, you get 6 km and 870 m remaining. So 6,870 m = **6 km 870 m**. Think of it like going to the bank with 6,870 one-rupee coins and exchanging — you'd get 6 notes of ₹1,000 and 870 coins left over!

**Kiran:** So big to small = multiply, small to big = divide?

**Ravi:** That's the golden rule! Write it in your notebook and underline it twice!

## The Statue of Unity — India's Pride

**Kiran:** Papa, teacher told us about the Statue of Unity. How tall is it?

**Ravi:** **182 metres!** It's in Gujarat, and it's the tallest statue in the **entire world**. Even taller than the Statue of Liberty in America, which is 93 metres.

**Kiran:** How much taller?

**Ravi:** You tell me! 182 − 93 = ?

**Kiran:** 89 metres! That's almost double the Statue of Liberty!

**Ravi:** Right! And in centimetres, the Statue of Unity would be 18,200 cm. In millimetres, it would be 1,82,000 mm. See how the number changes depending on the unit, but the actual height stays the same?

**Kiran:** Like how ₹100 = 10,000 paise — same value, different number!

**Ravi:** Perfect comparison, beta!

## The Vivek Express — Maths on the Railway

**Kiran:** Papa, what's the longest train journey in India?

**Ravi:** The **Vivek Express** — it runs from Dibrugarh in Assam all the way to Kanniyakumari in Tamil Nadu. The total distance is **4,187 km!**

**Kiran:** Wow! How many metres is that?

**Ravi:** Let's calculate — 4,187 × 1,000 = **41,87,000 metres**! That's over 41 lakh metres!

**Kiran:** And in centimetres?

**Ravi:** 41,87,000 × 100 = **41,87,00,000 cm** — over 41 crore centimetres! See how quickly numbers get HUGE when you change units?

**Kiran:** No wonder we use kilometres for long distances. Imagine the railway ticket saying "41 crore 87 lakh centimetres"!

**Ravi:** Ha ha! The ticket would need to be bigger than the train! That's exactly why choosing the right unit matters. Kilometres for train journeys, centimetres for your height, millimetres for your jewellery.

## Why This Chapter Matters

**Kiran:** Papa, one last question. Will I really need to convert units in real life?

**Ravi:** Beta, you already do it without realising! When Mummy buys cloth, the shopkeeper measures in metres — "2 metre 50 cm" for your kurta. When the doctor measures your height, it's in centimetres — "135 cm." When you check Google Maps for Nani's house, it shows kilometres. And when Papa buys screws from the hardware shop, they're measured in millimetres!

**Kiran:** And I need to convert if I want to compare — like whether 3 km is more or less than 2,800 m.

**Ravi:** Exactly! 3 km = 3,000 m, which is more than 2,800 m. Without converting, you'd be guessing. With converting, you **know**. And knowing is always better than guessing in maths — and in life!

**Kiran:** Maths is really just... measuring the world properly, isn't it?

**Ravi:** That's a very grown-up way to put it, beta. I'm proud of you!
