## Adding Larger Numbers

In Chapter 1, we learnt to read and write 5-digit numbers. Now let's learn to **add and subtract** them — using the same place value method we already know, just with more digits!

---

## Addition Using Place Value

To add large numbers, line up the digits by their **place value** — Ones under Ones, Tens under Tens, and so on. Then add column by column from right to left.

### Example: 1,855 + 1,862

```
  Th  H  T  O
   1  8  5  5
+  1  8  6  2
-----------
   3  7  1  7
```

- Ones: 5 + 2 = 7
- Tens: 5 + 6 = 11 → write 1, carry 1
- Hundreds: 8 + 8 + 1 = 17 → write 7, carry 1
- Thousands: 1 + 1 + 1 = 3

**Answer:** The North–South Corridor from Srinagar to Kanniyakumari was **3,717 km** long!

### Adding 5-digit numbers: 21,880 + 38,900

```
  TTh  Th  H  T  O
    2   1  8  8  0
+   3   8  9  0  0
------------------
    6   0  7  8  0
```

**Answer:** Mahesh spent a total of **60,780** on his road trip.

> **Tip:** You don't need to write TTh, Th, H, T, O every time. Just make sure the digits are **aligned properly** — Ones under Ones, Tens under Tens, and so on.

## Subtraction Using Place Value

Subtraction also works column by column, from right to left. When a digit on top is smaller, **regroup** (borrow) from the next column.

### Example: 2,700 − 1,083

```
  Th  H  T  O
   2  7  0  0
−  1  0  8  3
-----------
   1  6  1  7
```

- Ones: Can't do 0 − 3, so borrow. 10 − 3 = 7
- Tens: After borrowing, 9 − 8 = 1 (we borrowed 1 from Hundreds, so Tens became 10, then we lent 1 to Ones, leaving 9)
- Hundreds: 6 − 0 = 6
- Thousands: 2 − 1 = 1

**Answer:** The ship has **1,617 km** more to travel.

> **Always check** your subtraction by adding: 1,617 + 1,083 = 2,700 ✓

### Subtracting 5-digit numbers: 15,150 − 10,228

```
  TTh  Th  H  T  O
    1   5  1  5  0
−   1   0  2  2  8
------------------
    0   4  9  2  2
```

**Answer:** **4,922 km** difference.

## Relationship Between Addition and Subtraction

Addition and subtraction are **reverse operations**. If you know one, you can find the other!

- If **46 + 21 = 67**, then **67 − 21 = 46** and **67 − 46 = 21**
- If **189 + 98 = 287**, then **287 − 98 = 189** and **287 − 189 = 98**

> This is a great way to **check your work** — after subtracting, add your answer back to see if you get the original number.

## Making Sums Equal

Here's a fun puzzle: Given two groups of numbers, can you **swap pairs** between them to make both sums equal?

**Example:**
- Group A: 1, 2, 7, 9 → sum = 19
- Group B: 3, 4, 5, 9 → sum = 21

The total of everything is 19 + 21 = 40. For equal sums, each group needs 40 ÷ 2 = **20**.

Group A is 1 short, Group B has 1 extra. So swap two numbers whose difference is 1: swap **2** (from A) with **3** (from B).
- New Group A: 1, 3, 7, 9 = 20 ✓
- New Group B: 2, 4, 5, 9 = 20 ✓

## Sums of Consecutive Numbers

**Consecutive numbers** are numbers that follow one after another without gaps: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5…

### Interesting patterns:

![Number line showing consecutive numbers with their sums highlighted](consecutive-numbers-pattern.png)

**Sum of 2 consecutive numbers:** 1+2=3, 2+3=5, 3+4=7, 4+5=9
- The sums are always **odd**!

**Sum of 3 consecutive numbers:** 1+2+3=6, 2+3+4=9, 3+4+5=12
- The sums are always **multiples of 3**
- Each sum is **3 × the middle number**!

**Sum of 4 consecutive numbers:** 1+2+3+4=10, 2+3+4+5=14, 3+4+5+6=18
- The sums are always **even**
- The difference between successive sums is always **4**

### Quick trick for adding consecutive numbers:

- **3 consecutive numbers:** Sum = **3 × middle number**
  - 67 + 68 + 69 = 3 × 68 = **204**

- **5 consecutive numbers:** Sum = **5 × middle number**
  - 48 + 49 + 50 + 51 + 52 = 5 × 50 = **250**

## Quick Sums and Differences

### Finding what to add to make 100, 1,000, or 10,000:

**Sukanta's trick:** To find 32 + ? = 100:
1. First find what makes 99: 32 + 67 = 99
2. Add 1 more: 32 + **68** = 100

**More examples:**
- 59 + **41** = 100
- 877 + **123** = 1,000
- 4,103 + **5,897** = 10,000

### Subtracting 9 or 99 quickly:

- Subtracting 9 = subtract 10, then add 1
  - 67 − 9 = 67 − 10 + 1 = **58**
  - 83 − 9 = **74**

- Subtracting 99 = subtract 100, then add 1
  - 187 − 99 = 187 − 100 + 1 = **88**
  - 763 − 99 = **664**

## Even and Odd Numbers

- **Even numbers** end in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8. They can be arranged in pairs with nothing left over.
- **Odd numbers** end in 1, 3, 5, 7, or 9. When arranged in pairs, there's always 1 left over.

### What happens when you add them?

| You add | Result is |
|---------|----------|
| Even + Even | Always **Even** |
| Odd + Odd | Always **Even** |
| Even + Odd | Always **Odd** |

> **Why?** When two odd numbers are added, the two "leftover" ones pair up, making the result even!

## Palindrome Numbers

A **palindrome number** reads the same forwards and backwards.

**Examples:** 22, 363, 404, 8558, 25,052

**Palindromes between 100 and 200:** 101, 111, 121, 131, 141, 151, 161, 171, 181, 191

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Regroup** | Exchange 10 of one unit for 1 of the next bigger unit (or vice versa) during addition/subtraction |
| **Consecutive numbers** | Numbers that follow one after another without gaps |
| **Palindrome** | A number (or word) that reads the same forwards and backwards |
| **Even number** | A number divisible by 2 (ends in 0, 2, 4, 6, 8) |
| **Odd number** | A number not divisible by 2 (ends in 1, 3, 5, 7, 9) |
| **Estimate** | A rough calculation or guess close to the actual answer |
