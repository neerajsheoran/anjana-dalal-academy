## Adding Big Numbers — It's Still Column by Column

**Kiran:** Papa, I can add small numbers easily. But when the numbers have 4 or 5 digits, I get scared and make mistakes!

**Ravi:** Don't be scared! It's the exact same method — just more columns. Think of it like eating a thali. If someone puts 3 bowls of sabzi in front of you or 5 bowls, do you eat differently?

**Kiran:** No, I eat one bowl at a time!

**Ravi:** Same with addition! You work one column at a time — Ones first, then Tens, then Hundreds, and so on. Whether it's 2 digits or 5 digits, the method doesn't change.

**Kiran:** But what about carrying? I keep forgetting to carry!

**Ravi:** Think of carrying like this. You have 10 one-rupee coins. Instead of counting "1, 2, 3... 10 coins," you exchange them for one ₹10 note. That's carrying! When any column adds up to 10 or more, you "exchange" 10 ones for 1 ten, or 10 tens for 1 hundred. You **carry** that 1 to the next column.

**Kiran:** Oh! So it's like going to the shopkeeper with 10 coins and getting a note back?

**Ravi:** Perfect analogy! Let's try: 21,880 + 38,900. Start from the right. Ones: 0 + 0 = 0. Tens: 8 + 0 = 8. Hundreds: 8 + 9 = 17 — write 7, carry 1. Thousands: 1 + 8 + 1 = 10 — write 0, carry 1. Ten Thousands: 2 + 3 + 1 = 6. Answer: 60,780!

**Kiran:** That's Papa's road trip budget, isn't it?

**Ravi:** Ha ha, something like that! 😄

## Subtraction with Borrowing — Don't Panic!

**Kiran:** Papa, subtraction is fine until I need to borrow. Like 2,700 minus 1,083 — how do I subtract 3 from 0?

**Ravi:** Great question! You can't take 3 from 0 — just like you can't give ₹3 to someone when you have ₹0 in your pocket. So what do you do?

**Kiran:** Ask someone?

**Ravi:** Exactly! You **borrow** from your neighbour — the next column on the left! The Tens column has 0 too, so it borrows from the Hundreds column. The Hundreds column has 7, so it gives 1 to the Tens column. Now Tens has 10. Then Tens gives 1 to the Ones column. Now Ones has 10, and 10 − 3 = 7.

**Kiran:** It's like a chain of borrowing? Like when I borrow a pencil from Priya, and she borrowed it from Arjun?

**Ravi:** Ha ha, exactly! The borrowing chain. And here's a tip — always **check your subtraction by adding**. If 2,700 − 1,083 = 1,617, then 1,617 + 1,083 should give back 2,700. It's like checking your own homework!

**Kiran:** That's smart! So I can catch my own mistakes!

## Addition and Subtraction Are Best Friends

**Kiran:** Papa, teacher said addition and subtraction are "reverse operations." What does that mean?

**Ravi:** Think about a train journey. You board the train at Delhi and travel 1,600 km to Mumbai. Then you return from Mumbai to Delhi — another 1,600 km. You're back where you started! Addition takes you forward, subtraction brings you back.

**Kiran:** So if 46 + 21 = 67, then 67 − 21 = 46?

**Ravi:** Exactly! They **undo** each other. Like putting on your shoes and taking them off. Wrapping a gift and unwrapping it. Depositing money in the bank and withdrawing it. One does, the other undoes.

**Kiran:** That also means if I know 287 − 98 = 189, then 189 + 98 = 287!

**Ravi:** Shabash! You can always use one to check the other. Never submit your maths exam without checking at least the big ones!

## Even and Odd — A Pattern That Never Breaks

**Kiran:** Papa, what's special about even and odd numbers? They seem boring.

**Ravi:** Boring? They're one of the most reliable patterns in maths! Tell me — at your birthday party, if 8 friends come, can everyone have a buddy to pair with?

**Kiran:** Yes! 8 is even, so 4 pairs.

**Ravi:** What if 7 friends come?

**Kiran:** One person will be left alone... because 7 is odd.

**Ravi:** Right! Now here's the magical part. Add two odd numbers — say 3 + 5. What do you get?

**Kiran:** 8. That's even!

**Ravi:** Always! Odd + Odd = Even. Why? Because each odd number has one "leftover" person without a pair. Put two groups of odd together, and those two leftover people pair up! No one is left out.

**Kiran:** What about even + odd?

**Ravi:** Try it. 4 + 3 = 7. Even + Odd always gives Odd. The even group is fully paired, but the odd group still has that one lonely leftover.

**Kiran:** And even + even?

**Ravi:** 6 + 4 = 10. Always even! Both groups are fully paired, so the combined group is fully paired too. This pattern works for ALL numbers, no matter how big. Even 1,00,000 + 1 = 1,00,001 — even + odd = odd. It NEVER breaks!

**Kiran:** Wow, maths patterns are like rules of nature!

## Consecutive Numbers — The Hidden Patterns

**Kiran:** Papa, what are consecutive numbers?

**Ravi:** Numbers that come one after another — like people standing in a line at the chai stall. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... or 47, 48, 49, 50... No gaps, no jumps.

**Kiran:** That's simple. But what's special about them?

**Ravi:** Here's a magic trick. Pick any 3 consecutive numbers.

**Kiran:** Okay — 10, 11, 12.

**Ravi:** Add them up.

**Kiran:** 10 + 11 + 12 = 33.

**Ravi:** Now multiply the **middle number** by 3.

**Kiran:** 11 × 3 = 33! Same answer!

**Ravi:** It works EVERY time! The sum of 3 consecutive numbers is always 3 times the middle one. Try 20, 21, 22.

**Kiran:** 20 + 21 + 22 = 63. And 21 × 3 = 63. It works!

**Ravi:** The reason is beautiful — one number is 1 less than the middle, one is 1 more. The "minus 1" and "plus 1" cancel out, leaving 3 times the middle. You see, maths always has a **reason** behind the magic!

## Quick Mental Maths — Impress Your Friends!

**Kiran:** Papa, Aryan can do addition in his head super fast. How?

**Ravi:** Let me teach you a trick. To subtract 99, what do you do?

**Kiran:** Umm... borrow and carry... it takes time.

**Ravi:** Forget all that! 99 is just 100 − 1. So subtracting 99 is the same as subtracting 100 and then adding 1 back! Try 763 − 99.

**Kiran:** 763 − 100 = 663... plus 1 = **664**! That was so fast!

**Ravi:** Same trick works for adding 99. Instead of adding 99, add 100 and subtract 1. Try 456 + 99.

**Kiran:** 456 + 100 = 556, minus 1 = **555**! Three fives!

**Ravi:** And for 999? Subtract 1000, add 1. For 9? Subtract 10, add 1. This trick works with any number that's close to a round number. Sabzi-wale bhaiya also uses this trick when giving change — if your bill is ₹97, he thinks "₹100 minus ₹3 change"!

**Kiran:** I'm going to use this in the mental maths test tomorrow!

**Ravi:** Now you'll be the fast one, beta! 😄
