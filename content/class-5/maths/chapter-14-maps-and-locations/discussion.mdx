## The Four Directions

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I remember which direction is which? I always mix up East and West.

**Ravi:** Here is a trick. Face the rising sun in the morning — that is East. Your left hand points North, your right hand points South, and behind you is West. You can also remember the clockwise order: N-E-S-W, which sounds like "Never Eat Soggy Waffles"!

**Kiran:** Ha ha! I will never forget that. But what if it is a cloudy day and I cannot see the sun?

**Ravi:** That is when a compass helps. A compass needle always points North because of Earth's magnetic field. Once you know North, you know all four directions. Indian sailors used compasses hundreds of years ago to navigate across the Indian Ocean.

**Kiran:** And birds like the Siberian Crane — they fly thousands of kilometres from Russia to India without any compass!

**Ravi:** Yes, migratory birds have a natural sense of direction — scientists think they use the Earth's magnetic field, the position of the sun, and even the stars to navigate. Nature's GPS system!

## Reading a Street Map

**Kiran:** Papa, when I look at a map, how do I know which way is which?

**Ravi:** Most maps follow a simple rule — North is at the top. So the right side is East, left side is West, and bottom is South. Look for a small arrow or compass symbol on the map that confirms this.

**Kiran:** OK. So if the map shows my school to the right of my house, my school is to the East?

**Ravi:** Exactly! And if you need to walk from your house to school, you would walk East. Now imagine you are at school and want to go to the park which is shown below the school on the map. Which direction is that?

**Kiran:** South! Because down on the map is South.

**Ravi:** Perfect. Now here is a tricky one — if you walk East and then turn right, which direction are you now facing?

**Kiran:** If I face East and turn right... I am now facing South!

**Ravi:** Right turns are always clockwise: N to E, E to S, S to W, W to N. And left turns are counter-clockwise. This is how bus routes are described — "go North on MG Road, turn right onto Hospital Road" means you are now heading East on Hospital Road.

## The Metro Map — Finding Routes

**Kiran:** Papa, I love looking at the Delhi Metro map. So many coloured lines! But how do I plan a journey if my start and end stations are on different lines?

**Ravi:** Let me give you an example. Say you want to go from Rajiv Chowk on the Blue Line to Botanical Garden on the Magenta Line. First, check if they share any line — they do not. So you need an interchange station where two lines cross.

**Kiran:** Like Hauz Khas? That is on both the Yellow and Magenta lines!

**Ravi:** Exactly! You could take the Blue Line to Rajiv Chowk, switch to the Yellow Line, ride to Hauz Khas, and switch to the Magenta Line to reach Botanical Garden. But there might be a more direct route — always look for the one with fewer interchanges.

**Kiran:** It is like solving a puzzle — finding the shortest path from start to finish!

**Ravi:** And this is actually a real branch of mathematics called graph theory. Metro maps, Google Maps, and even internet routing all use the same mathematical ideas to find the best path.

## Grid Coordinates — Pinpointing Exact Locations

**Kiran:** Papa, what are coordinates? The chapter says they are like an address on a grid.

**Ravi:** Think of a chessboard. If I say "move to column D, row 3," you know exactly which square I mean. Coordinates work the same way — they give the exact position using two numbers: first the horizontal position (column), then the vertical position (row).

**Kiran:** So (4, 1) means go 4 steps across and 1 step up?

**Ravi:** Exactly! Always remember — across first, then up. The trick I use is "walk along the corridor, then climb the stairs." Corridor is horizontal (across), stairs are vertical (up).

**Kiran:** Like in the zoo map example — the panda is at (1, 1) and the tortoise is at (4, 1). They are in the same row but different columns!

**Ravi:** Right. And if a parrot is at (1, 3), it is in the same column as the panda but higher up. Coordinates make it easy to describe exact positions without any confusion.

**Kiran:** Is this how GPS works too?

**Ravi:** Yes! GPS uses latitude and longitude, which are like coordinates on the globe. Our school might be at something like 28.6 degrees North, 77.2 degrees East. Same idea — two numbers to pinpoint a location on Earth.

## Giving Directions on a Grid

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter shows an ant on a grid that needs to reach sugar at a certain point. It has to crawl along grid lines. How do I find the best path?

**Ravi:** Let us say the sugar is 3 cm East and 2 cm North from the ant. The ant could go 3 East first, then 2 North. Or 2 North first, then 3 East. Or even zigzag — 1 East, 1 North, 2 East, 1 North. All these paths reach the sugar!

**Kiran:** But which is shortest?

**Ravi:** As long as the ant does not go backwards — does not go West or South — every path is the same total length: 3 plus 2 equals 5 cm. This is called the Manhattan distance, named after New York's grid-like streets.

**Kiran:** So on a grid, there is no single "shortest" path — many paths tie!

**Ravi:** Right. But in the real world, with angled roads and obstacles, the shortest path would be a straight diagonal line — about 3.6 cm using the Pythagorean theorem, which you will learn in higher classes.

**Kiran:** Now I want to learn that too!

**Ravi:** Patience, beta — one chapter at a time!

## Making Your Own Map

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter asks us to draw a map of our neighbourhood. How do I start?

**Ravi:** First, decide on North — draw a compass arrow. Then think about the key landmarks: our house, the park, the school, the temple, the sabzi-wala. Place them roughly in the right directions — school is to the East, park is to the North, and so on.

**Kiran:** Do I need to measure exact distances?

**Ravi:** Not exactly, but you should keep things proportional. If the school is twice as far as the park, show it twice as far on your map. Use a simple key — a small square for buildings, a tree symbol for the park, a cross for the hospital.

**Kiran:** Can I use a grid to make it more accurate?

**Ravi:** Absolutely! Draw a grid first and assign coordinates to each landmark. Then your map becomes a mathematical tool — anyone can find any place using coordinates. That is exactly how real cartographers make maps. You could even add coordinates like (3, 5) for school and (1, 2) for home!

**Kiran:** My own coordinate system for our neighbourhood. That is so cool!

**Ravi:** And that, beta, is the power of mathematical thinking — turning the messy real world into something organised and precise.
