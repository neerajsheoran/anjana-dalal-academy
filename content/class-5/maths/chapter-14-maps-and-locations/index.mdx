## The Four Directions

There are four main (cardinal) directions:

```
        North (N)
          ↑
West (W) ← → East (E)
          ↓
        South (S)
```

### How to find directions:
- **Face the rising Sun** — that's **East**.
- Your **left hand** points **North**.
- Your **right hand** points **South**.
- **Behind you** is **West**.

> Birds like the **Siberian Crane** fly thousands of kilometres from Siberia to India every winter — they somehow know the directions!

---

## Using Directions in Daily Life

### In Your Room:
Stand in the centre of your room and figure out which direction each wall faces. Then you can say things like:
- "My bed is to the **North**"
- "The window is on the **East** wall"
- "The cupboard is to the **West**"

### On a Street Map:
Maps usually show **North at the top**. Using this:
- Right side = **East**
- Left side = **West**
- Bottom = **South**

## Reading a Street Map

A street map shows roads, buildings, parks, and other landmarks from above.

### Following a Route:
**Example:** A bus starts from the parking area.
1. Goes **North** on the road
2. Takes a **right turn** → now heading **East** on Hospital Road to reach Stop 1
3. Takes a **left turn** → now heading **North** on Park Road to reach Stop 2

### Answering Questions from Maps:
- "Whose houses are to the East of Jaideep's house?" → Look to the right of Jaideep's house on the map.
- "Which bus stop is closer to Lali's house?" → Compare distances.
- "In which direction should Prem walk to reach Stop 2?" → Check the map.

## Reading a Metro Map

A metro map shows different **coloured lines** (routes) and **stations** where trains stop.

### Key features:
- Different colours = different metro lines (Yellow, Blue, Magenta, etc.)
- Where two lines cross = **interchange stations** (you can switch lines)
- Symbols in the key/legend explain what each marking means

### Finding Routes:
To travel from one station to another:
1. Find both stations on the map
2. See if they're on the **same line** → ride directly
3. If on **different lines** → find an interchange station to switch lines
4. There may be **multiple possible routes** — the best one usually has the fewest station changes

## Grid Maps and Coordinates

A **grid** divides a map into squares using numbered rows and columns. This makes it easy to describe **exact locations**.

### How to read grid coordinates:

Coordinates are written as **(column, row)** — always **horizontal first, then vertical**.

**Example:** To find position **(4, 1)**:
1. Start at (0, 0) — the bottom-left corner
2. Move **4 steps East** (horizontal) to reach column 4
3. Move **1 step North** (vertical) to reach row 1
4. The point where they meet is (4, 1)

### Zoo Map Example:
- **Panda** is at **(1, 1)** — column 1, row 1
- **Tortoise** is at **(4, 1)** — column 4, row 1

> **Remember:** Coordinates always go **(across, up)** — think of it as "walk along the corridor, then climb the stairs."

## Directions and the Ant Grid

On a grid, an ant can only crawl along the grid lines. We describe its path using **directions and distances**.

**Example:** To reach the sugar:
- "Crawl 3 cm towards the **East**"

**Example:** To reach the bread:
- "Crawl 2 cm **East**, then 3 cm **North**"

There can be **multiple routes** to the same destination — but the **shortest route** goes in straight lines.

## Making Your Own Map

You can make a map of any place:
1. **Choose a place** — your school, your room, your neighbourhood
2. **Decide on North** — draw a direction arrow (compass rose)
3. **Draw the outline** — buildings, roads, trees, etc.
4. **Add a key/legend** — explain what each symbol means
5. **Label important places**

> Maps use **symbols** to represent real things — a small square for a building, a tree shape for a park, etc.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Cardinal directions** | The four main directions — North, South, East, West |
| **Map** | A flat drawing that shows places from above |
| **Key / Legend** | A box on a map explaining what the symbols mean |
| **Grid** | A pattern of horizontal and vertical lines dividing a map into squares |
| **Coordinates** | A pair of numbers (column, row) that describe an exact position on a grid |
| **Route** | The path taken to get from one place to another |
| **Compass** | A tool that always points North, helping you find directions |
