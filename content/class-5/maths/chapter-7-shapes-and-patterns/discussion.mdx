## Weaving Patterns — Maths in Art

**Kiran:** Papa, I saw a woman weaving a beautiful mat at the handicraft fair. Is that really maths?

**Ravi:** Absolutely! Weaving follows a strict rule — one thread goes over, then under, then over, then under. The next row does the opposite. If you change the rule — say 2 over, 1 under — you get a completely different pattern. The weaver is really creating a mathematical pattern with her hands!

**Kiran:** So the pattern is like a code?

**Ravi:** Exactly. And different codes create different textures. The famous Banarasi sarees and Pochampalli ikats from Telangana all use specific mathematical patterns woven into the fabric. Next time you see a woven basket or a darri at home, look closely — you will see the over-under rule repeating perfectly.

**Kiran:** Maths is hiding in the most unexpected places!

## Tessellation — Covering Without Gaps

**Kiran:** Papa, why do floor tiles in our house use squares and not circles?

**Ravi:** Try placing four coins on a table so they cover the surface completely. Can you do it?

**Kiran:** No! There are always gaps between the round coins.

**Ravi:** Exactly! Circles do not tessellate — they cannot cover a flat surface without leaving gaps. But squares fit perfectly side by side. So do equilateral triangles and regular hexagons. In fact, only these three regular shapes can tessellate all by themselves.

**Kiran:** What about the honeycomb? Bees use hexagons, right?

**Ravi:** Yes! Bees figured this out millions of years before humans. Hexagons tessellate perfectly AND use the least amount of wax for the most storage space. Bees are natural mathematicians!

**Kiran:** What if I want to use pentagons?

**Ravi:** Pentagons leave gaps. But here is the clever part — you can mix shapes. Octagons plus small squares together can tile a floor beautifully. You might have seen this pattern in old Indian temples and mosques.

## Types of Triangles — Every Triangle Has a Personality

**Kiran:** Papa, are all triangles the same?

**Ravi:** Not at all! Think of triangles like people in a family. An equilateral triangle is perfectly balanced — all three sides equal, all three angles equal. It is like a person who treats everyone exactly the same.

**Kiran:** What about isosceles?

**Ravi:** An isosceles triangle has two sides equal — like a pair of matching earrings. If you fold it along its line of symmetry, the two halves match perfectly. The traffic signs on the road — that triangular "Yield" sign — is often isosceles.

**Kiran:** And scalene?

**Ravi:** A scalene triangle has no equal sides at all — every side is different, every angle is different. It is like a puzzle piece that is completely unique.

**Kiran:** So if I measure a triangle and find two sides are 5 cm each and one is 8 cm, it is isosceles?

**Ravi:** Right! And if all three are 5 cm, it is equilateral. And if they are 3, 5, and 7 — all different — it is scalene. See? Triangles tell you their name if you just measure them!

## Quadrilaterals — The Shape Family Tree

**Kiran:** Papa, I get confused between rectangles, squares, rhombuses, and parallelograms. They all look similar!

**Ravi:** Think of them as a family tree. The parallelogram is the grandparent — opposite sides are equal and parallel. Now, if you add right angles to a parallelogram, you get a rectangle. If you make all sides equal in a parallelogram, you get a rhombus. And if you do BOTH — all sides equal AND all right angles — you get a square.

**Kiran:** So a square is both a special rectangle AND a special rhombus?

**Ravi:** Exactly! Every square is a rectangle, but not every rectangle is a square. Just like every mango is a fruit, but not every fruit is a mango.

**Kiran:** What about a kite? Like the patang we fly on Makar Sankranti?

**Ravi:** A kite has two pairs of adjacent sides that are equal — the two shorter sides next to each other, and the two longer sides next to each other. Your Sankranti patang is a perfect maths example! When you make a kite at home, you are actually constructing a quadrilateral with a specific mathematical property.

## Tangram — Seven Pieces, Endless Shapes

**Kiran:** Papa, I made a cat, a house, and a rocket from just seven tangram pieces! How is that possible?

**Ravi:** The tangram is an ancient Chinese puzzle — just 2 large triangles, 1 medium triangle, 2 small triangles, 1 square, and 1 parallelogram. All cut from a single square. But the combinations are endless because you can rotate and flip each piece.

**Kiran:** It reminds me of how we rearrange furniture in a room — same pieces, totally different look!

**Ravi:** Great analogy! And here is the mathematical secret — the total area of all 7 pieces always equals the area of the original square. No matter what shape you make — a cat, a swan, or a house — the area stays the same. Only the perimeter changes.

**Kiran:** So area stays fixed but perimeter changes? That is like what we learned in the quilt chapter!

**Ravi:** You are connecting ideas beautifully, beta. That is what real maths learning looks like.

## 3D Shapes — The Painted Cube Puzzle

**Kiran:** Papa, the painted cube problem is amazing. You take 27 small cubes, glue them into a big 3 by 3 by 3 cube, paint the outside, and then ask how many small cubes have paint on different numbers of faces. I figured out 8 corner cubes have 3 painted faces, but how do I find the others?

**Ravi:** Think about where each small cube sits. Corner cubes touch three outside faces — so 3 painted faces. Edge cubes sit along the edges but not at corners — they touch exactly 2 outside faces. Count the edges of a cube — 12 edges, and each edge on a 3 by 3 by 3 cube has exactly 1 cube in the middle. So 12 edge cubes with 2 painted faces.

**Kiran:** And the faces? Each face of the big cube has one cube right in the centre that touches only that face. Six faces, so 6 cubes with 1 painted face!

**Ravi:** And the one hidden in the very centre of the big cube?

**Kiran:** Zero painted faces! It is completely surrounded. So 8 plus 12 plus 6 plus 1 equals 27 — it all adds up!

**Ravi:** That is the beauty of maths, Kiran. Every piece fits together like a perfect jigsaw puzzle.
