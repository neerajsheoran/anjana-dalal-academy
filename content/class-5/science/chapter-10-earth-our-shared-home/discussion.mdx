## Seeing Earth from Space

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter quotes Group Captain Shubhanshu Shukla saying that from space, no borders are visible on Earth. Does India really look like just one piece of a big blue ball?

**Ravi:** Yes! Astronauts say that one of the most powerful experiences of space travel is seeing Earth without borders. No lines between countries, no fences, no walls — just blue oceans, green land, brown mountains, and swirling white clouds. Wing Commander Rakesh Sharma, the first Indian in space, was asked how India looked from above. He replied: "Saare Jahaan Se Achcha" — the best in the entire world!

**Kiran:** If there are no borders in nature, why do we have countries?

**Ravi:** Countries are human creations — ways to organise ourselves and govern. But nature does not know borders. Air flows freely between countries. Rivers cross national boundaries. Birds migrate across continents. Pollution in one country affects the whole world. That is why environmental problems need global cooperation.

**Kiran:** So protecting the Earth is everyone's job, not just one country's?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Climate change, ocean pollution, and deforestation — these are problems that do not respect borders. The ancient Indian saying "Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam" — the world is one family — is more relevant today than ever before.

## Things India Gave the World

**Kiran:** Papa, I was amazed to learn that sugar, yoga, chess, and even the concept of zero came from India! What else?

**Ravi:** So much! India was the first place where people learned to make jaggery and sugar from sugarcane juice. This knowledge spread through trade to the Arab world and then to Europe. Yoga, which began in India over 3,000 years ago, is now practised in almost every country — the United Nations declared 21 June as International Day of Yoga!

**Kiran:** And zero? We invented zero?

**Ravi:** Indian mathematicians were the first to develop zero as a number and use it in calculations. Without zero, there would be no modern mathematics, no computers, no phones! The game of chess was invented in India — it was called Chaturanga. Even Snakes and Ladders originated here — it was originally a moral game about good and bad deeds.

**Kiran:** India shared so many important things with the world. Did anything come to India from other places?

**Ravi:** Of course! Chillies, tomatoes, potatoes, peanuts, and cashews all came from the Americas, brought by Portuguese traders about 400 to 500 years ago. Can you imagine Indian food without chillies?

**Kiran:** No chillies? No mirchi! That is unthinkable!

**Ravi:** Before chillies, Indians used black pepper for spiciness. And the marigold flower that we use in every puja and wedding? It originally came from Mexico!

## The Travelling Rosy Starling

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter mentions Rosy Starlings — birds that fly from Russia to India. Why do they travel so far?

**Ravi:** Rosy Starlings breed in the grasslands of southern Russia and Mongolia during summer. But when winter arrives, those regions become too cold and food becomes scarce. So the birds fly thousands of kilometres south to India, where it is warm and food is plentiful.

**Kiran:** What do they eat in India?

**Ravi:** They eat insects like locusts and grasshoppers that damage crops. Indian farmers actually welcome Rosy Starlings because they act as natural pest controllers! One flock can devour millions of crop-eating insects.

**Kiran:** So a bird from Russia helps Indian farmers grow food? That is global teamwork!

**Ravi:** Nature's version of international cooperation. The birds get food and warmth, and Indian farmers get pest control. Everyone benefits. This is why protecting migratory bird routes matters — if a wetland along their journey is destroyed, the birds cannot rest and may not survive the trip.

## Indian Cows in Brazil

**Kiran:** Papa, the story about Indian cow breeds in Brazil surprised me. How did our cows end up in South America?

**Ravi:** About 200 years ago, Portuguese traders took some Indian cows — Gir, Kankrej, and Ongole breeds — to Brazil. These cows adapted well to the hot Brazilian climate because India and Brazil have similar tropical weather. They were strong, gave good milk, and were resistant to diseases.

**Kiran:** And now most of Brazil's milk comes from Indian breeds?

**Ravi:** More than three-fourths! These Indian breeds became so important to Brazil that they appear on Brazilian stamps and coins. It is a reminder that India's agricultural heritage has influenced the world in ways most people do not realise.

**Kiran:** Just like yoga and sugar, our cows also travelled the world!

**Ravi:** And spices too. India was called the "Land of Spices." Traders sailed dangerous oceans just to buy Indian pepper, cardamom, and cinnamon. Vasco da Gama crossed entire oceans to reach India for black pepper — which was called "black gold" because it was so valuable.

## Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam — The World Is One Family

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter talks about "Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam." What does it really mean for us today?

**Ravi:** It is a Sanskrit phrase from ancient Indian texts meaning "the whole world is one family." It means we should see every person — regardless of their country, language, religion, or colour — as part of our own family. Just as you would help your cousin in need, we should care about people and nature everywhere.

**Kiran:** But in daily life, how can I practise this?

**Ravi:** When you save water at home, you are helping farmers downstream who need that water. When you plant a tree, you are creating oxygen for everyone who breathes — not just your family. When you reduce plastic use, you are protecting oceans where sea turtles and dolphins live. Every small action connects you to the whole world.

**Kiran:** And DIGIPIN — the digital address system — connects even the smallest village to the rest of India, right?

**Ravi:** Yes! DIGIPIN gives every place in India a unique 10-character code. A delivery person, an ambulance, or even the postman can find any location quickly — even in a remote village without a proper street name. Technology connecting every Indian to every other Indian.

## Caring for Our Shared Home

**Kiran:** Papa, Earth is the only planet we know that has life. What if we do not take care of it?

**Ravi:** That is the most important question of our time. Earth has supported life for billions of years — giving us air, water, food, and shelter. But human activities — burning fossil fuels, cutting forests, polluting rivers, producing mountains of plastic waste — are putting enormous pressure on our planet.

**Kiran:** What can one person do?

**Ravi:** Save water — every drop matters. Plant trees — they clean the air and give homes to birds. Reduce waste — carry your own bag, say no to single-use plastic. Walk or cycle for short distances. Choose local and seasonal foods. And most importantly — talk about it! Tell your friends, your family, your school. Change starts with awareness.

**Kiran:** The chapter says Earth is a gift we must protect. I think of it more like a home — you do not trash your own home.

**Ravi:** And this home does not just belong to us. It belongs to the elephants in the forest, the fish in the rivers, the birds in the sky, and the children who will come after us. Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam — the Earth family includes every living being, not just humans.

**Kiran:** Then I want to be a good family member to the Earth.

**Ravi:** You already are, beta. By learning, by caring, and by acting — you are already making a difference.
