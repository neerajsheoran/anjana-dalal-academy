## What Is Energy?

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher says energy is what makes things happen. But that sounds so vague! Can you explain it better?

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. When you kick a football, the ball moves — your legs provided movement energy. When Mummy turns on the stove, the gas burns and heats the tawa — that is heat energy. When you switch on a light, the bulb glows — that is light energy. Energy is the ability to cause a change — to move something, heat something, light something, or make a sound.

**Kiran:** So without energy, nothing would move, no lights would glow, and no food would cook?

**Ravi:** Exactly! The entire world would be completely still, dark, and cold. Energy is literally what makes the universe alive. And here is the amazing part — energy is never created or destroyed. It only changes from one form to another.

**Kiran:** Like when I eat food, the chemical energy in the food becomes movement energy when I run?

**Ravi:** Perfect example! Your body is an energy-converting machine. Food energy becomes movement, heat (that is why you feel warm after running), and even sound (when you shout "goal!").

## Sources of Energy — Food, Fuel, and Electricity

**Kiran:** Papa, if I skip breakfast, I feel so tired by lunch. Why?

**Ravi:** Because your body runs on food energy. Just like a car needs petrol to run, your body needs food. When you eat, your body converts food into a form of energy your muscles and brain can use. Skip a meal, and your body has less fuel — so you feel weak and tired.

**Kiran:** Is that why athletes eat special diets?

**Ravi:** Yes! A cricket player like Virat Kohli needs more energy than someone sitting at a desk all day. So athletes eat foods rich in carbohydrates and proteins. But everyone needs a balanced diet — the right mix of roti, dal, sabzi, fruits, and milk.

**Kiran:** The chapter also talks about fuel for vehicles. Is petrol similar to food?

**Ravi:** In a way, yes! Both are sources of stored chemical energy. When petrol burns in an engine, it releases heat and movement energy that makes the car go. When food is "burned" (digested) in your body, it releases energy for your activities. The difference is — burning petrol creates pollution, but digesting food does not pollute the air!

**Kiran:** So fuel for vehicles is not as clean as food for our bodies?

**Ravi:** Much dirtier. That is why scientists are developing electric vehicles that run on batteries charged by clean energy from the sun and wind.

## Electricity — Powerful but Dangerous

**Kiran:** Papa, we use electricity for everything — fans, lights, fridge, TV, charging phones. Where does all this electricity come from?

**Ravi:** Most of India's electricity comes from power plants that burn coal. Coal heats water, the steam spins a turbine, and the turbine generates electricity. But burning coal creates smoke and harmful gases that pollute the air and contribute to climate change.

**Kiran:** Is there a cleaner way?

**Ravi:** Yes! Solar panels convert sunlight directly into electricity. Wind turbines use wind energy. Hydroelectric dams use flowing water. India has some amazing clean energy projects — the Bhadla Solar Park in Rajasthan is one of the largest solar power plants in the world. And the Cochin International Airport in Kerala is the world's first airport running entirely on solar energy!

**Kiran:** The chapter also says electricity can be dangerous. How do I stay safe?

**Ravi:** Never touch switches or plugs with wet hands — water conducts electricity and you could get a shock. Never put fingers or metal objects into electrical sockets. Stay away from fallen electric wires — they could be live. And if you see anything that looks unsafe — a sparking wire, a broken socket — tell an adult immediately. Never try to fix it yourself.

## Clean Energy — Sun, Wind, and Water

**Kiran:** Papa, why is solar energy considered "clean"?

**Ravi:** Because the sun shines for free and producing solar electricity creates no smoke, no waste, and no pollution. Compare that to a coal plant — you need to dig coal from the ground, transport it, burn it (creating tonnes of smoke and ash), and then deal with the waste. Solar panels just sit quietly on a rooftop, converting sunlight into electricity.

**Kiran:** What about on cloudy days? Do solar panels stop working?

**Ravi:** They produce less electricity on cloudy days but do not stop completely. And we can store solar energy in batteries for use at night. This technology is getting better and cheaper every year.

**Kiran:** The chapter mentions watermills called gharaats in hilly areas. Those are ancient, right?

**Ravi:** Yes! In Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand, people have used gharaats for centuries to grind wheat and rice using the energy of flowing streams. No electricity, no fuel — just water power. It is the same principle as a modern hydroelectric dam, just on a smaller scale.

**Kiran:** So our ancestors were already using clean energy!

**Ravi:** Absolutely. And ancient Indian architecture used clever designs — large windows for natural light, thick walls for insulation, courtyards for ventilation — all to use less energy. Modern "green buildings" are rediscovering these same principles.

## Energy Efficiency — Getting More from Less

**Kiran:** Papa, what does "energy efficiency" mean?

**Ravi:** It means getting the same result using less energy. The best example is LED bulbs. An old incandescent bulb uses 60 watts of electricity but wastes most of it as heat — only a small fraction becomes light. An LED bulb gives the same brightness using just 9 watts. Same light, one-sixth the electricity!

**Kiran:** So if we replace all the old bulbs in our house with LEDs, we save a lot of electricity?

**Ravi:** Yes! If you have 10 bulbs running 6 hours a day, switching from 60-watt bulbs to 9-watt LEDs saves about 300 watt-hours per day — that is about 110 units of electricity per year. At 8 rupees per unit, you save around 880 rupees just on lighting!

**Kiran:** That is almost enough to buy a new cricket bat!

**Ravi:** And multiply that by crores of homes across India — the energy savings are enormous. Small individual actions create massive collective impact.

## Saving Energy Every Day

**Kiran:** Papa, besides LED bulbs, what else can I do to save energy?

**Ravi:** Switch off lights and fans when you leave a room — sounds simple, but most people forget. Open curtains during the day and use natural sunlight instead of electric lights. Walk or cycle for short distances instead of asking for a car ride. Wear a sweater at home in winter instead of using a heater.

**Kiran:** At school, I notice fans running in empty classrooms all the time.

**Ravi:** If your class has 3 fans at 75 watts each, running them in an empty room for 2 hours wastes 450 watt-hours of electricity. Over a school year, that adds up to a huge amount. You could be the one who makes sure fans are switched off when the class goes for sports or assembly.

**Kiran:** The sun gives more energy to Earth in one hour than all humans use in a whole year. If we could capture even a tiny bit of that, we would never need coal!

**Ravi:** That is the dream, beta. And India is working towards it with massive solar energy investments. The future is bright — quite literally!
