## What Makes a School "Green"?

**Kiran:** Papa, two students in the chapter — Aasha and Mohit — want to make their school a "green school." What does that mean exactly?

**Ravi:** A green school is one where everyone works together to manage waste, save water, use electricity wisely, and make the campus full of trees and plants. It is not just about painting walls green — it is about changing daily habits.

**Kiran:** Like what?

**Ravi:** Simple things that add up! Switching off fans and lights when you leave a room. Separating wet and dry waste into different bins. Reporting leaking taps instead of ignoring them. Planting trees and taking care of them. And treating everyone — classmates, teachers, and staff — with kindness and respect.

**Kiran:** Our school has a "Green Team" too! We check if classrooms are clean and bins are used properly.

**Ravi:** That is wonderful. The Swachh Bharat Swachh Vidyalaya programme encourages exactly this — students becoming responsible for their school environment. Mawlynnong village in Meghalaya is called "Asia's Cleanest Village" because every person takes responsibility for keeping it clean. A school can do the same!

## Waste Management — Wet, Dry, and Beyond

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we need two different bins — green for wet waste and blue for dry waste? Why not just one big bin?

**Ravi:** Because wet waste and dry waste have completely different futures. Wet waste — like food peels, tea leaves, and leftovers — can be composted. You put it in a compost pit, and over time, microbes break it down into rich soil fertiliser. But if you mix a plastic bottle with banana peels, the plastic contaminates the compost and neither can be processed properly.

**Kiran:** What about dry waste?

**Ravi:** Paper, plastic bottles, metal cans, and glass can be recycled. The kabadiwala collects these and sends them to recycling units where they become new products. An old newspaper can become a new notebook. A used plastic bottle can become a school bag.

**Kiran:** But I see people just throwing everything into one bin. Does it matter?

**Ravi:** It matters hugely. When waste is mixed, most of it ends up in landfills — those massive garbage mountains you can sometimes smell from a distance. Separating waste at the source — in your classroom or home — is the single most important step. It is also a sign of respect for the sanitation workers who handle our waste.

## Saving Water at School

**Kiran:** Papa, a dripping tap does not seem like a big deal. It is just a few drops.

**Ravi:** Let us do the maths. A slow drip loses about 1 litre per hour. That is 24 litres per day. In a month — about 720 litres. In a year — about 8,760 litres! One dripping tap wastes nearly 9,000 litres per year.

**Kiran:** That is enough water for a family for weeks!

**Ravi:** Now imagine your school has three dripping taps. That is 27,000 litres wasted. Fixing those taps costs almost nothing but saves thousands of litres. This is why the "Water Watchers" team in the chapter is so important — they walk around checking for leaks.

**Kiran:** I will report the dripping tap near the library tomorrow. I have been walking past it for weeks!

**Ravi:** That is leadership, Kiran — seeing a problem and doing something about it. And you know what else helps? Rainwater harvesting. If your school collects rainwater from its large roof, that water can be stored and used for the garden, or directed into the ground to recharge groundwater.

## Keeping Cool the Smart Way

**Kiran:** Papa, why are some classrooms cooler than others even without AC?

**Ravi:** Several reasons! Rooms with big windows that allow cross-ventilation — breeze flowing in from one side and out the other — stay naturally cool. Rooms shaded by trees are cooler than those in direct sunlight. And here is a fun fact: white or light-coloured roofs reflect sunlight, while dark roofs absorb heat.

**Kiran:** Like how wearing a white t-shirt in summer feels cooler than a black one?

**Ravi:** Exactly the same principle! In Rajasthan, many houses are painted white or light blue — it keeps them cooler in the extreme heat. And in Ladakh, schools and homes sometimes keep animals on the ground floor during winter. The body heat from the animals rises up and warms the rooms above — a clever natural heating system!

**Kiran:** Nature's air conditioning and heating — no electricity needed!

**Ravi:** Traditional Indian architecture — Vaastu Shastra — understood all this thousands of years ago. Courtyards allowed hot air to rise and escape, while thick walls kept the inside cool. Sometimes the best technology is the oldest technology.

## Fire Safety and Traffic Safety

**Kiran:** Papa, we had a fire drill at school last week. I was confused about what to do. Can you explain?

**Ravi:** Fire drills save lives because they teach you the right response BEFORE an emergency. Rule one — do not panic. Walk quickly to the nearest exit, do not run. Rule two — crawl low if there is smoke, because cleaner air is near the floor. Cover your mouth with a cloth. Rule three — never hide in cupboards or bathrooms. Rule four — once you are out, go to the assembly point and NEVER go back inside.

**Kiran:** What about helping others?

**Ravi:** Help if it is safe — guide younger children, support someone who is scared. But never put yourself in danger. And always tell a teacher if someone is missing. The most important thing is that everyone gets out safely.

**Kiran:** What about road safety near school?

**Ravi:** Always use the pedestrian crossing. Look left, then right, then left again before crossing. Never run across the road. And if you ride a bicycle, wearing a helmet is not optional — it is essential. A helmet can be the difference between a small bump and a serious injury.

## Being Kind — The Most Important Rule

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter says a happy school is not just about clean buildings. It is about how we treat each other. Why is that in a science book?

**Ravi:** Because well-being is a science too! Studies show that students learn better when they feel safe, respected, and happy. Bullying, teasing, and being left out cause real stress — it affects how the brain works. A kind word can change someone's entire day.

**Kiran:** I saw a new student last week who was sitting alone at lunch. I invited her to sit with us, and now she is in our friend group!

**Ravi:** That is exactly what the chapter is about. Waiting for your turn in queues, using kind words, not teasing others, keeping shared spaces clean — these are habits that make everyone around you happier. And here is the beautiful part — kindness is contagious. When you are kind to one person, they are more likely to be kind to someone else.

**Kiran:** So one act of kindness can spread through the whole school?

**Ravi:** Like ripples in a pond, beta. You throw one small stone, and the ripples reach every shore.
