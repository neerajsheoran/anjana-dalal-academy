## How Little Freshwater Do We Really Have?

**Kiran:** Papa, we learned that most of Earth is covered with water. So why do people keep saying "save water"?

**Ravi:** Here is a way to understand it. If all the water on Earth could fit inside a 200 ml glass — like the steel glass you drink water from — the freshwater we can actually use would be just one teaspoon. About 5 ml out of 200!

**Kiran:** That is less than 3 percent! But where is all the rest?

**Ravi:** Most of it is salty ocean water — you cannot drink it or use it for farming. Some freshwater is locked away in glaciers and ice caps, far from where people live. The tiny fraction left is in rivers, lakes, and underground. That is what all 800 crore people on Earth share.

**Kiran:** Now I understand why even a leaking tap matters.

**Ravi:** A tap dripping once per second wastes about 10,000 litres a year. That is enough drinking water for a family of four for nearly two months! Numbers tell the real story.

## The Three Forms of Water

**Kiran:** Papa, I know water can be ice, liquid, and steam. But how does the same thing become three different forms?

**Ravi:** It is all about temperature and energy. Think of water molecules as tiny people. When they are cold, they hold hands tightly and stand still — that is ice. When they warm up, they start moving around but stay close — that is liquid water. When they get really hot, they run away from each other and fly off — that is steam!

**Kiran:** So heating gives them more energy to move?

**Ravi:** Exactly! When Mummy boils water for chai, the water molecules get so much energy that they escape as steam. And when you put water in the freezer, they slow down so much that they lock together into ice crystals.

**Kiran:** Is that why ice floats? I saw ice cubes floating in my lemon water.

**Ravi:** Great observation! Ice is actually less dense than liquid water — the molecules arrange themselves in a pattern that takes up more space. This is very unusual — most solids sink in their own liquid form. But ice floating is a gift of nature. If ice sank, lakes and rivers would freeze from the bottom up, killing all the fish!

## The Water Cycle — Nature's Recycling System

**Kiran:** Papa, where does rain actually come from? Is there a giant tank in the sky?

**Ravi:** Ha ha, no! The sun heats up water in rivers, lakes, and oceans. This water evaporates — turns into invisible water vapour that rises up into the sky. High up, the air is cold, so the vapour cools and forms tiny droplets that clump together as clouds. When the clouds get heavy enough, the water falls back as rain!

**Kiran:** So the water in my glass might have once been in the Ganga or even the ocean?

**Ravi:** In fact, the water you drink today might have been rain in Rajasthan last month, part of the Bay of Bengal last year, or even drunk by a dinosaur millions of years ago! Water is never created or destroyed — it just keeps cycling through the water cycle.

**Kiran:** Dinosaur water! That is amazing!

**Ravi:** And it is exactly why we must keep our water clean. Whatever we put into rivers and oceans eventually comes back to us through rain.

## Groundwater — The Hidden Treasure

**Kiran:** Papa, when we visited Nani's village, they got water from a borewell. Where does that water come from?

**Ravi:** When rain falls, some water flows into rivers and some seeps down through the soil. It travels through layers of soil and rock until it reaches a layer it cannot pass through. The water collects there — underground. This is groundwater, and it is like a hidden reservoir beneath our feet.

**Kiran:** But in our city, the roads are all concrete and tar. Can water seep through concrete?

**Ravi:** That is exactly the problem! In villages with open soil, rainwater naturally recharges groundwater. But in cities like Delhi and Bengaluru, the concrete prevents water from seeping in. So groundwater levels keep falling because we pump it out but do not refill it.

**Kiran:** Is there a solution?

**Ravi:** Yes! Rainwater harvesting — collecting rainwater from rooftops and directing it into the ground through soak pits. In Rajasthan, villagers build traditional structures called johads to collect rainwater. Hiware Bazar village in Maharashtra went from drought to water abundance using community-led rainwater harvesting. These are examples of how ancient Indian wisdom solves modern problems!

## Life in and Around Water

**Kiran:** Papa, I saw a dragonfly near the pond in the park. Does it actually live in water?

**Ravi:** Dragonflies lay their eggs in water, and the babies — called nymphs — live underwater for months! When they grow up, they climb out and become the beautiful flying insects you see. They eat mosquitoes, so they are very helpful.

**Kiran:** And the lotus? Its roots are in the muddy water but the flower is so clean and beautiful.

**Ravi:** That is because lotus leaves have a special waxy coating that repels water. Mud and water just roll off. Scientists actually studied lotus leaves to invent self-cleaning surfaces! And did you know — the lotus is our national flower, and it can be found in ponds across India.

**Kiran:** The chapter mentions the Fishing Cat. A cat that swims?

**Ravi:** Yes! The Fishing Cat has partially webbed paws and dives right into the water to catch fish. It lives in India's wetlands and mangrove forests. It shows that nature has adapted animals to every environment — even cats that swim!

## Saving Water — Every Drop Counts

**Kiran:** Papa, what can I actually do to save water? I am just one person.

**Ravi:** Let me give you the maths. If you turn off the tap while brushing, you save about 6 litres of water twice a day — that is 12 litres per day. In a year, that is 12 times 365 equals 4,380 litres! Now imagine if all 40 students in your class did the same — that is 40 times 4,380 equals 1,75,200 litres saved in one year, just from brushing!

**Kiran:** Almost 2 lakh litres from one simple habit?

**Ravi:** Yes! And reusing water is just as powerful. The water from washing vegetables can water your plants. The water from rinsing rice is actually nutritious for plants. Every little action adds up.

**Kiran:** I will start by timing my baths and making sure nobody leaves taps running at school.

**Ravi:** And you can keep a "water diary" — track how much water your family uses each day. Once you see the numbers, you will find creative ways to reduce them. Data drives change, beta — you learned that in maths too!
