## Why Does Food Spoil?

**Kiran:** Papa, I forgot my tiffin box in school over the weekend. When I opened it on Monday, there were coloured patches on my paratha and it smelled awful! What happened?

**Ravi:** Those coloured patches are mould — a type of microbe. Microbes are tiny living things, so small you need a microscope to see them. They are everywhere — in the air, in water, on your hands, even inside your body. When they land on food, they start eating it and growing. That changes the colour, smell, and taste of the food.

**Kiran:** But why did my paratha spoil in just two days while Nani's pickle lasts for months?

**Ravi:** Microbes need three things to grow: moisture, air, and warmth. Your paratha had all three — it was moist, exposed to air, and the classroom was warm. But Nani's pickle is covered in oil, which keeps air out. It also has salt and spices that fight microbes. Without air and with all that salt, microbes cannot grow easily.

**Kiran:** So if we remove even one of those three things, food stays fresh longer?

**Ravi:** Exactly! That is the whole secret of food preservation.

## Food Preservation — Ancient Indian Wisdom

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter talks about drying, pickling, and refrigeration. Which method is the oldest?

**Ravi:** Drying! Long before refrigerators existed, people in India dried chillies, fish, and papads under the sun. When you remove moisture, microbes cannot grow. That is why dried food lasts for months. Aam papad — your favourite — is just mango pulp dried in the sun.

**Kiran:** And pickling uses oil?

**Ravi:** Yes! When Mummy makes mango pickle, she coats the mango pieces in mustard oil. The oil creates a seal that keeps air away from the food. Salt and turmeric in the pickle also slow down microbe growth. Some Indian pickles last for years!

**Kiran:** What about adding sugar? Like in the murabba Nani makes?

**Ravi:** Sugar draws moisture out of the fruit, creating an environment where microbes cannot survive. That is why jams, murabbas, and jellies last so long. Even honey never spoils — it has been found in Egyptian tombs that are thousands of years old, and it was still edible!

**Kiran:** Ancient Egyptians and ancient Indians — both figured out preservation without any science lab!

**Ravi:** Their kitchen was their science lab. Traditional knowledge is often brilliant science.

## Good Microbes — Our Tiny Helpers

**Kiran:** Papa, if microbes spoil food, are they all bad?

**Ravi:** Not at all! Some microbes are our best friends. When Mummy adds a spoonful of old curd to warm milk and leaves it overnight, what happens?

**Kiran:** It becomes fresh curd by morning!

**Ravi:** That is because the good bacteria in the old curd multiply in the warm milk and turn it into curd. They change the milk's protein structure, making it thicker and slightly sour. Without these friendly microbes, we would have no curd, no dahi, and no lassi!

**Kiran:** What about idli batter? It rises overnight.

**Ravi:** Yes! Wild microbes from the air enter the batter and start fermenting it. They produce tiny gas bubbles that make the batter rise and give idlis their fluffy texture. Dosa, appam, dhokla — all these foods depend on friendly microbes.

**Kiran:** And the chapter says good microbes in our stomach help us digest food?

**Ravi:** Your gut has trillions of helpful bacteria — more bacteria than there are cells in your body! They break down food, produce vitamins, and even protect you from harmful microbes. So next time you eat curd, thank the tiny bacteria doing all the work.

## Seasonal Fruits — Why Timing Matters

**Kiran:** Papa, why does Mummy insist on eating fruits "in their season"? Mangoes are available in winter too — imported from other countries.

**Ravi:** Seasonal fruits are picked when they are perfectly ripe and full of nutrients. An Indian mango in June is sweet, juicy, and full of vitamins because the summer sun helped it grow naturally. But an imported mango in January was likely picked early, stored in cold rooms, and transported thousands of kilometres. It may look good but lacks the flavour and nutrition.

**Kiran:** So seasonal fruits are tastier AND healthier?

**Ravi:** Yes, and also better for the environment. Transporting fruits from far away uses fuel and creates pollution. Local, seasonal fruits travel shorter distances and are often grown by farmers near your city.

**Kiran:** In summer we eat mangoes, lychees, and watermelon. In winter, we eat amla, oranges, and guava. What about monsoon?

**Ravi:** Plums, peaches, and pomegranates! India is one of the largest fruit producers in the world — we grow over 90 types of fruits across different seasons and regions. That is the beauty of our diverse climate.

## Our Teeth — Tools for Eating

**Kiran:** Papa, I learned that we have four types of teeth. Why not just one type?

**Ravi:** Think of your teeth as a toolkit. Your front teeth — incisors — are flat and sharp, like a knife for cutting. You use them to bite into an apple. Your canines — the pointed ones next to the incisors — are for tearing, like pulling apart a chapati. Your premolars crush food, and your big molars at the back grind it into a paste.

**Kiran:** Like a factory assembly line! Cut, tear, crush, grind.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Each tooth has a specific job. If you swallow food without chewing properly, your stomach has to work much harder. Chewing also mixes saliva with food, and saliva starts the digestion process right in your mouth.

**Kiran:** Is that why we are told to chew each bite 20 to 30 times?

**Ravi:** Yes! Eating slowly gives your brain time to realise you are full, so you do not overeat. It prevents choking too. Never talk or laugh with food in your mouth — that is how food can go into the wrong pipe and cause trouble.

## Taking Care of Our Teeth

**Kiran:** Papa, I had a cavity last year. The dentist said bacteria caused it. But I brush every day!

**Ravi:** Brushing once is not enough — you need to brush twice, morning and night. When you eat sweets or sticky food, tiny food particles stay on your teeth. Bacteria in your mouth feast on these particles and produce acid. This acid slowly eats away the tooth surface, creating a cavity — a small hole.

**Kiran:** So the bacteria make acid that dissolves my tooth? That sounds scary!

**Ravi:** It is preventable, though. Brush twice, rinse your mouth after every meal, and eat fewer sticky sweets. The fluoride in toothpaste actually strengthens your tooth surface against acid. And visiting the dentist every six months catches problems early.

**Kiran:** Nani uses a neem twig instead of a toothbrush. Does that work?

**Ravi:** Neem has natural antibacterial properties — it kills harmful bacteria in the mouth. This traditional practice has been used in India for centuries. Modern science confirms that neem is effective! But whether you use a toothbrush or neem twig, the important thing is cleaning your teeth properly, twice a day.
