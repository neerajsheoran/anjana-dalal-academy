## Introduction

The word **geometry** comes from the Greek words 'geo' (earth) and 'metrein' (to measure). Geometry originated from the practical need of measuring land. It was studied in various forms in every ancient civilisation -- Egypt, Babylonia, China, India, Greece, and more.

In Egypt, the annual flooding of the Nile required redrawing of field boundaries, leading to the development of geometric techniques. In India, the **Sulbasutras** (800-500 BCE) served as manuals for geometrical constructions used in Vedic rituals. While these civilisations developed geometry practically, it was the Greeks who emphasised logical reasoning and proof.

**Euclid** (325 BCE - 265 BCE), a teacher of mathematics at Alexandria in Egypt, collected all known geometric work and arranged it systematically in his famous treatise called **Elements**, consisting of 13 books (chapters). This work influenced the world's understanding of geometry for generations.

> **Key idea:** Euclid's approach was revolutionary because he started with a few basic assumptions (axioms and postulates) and used logical reasoning to derive all other results (theorems).

---

## Euclid's Definitions

Euclid began with **23 definitions** in Book 1 of his Elements. Some important ones:

1. **A point** is that which has no part.
2. **A line** is breadthless length.
3. The ends of a line are points.
4. **A straight line** is a line which lies evenly with the points on itself.
5. **A surface** is that which has length and breadth only.
6. The edges of a surface are lines.
7. **A plane surface** is a surface which lies evenly with the straight lines on itself.

The progression from solids to points involves losing one dimension at each step:

* A **solid** has 3 dimensions (length, breadth, height)
* A **surface** has 2 dimensions (length, breadth)
* A **line** has 1 dimension (length)
* A **point** has 0 dimensions

> **Key idea:** Some terms like "point", "line", and "plane" are left as **undefined terms** in modern mathematics, because defining them leads to an endless chain of definitions. We understand them intuitively.

## Euclid's Axioms

**Axioms** (or common notions) are basic assumptions that are self-evidently true and used throughout mathematics. Euclid stated seven axioms:

1. **Things which are equal to the same thing are equal to one another.** (If A = C and B = C, then A = B.)
2. **If equals are added to equals, the wholes are equal.** (If A = B, then A + C = B + C.)
3. **If equals are subtracted from equals, the remainders are equal.** (If A = B, then A - C = B - C.)
4. **Things which coincide with one another are equal to one another.** (Principle of superposition.)
5. **The whole is greater than the part.**
6. **Things which are double of the same things are equal to one another.**
7. **Things which are halves of the same things are equal to one another.**

---

## Euclid's Postulates

**Postulates** are assumptions specific to geometry. Euclid stated five postulates:

**Postulate 1:** A straight line may be drawn from any one point to any other point.

This implies that at least one straight line passes through any two distinct points. In modern terms:

> **Key idea (Axiom 5.1):** Given two distinct points, there is a **unique** line that passes through them.

**Postulate 2:** A terminated line can be produced indefinitely.

A "terminated line" is what we now call a **line segment**. This postulate says a line segment can be extended on either side to form a full line.

**Postulate 3:** A circle can be drawn with any centre and any radius.

**Postulate 4:** All right angles are equal to one another.

**Postulate 5:** If a straight line falling on two straight lines makes the interior angles on the same side of it taken together less than two right angles (180 degrees), then the two straight lines, if produced indefinitely, meet on that side on which the sum of angles is less than 180 degrees.

> **Key idea:** Postulate 5 is the most complex of the five. It essentially deals with parallel lines -- if the interior angles sum to exactly 180 degrees, the lines are parallel and never meet.

## Theorems from Euclid's Axioms

Using definitions, axioms, and postulates, Euclid proved 465 propositions (theorems) through logical deduction.

### Example: Proving AB + BC = AC

If A, B, and C are three points on a line with B between A and C, then AC coincides with AB + BC. By Axiom 4 (things that coincide are equal), we conclude **AB + BC = AC**.

### Example: Constructing an equilateral triangle

Given any line segment AB:

1. Draw a circle with centre A and radius AB (Postulate 3).
2. Draw a circle with centre B and radius BA (Postulate 3).
3. Let the circles meet at point C.
4. Since AB = AC (radii of circle centred at A) and AB = BC (radii of circle centred at B), by Axiom 1, AB = BC = AC. So triangle ABC is equilateral.

### Theorem 5.1: Two distinct lines cannot have more than one point in common.

**Proof:** Suppose two lines intersect at two distinct points P and Q. Then there would be two lines passing through the same two points, which contradicts Axiom 5.1 (a unique line passes through two distinct points). Therefore, two distinct lines can have **at most one** point in common.

## Important Results

* **If a point C lies between A and B such that AC = BC**, then AC = AB/2. This means C is the **midpoint** of AB.
* **Every line segment has one and only one midpoint.** (Proved using Euclid's axioms.)
* **If AC = BD on a line segment ABCD**, then AB = CD. (Proved using Axiom 3: if equals are subtracted from equals, the remainders are equal.)

---

## Summary

1. **Undefined terms:** Point, line, and plane are taken as undefined in modern geometry.
2. **Axioms/Postulates** are unproved assumptions accepted as universal truths.
3. **Theorems** are proved statements using definitions, axioms, and deductive reasoning.
4. Euclid's axioms include: equals of equals are equal, the whole is greater than the part, and things that coincide are equal.
5. Euclid's five postulates cover: drawing lines, extending segments, drawing circles, equality of right angles, and the parallel postulate.
6. Two distinct lines cannot have more than one common point.
