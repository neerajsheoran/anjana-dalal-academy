## Introduction

You have already studied how to locate a point on a number line. But in many real-life situations, we need to describe the position of a point in a **plane** -- which requires **two** pieces of information. For instance, to find a house on a street map, you need both the street number and the house number. This idea of describing positions in a plane gave rise to an important branch of mathematics called **Coordinate Geometry**, developed by the French mathematician **Rene Descartes** (1596-1650).

> **Key idea:** The position of any point in a plane can be described using two perpendicular reference lines and the distances of the point from these lines.

---

## The Cartesian System

The **Cartesian coordinate system** (named after Descartes) is set up by drawing two perpendicular number lines in a plane.

### The axes

* The **horizontal** number line is called the **x-axis** (denoted XX').
* The **vertical** number line is called the **y-axis** (denoted YY').
* The point where the two axes intersect is called the **origin**, denoted by **O**.

The positive direction of the x-axis is to the right of the origin, and the positive direction of the y-axis is above the origin.

![Cartesian plane showing x-axis, y-axis, origin O, and the four quadrants labeled I, II, III, IV with sign conventions](cartesian-plane-quadrants.png)

### The four quadrants

The coordinate axes divide the plane into **four quadrants**, numbered I, II, III, and IV in the anticlockwise direction starting from the positive x-axis:

| Quadrant | x-coordinate | y-coordinate | Sign Pattern |
| -------- | ------------ | ------------ | ------------ |
| **I**    | Positive     | Positive     | (+, +)       |
| **II**   | Negative     | Positive     | (-, +)       |
| **III**  | Negative     | Negative     | (-, -)       |
| **IV**   | Positive     | Negative     | (+, -)       |

> **Key idea:** The plane consisting of the axes and the four quadrants is called the **Cartesian plane**, the **coordinate plane**, or the **xy-plane**.

---

## Coordinates of a Point

Every point in the Cartesian plane is described by an **ordered pair (x, y)** where:

* The **x-coordinate** (also called the **abscissa**) is the perpendicular distance of the point from the y-axis, measured along the x-axis. It is positive to the right and negative to the left.
* The **y-coordinate** (also called the **ordinate**) is the perpendicular distance of the point from the x-axis, measured along the y-axis. It is positive upward and negative downward.

**Convention:** The x-coordinate is always written **first**, followed by the y-coordinate. So (3, 4) is NOT the same as (4, 3), unless both coordinates happen to be equal.

### Special points

* **Origin:** The coordinates of the origin are **(0, 0)**.
* **Points on the x-axis:** Any point on the x-axis has coordinates of the form **(x, 0)** -- the y-coordinate is always zero.
* **Points on the y-axis:** Any point on the y-axis has coordinates of the form **(0, y)** -- the x-coordinate is always zero.

**Example:** A point P is 4 units to the right of the y-axis and 3 units above the x-axis. Then the coordinates of P are **(4, 3)**.

**Example:** A point Q is 6 units to the left of the y-axis and 2 units below the x-axis. Then the coordinates of Q are **(-6, -2)**. This point lies in Quadrant III.

## Plotting Points on the Cartesian Plane

To plot a point with coordinates (a, b):

1. Start at the origin O.
2. Move **a units** along the x-axis (right if a > 0, left if a \< 0).
3. From that position, move **b units** parallel to the y-axis (up if b > 0, down if b \< 0).
4. Mark the point.

**Example:** Plot the point (-3, 4).

![Step-by-step plotting of point (-3, 4) showing horizontal movement left then vertical movement up from the origin](plotting-point-step-by-step.png)

* Start at O.
* Move 3 units to the left along the x-axis.
* Move 4 units upward parallel to the y-axis.
* This point is in **Quadrant II**.

**Example:** Plot the point (5, 0).

* Start at O.
* Move 5 units to the right along the x-axis.
* Since the y-coordinate is 0, the point lies on the x-axis itself.

## Reading Coordinates from a Graph

To read the coordinates of a point from a graph:

1. Draw a perpendicular from the point to the x-axis. The foot of this perpendicular on the x-axis gives the **x-coordinate**.
2. Draw a perpendicular from the point to the y-axis. The foot of this perpendicular on the y-axis gives the **y-coordinate**.
3. Write the coordinates as (x, y).

**Example:** If a point B is at a perpendicular distance of 4 units from the y-axis (to the right) and 3 units from the x-axis (above), then B = (4, 3), and it lies in Quadrant I.

**Example:** If a point L is at a perpendicular distance of 5 units from the y-axis (to the left) and 4 units from the x-axis (below), then L = (-5, -4), and it lies in Quadrant III.

---

## Summary

1. Two perpendicular lines are needed to locate the position of a point in a plane.
2. The horizontal line is the **x-axis** and the vertical line is the **y-axis**; together they are the **coordinate axes**.
3. The axes divide the plane into four **quadrants**: I (+, +), II (-, +), III (-, -), IV (+, -).
4. The intersection of the axes is the **origin** with coordinates **(0, 0)**.
5. The **x-coordinate** (abscissa) is the distance from the y-axis; the **y-coordinate** (ordinate) is the distance from the x-axis.
6. A point's position is written as **(x, y)**, with x-coordinate first.
7. Points on the x-axis have coordinates (x, 0); points on the y-axis have coordinates (0, y).
8. If x is not equal to y, then (x, y) is not equal to (y, x).
