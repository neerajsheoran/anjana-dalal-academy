## Introduction

A **triangle** is a closed figure formed by three intersecting line segments. It has three sides, three angles, and three vertices. In this chapter, you will study the **congruence of triangles**, the various criteria (rules) for congruence, important properties of triangles, and inequalities in triangles.

---

## Congruence of Triangles

Two figures are **congruent** if they have the same shape and size. When one figure can be placed on the other such that they cover each other completely, they are congruent.

Two triangles are **congruent** if all their corresponding sides and corresponding angles are equal. If triangle PQR is congruent to triangle ABC, we write:

**triangle PQR is congruent to triangle ABC**

> **Key idea:** When writing congruence, the **order of vertices matters**. The correspondence must be correct: P corresponds to A, Q to B, R to C. We use **CPCT** (Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles) to refer to equal parts.

---

## Criteria for Congruence of Triangles

### SAS (Side-Angle-Side) Congruence Rule

> **Axiom 7.1:** Two triangles are congruent if **two sides and the included angle** of one triangle are equal to the two sides and the included angle of the other triangle.

The angle must be the one **between** (included by) the two sides. The SSA or ASS rule does NOT work.

![Diagram showing two triangles with SAS congruence: two sides and the included angle marked as equal](sas-congruence-diagram.png)

**Example:** In triangles AOD and BOC, if OA = OB, OD = OC, and angle AOD = angle BOC (vertically opposite), then triangle AOD is congruent to triangle BOC by SAS.

### ASA (Angle-Side-Angle) Congruence Rule

> **Theorem 7.1:** Two triangles are congruent if **two angles and the included side** of one triangle are equal to two angles and the included side of the other triangle.

### AAS (Angle-Angle-Side) Congruence Rule

> **Theorem 7.2:** Two triangles are congruent if **two angles and a non-included side** of one triangle are equal to the corresponding two angles and side of the other triangle.

This follows from ASA because the third angle is automatically determined (angle sum property).

### SSS (Side-Side-Side) Congruence Rule

> **Theorem 7.4:** Two triangles are congruent if all **three sides** of one triangle are equal to the three sides of the other triangle.

### RHS (Right angle-Hypotenuse-Side) Congruence Rule

> **Theorem 7.5:** Two right triangles are congruent if the **hypotenuse and one side** of one triangle are equal to the hypotenuse and one side of the other triangle.

## Properties of Isosceles Triangles

An **isosceles triangle** has at least two equal sides. The angles opposite to the equal sides have a special relationship.

> **Theorem 7.2 (Isosceles Triangle Theorem):** Angles opposite to equal sides of a triangle are **equal**.

![Isosceles triangle with equal sides marked and the base angles shown as equal](isosceles-triangle-equal-angles.png)

If AB = AC in triangle ABC, then angle B = angle C.

> **Theorem 7.3 (Converse):** The sides opposite to equal angles of a triangle are **equal**.

If angle B = angle C in triangle ABC, then AB = AC.

**Corollary:** Each angle of an **equilateral triangle** is **60 degrees** (since all sides are equal, all angles are equal, and they sum to 180 degrees).

---

## Inequalities in a Triangle

> **Theorem 7.6:** If two sides of a triangle are unequal, then the angle opposite to the **longer side is larger**.

> **Theorem 7.7 (Converse):** In a triangle, the side opposite to the **larger angle is longer**.

> **Theorem 7.8 (Triangle Inequality):** The sum of any two sides of a triangle is **greater** than the third side.

In triangle ABC: AB + BC > AC, AB + AC > BC, and BC + AC > AB.

![Triangle showing the triangle inequality property with all three side-sum comparisons labeled](triangle-inequality-diagram.png)

**Example:** Can a triangle have sides 3, 4, and 8? Check: 3 + 4 = 7, which is NOT greater than 8. So no, such a triangle cannot exist.

**Example:** Can a triangle have sides 5, 7, and 10? Check: 5 + 7 = 12 > 10, 5 + 10 = 15 > 7, 7 + 10 = 17 > 5. Yes, this triangle is valid.

---

## Summary

1. Two figures are **congruent** if they have the same shape and size.
2. Congruence criteria: **SAS**, **ASA**, **AAS**, **SSS**, and **RHS**.
3. In an isosceles triangle, angles opposite to equal sides are equal (and vice versa).
4. Each angle of an equilateral triangle is **60 degrees**.
5. The angle opposite the longer side is larger, and the side opposite the larger angle is longer.
6. **Triangle inequality:** The sum of any two sides is greater than the third side.
