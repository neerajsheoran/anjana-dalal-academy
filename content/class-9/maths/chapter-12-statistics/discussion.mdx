## The Power of Data

**Kiran:** Papa, statistics seems very different from the rest of maths. It is about data and graphs, not proofs and theorems.

**Ravi:** Statistics is the bridge between mathematics and the real world. Every decision -- from government policy to cricket team selection to medical treatment -- relies on data analysis. The word "statistics" itself comes from the Latin "status," meaning state, because governments originally collected data about population and resources. Today, data science is one of the most important career fields globally.

**Kiran:** India conducts a census every ten years, right?

**Ravi:** Yes, the Census of India is one of the world's largest data collection exercises. It gathers information on population, literacy, occupation, housing, and more. The 2011 census covered over 121 crore people. All this data needs to be organised, summarised, and visualised -- exactly the skills you are learning in this chapter.

## Organising and Presenting Data

**Kiran:** What is the difference between primary and secondary data?

**Ravi:** Primary data is collected directly by you for a specific purpose -- like surveying your classmates' heights. Secondary data comes from existing sources -- like government reports or websites. For board exams, you mainly work with given data sets, but understanding the distinction matters for project work and competitive exams.

**Kiran:** When do we use grouped versus ungrouped frequency distributions?

**Ravi:** When data values are few and repeated, use ungrouped -- list each value with its frequency. When there are many distinct values spread over a wide range, group them into class intervals. The class size (width) should be uniform, and the class mark (midpoint) represents all values in that interval.

**Kiran:** Board exam tip -- what should I remember about class intervals?

**Ravi:** In the NCERT convention, the upper limit of one class equals the lower limit of the next class (like 10-20, 20-30). The convention is that the upper limit is excluded from that class but included in the next class. Also, remember: class mark equals (lower limit plus upper limit) divided by 2. These details matter for drawing histograms correctly.

## Histograms and Frequency Polygons

**Kiran:** How is a histogram different from a bar graph?

**Ravi:** A bar graph represents discrete or categorical data -- like the number of students in different houses. The bars have gaps between them. A histogram represents continuous numerical data grouped into class intervals -- there are no gaps between bars because the data is continuous. The width of each bar equals the class size.

![Side-by-side comparison of a bar graph with gaps and a histogram without gaps](bar-graph-vs-histogram.png)

**Kiran:** What if the class sizes are unequal?

**Ravi:** This is an important exam topic. When class widths vary, you cannot use frequency directly for bar heights. Instead, use frequency density: height equals frequency divided by class width. This ensures the area of each bar (not just the height) represents the frequency. Many students lose marks by ignoring this adjustment.

**Kiran:** And frequency polygons?

**Ravi:** A frequency polygon is created by plotting class marks on the x-axis against frequencies on the y-axis, and joining the points with straight lines. Close the polygon by extending to the midpoints of imaginary classes (with frequency zero) before the first and after the last class. Frequency polygons are especially useful for comparing two distributions on the same graph -- something histograms cannot do easily.

## Mean, Median, and Mode

**Kiran:** When should I use mean versus median versus mode?

**Ravi:** Each measure has its strengths. The mean uses every data value, so it is the most comprehensive, but it is sensitive to extreme values (outliers). If one student in a class scores 100 and the rest score around 50, the mean will be pulled up. The median, being the middle value, is unaffected by outliers and gives the "typical" value. The mode tells you the most common value -- useful for categorical data like the most popular colour.

**Kiran:** Can you give an Indian context example?

**Ravi:** Consider the income of families in a village. A few wealthy families can pull the mean income up dramatically, making the village seem richer than most families actually are. The median income would give a more realistic picture of the "typical" family. This is why economists often report median household income rather than mean income. In cricket, a batsman's median score tells you more about consistency than the average, which can be inflated by one or two big innings.

**Kiran:** For the board exam, what formulas must I know?

**Ravi:** Mean for ungrouped data: sum of all observations divided by the number of observations. Mean for a frequency distribution: sum of (frequency times value) divided by sum of frequencies. Median for odd n: the ((n plus 1) divided by 2)-th observation. Median for even n: the average of the (n/2)-th and ((n/2) plus 1)-th observations. Mode: the most frequently occurring value.

## Exam Strategies

**Kiran:** What types of questions appear most often?

**Ravi:** Drawing histograms (especially with unequal class widths), constructing frequency polygons, calculating mean from a frequency table, and finding median and mode from given data. Application-based questions are increasingly common -- interpreting data from real situations.

**Kiran:** Any presentation tips for graph-based questions?

**Ravi:** Use a ruler and sharp pencil. Label both axes clearly -- the x-axis should show the variable (class intervals or categories), the y-axis should show frequency. Write the scale. Give the graph a title. For histograms, shade or cross-hatch the bars. For frequency polygons, plot points clearly before joining them. These presentation details carry marks and reflect the discipline of statistical practice.

**Kiran:** How does this chapter connect to higher classes?

**Ravi:** In Class 10, you will study mean, median, and mode for grouped data using more advanced formulas, and learn about cumulative frequency curves (ogives). In Class 11 and 12, statistics extends to probability, standard deviation, and regression analysis. Data science careers -- which are among the highest-paying globally -- all build on these fundamentals. The great Indian statistician P.C. Mahalanobis founded the Indian Statistical Institute and developed methods that are used worldwide to this day.
