## Introduction

In earlier classes, you learnt about the number line and how to represent various types of numbers on it. Walking along the number line in the positive direction, you encounter natural numbers, whole numbers, integers, and rational numbers. This chapter takes you further into the world of numbers by introducing **irrational numbers** and the complete **real number system**.

The different sets of numbers form a hierarchy:

* **Natural Numbers (N):** The counting numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, ... that go on forever.
* **Whole Numbers (W):** Natural numbers together with zero: 0, 1, 2, 3, ...
* **Integers (Z):** Whole numbers together with negative numbers: ..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ... (The letter Z comes from the German word "zahlen", meaning "to count".)
* **Rational Numbers (Q):** Numbers that can be written in the form p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not equal to 0. (The letter Q comes from "quotient".)

> **Key idea:** Every natural number is a whole number, every whole number is an integer, and every integer is a rational number. The sets N, W, Z, and Q are nested inside one another.

---

## Rational Numbers

A number **r** is called a **rational number** if it can be written in the form **p/q**, where p and q are integers and q is not equal to 0.

All natural numbers, whole numbers, and integers are rational numbers. For example, -25 can be written as -25/1.

Rational numbers do not have a unique representation. For instance:

1/2 = 2/4 = 10/20 = 25/50

When we represent p/q on the number line, we assume that p and q are **co-prime** (they share no common factor other than 1).

**Finding rational numbers between two numbers:**

There are **infinitely many** rational numbers between any two given rational numbers. Two methods to find them:

1. **Averaging method:** To find a rational number between r and s, compute (r + s)/2.
2. **Common denominator method:** To find n rational numbers between two numbers, rewrite both with denominator (n + 1) and pick the numerators in between.

**Example:** Find five rational numbers between 1 and 2.

Rewrite 1 = 6/6 and 2 = 12/6. Then the five rational numbers are 7/6, 8/6, 9/6, 10/6, and 11/6.

---

## Irrational Numbers

After placing all rational numbers on the number line, there are still infinitely many "gaps" left. The numbers that fill these gaps are called **irrational numbers**.

A number **s** is called an **irrational number** if it **cannot** be written in the form p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not equal to 0.

> **Key idea:** Irrational numbers were first discovered by the Pythagoreans around 400 BC. Hippasus of Croton is credited with proving that the square root of 2 is irrational.

Some examples of irrational numbers:

* Square roots of non-perfect squares: sqrt(2), sqrt(3), sqrt(5), sqrt(7), ...
* The number pi (approximately 3.14159265...)
* Non-terminating, non-recurring decimals like 0.10110111011110...

**Locating sqrt(2) on the number line:**

Consider a unit square (side = 1). By the Pythagoras theorem, the diagonal has length sqrt(1^2 + 1^2) = sqrt(2). Place the square so one vertex is at the origin on the number line. Using a compass with the diagonal as radius, mark a point on the number line. This point represents sqrt(2).

**Locating sqrt(3) on the number line:**

Once sqrt(2) is located, construct a right triangle with one side = sqrt(2) and the other = 1. The hypotenuse = sqrt(2 + 1) = sqrt(3). Use a compass to transfer this length to the number line.

In general, sqrt(n) for any positive integer n can be located on the number line once sqrt(n - 1) has been located.

## Real Numbers

The collection of **all rational numbers and all irrational numbers** together makes up the set of **real numbers**, denoted by **R**.

> **Key idea:** Every real number is represented by a unique point on the number line, and every point on the number line represents a unique real number. This is why we call it the **real number line**.

This fundamental result was established by the German mathematicians **Cantor** and **Dedekind** in the 1870s.

A real number is either rational or irrational -- there is no third option.

---

## Decimal Expansions of Real Numbers

The decimal expansion of a number provides a powerful way to distinguish between rational and irrational numbers.

### Decimal expansions of rational numbers

When you divide p by q, one of two things happens:

**Case 1: Terminating decimal** -- The remainder eventually becomes zero.

* 7/8 = 0.875
* 1/2 = 0.5
* 639/250 = 2.556

**Case 2: Non-terminating recurring (repeating) decimal** -- The remainders start repeating, causing a block of digits to repeat forever.

* 10/3 = 3.333... = 3.3 (bar over 3)
* 1/7 = 0.142857142857... = 0.142857 (bar over 142857)

> **Key idea:** The decimal expansion of a rational number is either **terminating** or **non-terminating recurring**. Conversely, any number with a terminating or non-terminating recurring decimal expansion is rational.

### Decimal expansions of irrational numbers

Since irrational numbers cannot be expressed as p/q, their decimal expansions are **non-terminating and non-recurring** -- they go on forever without any repeating pattern.

* sqrt(2) = 1.4142135623730950488...
* pi = 3.14159265358979323846...

> **Key idea:** The decimal expansion of an irrational number is **non-terminating non-recurring**. Conversely, any number with such a decimal expansion is irrational.

### Converting recurring decimals to fractions

**Example:** Express 0.333... in the form p/q.

Let x = 0.333...
Then 10x = 3.333... = 3 + x
So 9x = 3, giving x = 3/9 = 1/3.

**Example:** Express 1.272727... in the form p/q.

Let x = 1.272727...
Then 100x = 127.2727... = 126 + x
So 99x = 126, giving x = 126/99 = 14/11.

**Example:** Express 0.2353535... in the form p/q.

Let x = 0.2353535...
Then 100x = 23.53535... = 23.3 + x
So 99x = 233/10, giving x = 233/990.

### An interesting result

0.9999... = 1 (exactly). Let x = 0.9999..., then 10x = 9.9999... = 9 + x, so 9x = 9, giving x = 1.

### Condition for terminating decimals

A rational number p/q (in lowest terms) has a terminating decimal expansion if and only if the prime factorisation of q contains only powers of 2 or powers of 5 (or both).

## Operations on Real Numbers

Rational numbers are **closed** under addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division (except by zero). Irrational numbers satisfy the commutative, associative, and distributive laws, but the result of operations on two irrationals may be rational or irrational.

**Important results:**

1. The **sum or difference** of a rational and an irrational number is always **irrational**.
2. The **product or quotient** of a non-zero rational number and an irrational number is always **irrational**.
3. The sum, difference, product, or quotient of two irrational numbers **may be rational or irrational**.

**Examples:**

* sqrt(6) + (-sqrt(6)) = 0 (rational)
* sqrt(2) times sqrt(2) = 2 (rational)
* 2 + sqrt(3) is irrational
* 7 times sqrt(5) = 7sqrt(5) is irrational

### Identities involving square roots

For positive real numbers a and b:

1. sqrt(ab) = sqrt(a) times sqrt(b)
2. sqrt(a/b) = sqrt(a) / sqrt(b)
3. (sqrt(a) + sqrt(b))(sqrt(a) - sqrt(b)) = a - b
4. (a + sqrt(b))(a - sqrt(b)) = a^2 - b
5. (sqrt(a) + sqrt(b))^2 = a + 2sqrt(ab) + b

**Example:** Simplify (sqrt(5) + sqrt(7))(2 + sqrt(5))
\= 2sqrt(5) + sqrt(25) + 2sqrt(7) + sqrt(35)
\= 2sqrt(5) + 5 + 2sqrt(7) + sqrt(35)

**Example:** Simplify (sqrt(11) - sqrt(7))(sqrt(11) + sqrt(7))
\= 11 - 7 = 4

## Rationalising the Denominator

When the denominator of an expression contains a square root, we convert it to an equivalent expression with a **rational denominator**. This process is called **rationalising the denominator**.

**Example:** Rationalise 1/sqrt(2)

Multiply numerator and denominator by sqrt(2):
1/sqrt(2) = sqrt(2)/2

**Example:** Rationalise 1/(2 + sqrt(3))

Multiply by (2 - sqrt(3))/(2 - sqrt(3)):
1/(2 + sqrt(3)) = (2 - sqrt(3))/(4 - 3) = 2 - sqrt(3)

**Example:** Rationalise 1/(7 + 3sqrt(2))

Multiply by (7 - 3sqrt(2))/(7 - 3sqrt(2)):
1/(7 + 3sqrt(2)) = (7 - 3sqrt(2))/(49 - 18) = (7 - 3sqrt(2))/31

> **Key idea:** To rationalise 1/(a + sqrt(b)), multiply by (a - sqrt(b))/(a - sqrt(b)). The denominator becomes a^2 - b, which is rational.

## Laws of Exponents for Real Numbers

You have already learnt the laws of exponents for natural number bases and integer exponents. These laws can be **extended to real number bases and rational exponents**.

### Definitions

For a positive real number a and positive integer n:

* **n-th root:** The n-th root of a, written as a^(1/n), equals b if b^n = a and b > 0.
* **Rational exponent:** For integers m and n (n > 0, m and n co-prime), a^(m/n) = (a^(1/n))^m = (a^m)^(1/n).

### The extended laws

For a > 0 (a real number) and rational numbers p and q:

1. **a^p times a^q = a^(p+q)**
2. **(a^p)^q = a^(pq)**
3. **a^p / a^q = a^(p-q)**
4. **a^p times b^p = (ab)^p**

### Worked examples

**Find 64^(1/2):**
64 = 8^2, so 64^(1/2) = 8.

**Find 32^(1/5):**
32 = 2^5, so 32^(1/5) = 2.

**Find 125^(1/3):**
125 = 5^3, so 125^(1/3) = 5.

**Find 9^(3/2):**
9^(3/2) = (9^(1/2))^3 = 3^3 = 27.

**Find 32^(2/5):**
32^(2/5) = (32^(1/5))^2 = 2^2 = 4.

**Find 16^(3/4):**
16^(3/4) = (16^(1/4))^3 = 2^3 = 8.

**Simplify 2^(2/3) times 2^(1/3):**
\= 2^(2/3 + 1/3) = 2^1 = 2.

**Simplify 7^(1/5) / 7^(1/3):**
\= 7^(1/5 - 1/3) = 7^(-2/15).

**Simplify 11^(1/2) / 11^(1/4):**
\= 11^(1/2 - 1/4) = 11^(1/4).

**Simplify 13^(1/5) times 17^(1/5):**
\= (13 times 17)^(1/5) = 221^(1/5).

## Summary

1. A **rational number** can be written as p/q where p, q are integers and q is not 0.
2. An **irrational number** cannot be written in the form p/q.
3. The decimal expansion of a rational number is either **terminating** or **non-terminating recurring**.
4. The decimal expansion of an irrational number is **non-terminating non-recurring**.
5. All rational and irrational numbers together form the set of **real numbers (R)**.
6. If r is rational and s is irrational, then r + s, r - s are irrational; rs and r/s (r not 0) are irrational.
7. For positive real numbers a and b: sqrt(ab) = sqrt(a)sqrt(b); sqrt(a/b) = sqrt(a)/sqrt(b).
8. To rationalise 1/(a + sqrt(b)), multiply by (a - sqrt(b))/(a - sqrt(b)).
9. Laws of exponents: a^p times a^q = a^(p+q); (a^p)^q = a^(pq); a^p/a^q = a^(p-q); a^p times b^p = (ab)^p.
