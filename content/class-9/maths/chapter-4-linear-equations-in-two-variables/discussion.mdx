## From One Variable to Two

**Kiran:** Papa, in Class 8 we solved equations like 2x plus 3 equals 7, which gave one answer. Now we have 2x plus 3y equals 12, and the textbook says it has infinitely many solutions. How can one equation have infinite answers?

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. With one variable, you are finding a point on a number line -- there is usually exactly one point. With two variables, you are finding points on a plane -- and the solutions form an entire straight line. Every single point on that line satisfies the equation. Since a line has infinitely many points, you get infinitely many solutions.

**Kiran:** So each solution is an ordered pair (x, y)?

**Ravi:** Exactly. For 2x plus 3y equals 12, the pair (0, 4) works because 0 plus 12 equals 12. The pair (6, 0) works because 12 plus 0 equals 12. The pair (3, 2) works because 6 plus 6 equals 12. You can keep generating solutions by choosing any value for x and computing y, or vice versa.

**Kiran:** This connects directly to coordinate geometry from Chapter 3!

**Ravi:** That is the beauty. Algebra and geometry are two languages for the same idea. An equation is an algebraic description; the graph is the geometric picture. This connection, pioneered by Descartes, is central to all of higher mathematics.

## Drawing Graphs of Linear Equations

**Kiran:** To draw the graph, the textbook says we need at least two points. Why not just one?

**Ravi:** One point tells you a location, but not a direction. Through any single point, infinitely many lines can pass. Two points uniquely determine a straight line -- this is Euclid's postulate from Chapter 5. In practice, find three points: two to draw the line and the third to verify. If the third point does not lie on your line, you know there is a calculation error.

**Kiran:** What do the special cases x equals a and y equals b look like?

**Ravi:** The equation x equals 3 is a vertical line passing through (3, 0), parallel to the y-axis. Every point on this line has x-coordinate 3, regardless of y. Similarly, y equals minus 2 is a horizontal line passing through (0, -2), parallel to the x-axis. Board exams love asking students to draw these -- many mistakenly draw x equals 3 as a point instead of a line.

**Kiran:** And y equals mx passes through the origin?

**Ravi:** Yes. The equation y equals 2x, for instance, passes through (0, 0), (1, 2), (2, 4). This form is important because it represents direct proportionality -- y is directly proportional to x. In physics, Ohm's law V equals IR is exactly this form when resistance is constant.

## Real-World Applications

**Kiran:** Where do linear equations in two variables appear in real life?

**Ravi:** Everywhere. When a shopkeeper says "2 kg of apples and 3 kg of oranges cost 500 rupees," that is 2x plus 3y equals 500, where x is the price per kg of apples and y is the price per kg of oranges. When an engineer designs a ramp, the slope is described by a linear equation. Even the fare structure of auto-rickshaws in Indian cities -- a fixed charge plus a per-km rate -- follows the form y equals mx plus c.

**Kiran:** But with one equation and two unknowns, we cannot find unique prices for apples and oranges, right?

**Ravi:** Correct -- you need as many independent equations as unknowns. One equation gives a line of possibilities. In Class 10, you will study pairs of linear equations -- two lines that intersect at exactly one point, giving a unique solution. That is where you find the exact price of apples and oranges.

## Lines Parallel to the Axes

**Kiran:** Why does x equals 5 represent a line and not just a number?

**Ravi:** In the context of a number line (one dimension), x equals 5 is a point. But in the Cartesian plane (two dimensions), x equals 5 really means "x equals 5 and y can be anything." You can rewrite it as 1 times x plus 0 times y minus 5 equals 0. Since y has coefficient 0, it is free to take any value. The result is an entire vertical line.

**Kiran:** Similarly, y equals 0 would be the x-axis itself?

**Ravi:** Yes! And x equals 0 is the y-axis. These are fundamental -- the axes are themselves graphs of linear equations. For exams, remember that equations like 2x plus 3 equals 0 in two-variable form become 2x plus 0y plus 3 equals 0, which is a vertical line at x equals minus 3/2.

**Kiran:** One more exam tip?

**Ravi:** When drawing graphs, always label the axes, mark the scale, and write the equation next to the line. These are presentation marks that students often lose unnecessarily. Also, extend the line beyond your plotted points with arrows to show it continues infinitely.

## Preparing for Board Exams

**Kiran:** What types of questions from this chapter appear most often in boards?

**Ravi:** Three types dominate. First, expressing equations in the standard form ax plus by plus c equals 0 and identifying a, b, c. Second, finding multiple solutions and plotting the graph. Third, questions on lines parallel to axes. There are also application-based questions where you model a real situation as a linear equation.

**Kiran:** Any tricks to avoid errors?

**Ravi:** Always verify your solutions by substituting back into the original equation. If the question says "find four solutions," present them in a neat table format -- this impresses examiners and reduces mistakes. And remember: (x, y) is an ordered pair, so never swap the values. The Indian mathematician Aryabhata worked with linear equations in his astronomical calculations over 1500 years ago -- systematic substitution was already a tool in his Aryabhatiyam.

**Kiran:** So this chapter builds the bridge to simultaneous equations in Class 10?

**Ravi:** Precisely. You are learning to work with one equation now. In Class 10, you will learn to solve two equations simultaneously -- graphically, by substitution, and by elimination. The graphical intuition you develop here will make those methods much clearer.
