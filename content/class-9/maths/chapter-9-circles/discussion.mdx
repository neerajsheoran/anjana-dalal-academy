## Chords and Their Properties

**Kiran:** Papa, a circle seems simple -- just a set of points equidistant from the centre. But this chapter has so many theorems. Where do I start?

**Ravi:** Start with chords. A chord is a line segment joining any two points on the circle, and the diameter is the longest chord. The first key result is that equal chords subtend equal angles at the centre, and vice versa. The proof uses SSS congruence -- if two chords are equal, the triangles formed by joining their endpoints to the centre are congruent.

**Kiran:** And the perpendicular from the centre to a chord?

**Ravi:** It bisects the chord -- this is Theorem 9.3. If OM is perpendicular to chord AB, then AM equals MB. The converse is also true: the line from the centre to the midpoint of a chord is perpendicular to the chord. These results are workhorses in circle geometry -- you will use them repeatedly.

**Kiran:** What about the relationship between chord length and distance from the centre?

**Ravi:** Equal chords are equidistant from the centre, and chords equidistant from the centre are equal. The diameter, being the longest chord, passes through the centre, so its distance from the centre is zero. As chords get shorter, they move further from the centre. Think of it like this -- if you slice a circular roti at different positions, the slice through the centre is longest, and slices near the edge are shorter.

## Angles Subtended by Arcs

**Kiran:** The most important theorem seems to be that the angle at the centre is double the angle at the circumference for the same arc. Why does this work?

**Ravi:** Consider arc PQ subtending angle POQ at the centre O and angle PRQ at point R on the circle. Draw line RO extended. Using the isosceles triangle property (OP equals OR, both radii) and the exterior angle theorem, you can show that angle POQ equals 2 times angle PRQ. The proof has three cases depending on whether the centre lies inside, outside, or on the angle PRQ.

![Three cases for the inscribed angle theorem: centre inside, on, and outside the inscribed angle](inscribed-angle-theorem-three-cases.png)

**Kiran:** And angles in the same segment are equal?

**Ravi:** Yes -- since all angles subtended by the same arc at the circumference are half the central angle, they are all equal to each other. This gives Theorem 9.8: angles in the same segment are equal. The most celebrated special case is the angle in a semicircle: since the diameter subtends 180 degrees at the centre, the angle at the circumference is 90 degrees.

**Kiran:** Thales is credited with discovering the semicircle result, right?

**Ravi:** Yes, Thales of Miletus proved that the angle in a semicircle is a right angle around 600 BCE. But Indian astronomers like Aryabhata and Bhaskara II used circle properties extensively in their astronomical calculations centuries later. The theorem connects to practical applications too -- construction workers use the semicircle property to check right angles on building sites.

## Cyclic Quadrilaterals

**Kiran:** A cyclic quadrilateral has all four vertices on a circle, and opposite angles are supplementary. How do I prove this?

**Ravi:** In cyclic quadrilateral ABCD inscribed in a circle, arc BCD subtends angle A at the circumference, and arc BAD subtends angle C. The two arcs together make the full circle, so the central angles sum to 360 degrees. Since each circumscribed angle is half its central angle, angle A plus angle C equals 180 degrees.

**Kiran:** Is the converse true?

**Ravi:** Yes -- if opposite angles of a quadrilateral are supplementary, the quadrilateral is cyclic. This converse is very useful in proofs where you need to show four points are concyclic (lie on a common circle).

**Kiran:** What are the typical board exam questions on cyclic quadrilaterals?

**Ravi:** Usually you are given some angles and asked to find others using the supplementary property. Sometimes you need to prove that a quadrilateral is cyclic by showing opposite angles sum to 180 degrees. A common trap: students apply the supplementary property to adjacent angles instead of opposite angles. Always double-check which angles are opposite.

## Problem-Solving Approach

**Kiran:** With so many theorems in this chapter, how do I choose the right one for a problem?

**Ravi:** Develop a mental checklist. If the problem involves chords, think about perpendicular from the centre, equal chords, and equidistance. If it involves angles, determine whether you need the central angle theorem, the same-segment theorem, or the semicircle property. If it involves a quadrilateral inscribed in a circle, go to the supplementary angles result.

**Kiran:** Should I draw the diagram first?

**Ravi:** Always. Mark the centre, draw all relevant radii, chords, and angles. Label everything. Many students try to solve circle problems without a diagram and make errors in identifying which angles correspond to which arcs. A good diagram is half the solution.

**Kiran:** Any connections to competitive exams?

**Ravi:** Circle geometry is heavily tested in olympiads and exams like NTSE. The power of a point theorem, tangent properties, and Ptolemy's theorem -- which you will study in higher classes -- all build on the foundations laid here. Mastering this chapter gives you a significant advantage in competitive mathematics.
