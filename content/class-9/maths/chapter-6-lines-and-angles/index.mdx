## Introduction

In this chapter, you will study the properties of angles formed when two lines intersect each other, and the properties of angles formed when a line intersects two or more parallel lines. These properties are fundamental to geometry and are used extensively in proofs and constructions.

---

## Basic Terms and Definitions

A **line segment** is a part of a line with two endpoints. A **ray** is a part of a line with one endpoint. A **line** extends indefinitely in both directions.

**Collinear points** are three or more points that lie on the same line. Points that do not lie on the same line are called **non-collinear points**.

### Types of Angles

| Type               | Measure                     |
| ------------------ | --------------------------- |
| **Acute angle**    | Between 0 and 90 degrees    |
| **Right angle**    | Exactly 90 degrees          |
| **Obtuse angle**   | Between 90 and 180 degrees  |
| **Straight angle** | Exactly 180 degrees         |
| **Reflex angle**   | Between 180 and 360 degrees |

### Angle Pairs

* **Complementary angles:** Two angles whose sum is **90 degrees**.
* **Supplementary angles:** Two angles whose sum is **180 degrees**.
* **Adjacent angles:** Two angles with a common vertex, a common arm, and non-common arms on different sides of the common arm.
* **Linear pair:** Adjacent angles whose non-common arms form a straight line (sum = 180 degrees).
* **Vertically opposite angles:** Angles formed opposite each other when two lines intersect.

## Intersecting Lines and Non-intersecting Lines

Two lines drawn in a plane either **intersect** (meet at a point) or are **parallel** (never meet, remaining the same distance apart at all points).

**Parallel lines** are lines that do not intersect and are equidistant from each other at every point. The constant perpendicular distance between them is called the **distance between two parallel lines**.

---

## The Linear Pair Axiom

> **Axiom 6.1:** If a ray stands on a line, then the sum of the two adjacent angles so formed is **180 degrees**.

When the sum of two adjacent angles is 180 degrees, they form a **linear pair**.

> **Axiom 6.2 (Converse):** If the sum of two adjacent angles is 180 degrees, then the non-common arms of the angles form a line.

Together, these two axioms constitute the **Linear Pair Axiom**.

**Example:** If ray OC stands on line AB, then angle AOC + angle COB = 180 degrees.

> **Key idea:** When four rays emerge from a point, the sum of all angles around the point is **360 degrees**.

---

## Vertically Opposite Angles

**Theorem 6.1:** If two lines intersect each other, then the **vertically opposite angles are equal**.

**Proof:** Let lines AB and CD intersect at O.

Ray OA stands on line CD, so angle AOC + angle AOD = 180 degrees ... (1)

Ray OD stands on line AB, so angle AOD + angle BOD = 180 degrees ... (2)

From (1) and (2): angle AOC + angle AOD = angle AOD + angle BOD

Therefore, **angle AOC = angle BOD** (subtracting angle AOD from both sides).

Similarly, **angle AOD = angle BOC**.

**Example:** If lines PQ and RS intersect at O and angle POR : angle ROQ = 5 : 7, find all angles.

angle POR + angle ROQ = 180 degrees (linear pair)

angle POR = (5/12) x 180 = 75 degrees

angle ROQ = (7/12) x 180 = 105 degrees

angle POS = angle ROQ = 105 degrees (vertically opposite)

angle SOQ = angle POR = 75 degrees (vertically opposite)

## Parallel Lines and a Transversal

A line that intersects two or more lines at distinct points is called a **transversal**.

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines, it creates **eight angles**. These angles have special relationships:

### Types of angle pairs formed by a transversal

1. **Corresponding angles:** Angles on the same side of the transversal, one interior and one exterior. They are in the same relative position at each intersection.
2. **Alternate interior angles:** Angles on opposite sides of the transversal, both between the parallel lines.
3. **Alternate exterior angles:** Angles on opposite sides of the transversal, both outside the parallel lines.
4. **Co-interior angles (same-side interior angles):** Angles on the same side of the transversal, both between the parallel lines.

![Transversal cutting two parallel lines showing corresponding angles, alternate interior angles, and co-interior angles labeled](parallel-lines-transversal-angles.png)

### Axioms and Theorems

> **Axiom 6.3 (Corresponding Angles Axiom):** If a transversal intersects two parallel lines, then each pair of **corresponding angles is equal**.

> **Axiom 6.4 (Converse):** If a transversal intersects two lines making a pair of corresponding angles equal, then the two lines are **parallel**.

**Theorem 6.2:** If a transversal intersects two parallel lines, then each pair of **alternate interior angles is equal**.

**Theorem 6.3:** If a transversal intersects two parallel lines, then each pair of **co-interior angles (same-side interior angles) is supplementary** (sum = 180 degrees).

**Theorem 6.4 (Converse of 6.2):** If alternate interior angles are equal, the lines are parallel.

**Theorem 6.5 (Converse of 6.3):** If co-interior angles are supplementary, the lines are parallel.

---

## Lines Parallel to the Same Line

**Theorem 6.6:** Lines which are parallel to the same line are **parallel to each other**.

If line m is parallel to line l, and line n is parallel to line l, then line m is parallel to line n.

**Proof:** Draw a transversal t. Since m || l, corresponding angles are equal: angle 1 = angle 2. Since n || l, corresponding angles are equal: angle 1 = angle 3. Therefore angle 2 = angle 3, and by the converse of corresponding angles axiom, m || n.

---

## Angle Sum Property of a Triangle

**Theorem 6.7:** The sum of the angles of a triangle is **180 degrees**.

**Proof outline:** Draw a line through one vertex parallel to the opposite side. Use alternate interior angles to show that the three angles of the triangle together form a straight angle (180 degrees).

![Proof diagram for the angle sum property of a triangle showing a line through vertex A parallel to BC with alternate angles marked](triangle-angle-sum-proof.png)

**Theorem 6.8:** If a side of a triangle is produced, the **exterior angle** so formed is equal to the **sum of the two interior opposite angles**.

**Example:** In triangle ABC, if angle A = 30 degrees and angle B = 50 degrees, then angle C = 180 - 30 - 50 = 100 degrees.

If side BC is produced to D, then the exterior angle ACD = angle A + angle B = 30 + 50 = 80 degrees.

![Triangle ABC with side BC extended to D, showing the exterior angle ACD equal to the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles](exterior-angle-theorem.png)

> **Key idea:** An exterior angle of a triangle is always greater than either of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

---

## Summary

1. If a ray stands on a line, the two adjacent angles sum to **180 degrees** (Linear Pair Axiom).
2. **Vertically opposite angles** are equal.
3. When a transversal cuts two parallel lines: corresponding angles are equal, alternate interior angles are equal, and co-interior angles are supplementary.
4. Lines parallel to the same line are parallel to each other.
5. The **angle sum** of a triangle is **180 degrees**.
6. The **exterior angle** of a triangle equals the sum of the two interior opposite angles.
