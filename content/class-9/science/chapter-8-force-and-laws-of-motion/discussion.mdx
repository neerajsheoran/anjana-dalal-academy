## Newton's First Law and Inertia

**Kiran:** Papa, Newton's First Law says an object stays at rest or moves with uniform velocity unless acted upon by an unbalanced force. But why does a ball rolling on the ground eventually stop even though nobody pushes it to stop?

**Ravi:** Because friction is the unbalanced force. The ground exerts a frictional force opposite to the ball's motion, gradually decelerating it. In deep space, where there is virtually no friction, a ball would keep moving forever at the same speed. The Voyager 1 spacecraft, launched in 1977, is still moving through interstellar space -- no engine is pushing it; it simply continues because there is almost no force to stop it.

**Kiran:** Inertia is the tendency to resist change. Heavier objects have more inertia?

**Ravi:** Yes -- mass is the measure of inertia. That is why it is harder to push a loaded truck than an empty one. When a bus suddenly brakes, passengers fall forward because their bodies have inertia -- they tend to keep moving even though the bus has stopped. When you shake a tree branch, fruits fall because the branch moves but the fruits resist the change due to inertia.

**Kiran:** So the First Law defines what happens when there is no unbalanced force. The Second Law tells us what happens when there is one?

**Ravi:** Precisely. The First Law is actually a special case of the Second Law. When the net force is zero, acceleration is zero, so velocity does not change. The First Law also establishes that such a thing as an "inertial reference frame" exists -- a crucial concept in advanced physics and Einstein's relativity theory.

## Newton's Second Law: F equals ma

**Kiran:** Force equals mass times acceleration. This formula seems simple, but it is incredibly powerful.

**Ravi:** It is arguably the most important equation in classical physics. It tells you three things: for a fixed mass, more force produces more acceleration (push harder, the object speeds up faster). For a fixed force, more mass produces less acceleration (heavier objects are harder to accelerate). And the direction of acceleration is the same as the direction of the net force.

**Kiran:** Momentum is mass times velocity. How does it connect to the Second Law?

**Ravi:** Newton originally stated the Second Law in terms of momentum: force equals the rate of change of momentum. Mathematically, F equals (mv minus mu) divided by t, which simplifies to ma when mass is constant. Momentum is a more fundamental quantity than force in many contexts -- it is conserved in collisions, which force is not.

**Kiran:** Can you give a numerical example?

**Ravi:** A cricket ball of mass 0.15 kg is bowled at 40 m/s and is hit back by the batsman at 60 m/s. If the ball is in contact with the bat for 0.05 seconds, what force does the bat exert? Change in momentum equals 0.15 times (60 minus (-40)) equals 0.15 times 100 equals 15 kg m/s (taking the batsman's direction as positive). Force equals 15 divided by 0.05 equals 300 N. The sign convention matters -- the initial velocity is opposite to the final velocity.

## Newton's Third Law

**Kiran:** Every action has an equal and opposite reaction. But if forces always come in pairs, how does anything ever move?

**Ravi:** Because the action and reaction act on different objects. When you walk, your foot pushes the ground backward (action on the ground) and the ground pushes your foot forward (reaction on you). The forces do not cancel because they act on different bodies. If you push a wall while standing on ice, you slide backward because the wall pushes you back -- the action-reaction pair acts on wall and you separately.

**Kiran:** What about a rocket in space? There is no ground to push against.

**Ravi:** The rocket pushes hot exhaust gases downward (action), and the gases push the rocket upward (reaction). No external surface is needed. ISRO's PSLV and GSLV launch vehicles use exactly this principle. The mass of gases expelled at high velocity produces a reaction force that lifts hundreds of tonnes of rocket and payload into orbit. India's Chandrayaan and Mars Orbiter missions all relied on Newton's Third Law.

**Kiran:** Board exam tip -- how do I identify the action-reaction pair correctly?

**Ravi:** Always identify two objects. The action is the force exerted by object A on object B. The reaction is the force exerted by object B on object A. They are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different bodies. Never say action and reaction cancel each other -- they cannot, because they act on different objects.

## Conservation of Momentum

**Kiran:** The textbook says total momentum is conserved when no external force acts. Can you show this with the gun-recoil example?

**Ravi:** Before firing, both the gun and bullet are at rest, so total momentum is zero. After firing, the bullet (mass m) moves forward with velocity v, and the gun (mass M) recoils with velocity V. Conservation gives: 0 equals mv plus MV, so V equals minus mv divided by M. The negative sign shows the gun moves backward. Since M is much larger than m, V is much smaller than v -- the heavy gun recoils slowly while the light bullet moves fast.

**Kiran:** This explains why a heavy gun has less recoil?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Military engineers design heavy gun barrels and add recoil-absorbing mechanisms to minimise kickback. In cricket, when a fielder catches a fast ball, they pull their hands back to increase the stopping time, which reduces the force (Second Law) and makes the catch less painful.

**Kiran:** What types of problems should I expect in the board exam?

**Ravi:** Three types. First, calculating force using F equals ma or F equals change in momentum over time. Second, collision problems using conservation of momentum. Third, conceptual questions identifying action-reaction pairs. Always state Newton's law by name and number in your answer -- examiners award marks for correctly citing the relevant law.
