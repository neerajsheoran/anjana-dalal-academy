## What is Force?

A **force** is a push or pull that can change the state of motion of an object. We cannot see a force, but we can see and feel its **effects**.

**Effects of force:**
- Can make a stationary object **move**
- Can **stop** a moving object
- Can change the **speed** of a moving object (faster or slower)
- Can change the **direction** of motion
- Can change the **shape and size** of an object

---

## Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

### Balanced Forces

When two **equal forces** act on an object in **opposite directions**, they cancel each other out. The net force is zero. The object remains in its current state -- if at rest, it stays at rest; if moving, it continues moving at the same speed.

### Unbalanced Forces

When the forces acting on an object are **not equal**, the net force is not zero. The object will **accelerate** (change its speed or direction) in the direction of the greater force.

![Balanced forces on a box (equal push from both sides, no movement) vs unbalanced forces (net force causes acceleration)](balanced-vs-unbalanced-forces.png)

> **Key idea:** An unbalanced force is required to change the state of motion of an object. Balanced forces do not change the state of motion.

**Example:** When children push a box on a rough floor, friction opposes their push. If the pushing force equals friction, the box does not move (balanced). If they push harder than friction, the box moves (unbalanced).

---

## Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

**Newton's First Law:** An object remains in a state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled to change that state by an applied (unbalanced) force.

### Inertia

**Inertia** is the tendency of an object to resist a change in its state of motion. Every object has inertia.

- An object at rest tends to **stay at rest**
- An object in motion tends to **keep moving** with the same speed and direction

**The mass of an object is a measure of its inertia.** Heavier objects have more inertia and are harder to move or stop.

### Everyday Examples of Inertia

- When a bus **suddenly starts**, passengers fall backward (their body was at rest and resists the change)
- When a bus **suddenly stops**, passengers fall forward (their body was moving and resists stopping)
- When you shake a branch of a tree, **fruits fall** (the branch moves but the fruits resist the change)
- A coin placed on cardboard over a glass **falls into the glass** when the cardboard is flicked away (the coin resists horizontal motion)
- A person jumping out of a moving bus tends to **fall forward** (the feet stop but the upper body continues to move)

> **Key idea:** Inertia depends on mass. A heavier object has more inertia. It is harder to push a loaded truck than an empty one.

---

## Newton's Second Law of Motion

**Newton's Second Law:** The force acting on an object is equal to the mass of the object multiplied by its acceleration.

**F = m x a**

Or equivalently: The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied unbalanced force, and the change takes place in the direction of the force.

### Momentum

**Momentum** is the product of mass and velocity of an object.

**p = m x v**

- **SI unit of momentum:** kg m/s (kg m s-1)
- Momentum is a **vector quantity** (has direction)
- A heavier object or a faster object has more momentum

**Example:** A truck moving slowly can have more momentum than a car moving fast, because the truck has much more mass.

### Mathematical Form of Second Law

The rate of change of momentum = Force

**F = (mv - mu) / t = m(v - u) / t = ma**

Therefore: **F = ma**

- **SI unit of force:** Newton (N)
- **1 Newton** is the force that produces an acceleration of 1 m/s squared in an object of mass 1 kg
- 1 N = 1 kg x 1 m/s squared = 1 kg m s-2

> **Key idea:** F = ma tells us that for a given force, acceleration is inversely proportional to mass (heavier objects accelerate less), and for a given mass, acceleration is directly proportional to force (more force produces more acceleration).

## Newton's Third Law of Motion

**Newton's Third Law:** To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

When object A exerts a force on object B (**action**), object B exerts an **equal force** on object A in the **opposite direction** (**reaction**).

**Important:** Action and reaction always act on **two different objects**, not on the same object. That is why they do not cancel each other.

### Examples of Newton's Third Law

- When you **walk**, your foot pushes the ground backward (action), and the ground pushes your foot forward (reaction)
- When a **gun fires** a bullet, the bullet goes forward (action), and the gun recoils backward (reaction)
- A **swimmer** pushes water backward with hands (action), and water pushes the swimmer forward (reaction)
- A **rocket** pushes hot gases downward (action), and the gases push the rocket upward (reaction)
- When a **sailor jumps** forward from a boat, the boat moves backward

![Newton's third law illustrated with a rocket: action force pushes gases downward, reaction force pushes rocket upward](newtons-third-law-rocket.png)

> **Key idea:** Forces always come in pairs. You cannot have an action without a reaction. The two forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects.

## Conservation of Momentum

**Law of Conservation of Momentum:** The total momentum of a system of objects remains constant if no external unbalanced force acts on it.

![Two objects before and after collision showing conservation of total momentum](conservation-of-momentum-collision.png)

**Total momentum before collision = Total momentum after collision**

**m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2**

Where:
- m1, m2 = masses of the two objects
- u1, u2 = velocities before collision
- v1, v2 = velocities after collision

### Applications

**Recoil of a gun:** Before firing, both the gun and bullet are at rest (total momentum = 0). After firing, the bullet moves forward with high velocity, and the gun moves backward (recoils) so that total momentum remains zero.

If bullet mass = m, bullet velocity = v, gun mass = M, gun recoil velocity = V:

0 = mv + MV

V = -mv / M

The negative sign shows the gun moves in the opposite direction to the bullet.

> **Key idea:** Conservation of momentum explains why a heavy gun recoils slowly while the light bullet moves fast. The heavier the gun, the smaller the recoil.

## Summary of Newton's Laws

| Law | Statement | Key Concept |
|---|---|---|
| **First Law** | An object stays at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an unbalanced force | Inertia |
| **Second Law** | F = ma (Force = mass x acceleration) | Force and acceleration |
| **Third Law** | Every action has an equal and opposite reaction | Action-reaction pairs |

---

## Key Formulae

- **Momentum:** p = mv
- **Force:** F = ma
- **Acceleration:** a = (v - u) / t
- **Second Law:** F = m(v - u) / t
- **Conservation of momentum:** m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2
- **1 Newton** = 1 kg m/s squared
- **1 kgf** = 9.8 N

---

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Force** -- Push or pull that changes the state of motion
- **Inertia** -- Tendency to resist change in state of motion
- **Momentum** -- Product of mass and velocity (p = mv)
- **Newton** -- SI unit of force
- **Balanced forces** -- Equal opposite forces with zero net effect
- **Unbalanced forces** -- Unequal forces that cause acceleration
- **Action and reaction** -- Equal and opposite forces on different objects
- **Conservation of momentum** -- Total momentum remains constant in absence of external force
