## How Sound Is Produced and Travels

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook says sound is produced by vibrating objects. Can you explain how these vibrations reach our ears?

**Ravi:** When an object vibrates -- say, a tuning fork -- it pushes the air molecules next to it, creating a region of high pressure called a compression. When the fork swings back, it creates a region of low pressure called a rarefaction. These alternating compressions and rarefactions travel outward through the air as a wave, transferring energy from the source to your ear. The air molecules themselves do not travel from the fork to your ear -- they just oscillate back and forth.

**Kiran:** So sound is a longitudinal wave because the particles vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels?

**Ravi:** Exactly. In a longitudinal wave, particle vibration is along the direction of wave propagation. Contrast this with a transverse wave (like waves on a string), where particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of travel. An important consequence: sound needs a material medium -- solid, liquid, or gas. It cannot travel through a vacuum, which is why space is silent.

**Kiran:** The bell-jar experiment proves this?

**Ravi:** Yes. Place a ringing bell inside a glass jar and pump out the air. As air is removed, the sound becomes fainter and eventually inaudible, even though you can still see the bell vibrating. This experiment beautifully demonstrates that sound requires a medium for propagation.

## Characteristics of Sound Waves

**Kiran:** Wavelength, frequency, amplitude, time period -- how do they relate to what we hear?

**Ravi:** Frequency determines pitch -- a high-frequency sound (like a flute or a child's voice) has a high pitch, while a low-frequency sound (like a tabla or a lion's roar) has a low pitch. Amplitude determines loudness -- a larger amplitude means a louder sound. Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions. The speed of sound equals wavelength times frequency: v equals lambda times f.

**Kiran:** Why does sound travel faster in solids than in gases?

**Ravi:** Because particles in solids are packed tightly together and can transmit vibrations very quickly. In steel, sound travels at about 5960 m/s. In water, about 1531 m/s. In air, only about 346 m/s. This is why you can sometimes hear a distant train by placing your ear on the rail before you hear it through the air -- the sound through the steel rail arrives first.

**Kiran:** What is the speed of sound in air at room temperature?

**Ravi:** About 344 m/s at 22 degrees Celsius. Speed increases with temperature because warmer air molecules move faster and transmit vibrations more quickly. For board exams, use 340-346 m/s unless the problem specifies a different value.

## Reflection of Sound: Echo and SONAR

**Kiran:** An echo is just reflected sound, right? Why do we hear echoes only in large open spaces?

**Ravi:** The human brain retains a sound for about 0.1 seconds. To hear an echo distinctly, the reflected sound must arrive at least 0.1 seconds after the original. In that time, sound travels about 34.4 metres (at 344 m/s). Since the sound must travel to the reflecting surface and back, the minimum distance is half of 34.4, which is about 17.2 metres. In a small room, the reflection arrives too quickly and merges with the original sound -- this is reverberation, not echo.

**Kiran:** Reverberation can be a problem in large halls?

**Ravi:** Yes. Excessive reverberation makes speech unclear. Auditorium designers use sound-absorbing materials on walls and ceilings -- curtains, carpets, acoustic panels -- to reduce reflections. Concert halls are designed with precise acoustic calculations. The ITC Sangeet Research Academy in Kolkata and the National Centre for Performing Arts in Mumbai are examples of acoustically engineered spaces.

**Kiran:** SONAR uses ultrasound for underwater detection. How does it calculate distance?

**Ravi:** SONAR sends an ultrasonic pulse toward the sea bed or an underwater object. The pulse reflects and returns to the detector. If the total time is t seconds and the speed of sound in water is v, then the distance d equals v times t divided by 2 (dividing by 2 because the sound travels to the object and back). The Indian Navy uses SONAR extensively for submarine detection and ocean mapping.

## Ultrasound and Its Applications

**Kiran:** Humans can hear from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Ultrasound is above 20,000 Hz. What makes it useful?

**Ravi:** Ultrasound has several properties that make it valuable. It can penetrate dense materials, so it is used to detect cracks in metal structures (non-destructive testing). In medicine, ultrasonography uses reflected ultrasound to create images of internal organs -- it is safe (no radiation) and widely used during pregnancy to monitor foetal development. Ultrasound can also break kidney stones (lithotripsy) and clean hard-to-reach parts.

**Kiran:** Bats use ultrasound for navigation?

**Ravi:** Yes -- bats emit ultrasonic squeaks and listen for echoes to detect obstacles and prey. This is called echolocation. Dolphins and porpoises do the same. The principle is identical to SONAR. Engineers drew inspiration from bats when developing radar and sonar systems -- this is an example of biomimicry, where technology imitates nature.

## The Human Ear and Exam Tips

**Kiran:** How does our ear convert sound waves into something the brain understands?

**Ravi:** The outer ear (pinna) collects sound waves and funnels them through the ear canal to the eardrum (tympanic membrane). The eardrum vibrates, and these vibrations are amplified by three tiny bones in the middle ear -- the hammer, anvil, and stirrup (the smallest bones in the body). The amplified vibrations reach the cochlea in the inner ear, which contains fluid and hair cells. These hair cells convert mechanical vibrations into electrical signals, which travel via the auditory nerve to the brain. The brain interprets these signals as sound.

**Kiran:** What should I focus on for the board exam?

**Ravi:** Five key areas: the properties of longitudinal waves (compressions, rarefactions), the speed formula v equals lambda times f, the conditions for hearing an echo (minimum distance calculation), applications of ultrasound, and the working of SONAR. For numerical problems, practise echo distance calculations and frequency-wavelength conversions. Diagram-based questions on the human ear and SONAR working are also commonly asked. The physicist C.V. Raman, India's first Nobel laureate in science, did foundational work on the scattering of light and sound -- understanding waves was central to his research.
