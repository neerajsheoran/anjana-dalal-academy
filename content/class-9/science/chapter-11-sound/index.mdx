## What is Sound?

**Sound** is a form of energy that produces the sensation of hearing. We hear different sounds in our daily life -- people talking, birds chirping, horns honking, music playing. But what produces sound and how does it reach our ears?

### How is Sound Produced?

Sound is produced by **vibrating objects**. When an object vibrates, it creates disturbances in the surrounding medium (air, water, etc.) that travel as waves.

**Examples:**
- When you pluck a string of a guitar, the string vibrates and produces sound
- When you hit a drum, the membrane vibrates
- When you speak, your **vocal cords** vibrate
- A tuning fork produces sound when its prongs vibrate

> **Key idea:** Sound is produced by vibrations. When vibrations stop, the sound also stops. You can feel the vibration of a tabla or guitar string when it produces sound.

---

## Sound as a Wave

### How Does Sound Travel?

Sound travels as a **wave**. When an object vibrates, it pushes the air molecules near it. These molecules push the next set of molecules, and so on. In this way, sound energy is transferred from one point to another through the medium.

### Longitudinal Waves

Sound waves are **longitudinal waves**. In a longitudinal wave, the particles of the medium vibrate **back and forth** in the **same direction** as the wave travels.

This creates regions of:
- **Compression** -- where particles are pushed close together (high density, high pressure)
- **Rarefaction** -- where particles are spread apart (low density, low pressure)

Sound waves are a series of alternating compressions and rarefactions that travel through the medium.

> **Key idea:** In a longitudinal wave, the medium's particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Sound is a longitudinal wave.

### Transverse Waves

In a **transverse wave**, the particles vibrate **perpendicular** (at right angles) to the direction of wave travel. Light waves and water surface waves are transverse waves.

**Sound cannot travel through a vacuum** -- it needs a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel. This was demonstrated by the bell-jar experiment: when air is removed from a jar containing a ringing bell, the sound gradually becomes fainter and finally cannot be heard.

---

## Characteristics of Sound Waves

### 1. Wavelength

**Wavelength** is the distance between two consecutive compressions or two consecutive rarefactions. It is denoted by the Greek letter **lambda**.

**SI unit:** metre (m)

### 2. Frequency

**Frequency** is the number of complete oscillations (vibrations) per unit time.

**Frequency = Number of oscillations / Time**

**f = 1 / T**

- **SI unit:** Hertz (Hz)
- 1 Hz = 1 vibration per second
- **T** is the **time period** -- the time taken for one complete oscillation

### 3. Time Period

**Time period (T)** is the time taken to complete one oscillation.

**T = 1 / f**

**SI unit:** second (s)

### 4. Amplitude

**Amplitude** is the maximum displacement of a vibrating particle from its mean (rest) position. It determines the **loudness** of the sound.

**SI unit:** metre (m)

---

## Speed of Sound

The **speed of sound** is the distance travelled by the wave per unit time.

**Speed = Wavelength x Frequency**

**v = lambda x f**

### Speed of Sound in Different Media

Sound travels at different speeds in different media:
- **Solids:** fastest (about 5960 m/s in steel)
- **Liquids:** intermediate (about 1531 m/s in water)
- **Gases:** slowest (about 346 m/s in air at room temperature)

> **Key idea:** Sound travels fastest in solids because particles are closest together, and slowest in gases because particles are farthest apart.

The speed of sound in air increases with **temperature**. At 0 degrees C, it is about 331 m/s, and at room temperature (about 22 degrees C), it is about 344 m/s.

---

## Loudness and Pitch

### Loudness

**Loudness** depends on the **amplitude** of the sound wave. Greater amplitude means louder sound.

- Loudness is measured in **decibels (dB)**
- Normal conversation: about 60 dB
- Whisper: about 20 dB
- Painful sound: above 80 dB

Loudness also depends on the distance from the source -- it decreases as you move farther away.

### Pitch

**Pitch** depends on the **frequency** of the sound wave.

- **High frequency** = high pitch (shrill sound) -- e.g., a whistle, a baby's cry
- **Low frequency** = low pitch (bass sound) -- e.g., a lion's roar, a drum

Women and children generally have higher-pitched voices than men because their vocal cords vibrate at higher frequencies.

### Quality (Timbre)

**Quality** or **timbre** helps us distinguish between two sounds of the same pitch and loudness but produced by different instruments. It depends on the **waveform** of the sound.

This is why you can tell the difference between a guitar and a flute playing the same note.

## Audible and Inaudible Sounds

### Range of Hearing

- **Audible range** for humans: **20 Hz to 20,000 Hz** (20 kHz)
- Sounds below 20 Hz are called **infrasound** -- humans cannot hear these, but some animals (like elephants and whales) can
- Sounds above 20,000 Hz are called **ultrasound** -- humans cannot hear these, but dogs and bats can

### Applications of Ultrasound

Ultrasound has many important applications:
- **Medical imaging (ultrasonography/sonography)** -- to produce images of internal organs, examine the foetus during pregnancy
- **Echocardiography** -- to examine the heart
- **Cleaning** -- ultrasonic waves can clean hard-to-reach parts of machinery
- **Breaking kidney stones (lithotripsy)** -- high-energy ultrasound can break kidney stones into small pieces
- **Quality testing (SONAR principle)** -- detecting cracks and flaws in metal blocks

## Reflection of Sound

Sound waves obey the **laws of reflection**:
1. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection
2. The incident sound, the reflected sound, and the normal all lie in the same plane

### Echo

An **echo** is the repetition of sound caused by the **reflection of sound waves** from a hard surface (like a wall, cliff, or building).

**Conditions for hearing an echo:**
- The reflected sound must reach the ear **at least 0.1 seconds** after the original sound
- The minimum distance between the source and the reflecting surface should be about **17.2 m** (at 20 degrees C in air, where speed of sound = 344 m/s)

**Calculation:** Distance = speed x time / 2 = 344 x 0.1 / 2 = 17.2 m

### Reverberation

When sound reflects multiple times in an enclosed space (like a hall), the reflected sounds mix with the original sound, creating a **prolonged effect**. This is called **reverberation**.

To reduce reverberation in auditoriums and concert halls:
- Walls and ceilings are covered with **sound-absorbing materials** like curtains, carpets, compressed fibreboard
- Curved surfaces are avoided to prevent focusing of sound

---

## Uses of Multiple Reflection of Sound

- **Megaphones and horns** -- funnel-shaped tubes that prevent sound from spreading in all directions
- **Stethoscope** -- multiple reflections carry heart and lung sounds to the doctor's ears
- **Soundboards** -- curved boards behind the stage in concert halls that reflect sound towards the audience

---

## SONAR

**SONAR** stands for **Sound Navigation And Ranging**. It uses ultrasonic waves to measure distances, detect objects, and map the ocean floor.

**How SONAR works:**
1. A transmitter produces and sends ultrasonic waves towards the ocean floor
2. The waves travel through water, hit an object (or the sea bed), and are reflected back
3. A detector receives the reflected waves
4. The time between sending and receiving the signal is measured
5. Distance is calculated using: **d = v x t / 2** (divide by 2 because sound travels to the object and back)

### Applications of SONAR

- Measuring **depth of the sea**
- Locating **submarines** and underwater obstacles
- Finding **schools of fish**
- Mapping the **ocean floor**

## Auditory System: How We Hear

The **ear** is the organ of hearing. It has three main parts:

**Outer ear (pinna)** -- collects sound waves from the surroundings and directs them into the ear canal

**Middle ear** -- contains three tiny bones (hammer, anvil, stirrup) that amplify the vibrations. The eardrum (tympanic membrane) vibrates when sound waves hit it, and the three bones transmit and amplify these vibrations.

**Inner ear (cochlea)** -- converts vibrations into electrical signals that are sent to the brain via the auditory nerve. The brain interprets these signals as sound.

> **Key idea:** The ear converts sound waves (mechanical energy) into electrical signals that the brain can understand.

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Compression** -- Region of high pressure in a longitudinal wave
- **Rarefaction** -- Region of low pressure in a longitudinal wave
- **Wavelength** -- Distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions
- **Frequency** -- Number of oscillations per second (Hz)
- **Amplitude** -- Maximum displacement from mean position
- **Loudness** -- Depends on amplitude (measured in decibels)
- **Pitch** -- Depends on frequency (high frequency = high pitch)
- **Timbre** -- Quality of sound that distinguishes different sources
- **Echo** -- Reflected sound heard after a delay
- **Reverberation** -- Prolonged sound due to multiple reflections
- **Ultrasound** -- Sound above 20,000 Hz
- **Infrasound** -- Sound below 20 Hz
- **SONAR** -- Uses ultrasound for underwater detection and measurement
