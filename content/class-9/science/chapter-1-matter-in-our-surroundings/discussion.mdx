## What Is Matter?

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook says that matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. But what about light? It has energy -- is it matter?

**Ravi:** Excellent question. Light has energy but no mass (in the classical sense) and does not occupy space the way a solid or liquid does. So light is not matter -- it is a form of energy. Similarly, heat, sound, and electricity are energy, not matter. The key test is: does it have mass and volume? If yes, it is matter. Air passes this test even though we cannot see it -- a blown-up balloon is heavier than an empty one.

**Kiran:** The textbook mentions the Panch Tatva -- five elements that ancient Indian philosophers proposed. Was that scientific?

**Ravi:** The Panch Tatva -- earth, water, fire, air, and sky (akash) -- was a philosophical classification, not a scientific one in the modern sense. But it showed that Indian thinkers were asking the right questions about the nature of the universe over 2500 years ago. Modern science classifies matter differently -- by physical state (solid, liquid, gas) and by chemical nature (elements, compounds, mixtures). The Greek philosopher Democritus proposed the idea of atoms around the same time as the Indian philosopher Maharishi Kanad proposed parmanu.

## The Three States of Matter

**Kiran:** I know solids have fixed shape and volume, liquids have fixed volume but take the shape of the container, and gases have neither. But why? What is happening at the particle level?

**Ravi:** It all comes down to three factors: how close the particles are, how strongly they attract each other, and how much kinetic energy they have. In solids, particles are tightly packed with strong attractions and can only vibrate -- that is why solids are rigid. In liquids, particles are close but can slide over each other -- that is why liquids flow. In gases, particles are far apart with very weak attractions and move rapidly in all directions -- that is why gases fill any container.

**Kiran:** Why does a sponge seem compressible even though it is a solid?

**Ravi:** The sponge itself is a solid, but it has tiny holes filled with air. When you press a sponge, you are squeezing out the air -- the solid material itself is not being compressed. This is a favourite board exam question designed to test conceptual understanding.

**Kiran:** And why is diffusion faster in gases than in liquids?

**Ravi:** Because gas particles move faster and have much more space between them. When someone opens a bottle of perfume across the room, you smell it within seconds -- the perfume molecules diffuse through air rapidly. In contrast, if you drop ink into water, it takes minutes to spread. In solids, diffusion is extremely slow -- it can take years for atoms to migrate through a solid.

## Change of State

**Kiran:** When ice melts, we keep adding heat but the temperature stays at 0 degrees Celsius. Where does the heat energy go?

**Ravi:** This is the concept of latent heat -- "latent" means hidden. The heat is used to break the bonds between particles, changing their arrangement from rigid solid to flowing liquid. The particles gain potential energy (they move further apart) without gaining kinetic energy (temperature stays the same). Only after all the ice has melted does the temperature start rising again.

**Kiran:** And steam at 100 degrees Celsius causes worse burns than boiling water at 100 degrees?

**Ravi:** Yes, because steam carries additional latent heat of vaporisation. When steam touches your skin, it first releases this latent heat as it condenses to water, and then the water also releases heat as it cools. So steam delivers more energy to your skin than boiling water at the same temperature. This concept is used in steam-based power plants -- the latent heat of steam drives turbines that generate electricity.

**Kiran:** What about sublimation? Camphor and naphthalene balls just disappear without melting.

**Ravi:** Sublimation occurs when particles at the surface of a solid gain enough energy to escape directly into the gas phase, bypassing the liquid state. Dry ice (solid CO2) is a dramatic example -- it produces white fog as it sublimes. In India, naphthalene balls are commonly used in wardrobes to repel insects; they slowly sublime and eventually vanish completely.

## Evaporation and Its Effects

**Kiran:** Evaporation happens at any temperature, not just at the boiling point. How is that possible?

**Ravi:** At any temperature, particles in a liquid have a range of kinetic energies. Most particles do not have enough energy to escape, but a few near the surface do. These high-energy particles break free and become vapour. Since the escaping particles carry away energy, the remaining liquid becomes cooler. This is why evaporation causes cooling.

**Kiran:** So that is why we feel cold when we step out of a swimming pool?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The water on your skin evaporates, absorbing heat from your body. This is also why earthen pots (matkas) keep water cool in Indian summers -- water seeps through the pores and evaporates on the outer surface, cooling the water inside. Desert coolers work on the same principle. Perspiration cools our body the same way.

**Kiran:** What factors affect the rate of evaporation?

**Ravi:** Four factors: surface area (larger area means faster evaporation -- that is why we spread clothes to dry), temperature (hotter means faster), humidity (drier air means faster -- clothes take longer to dry in rainy season), and wind speed (wind blows away vapour, allowing more evaporation). For board exams, be ready to explain each factor with an everyday Indian example.

## Temperature Scales and SI Units

**Kiran:** Why does science use Kelvin instead of Celsius?

**Ravi:** The Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero -- the lowest possible temperature where particles have minimal energy. There are no negative temperatures on the Kelvin scale, which makes mathematical formulas simpler and more elegant. The conversion is straightforward: add 273 to Celsius to get Kelvin. So 0 degrees Celsius equals 273 K, and 100 degrees Celsius equals 373 K.

**Kiran:** Any last exam tips for this chapter?

**Ravi:** Remember the distinction between boiling and evaporation -- boiling is a bulk phenomenon that occurs at a fixed temperature, while evaporation is a surface phenomenon that occurs at any temperature. Know the conditions for state changes: melting point, boiling point, and the role of pressure. And always express temperatures in Kelvin when doing scientific calculations. The Indian scientists at ISRO work with temperatures ranging from near absolute zero in space to thousands of degrees during re-entry -- understanding these concepts is fundamental to space science.
