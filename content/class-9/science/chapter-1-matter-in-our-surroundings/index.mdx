## What is Matter?

Look around you -- everything you can see, touch, or smell is made up of **matter**. The chair you sit on, the air you breathe, the water you drink, the food you eat -- all of these are matter.

**Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space (has volume).**

Even things you cannot see, like air, are matter because they have mass and take up space. However, things like love, hate, thoughts, and smell are **not matter** because they do not have mass or volume.

> **Key idea:** If something has both mass and volume, it is matter. Feelings, thoughts, and emotions are not matter.

Early Indian philosophers classified all matter into five basic elements called **Panch Tatva** -- air, earth, fire, sky, and water. They believed everything in the universe, living or non-living, was made up of these five elements. Ancient Greek philosophers had a similar idea.

Modern scientists classify matter in two ways:
- Based on its **physical properties** (solid, liquid, gas)
- Based on its **chemical nature** (elements, compounds, mixtures)

This chapter focuses on the **physical properties** of matter.

## Physical Nature of Matter

### Matter is Made Up of Particles

When you dissolve sugar or salt in water, the sugar or salt seems to disappear. But the water tastes sweet or salty. Where did the sugar or salt go?

The particles of sugar or salt spread out and fit into the **spaces between the particles of water**. This tells us an important fact:

> **Key idea:** Matter is not continuous -- it is made up of tiny particles. These particles are so small that we cannot see them with our eyes.

### How Small Are These Particles?

If you dissolve just 2-3 crystals of **potassium permanganate** (KMnO4) in 100 mL of water, you get a deep purple solution. Now if you keep diluting this solution (taking 10 mL and adding 90 mL of water), even after 5-8 dilutions the water still appears coloured. This means just a few crystals contain **millions of tiny particles**.

> **Key idea:** The particles of matter are extremely small -- far beyond what we can imagine or see with the naked eye.

## Characteristics of Particles of Matter

### 1. Particles of Matter Have Space Between Them

When you dissolve salt in water, the water level in the container does not rise. This is because the salt particles fit into the spaces between water particles.

Similarly, when you make tea, coffee, or **nimbu paani** (lemonade), the particles of one substance get into the spaces between particles of the other.

> **Key idea:** There is enough space between the particles of matter. This is why substances can dissolve in each other.

### 2. Particles of Matter Are Continuously Moving

Have you noticed that when someone lights an **agarbatti** (incense stick) in one corner of a room, the fragrance reaches the other corner within minutes? This happens because the particles of the scent mix with the particles of air and travel.

The smell of hot food reaches you faster than that of cold food. Why? Because **higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy**, making them move faster.

- Particles of matter possess **kinetic energy** -- the energy of motion
- As **temperature increases**, particles move faster
- When particles of two different substances mix on their own, it is called **diffusion**
- **Diffusion is faster at higher temperatures**

> **Key idea:** Particles of matter are always in motion. The hotter the substance, the faster its particles move. The mixing of particles on their own is called diffusion.

### 3. Particles of Matter Attract Each Other

There is a **force of attraction** between the particles of matter. This force holds the particles together.

- In an **iron nail**, the particles are held together very strongly -- it is very hard to break
- In a **piece of chalk**, the force is weaker -- it breaks more easily
- In **water**, the particles attract each other but can still flow over one another

> **Key idea:** The strength of the force of attraction between particles varies from one substance to another. This force determines many physical properties of matter.

## States of Matter

Matter exists in **three states** -- solid, liquid, and gas. These states arise because of differences in the arrangement, movement, and attraction of particles.

### The Solid State

**Examples:** pen, book, iron nail, wooden stick, sugar crystals, ice

**Properties of solids:**
- Have a **definite shape** and **distinct boundaries**
- Have a **fixed volume**
- Cannot be **compressed** easily (negligible compressibility)
- Are **rigid** -- they maintain their shape when a force is applied
- Particles are **tightly packed** and held together by strong forces of attraction
- Particles can only **vibrate** at their fixed positions
- They may break under force, but their shape is hard to change

**Common doubts:**
- **Rubber band** -- It stretches but returns to its original shape. It is still a solid.
- **Sugar and salt** -- Individual crystals have a fixed shape, even though they take the shape of the container as a group.
- **Sponge** -- It is a solid with tiny holes that trap air. When pressed, air is squeezed out, making it appear compressible.

### The Liquid State

**Examples:** water, milk, cooking oil, juice, cold drinks

**Properties of liquids:**
- Have a **fixed volume** but **no fixed shape**
- Take the **shape of the container** in which they are kept
- Can **flow** easily -- they are called **fluids**
- Are **not rigid** -- their shape changes easily
- Particles are **less tightly packed** than in solids
- Particles can **slip and slide** over each other
- The **rate of diffusion** in liquids is higher than in solids because particles have more space and move more freely

> **Key idea:** Gases from the atmosphere (especially oxygen and carbon dioxide) dissolve in water through diffusion. This dissolved oxygen is essential for aquatic animals to breathe.

### The Gaseous State

**Examples:** air, oxygen, carbon dioxide, steam, LPG, CNG

**Properties of gases:**
- Have **no fixed shape** and **no fixed volume**
- Fill the **entire container** in which they are kept
- Are **highly compressible** -- large volumes of gas can be compressed into small cylinders (like LPG or oxygen cylinders)
- Particles are **very far apart** and move **randomly at high speed**
- Particles hit each other and the walls of the container, creating **pressure**
- The **rate of diffusion** is highest in gases

This is why the smell of hot food reaches you quickly -- the particles of the food's aroma mix with air particles and travel fast.

> **Key idea:** Due to high compressibility, large amounts of gas can be stored in small cylinders. LPG (cooking gas) and CNG (vehicle fuel) are examples of compressed gases.

### Summary Table: Comparing Three States of Matter

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shape** | Fixed | Not fixed (takes container shape) | Not fixed |
| **Volume** | Fixed | Fixed | Not fixed |
| **Compressibility** | Negligible | Very little | Highly compressible |
| **Rigidity** | Rigid | Not rigid (fluid) | Not rigid (fluid) |
| **Density** | Generally high | Moderate | Low |
| **Kinetic energy** | Low | Moderate | High |
| **Particle arrangement** | Tightly packed, ordered | Close but can slip over each other | Far apart, random |
| **Diffusion rate** | Very slow | Slow | Very fast |

## Can Matter Change Its State?

Yes! The state of matter can be changed. Water is the best example:
- **Ice** (solid) melts to form **water** (liquid)
- **Water** (liquid) boils to form **steam/water vapour** (gas)

The change of state happens because the **energy** and **arrangement of particles** change.

### Effect of Change of Temperature

#### Solid to Liquid: Melting (Fusion)

When you heat a solid (like ice), its particles gain **kinetic energy** and start vibrating faster. At a certain temperature, the energy overcomes the **forces of attraction** between particles. The particles start moving more freely, and the solid turns into a liquid.

- The temperature at which a solid melts to become a liquid (at atmospheric pressure) is called the **melting point**
- The **melting point of ice** is **273.15 K** (0 degrees Celsius)
- The melting point tells us about the **strength of the forces of attraction** between particles -- the higher the melting point, the stronger the forces
- Melting is also called **fusion**

> **Key idea:** During melting, the temperature remains constant even though heat is being supplied. This heat energy is used to overcome the forces of attraction between particles, not to raise the temperature.

#### Latent Heat of Fusion

The heat that is absorbed during melting without any rise in temperature is called **latent heat** (latent means hidden).

**Latent heat of fusion** is the amount of heat energy required to change **1 kg of a solid into a liquid at atmospheric pressure, at its melting point**.

This is why particles in water at 0 degrees Celsius have **more energy** than particles in ice at the same temperature.

#### Liquid to Gas: Boiling (Vaporisation)

When you heat water, its particles move faster and faster. At a certain temperature, the particles have enough energy to break free from each other completely. The liquid starts changing into gas.

- The temperature at which a liquid starts boiling (at atmospheric pressure) is called the **boiling point**
- The **boiling point of water** is **373 K** (100 degrees Celsius)
- Boiling is a **bulk phenomenon** -- particles from the entire liquid change to vapour

**Latent heat of vaporisation** is the amount of heat energy required to change **1 kg of a liquid into gas at atmospheric pressure, at its boiling point**.

Particles in steam at 100 degrees Celsius have **more energy** than water at the same temperature because they have absorbed the latent heat of vaporisation.

> **Key idea:** Steam causes more severe burns than boiling water at the same temperature because steam has extra energy in the form of latent heat of vaporisation.

#### Temperature Scale: Kelvin and Celsius

- The **SI unit of temperature** is **Kelvin (K)**
- 0 degrees Celsius = 273 K
- To convert Celsius to Kelvin: **add 273**
- To convert Kelvin to Celsius: **subtract 273**

**Examples:**
- 25 degrees C = 25 + 273 = **298 K**
- 100 degrees C = 100 + 273 = **373 K**
- 300 K = 300 - 273 = **27 degrees C**
- 470 K = 470 - 273 = **197 degrees C**

### Sublimation and Deposition

Some substances change directly from **solid to gas** without passing through the liquid state. This is called **sublimation**.

The reverse process -- gas changing directly to solid -- is called **deposition**.

**Examples of sublimation:**
- **Camphor** -- when heated, it turns directly into vapour
- **Naphthalene balls** (used to keep moths away) gradually disappear without leaving any liquid behind
- **Dry ice** (solid CO2) -- stored under high pressure, it changes directly to CO2 gas when pressure is reduced to normal atmospheric pressure

> **Key idea:** Naphthalene balls disappear over time because they undergo sublimation -- they change directly from solid to gas without melting.

### Effect of Change of Pressure

Gases can be **liquefied** (turned into liquids) by applying **high pressure** and **reducing temperature**.

When pressure is applied, gas particles are forced closer together. Combined with cooling, the particles slow down enough for forces of attraction to hold them in the liquid state.

> **Key idea:** The state of a substance depends on both **temperature** and **pressure**. By changing these conditions, we can convert matter from one state to another.

## Evaporation

### What is Evaporation?

We do not always need to boil a liquid to change it into vapour. Have you noticed that:
- Water left in an open vessel slowly disappears?
- Wet clothes dry up even without heating?

At any temperature, a small number of particles at the **surface of a liquid** have enough kinetic energy to escape into the air as vapour. This process is called **evaporation**.

> **Key idea:** Evaporation is the change of a liquid into vapour at any temperature below its boiling point. It is a **surface phenomenon** (happens only at the surface), unlike boiling which is a **bulk phenomenon**.

### Factors Affecting Evaporation

**1. Surface area** -- Evaporation is faster when the surface area is larger. This is why we **spread out** wet clothes to dry them faster.

**2. Temperature** -- Higher temperature means more particles have enough energy to escape. Clothes dry faster on a hot day than a cold day.

**3. Humidity** -- Humidity is the amount of water vapour already present in the air. If the air is already full of moisture (high humidity), evaporation slows down. This is why clothes take longer to dry on a **rainy day**.

**4. Wind speed** -- On a windy day, water vapour near the surface is blown away, allowing more liquid particles to evaporate. Clothes dry faster on a **windy day**.

### How Does Evaporation Cause Cooling?

When liquid particles evaporate, they **absorb energy from the surroundings**. This makes the surroundings cooler.

**Examples:**
- When you put **acetone** (nail polish remover) or **petrol** on your palm, it feels cold because the liquid evaporates quickly, absorbing heat from your skin
- After a hot day, people **sprinkle water** on the roof or ground -- the water evaporates and cools the surface
- We **perspire** (sweat) during summer -- when sweat evaporates from our skin, it absorbs heat and cools our body
- We wear **cotton clothes in summer** because cotton absorbs sweat well and exposes it to air for quick evaporation
- A **desert cooler** works better on a hot, dry day because dry air allows faster evaporation, producing more cooling
- Water in an **earthen pot (matka)** stays cool because the pot has tiny pores through which water seeps out and evaporates, cooling the remaining water
- We can sip hot tea faster from a **saucer** than a cup because the saucer has a larger surface area, which increases evaporation and cools the tea faster

> **Key idea:** Evaporation causes cooling because the escaping particles take away heat energy from the liquid and its surroundings.

### Water Droplets on a Cold Glass

When you keep ice-cold water in a glass, you notice water droplets forming on the **outside** of the glass. This happens because the water vapour in the surrounding air comes in contact with the cold surface, loses energy, and **condenses** (changes from gas to liquid).

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Matter** -- Anything that has mass and occupies space
- **Diffusion** -- Intermixing of particles of different types of matter on their own
- **Melting point** -- Temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid
- **Boiling point** -- Temperature at which a liquid starts boiling
- **Latent heat of fusion** -- Heat required to change 1 kg of solid to liquid at its melting point
- **Latent heat of vaporisation** -- Heat required to change 1 kg of liquid to gas at its boiling point
- **Sublimation** -- Direct change from solid to gas without passing through liquid state
- **Deposition** -- Direct change from gas to solid without passing through liquid state
- **Evaporation** -- Change of liquid to vapour at any temperature below boiling point
- **Humidity** -- Amount of water vapour present in air
