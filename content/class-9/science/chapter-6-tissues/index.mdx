## What is a Tissue?

In multicellular organisms, millions of cells work together. Cells that are similar in structure and function are grouped together to form a **tissue**. Each tissue is designed to perform a specific job with maximum efficiency.

> **Key idea:** A tissue is a group of cells that are similar in structure and/or work together to achieve a particular function.

**Examples of tissues:** blood, muscle, phloem, bone

In **unicellular organisms** like Amoeba, a single cell performs all functions. In **multicellular organisms**, there is **division of labour** -- different groups of cells perform different functions.

---

## Differences Between Plant and Animal Tissues

Plants and animals have different types of tissues because they live very differently:

- **Plants are stationary** -- they need lots of **supportive tissue** (often made of dead cells)
- **Animals move around** -- they need more **living tissues** that use energy
- **Plants grow** only in certain regions (meristematic regions) -- animals grow more uniformly
- Plant organs are less specialised compared to the complex organ systems of animals

---

## Plant Tissues

Plant tissues are broadly classified into two types:
1. **Meristematic tissue** (dividing tissue)
2. **Permanent tissue** (non-dividing tissue)

### Meristematic Tissue

**Meristematic tissue** is made up of cells that can **divide actively**. Growth in plants occurs only in regions where meristematic tissue is present.

**Characteristics of meristematic cells:**
- Very **active** with dense cytoplasm
- Thin cellulose **cell walls**
- **Prominent nuclei**
- **No vacuoles** (because they are actively dividing, not storing)

**Types based on location:**
- **Apical meristem** -- present at the **growing tips** of stems and roots; increases the **length** of the plant
- **Lateral meristem (cambium)** -- present along the sides of stems and roots; increases the **girth** (thickness)
- **Intercalary meristem** -- present near the **nodes** (joints) in some plants; helps in growth at the base of leaves

> **Key idea:** Meristematic tissue is responsible for the growth of plants. It is located at specific growing regions of the plant.

---

### Permanent Tissue

When meristematic cells lose their ability to divide, they take up a **permanent shape, size, and function**. This process is called **differentiation**. The resulting tissue is called **permanent tissue**.

Permanent tissue is of two types:
1. **Simple permanent tissue** (made of one type of cell)
2. **Complex permanent tissue** (made of more than one type of cell)

---

### Simple Permanent Tissue

#### 1. Parenchyma

- Most **common** simple permanent tissue
- Cells are **living**, relatively unspecialised, with **thin cell walls**
- **Loosely arranged** with large **intercellular spaces**
- **Function:** Stores food
- **Chlorenchyma:** When parenchyma contains chlorophyll and performs photosynthesis
- **Aerenchyma:** In aquatic plants, parenchyma has large **air cavities** to help them float

#### 2. Collenchyma

- Cells are **living**, elongated, with **irregularly thickened corners**
- Very little intercellular space
- **Function:** Provides **flexibility** to plants (allows bending without breaking) and mechanical support
- Found in **leaf stalks** and below the epidermis of stems

#### 3. Sclerenchyma

- Cells are **dead**, long, and narrow
- Walls are **thickened with lignin** (so thick that sometimes there is no space inside the cell)
- **Function:** Makes the plant **hard and stiff**; provides **strength**
- Found in stems, around vascular bundles, veins of leaves, hard covering of seeds and nuts
- Example: The **husk of a coconut** is made of sclerenchyma

> **Key idea:** Parenchyma stores food, collenchyma provides flexibility, and sclerenchyma provides strength and hardness.

#### Epidermis

- The **outermost layer** of cells covering the entire plant surface
- Usually a **single layer** of cells without intercellular spaces (for continuous protection)
- **Function:** Protects against water loss, mechanical injury, and invasion by fungi
- Aerial parts often secrete a **waxy, water-resistant layer (cutin/cuticle)** on the outer surface
- Contains **stomata** -- small pores enclosed by two **kidney-shaped guard cells** for gas exchange and transpiration
- Root epidermal cells have **root hairs** to increase the surface area for water absorption
- Desert plants have a **thick waxy coating** of cutin to prevent water loss

#### Cork

- As plants grow older, the epidermis is replaced by **cork** -- formed by secondary meristem in the cortex
- Cork cells are **dead**, compactly arranged with **no intercellular spaces**
- Contain **suberin** in their walls, which makes them **impervious** (waterproof) to gases and water
- **Function:** Protects the plant from water loss and infection

---

### Complex Permanent Tissue

Complex tissues are made of **more than one type of cell**, all working together to perform a common function. The two main complex tissues are **xylem** and **phloem**, which together form the **vascular bundle**.

#### Xylem

Xylem transports **water and minerals** from roots to other parts of the plant (upward).

**Components:**
- **Tracheids** -- elongated dead cells with thick walls; transport water
- **Vessels** -- tubular dead cells; transport water
- **Xylem parenchyma** -- living cells; store food
- **Xylem fibres** -- provide support

> **Key idea:** Except for xylem parenchyma, most xylem cells are dead. The thick walls and tubular structure allow them to transport water efficiently.

#### Phloem

Phloem transports **food** (prepared by photosynthesis) from leaves to other parts of the plant (in both directions).

**Components:**
- **Sieve tubes** -- tubular cells with perforated (sieve-like) walls; transport food
- **Companion cells** -- help sieve tubes function
- **Phloem parenchyma** -- store food
- **Phloem fibres** -- provide support

> **Key idea:** Except for phloem fibres, all phloem cells are living. Phloem transports food, while xylem transports water.

## Animal Tissues

Animal tissues are classified into four main types:
1. **Epithelial tissue**
2. **Connective tissue**
3. **Muscular tissue**
4. **Nervous tissue**

---

### 1. Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial tissue forms the **covering or lining** of most organs and body cavities. It protects the underlying tissues and regulates the exchange of materials.

**Characteristics:**
- Cells are **tightly packed** with little or no intercellular spaces
- Forms a **continuous sheet**
- Rests on a **basement membrane**

**Types of epithelial tissue:**

| Type | Shape | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Squamous** | Flat, thin | Lining of blood vessels, lung alveoli, oesophagus | Transport and exchange of substances |
| **Stratified squamous** | Multiple layers of flat cells | Skin | Protection against wear and tear |
| **Cuboidal** | Cube-shaped | Kidney tubules, salivary gland ducts | Mechanical support, secretion |
| **Columnar** | Tall, pillar-like | Inner lining of intestine | Absorption and secretion |
| **Ciliated columnar** | Columnar with hair-like cilia | Respiratory tract | Pushes mucus forward |
| **Glandular** | Modified columnar | Glands | Secretion of substances |

---

### 2. Connective Tissue

Connective tissue **connects, supports, and binds** different tissues and organs. It has cells scattered in a **matrix** (a non-living material that fills the space between cells).

**Types of connective tissue:**

| Type | Matrix | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Blood** | Fluid (plasma) | Transports oxygen, food, hormones, and waste |
| **Bone** | Hard, calcium-rich | Provides framework and support; protects organs |
| **Cartilage** | Solid but flexible | Smoothens bone surfaces at joints; found in nose, ear, trachea |
| **Tendon** | Strong, limited flexibility | Connects **muscle to bone** |
| **Ligament** | Elastic, strong | Connects **bone to bone** |
| **Areolar tissue** | Loose, fills spaces | Found between skin and muscles; supports internal organs |
| **Adipose tissue** | Fat-storing cells | Stores fat; provides insulation |

> **Key idea:** Blood is a connective tissue with a fluid matrix (plasma). It contains RBCs, WBCs, and platelets suspended in plasma.

---

### 3. Muscular Tissue

Muscular tissue is made of elongated cells called **muscle fibres** that contain special **contractile proteins**. These proteins allow muscles to **contract and relax**, causing movement.

**Types of muscle tissue:**

| Feature | Striated (Skeletal) | Smooth (Unstriated) | Cardiac |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shape** | Long, cylindrical | Spindle-shaped (pointed ends) | Cylindrical, branched |
| **Nucleus** | Multinucleate (many) | Uninucleate (one) | Uninucleate (one) |
| **Striations** | Present (light and dark bands) | Absent | Present |
| **Control** | Voluntary | Involuntary | Involuntary |
| **Location** | Attached to bones (limbs) | Alimentary canal, blood vessels, iris | Heart only |
| **Function** | Body movement | Movement of food, contraction of blood vessels | Rhythmic pumping of heart |

> **Key idea:** Skeletal muscles are voluntary (we can control them). Smooth and cardiac muscles are involuntary (work automatically without our conscious control).

---

### 4. Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is specialised for receiving stimuli and transmitting impulses rapidly throughout the body.

**The nerve cell (neuron) consists of:**
- **Cell body** -- contains the nucleus and cytoplasm
- **Dendrites** -- short, branched projections that receive signals
- **Axon** -- a single long fibre that transmits signals away from the cell body
- **Nerve ending** -- the terminal part that connects with the next neuron or muscle

An individual neuron can be up to **a metre long**. Many nerve fibres bound together by connective tissue make up a **nerve**.

A **nerve impulse** is the electrical signal that travels along the nerve fibre. Impulses are transmitted from the nerve ending of one neuron to the dendrite of the next neuron.

> **Key idea:** The combination of nervous tissue and muscular tissue enables animals to respond rapidly to stimuli -- nerve cells detect the stimulus and transmit impulses, while muscles carry out the response.

---

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Tissue** -- Group of similar cells performing a specific function
- **Meristematic tissue** -- Actively dividing plant tissue at growing points
- **Permanent tissue** -- Differentiated plant tissue that has lost the ability to divide
- **Parenchyma** -- Living cells that store food
- **Collenchyma** -- Living cells that provide flexibility
- **Sclerenchyma** -- Dead cells that provide strength
- **Xylem** -- Transports water and minerals upward
- **Phloem** -- Transports food from leaves to other parts
- **Epithelial tissue** -- Covers and protects body surfaces
- **Connective tissue** -- Connects, supports, and binds tissues
- **Muscular tissue** -- Enables movement through contraction
- **Nervous tissue** -- Receives and transmits impulses
- **Stomata** -- Pores in the epidermis for gas exchange
- **Lignin** -- Substance that makes sclerenchyma walls hard
- **Suberin** -- Waterproof substance in cork cell walls
