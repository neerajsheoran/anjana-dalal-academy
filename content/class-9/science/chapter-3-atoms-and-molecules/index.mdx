## Introduction

The idea that matter is made up of tiny, indivisible particles is very old. Around 500 BC, the Indian philosopher **Maharishi Kanad** proposed that if we keep dividing matter, we will eventually reach the smallest particle that cannot be divided further. He called these particles **Parmanu**. Another Indian philosopher, **Pakudha Katyayama**, said that these particles normally exist in a combined form.

Around the same time, Greek philosophers **Democritus** and **Leucippus** suggested that matter is made of indivisible particles, which Democritus called **atoms** (meaning "indivisible").

By the 18th century, scientists began to study how elements combine to form compounds, leading to important laws of chemical combination.

---

## Laws of Chemical Combination

### 1. Law of Conservation of Mass

> **Key idea:** Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

This means that the **total mass of reactants** (substances that react) is always **equal to the total mass of products** (substances formed) in a chemical reaction.

**Example:** If 5.3 g of sodium carbonate reacts with 6 g of acetic acid, and the products are 2.2 g of carbon dioxide, 0.9 g of water, and 8.2 g of sodium acetate:
- Total mass of reactants = 5.3 + 6.0 = **11.3 g**
- Total mass of products = 2.2 + 0.9 + 8.2 = **11.3 g**

The masses are equal, which verifies the law of conservation of mass.

### 2. Law of Constant Proportions (Law of Definite Proportions)

> **Key idea:** In a chemical substance, the elements are always present in definite (fixed) proportions by mass, regardless of the source or method of preparation.

**Examples:**
- In **water (H2O)**, hydrogen and oxygen are always in the ratio **1:8 by mass**. If you decompose 9 g of water, you always get 1 g of hydrogen and 8 g of oxygen.
- In **ammonia (NH3)**, nitrogen and hydrogen are always in the ratio **14:3 by mass**.

This law tells us that a compound always has the same composition no matter where it comes from.

## Dalton's Atomic Theory

**John Dalton** (1766-1844), a British chemist, proposed the atomic theory based on the laws of chemical combination. His main postulates were:

1. All matter is made of very tiny particles called **atoms**
2. Atoms are **indivisible** -- they cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction
3. Atoms of a given element are **identical** in mass and chemical properties
4. Atoms of different elements have **different** masses and chemical properties
5. Atoms combine in the ratio of **small whole numbers** to form compounds
6. The relative number and kinds of atoms are **constant** in a given compound

> **Key idea:** Dalton's atomic theory explained the law of conservation of mass (atoms are not created or destroyed) and the law of definite proportions (atoms combine in fixed ratios).

**Note:** We now know that atoms are themselves made of still smaller particles (electrons, protons, neutrons), but Dalton's theory was a crucial starting point.

## What is an Atom?

An **atom** is the smallest particle of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. Atoms are the building blocks of all matter.

### How Small Are Atoms?

Atoms are incredibly small:
- Atomic radius is measured in **nanometres (nm)**
- 1 nm = 10 to the power -9 m (one billionth of a metre)
- Millions of atoms stacked together would barely be as thick as a sheet of paper

### Symbols of Elements

**Dalton** was the first to use symbols for elements. Later, **Berzelius** suggested using one or two letters from the element's name.

**Rules for writing symbols:**
- The **first letter** is always **capital** (uppercase)
- The **second letter** (if any) is always **small** (lowercase)

**Examples:**
- Hydrogen = **H**, Oxygen = **O**, Carbon = **C**
- Aluminium = **Al** (not AL)
- Cobalt = **Co** (not CO)
- Chlorine = **Cl**, Zinc = **Zn**

Some symbols come from **Latin names**:
- Iron = **Fe** (from Latin: ferrum)
- Sodium = **Na** (from Latin: natrium)
- Potassium = **K** (from Latin: kalium)
- Gold = **Au** (from Latin: aurum)
- Silver = **Ag** (from Latin: argentum)
- Copper = **Cu** (from Latin: cuprum)
- Lead = **Pb** (from Latin: plumbum)

**IUPAC** (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) approves the names and symbols of elements internationally.

### Atomic Mass

The **atomic mass** of an element is the average mass of its atoms compared to 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

**One atomic mass unit (u)** = exactly 1/12th the mass of one atom of carbon-12.

| Element | Atomic Mass (u) |
|---|---|
| Hydrogen (H) | 1 |
| Carbon (C) | 12 |
| Nitrogen (N) | 14 |
| Oxygen (O) | 16 |
| Sodium (Na) | 23 |
| Magnesium (Mg) | 24 |
| Sulphur (S) | 32 |
| Chlorine (Cl) | 35.5 |
| Calcium (Ca) | 40 |

> **Key idea:** Atomic mass is a relative mass -- it tells how heavy an atom is compared to 1/12th of a carbon-12 atom. The unit "u" stands for unified mass.

### How Do Atoms Exist?

Most atoms **cannot exist independently**. They combine with other atoms to form **molecules** or **ions**. These molecules or ions then group together in large numbers to form the matter we see around us.

## What is a Molecule?

A **molecule** is the smallest particle of an element or compound that can exist independently and shows all the properties of that substance. A molecule is a group of two or more atoms chemically bonded together.

### Molecules of Elements

When atoms of the **same element** join together, they form molecules of that element.

| Type | Element | Atomicity | Formula |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monoatomic | Argon, Helium | 1 | Ar, He |
| Diatomic | Oxygen | 2 | O2 |
| Diatomic | Hydrogen | 2 | H2 |
| Diatomic | Nitrogen | 2 | N2 |
| Diatomic | Chlorine | 2 | Cl2 |
| Triatomic | Ozone | 3 | O3 |
| Tetra-atomic | Phosphorus | 4 | P4 |
| Poly-atomic | Sulphur | 8 | S8 |

**Atomicity** is the number of atoms in a molecule of an element.

### Molecules of Compounds

When atoms of **different elements** join together in fixed ratios, they form molecules of compounds.

| Compound | Elements | Ratio by Mass | Formula |
|---|---|---|---|
| Water | Hydrogen, Oxygen | 1:8 | H2O |
| Ammonia | Nitrogen, Hydrogen | 14:3 | NH3 |
| Carbon dioxide | Carbon, Oxygen | 3:8 | CO2 |

## What is an Ion?

When atoms gain or lose electrons, they form **ions** -- particles that carry an electrical charge.

- **Cation** -- a positively charged ion (formed when an atom loses electrons)
  - Example: Na+ (sodium ion), Ca2+ (calcium ion)
- **Anion** -- a negatively charged ion (formed when an atom gains electrons)
  - Example: Cl- (chloride ion), O2- (oxide ion)

A **polyatomic ion** is a group of atoms that carries a charge.

**Examples of polyatomic ions:**
- Ammonium: NH4+
- Hydroxide: OH-
- Nitrate: NO3-
- Carbonate: CO3 2-
- Sulphate: SO4 2-
- Phosphate: PO4 3-

> **Key idea:** Ionic compounds are formed by the combination of cations and anions. The total positive charge must equal the total negative charge, making the compound electrically neutral.

## Writing Chemical Formulae

The **chemical formula** of a compound shows which elements are present and how many atoms of each element are in one unit of that compound.

### Rules for Writing Chemical Formulae

1. The **valencies** (or charges) of the ions must **balance** -- the total positive charge must equal the total negative charge
2. The **metal** is written **first**, followed by the **non-metal**
3. For **polyatomic ions**, use brackets with a subscript if more than one of the same ion is needed

### Valency

**Valency** is the combining capacity of an element. It tells us how many atoms of another element one atom can combine with.

| Valency | Metals | Non-metals | Polyatomic Ions |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Na+, K+, Ag+ | H+, Cl-, Br- | NH4+, OH-, NO3- |
| 2 | Mg2+, Ca2+, Zn2+, Cu2+ | O2-, S2- | CO3 2-, SO4 2- |
| 3 | Al3+, Fe3+ | N3- | PO4 3- |

### How to Write Formulae (Criss-Cross Method)

**Step 1:** Write the symbols of the two elements/ions side by side.

**Step 2:** Write their valencies below them.

**Step 3:** Cross over the valencies -- the valency of one becomes the subscript of the other.

**Step 4:** Simplify if possible.

**Examples:**

**Sodium oxide:** Na has valency 1, O has valency 2. Cross over to get **Na2O**.

**Aluminium chloride:** Al has valency 3, Cl has valency 1. Cross over to get **AlCl3**.

**Calcium oxide:** Ca has valency 2, O has valency 2. Cross over gives Ca2O2, simplify to **CaO**.

**Magnesium chloride:** Mg has valency 2, Cl has valency 1. Cross over to get **MgCl2**.

**Aluminium oxide:** Al has valency 3, O has valency 2. Cross over to get **Al2O3**.

**Calcium hydroxide:** Ca has valency 2, OH has valency 1. Formula is **Ca(OH)2**. Note the bracket around OH.

**Sodium carbonate:** Na has valency 1, CO3 has valency 2. Formula is **Na2CO3**.

**Ammonium sulphate:** NH4 has valency 1, SO4 has valency 2. Formula is **(NH4)2SO4**.

> **Key idea:** When writing formulae with polyatomic ions, use brackets if more than one of the same ion is needed. For example, Ca(OH)2 means two OH groups attached to one Ca.

## Molecular Mass and Formula Unit Mass

### Molecular Mass

The **molecular mass** of a substance is the sum of the atomic masses of all the atoms in one molecule.

**Example 1:** Molecular mass of water (H2O)
= 2 x (atomic mass of H) + 1 x (atomic mass of O)
= 2 x 1 + 1 x 16 = **18 u**

**Example 2:** Molecular mass of nitric acid (HNO3)
= 1 + 14 + 3 x 16 = 1 + 14 + 48 = **63 u**

**Example 3:** Molecular mass of sulphur molecule (S8)
= 8 x 32 = **256 u**

### Formula Unit Mass

For **ionic compounds** (which have ions rather than molecules), we use **formula unit mass** instead of molecular mass. It is calculated the same way.

**Example:** Formula unit mass of NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = **58.5 u**

**Example:** Formula unit mass of CaCl2 = 40 + 2 x 35.5 = 40 + 71 = **111 u**

**Example:** Formula unit mass of ZnO = 65 + 16 = **81 u**

**Example:** Formula unit mass of Na2O = 2 x 23 + 16 = 46 + 16 = **62 u**

> **Key idea:** Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in one molecule. For ionic compounds, we calculate the formula unit mass.

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Atom** -- The smallest particle of an element that retains its chemical properties
- **Molecule** -- The smallest particle of a substance that can exist independently
- **Ion** -- A charged atom or group of atoms
- **Cation** -- Positively charged ion
- **Anion** -- Negatively charged ion
- **Polyatomic ion** -- A group of atoms carrying a charge
- **Valency** -- The combining capacity of an element
- **Atomic mass unit (u)** -- 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom
- **Molecular mass** -- Sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule
- **Formula unit mass** -- Sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a formula unit (for ionic compounds)
- **Atomicity** -- Number of atoms in a molecule of an element
- **Chemical formula** -- Symbolic representation of the composition of a compound
