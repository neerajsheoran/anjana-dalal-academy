## The Atomic Idea

**Kiran:** Papa, the concept of atoms being the smallest particles of matter goes back thousands of years. Both Indian and Greek philosophers thought about this independently?

**Ravi:** Yes. Around 500 BCE, the Indian philosopher Maharishi Kanad proposed that matter can be divided until you reach the smallest indivisible particle, which he called parmanu. Another Indian thinker, Pakudha Katyayama, suggested that these particles normally exist in combined forms. Around the same time in Greece, Democritus coined the word "atom" from the Greek "atomos" meaning indivisible. It took over 2000 years for John Dalton to put these ideas on a scientific footing in 1808.

**Kiran:** What were Dalton's key contributions?

**Ravi:** Dalton's atomic theory had several postulates: all matter is made of tiny atoms, atoms of the same element are identical, atoms of different elements are different, atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds, and atoms cannot be created or destroyed in chemical reactions. His theory explained two critical laws -- the law of conservation of mass and the law of constant proportions.

**Kiran:** But we now know atoms are not truly indivisible?

**Ravi:** Correct. J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897, Rutherford found the nucleus, and Chadwick discovered the neutron. Atoms have internal structure. But Dalton's core insight -- that matter is discrete, not continuous -- remains the foundation of all chemistry.

## Laws of Chemical Combination

**Kiran:** The law of conservation of mass says total mass does not change in a reaction. Is this always exactly true?

**Ravi:** For chemical reactions, yes -- the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. Lavoisier established this law through careful experiments in the 18th century. In nuclear reactions, however, a tiny amount of mass converts to energy (Einstein's E equals mc-squared), but that is beyond the scope of this chapter.

**Kiran:** And the law of constant proportions?

**Ravi:** It states that a chemical compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass, regardless of its source. Water from the Ganges, from a laboratory, or from the moon always has hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio 1:8 by mass. This law was established by Proust and is fundamental to writing chemical formulas.

**Kiran:** Board exam tip -- how are these laws tested?

**Ravi:** Typically through numerical verification. You might be given the masses of reactants and products and asked to verify conservation of mass. Or given the composition of a compound from different sources and asked to verify constant proportions. Show your calculations clearly and state the law by name.

## Writing Chemical Formulas

**Kiran:** The criss-cross method for writing formulas seems mechanical. Can you explain why it works?

**Ravi:** A compound must be electrically neutral -- total positive charge equals total negative charge. If sodium has valency 1+ and oxygen has valency 2-, you need two sodium atoms for every oxygen atom to balance the charges: Na2O. The criss-cross method automates this: the valency of one element becomes the subscript of the other. It works because the product of valency and number of atoms gives the total charge, and balancing charges means setting these products equal.

**Kiran:** What about polyatomic ions like sulphate (SO4 with charge 2-)?

**Ravi:** Treat the entire polyatomic ion as a single unit. For calcium sulphate: calcium has valency 2+ and sulphate has valency 2-. One of each balances: CaSO4. For calcium hydroxide: calcium is 2+ and hydroxide (OH) is 1-. You need two hydroxides: Ca(OH)2. The brackets indicate that the entire OH group is taken twice.

**Kiran:** Writing Al2(SO4)3 always confuses me.

**Ravi:** Break it down. Aluminium has valency 3+ and sulphate has valency 2-. Criss-cross: 2 aluminiums and 3 sulphates. So the formula is Al2(SO4)3. The formula unit mass is 2(27) plus 3(32 plus 4 times 16) equals 54 plus 3(96) equals 54 plus 288 equals 342 u. Always verify your formula by checking that total positive charge equals total negative charge.

## Molecular Mass and Mole Concept Preview

**Kiran:** Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule. For ionic compounds, we use formula unit mass instead. Why the different name?

**Ravi:** Ionic compounds like NaCl do not exist as discrete molecules -- they form continuous crystal lattices where each sodium ion is surrounded by chloride ions and vice versa. So "molecule" is technically incorrect. We call the simplest unit a "formula unit" and its mass the "formula unit mass." The calculation is identical: NaCl has formula unit mass 23 plus 35.5 equals 58.5 u.

**Kiran:** Will we study the mole concept this year?

**Ravi:** The mole concept connects the atomic scale (measured in atomic mass units) to the laboratory scale (measured in grams). One mole of any substance contains 6.022 times 10 to the power 23 particles -- this is Avogadro's number. You will study this in detail in Class 10 and 11. For now, focus on atomic masses, molecular masses, and writing correct chemical formulas.

**Kiran:** How does this chapter connect to competitive exams?

**Ravi:** Stoichiometry -- the quantitative study of chemical reactions -- is built entirely on the concepts of atomic mass, molecular mass, and balanced equations. JEE and NEET problems routinely test your ability to calculate formula masses, determine empirical formulas, and apply the laws of chemical combination. Getting these basics right in Class 9 gives you a strong foundation. Indian chemists like Prafulla Chandra Ray pioneered chemical research in India, and their work relied on precisely these fundamental concepts.
