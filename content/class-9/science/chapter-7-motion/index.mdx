## Describing Motion

Everything around us is in motion -- birds fly, cars move, blood flows, planets orbit the sun. An object is said to be in **motion** when its position changes with time.

### Reference Point (Origin)

To describe the position of an object, we need a **reference point** called the **origin**. For example, "the school is 2 km north of the railway station" -- here the railway station is the reference point.

> **Key idea:** Motion is relative. An object may appear to be moving for one observer but stationary for another. For passengers in a bus, roadside trees appear to move backwards, while a person on the road sees the bus moving.

---

## Distance and Displacement

### Distance

**Distance** is the total **path length** covered by an object during its motion. It is always positive and only has magnitude (no direction). It is a **scalar quantity**.

### Displacement

**Displacement** is the **shortest distance** measured from the initial position to the final position of an object, along with the direction. It is a **vector quantity** (has both magnitude and direction).

**Key differences:**

| Property | Distance | Displacement |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Total path length | Shortest distance from start to end |
| Direction | No direction | Has direction |
| Can be zero? | Only if object does not move | Yes, if object returns to start |
| Can be negative? | No, always positive | Yes |
| Type | Scalar | Vector |

![Diagram showing an object moving from O to A and back to B, illustrating the difference between distance and displacement](distance-vs-displacement-diagram.png)

**Example:** If an object moves from O to A (60 km) and then back to B (25 km from A towards O):
- Distance = 60 + 25 = **85 km**
- Displacement = 60 - 25 = **35 km** (from O towards A)

If the object returns to O:
- Distance = 60 + 60 = **120 km**
- Displacement = **0 km** (start and end positions are the same)

> **Key idea:** Distance is always greater than or equal to the magnitude of displacement. They are equal only when the object moves in a straight line without changing direction.

---

## Uniform and Non-uniform Motion

- **Uniform motion:** An object covers **equal distances in equal intervals** of time (e.g., 5 m every second)
- **Non-uniform motion:** An object covers **unequal distances in equal intervals** of time (e.g., a car on a crowded street)

---

## Speed

**Speed** is the distance travelled by an object per unit time.

**Speed = Distance / Time**

**v = s / t**

- **SI unit:** metre per second (m/s or m s-1)
- Other units: km/h (km h-1), cm/s (cm s-1)
- Speed is a **scalar quantity** (has only magnitude, no direction)

### Average Speed

For non-uniform motion, we use **average speed**:

**Average speed = Total distance travelled / Total time taken**

**Conversion:** 1 km/h = (1000/3600) m/s = 5/18 m/s

**Example:** A car travels 100 km in 2 hours. Average speed = 100/2 = 50 km/h. The car may have travelled faster or slower at different times.

---

## Velocity

**Velocity** is the speed of an object in a **definite direction**. It is a **vector quantity**.

**Velocity = Displacement / Time**

### Average Velocity

When velocity changes at a **uniform rate**, the average velocity can be calculated as:

**Average velocity = (Initial velocity + Final velocity) / 2**

**v(avg) = (u + v) / 2**

where u = initial velocity and v = final velocity.

> **Key idea:** Speed tells us how fast an object moves. Velocity tells us how fast AND in which direction it moves. An object moving at constant speed in a circle has changing velocity (because direction changes).

---

## Acceleration

**Acceleration** is the rate of change of velocity with time.

**Acceleration = (Final velocity - Initial velocity) / Time**

**a = (v - u) / t**

- **SI unit:** m/s squared (m s-2)
- **Positive acceleration** -- velocity increases (in the direction of motion)
- **Negative acceleration (deceleration/retardation)** -- velocity decreases (opposite to motion)
- **Zero acceleration** -- velocity is constant (uniform motion)

### Uniform and Non-uniform Acceleration

- **Uniform acceleration:** Velocity changes by **equal amounts in equal intervals** of time (e.g., a freely falling body)
- **Non-uniform acceleration:** Velocity changes by **unequal amounts in equal intervals** of time

## Graphical Representation of Motion

### Distance-Time Graphs

| Graph Shape | Type of Motion |
|---|---|
| Horizontal straight line | Object at rest (distance not changing) |
| Straight line with slope | Uniform speed (equal distances in equal times) |
| Curved line | Non-uniform speed |

- The **slope** of a distance-time graph gives the **speed** of the object
- Steeper slope = higher speed

![Distance-time graphs showing uniform speed (straight line) and non-uniform speed (curved line)](distance-time-graphs.png)

### Velocity-Time Graphs

| Graph Shape | Type of Motion |
|---|---|
| Horizontal straight line | Uniform velocity (zero acceleration) |
| Straight line with positive slope | Uniform acceleration |
| Straight line with negative slope | Uniform deceleration |
| Curved line | Non-uniform acceleration |

- The **slope** of a velocity-time graph gives the **acceleration**
- The **area under** a velocity-time graph gives the **distance travelled**

![Velocity-time graphs showing uniform velocity, uniform acceleration, and uniform deceleration with shaded area representing distance](velocity-time-graphs.png)

> **Key idea:** Graphs are powerful tools for understanding motion. The slope of a distance-time graph gives speed, and the slope of a velocity-time graph gives acceleration.

## Equations of Motion

For an object moving with **uniform acceleration**, three equations relate initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), time (t), and displacement (s):

### First Equation of Motion

**v = u + at**

This relates velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

### Second Equation of Motion

**s = ut + (1/2)at squared**

This relates displacement, initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

### Third Equation of Motion

**v squared = u squared + 2as**

This relates velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and displacement.

**Where:**
- u = initial velocity (m/s)
- v = final velocity (m/s)
- a = acceleration (m/s squared)
- t = time (s)
- s = displacement (m)

> **Key idea:** These three equations can solve any problem involving uniform acceleration. Choose the equation based on what quantities are given and what you need to find.

---

## Deriving the Equations of Motion

### First Equation: v = u + at

From the definition of acceleration: a = (v - u) / t

Rearranging: **v = u + at**

### Second Equation: s = ut + (1/2)at squared

The distance travelled equals the area under a velocity-time graph.

For uniformly accelerated motion from velocity u to velocity v in time t:

s = Area of trapezium = (1/2)(u + v) x t

Substituting v = u + at:

s = (1/2)(u + u + at) x t = (1/2)(2u + at) x t

**s = ut + (1/2)at squared**

### Third Equation: v squared = u squared + 2as

From the first equation: t = (v - u) / a

Substituting in s = (u + v) x t / 2:

s = (u + v)(v - u) / (2a) = (v squared - u squared) / (2a)

**v squared = u squared + 2as**

## Uniform Circular Motion

When an object moves in a **circular path** with **constant speed**, it is in **uniform circular motion**.

Even though the speed is constant, the **velocity keeps changing** because the direction of motion changes at every point on the circle. Therefore, uniform circular motion is an **accelerated motion**.

![Object moving in a circular path with velocity arrows showing changing direction at different points](uniform-circular-motion-diagram.png)

### Examples of Uniform Circular Motion

- Motion of the **moon** around the earth
- Motion of the **earth** around the sun
- An **athlete** running on a circular track at constant speed
- The tip of the **second hand** of a clock
- A **stone** tied to a string and whirled in a circle

### Speed in Circular Motion

If an object completes one full circle of radius r in time t:

**Speed = Circumference / Time = 2 pi r / t**

**Example:** If an athlete completes one round of a circular track of radius 200 m in 40 s:

Speed = 2 x 3.14 x 200 / 40 = 1256 / 40 = **31.4 m/s**

> **Key idea:** In uniform circular motion, speed is constant but velocity is not (because direction keeps changing). The acceleration is directed towards the centre of the circle.

---

## Solved Examples

**Example 1:** A car travels a distance of 100 km in 2 h. Average speed = 100/2 = **50 km/h** = 50 x 5/18 = **13.9 m/s**

**Example 2:** A train starts from rest and attains a velocity of 20 m/s in 2 s. Acceleration = (20 - 0)/2 = **10 m/s squared**. Distance = 0 x 2 + (1/2)(10)(4) = **20 m**

**Example 3:** A car moving at 20 m/s comes to rest in 10 s. Acceleration = (0 - 20)/10 = **-2 m/s squared**. Distance = 20 x 10 + (1/2)(-2)(100) = 200 - 100 = **100 m**

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Distance** -- Total path length (scalar)
- **Displacement** -- Shortest distance from start to end with direction (vector)
- **Speed** -- Distance per unit time (scalar)
- **Velocity** -- Displacement per unit time (vector)
- **Acceleration** -- Rate of change of velocity
- **Uniform motion** -- Equal distances in equal time intervals
- **Non-uniform motion** -- Unequal distances in equal time intervals
- **Equations of motion** -- v = u + at, s = ut + (1/2)at squared, v squared = u squared + 2as
- **Uniform circular motion** -- Motion in a circle at constant speed
