## Discovery of Sub-Atomic Particles

**Kiran:** Papa, Dalton said atoms are indivisible. But then Thomson found electrons, Rutherford found the nucleus, and Chadwick found neutrons. How did scientists discover particles they cannot see?

**Ravi:** Through ingenious experiments and indirect evidence. Thomson used cathode ray tubes -- sealed glass tubes with electrodes -- and showed that a stream of negatively charged particles (electrons) was emitted regardless of which gas was inside. This meant electrons are present in all atoms. Goldstein discovered positively charged canal rays, leading to the discovery of protons. These experiments showed that atoms have internal structure, contradicting Dalton's "indivisible" hypothesis.

**Kiran:** Thomson won the Nobel Prize for discovering the electron. What was his model of the atom?

**Ravi:** Thomson proposed the "plum pudding" model -- he imagined the atom as a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded in it, like seeds in a watermelon. The positive and negative charges balance, making the atom neutral. It was a reasonable first attempt, but it could not explain what Rutherford discovered next.

## Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

**Kiran:** Rutherford's experiment is one of the most famous in physics. What exactly happened?

![Rutherford's gold foil experiment: alpha particles fired at thin gold foil, most passing straight through, a few deflected at angles, and very few bouncing straight back, with the tiny dense nucleus shown in the centre of the atom](rutherford-gold-foil-experiment-results.png)

**Ravi:** Rutherford's team fired alpha particles (helium nuclei, positively charged, mass 4 u) at a very thin gold foil -- only about 1000 atoms thick. Most alpha particles passed straight through, suggesting the atom is mostly empty space. A few were deflected at small angles, indicating they passed near something positive. And about 1 in 12,000 bounced straight back -- they must have hit something very small, very dense, and very positive.

**Kiran:** And that tiny dense centre is the nucleus?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Rutherford concluded that all the positive charge and nearly all the mass of an atom are concentrated in a tiny nucleus at the centre. Electrons orbit around this nucleus in the vast empty space. The nucleus is about 10 to the power 5 times smaller than the atom -- if the atom were the size of a cricket stadium, the nucleus would be smaller than a marble at the centre!

**Kiran:** But Rutherford's model had a problem?

**Ravi:** Yes. According to classical physics, a charged particle moving in a circle should continuously emit electromagnetic radiation and lose energy. The electron should spiral into the nucleus within a fraction of a second, collapsing the atom. But atoms are clearly stable -- we exist! This contradiction was resolved by Niels Bohr.

## Bohr's Model and Electron Shells

**Kiran:** How did Bohr fix Rutherford's problem?

**Ravi:** Bohr made a bold postulate: electrons can only occupy certain specific orbits (called shells or energy levels), and while in these orbits, they do not radiate energy. An electron can jump from one orbit to another by absorbing or emitting a specific amount of energy. This explained atomic stability and also the line spectra of elements.

**Kiran:** The shells are labelled K, L, M, N. How many electrons can each hold?

**Ravi:** The maximum number of electrons in the n-th shell is 2n-squared. So K (n equals 1) holds up to 2 electrons, L (n equals 2) holds up to 8, M (n equals 3) holds up to 18. But the outermost shell can hold a maximum of 8 electrons regardless. Fill inner shells first before moving to outer ones.

**Kiran:** Can you walk through the electronic configuration of sodium?

**Ravi:** Sodium has atomic number 11, so it has 11 electrons. K shell gets 2 (now full). L shell gets 8 (now full). Remaining 1 electron goes to M shell. So sodium's configuration is 2, 8, 1. That single electron in the outermost shell makes sodium highly reactive -- it readily loses that electron to achieve a stable configuration like neon (2, 8).

## Valency, Atomic Number, and Mass Number

**Kiran:** Valency determines how atoms combine. How do I find it from the electronic configuration?

**Ravi:** Look at the outermost shell electrons. If there are 1, 2, or 3 electrons, the valency equals that number (the atom tends to lose them). If there are 5, 6, or 7, the valency is 8 minus the number (the atom tends to gain electrons to complete the octet). If there are 4, the atom tends to share. If the outermost shell is full (8 electrons, or 2 for helium), valency is zero -- these are the noble gases.

**Kiran:** Atomic number Z is protons, mass number A is protons plus neutrons. And isotopes have the same Z but different A?

**Ravi:** Perfectly stated. Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons. Hydrogen has three isotopes: protium (0 neutrons), deuterium (1 neutron), and tritium (2 neutrons). All have 1 proton, so they are all hydrogen. Their chemical properties are identical because they have the same number of electrons, but their physical properties differ due to different masses.

**Kiran:** And isobars?

**Ravi:** Isobars are atoms of different elements with the same mass number but different atomic numbers. Calcium-40 and Argon-40 are isobars -- same A but different Z. They are completely different elements with different chemical properties. Board exams often test whether students can distinguish between isotopes and isobars.

## Applications and Exam Tips

**Kiran:** The textbook mentions that isotopes have practical applications. Which ones are important for exams?

**Ravi:** Three key applications. Cobalt-60 is used in cancer treatment (radiation therapy). Iodine-131 is used to treat thyroid disorders (goitre). Uranium-235 is used as fuel in nuclear reactors. India's nuclear programme, developed by Homi Bhabha and continued by scientists like A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, relies on understanding isotopes of uranium and thorium.

**Kiran:** How should I approach board exam questions on this chapter?

**Ravi:** Know the three models of the atom and their limitations -- Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr. Be able to describe Rutherford's experiment and its three key observations. Write electronic configurations accurately using the 2n-squared rule. Calculate valency from configuration. Distinguish isotopes from isobars clearly. For numerical questions, remember: number of neutrons equals mass number minus atomic number. These are the core competencies the examiners test.
