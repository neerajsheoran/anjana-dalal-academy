## Introduction

In the previous chapter, we learnt that atoms are the building blocks of matter. But are atoms truly indivisible, as Dalton proposed? Scientists discovered that atoms themselves contain even smaller particles called **sub-atomic particles**. This chapter explores the discovery of these particles and the different models proposed to explain the structure of the atom.

---

## Charged Particles in Matter

When you comb dry hair, the comb attracts small pieces of paper. When you rub a glass rod with silk, it attracts a balloon. These observations show that objects become **electrically charged** on rubbing. This charge comes from the particles inside the atom.

### Discovery of the Electron

**J.J. Thomson** (1856-1940), a British physicist, discovered the **electron** -- a negatively charged sub-atomic particle. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1906.

### Discovery of the Proton

**E. Goldstein** in 1886 discovered positively charged rays called **canal rays** in a gas discharge tube. These led to the discovery of the **proton** -- a positively charged sub-atomic particle.

### Properties of Electrons and Protons

| Property | Electron (e-) | Proton (p+) |
|---|---|---|
| **Charge** | -1 (negative) | +1 (positive) |
| **Mass** | Negligible (1/2000 of proton) | 1 u (one atomic mass unit) |
| **Location** | Outside the nucleus | Inside the nucleus |

> **Key idea:** An atom that contains equal numbers of electrons and protons is electrically neutral -- it has no net charge.

## Models of the Atom

### Thomson's Model of the Atom (Plum Pudding Model)

J.J. Thomson proposed the first model of atomic structure:

- An atom consists of a **sphere of positive charge** with **electrons embedded** in it
- The positive charge is spread uniformly throughout the atom (like the red part of a watermelon)
- The electrons are studded in the positive charge (like seeds in a watermelon)

![Thomson's plum pudding model showing electrons embedded in a sphere of positive charge](thomsons-plum-pudding-model.png)
- The negative and positive charges are **equal**, making the atom **electrically neutral**

**Limitation:** Thomson's model could not explain the results of later experiments (like Rutherford's alpha-particle scattering experiment).

---

### Rutherford's Model of the Atom (Nuclear Model)

**Ernest Rutherford** (1871-1937), known as the "Father of Nuclear Physics", designed a famous experiment to study atomic structure.

#### The Alpha-Particle Scattering Experiment

Rutherford bombarded a very thin **gold foil** (about 1000 atoms thick) with fast-moving **alpha particles** (doubly-charged helium ions, mass = 4 u).

![Rutherford's alpha-particle scattering experiment showing alpha particles hitting gold foil with most passing through, some deflecting, and very few bouncing back](rutherford-alpha-scattering-experiment.png)

**Observations:**
1. **Most** alpha particles passed **straight through** the gold foil without any deflection
2. **Some** alpha particles were deflected by **small angles**
3. Very few (about **1 in 12,000**) alpha particles **bounced back** (deflected by 180 degrees)

**Conclusions:**
1. Most of the space inside the atom is **empty** (since most particles passed straight through)
2. The positive charge occupies **very little space** (since very few particles were deflected)
3. All the positive charge and nearly all the mass of the atom are concentrated in a very small, dense centre called the **nucleus**

#### Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Based on his experiment, Rutherford proposed:

- There is a **positively charged centre** called the **nucleus** containing nearly all the mass
- **Electrons revolve** around the nucleus in **circular paths** (called orbits)
- The **size of the nucleus** is very small compared to the size of the atom (about 10 to the power 5 times smaller)

**Drawback:** According to physics, a charged particle moving in a circular orbit should continuously radiate energy. If electrons kept losing energy, they would slow down and spiral into the nucleus. This would make the atom **unstable**, which contradicts reality -- atoms are very stable.

> **Key idea:** Rutherford's experiment proved that atoms are mostly empty space with a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the centre.

---

### Bohr's Model of the Atom

**Neils Bohr** (1885-1962) improved upon Rutherford's model to explain the stability of atoms. He won the Nobel Prize in 1922.

**Postulates of Bohr's Model:**

1. Electrons can only revolve in certain **special orbits** called **discrete orbits** or **shells**
2. While revolving in these discrete orbits, electrons **do not radiate energy**
3. These orbits are also called **energy levels**

**The energy levels (shells) are designated as:**
- **K shell** (n = 1) -- closest to the nucleus
- **L shell** (n = 2)
- **M shell** (n = 3)
- **N shell** (n = 4) -- and so on

![Bohr's atomic model showing concentric electron shells K, L, M, N around the nucleus](bohr-atomic-model-shells.png)

> **Key idea:** Bohr's model explained why atoms are stable -- electrons in fixed orbits do not lose energy. Energy is only absorbed or released when an electron jumps from one orbit to another.

## Discovery of Neutrons

In 1932, **J. Chadwick** discovered a third sub-atomic particle in the nucleus -- the **neutron**.

**Properties of neutron:**
- **No charge** (neutral)
- Mass is nearly **equal to a proton** (1 u)
- Present in the nucleus of all atoms **except hydrogen** (protium)

### The Three Sub-Atomic Particles

| Particle | Symbol | Charge | Mass | Location |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Electron | e- | -1 | Negligible (1/1840 u) | Orbits around nucleus |
| Proton | p+ | +1 | 1 u | Inside nucleus |
| Neutron | n | 0 (neutral) | 1 u | Inside nucleus |

> **Key idea:** The mass of an atom comes mainly from its protons and neutrons (together called **nucleons**), which are present in the nucleus.

---

## Distribution of Electrons in Shells

The arrangement of electrons in the shells of an atom was proposed by **Bohr and Bury**. The following rules are used:

### Rules for Electron Distribution

**Rule 1:** The maximum number of electrons in a shell is given by **2n squared**, where n is the shell number.

| Shell | n | Maximum electrons (2n squared) |
|---|---|---|
| K | 1 | 2 x 1 x 1 = **2** |
| L | 2 | 2 x 2 x 2 = **8** |
| M | 3 | 2 x 3 x 3 = **18** |
| N | 4 | 2 x 4 x 4 = **32** |

**Rule 2:** The **outermost shell** can hold a maximum of **8 electrons** only.

**Rule 3:** Electrons fill shells **step by step** -- inner shells are filled first before outer shells.

### Electronic Configuration of Some Elements

| Element | Atomic Number | K | L | M | N |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hydrogen | 1 | 1 | - | - | - |
| Helium | 2 | 2 | - | - | - |
| Carbon | 6 | 2 | 4 | - | - |
| Nitrogen | 7 | 2 | 5 | - | - |
| Oxygen | 8 | 2 | 6 | - | - |
| Sodium | 11 | 2 | 8 | 1 | - |
| Magnesium | 12 | 2 | 8 | 2 | - |
| Aluminium | 13 | 2 | 8 | 3 | - |
| Chlorine | 17 | 2 | 8 | 7 | - |
| Argon | 18 | 2 | 8 | 8 | - |

> **Key idea:** If both the K and L shells of an atom are full, the total number of electrons is 2 + 8 = 10 (which is neon).

## Valency

The electrons in the **outermost shell** of an atom are called **valence electrons**. The **valency** of an element is the number of electrons it needs to gain, lose, or share to achieve a stable configuration (8 electrons in the outermost shell, called an **octet**).

### How to Find Valency

- If the outermost shell has **1, 2, or 3** electrons, the valency equals the number of electrons (the atom tends to **lose** them)
- If the outermost shell has **5, 6, or 7** electrons, the valency = 8 minus the number of outermost electrons (the atom tends to **gain** electrons)
- If the outermost shell has **4** electrons, the valency is 4 (the atom tends to **share**)
- If the outermost shell has **8** electrons (or 2 for helium), the valency is **0** (the element is unreactive/inert)

**Examples:**
- **Sodium** (2, 8, 1): Valency = 1 (loses 1 electron)
- **Magnesium** (2, 8, 2): Valency = 2 (loses 2 electrons)
- **Aluminium** (2, 8, 3): Valency = 3 (loses 3 electrons)
- **Oxygen** (2, 6): Valency = 8 - 6 = 2 (gains 2 electrons)
- **Chlorine** (2, 8, 7): Valency = 8 - 7 = 1 (gains 1 electron)
- **Argon** (2, 8, 8): Valency = 0 (stable, does not react)

> **Key idea:** Elements with a fully filled outermost shell (like helium, neon, argon) have zero valency and are called noble or inert gases. They are very stable and unreactive.

## Atomic Number and Mass Number

### Atomic Number (Z)

The **atomic number** is the total number of **protons** in the nucleus of an atom. It is denoted by **Z**.

- All atoms of an element have the **same atomic number**
- The atomic number defines the element
- Example: Hydrogen has Z = 1, Carbon has Z = 6, Oxygen has Z = 8

> **Key idea:** In a neutral atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons. So, atomic number = number of protons = number of electrons (in a neutral atom).

### Mass Number (A)

The **mass number** is the sum of the total number of **protons and neutrons** in the nucleus. It is denoted by **A**.

**Mass number (A) = Number of protons (Z) + Number of neutrons**

Therefore: **Number of neutrons = A - Z**

**Notation:** An atom is written as: A (superscript) Z (subscript) X, where X is the element symbol.

For example, nitrogen is written with mass number 14 and atomic number 7.

## Isotopes

**Isotopes** are atoms of the **same element** that have the **same atomic number** but **different mass numbers** (i.e., they have different numbers of neutrons).

### Isotopes of Hydrogen

| Isotope | Symbol | Protons | Neutrons | Mass Number |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Protium | H | 1 | 0 | 1 |
| Deuterium | D | 1 | 1 | 2 |
| Tritium | T | 1 | 2 | 3 |

All three have the same atomic number (1) but different mass numbers.

![Three isotopes of hydrogen: protium (no neutron), deuterium (one neutron), and tritium (two neutrons)](hydrogen-isotopes-diagram.png)

### Other Examples of Isotopes

- Carbon: C-12 (6 protons, 6 neutrons) and C-14 (6 protons, 8 neutrons)
- Chlorine: Cl-35 (17 protons, 18 neutrons) and Cl-37 (17 protons, 20 neutrons)

### Properties of Isotopes

- **Chemical properties** are the **same** (since they have the same number of electrons)
- **Physical properties** are **different** (since they have different masses)

### Average Atomic Mass

Since elements can have multiple isotopes, the atomic mass is calculated as a **weighted average**.

**Example:** Chlorine has two isotopes -- Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%).

Average atomic mass = (35 x 75/100) + (37 x 25/100) = 26.25 + 9.25 = **35.5 u**

### Applications of Isotopes

- An isotope of **uranium** is used as fuel in nuclear reactors
- An isotope of **cobalt** (Co-60) is used in the treatment of cancer
- An isotope of **iodine** (I-131) is used in the treatment of goitre (thyroid disorder)

---

## Isobars

**Isobars** are atoms of **different elements** that have **different atomic numbers** but the **same mass number**.

**Example:** Calcium (atomic number 20, mass number 40) and Argon (atomic number 18, mass number 40) are isobars. Both have a mass number of 40 but are completely different elements.

> **Key idea:** Isotopes have the same atomic number but different mass numbers. Isobars have different atomic numbers but the same mass number.

---

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Electron** -- Negatively charged sub-atomic particle, discovered by J.J. Thomson
- **Proton** -- Positively charged sub-atomic particle, discovered through canal rays
- **Neutron** -- Neutral sub-atomic particle, discovered by J. Chadwick
- **Nucleus** -- Small, dense, positively charged centre of an atom
- **Shell/Orbit** -- Fixed path around the nucleus where electrons revolve
- **Valence electrons** -- Electrons in the outermost shell of an atom
- **Valency** -- Combining capacity of an atom
- **Atomic number (Z)** -- Number of protons in the nucleus
- **Mass number (A)** -- Total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
- **Isotopes** -- Same element, same Z, different A
- **Isobars** -- Different elements, different Z, same A
- **Nucleons** -- Protons and neutrons (particles in the nucleus)
