## What is a Pure Substance?

When we go to the market and buy milk, ghee, or honey labelled "pure", it usually means the product has no adulteration. But in science, the word **pure** has a very specific meaning.

**A pure substance is one that is made up of only one type of particle.** All its particles have the same chemical nature.

For example:
- **Gold** is a pure substance -- it contains only gold atoms
- **Water** (distilled) is a pure substance -- it contains only water molecules
- **Milk** is NOT pure in the scientific sense -- it is a mixture of water, fat, proteins, and other substances

> **Key idea:** For a scientist, a pure substance consists of a single type of particle. Most things around us are actually mixtures of two or more pure substances.

---

## What is a Mixture?

A **mixture** is formed when two or more pure substances are mixed together. The substances in a mixture are not chemically combined and can be separated by physical methods.

**Examples:** sea water, soil, air, lemonade, milk

### Types of Mixtures

#### 1. Homogeneous Mixtures

A **homogeneous mixture** has a uniform composition throughout. You cannot see the individual components separately.

**Examples:**
- Salt dissolved in water
- Sugar dissolved in water
- Air (mixture of gases)
- Alloys like brass (copper + zinc)

In a homogeneous mixture, the components are evenly distributed. However, the composition can vary -- you can make a more concentrated or more dilute salt solution.

#### 2. Heterogeneous Mixtures

A **heterogeneous mixture** has a non-uniform composition. You can see the different components separately.

**Examples:**
- Oil and water
- Sand and iron filings
- Salt and sulphur powder
- Muddy water

> **Key idea:** In a homogeneous mixture, the components are uniformly distributed and cannot be seen separately. In a heterogeneous mixture, the components are not uniformly distributed and can often be seen as distinct parts.

## Solutions

A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. It is the most common type of mixture we encounter.

### Solute and Solvent

Every solution has two parts:
- **Solvent** -- the component that dissolves the other (usually present in larger amount)
- **Solute** -- the component that gets dissolved (usually present in smaller amount)

**Examples:**

| Solution | Solute | Solvent |
|---|---|---|
| Sugar in water | Sugar (solid) | Water (liquid) |
| Tincture of iodine | Iodine (solid) | Alcohol (liquid) |
| Soda water | Carbon dioxide (gas) | Water (liquid) |
| Air | Oxygen and other gases | Nitrogen (gas) |

### Properties of a Solution

- A solution is a **homogeneous mixture**
- The particles are **smaller than 1 nm** (nanometre) in diameter -- they cannot be seen even with a microscope
- A solution **does not scatter light** -- the path of a beam of light is not visible
- The solute **cannot be separated by filtration**
- A solution is **stable** -- particles do not settle down over time

### Alloys

**Alloys** are solid solutions. They are mixtures of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal. Alloys cannot be separated into their components by physical methods, but they are still considered mixtures because:
- They show properties of their constituents
- They can have variable composition

**Examples:**
- **Brass** = approximately 30% zinc + 70% copper
- **Steel** = iron + carbon
- **Bronze** = copper + tin

---

## Concentration of a Solution

Not all solutions have the same amount of solute. The **concentration** of a solution tells us how much solute is present in a given amount of solution.

### Types Based on Concentration

- **Dilute solution** -- contains a small amount of solute relative to solvent
- **Concentrated solution** -- contains a large amount of solute relative to solvent
- **Saturated solution** -- a solution in which no more solute can be dissolved at a given temperature
- **Unsaturated solution** -- a solution in which more solute can still be dissolved

> **Key idea:** The amount of solute that can be dissolved in a solvent at a particular temperature to form a saturated solution is called its **solubility**. Different substances have different solubilities. Solubility generally increases with temperature.

### How to Express Concentration

There are three main ways:

**1. Mass by mass percentage**

Concentration = (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) x 100

**2. Mass by volume percentage**

Concentration = (Mass of solute / Volume of solution) x 100

**3. Volume by volume percentage**

Concentration = (Volume of solute / Volume of solution) x 100

**Example:** A solution contains 40 g of salt in 320 g of water.

Mass of solution = 40 + 320 = 360 g

Mass percentage = (40 / 360) x 100 = **11.1%**

## Suspensions

A **suspension** is a heterogeneous mixture in which tiny solid particles are spread throughout a liquid without dissolving.

**Examples:** muddy water, chalk powder in water, flour in water

### Properties of a Suspension

- It is a **heterogeneous mixture**
- Particles are **visible to the naked eye**
- Particles **scatter a beam of light** passing through it (the path of light is visible)
- Particles are **unstable** -- they settle down when left undisturbed
- Particles can be **separated by filtration**

> **Key idea:** A suspension is unstable. If you leave it undisturbed, the particles settle to the bottom.

---

## Colloidal Solutions (Colloids)

A **colloid** (or colloidal solution) is a mixture where the particle size is between that of a solution and a suspension. The particles are too small to be seen with naked eyes but large enough to scatter light.

**Examples:** milk, ink, blood, fog, smoke, butter, jelly

### Properties of a Colloid

- It is a **heterogeneous mixture** (though it appears homogeneous)
- Particles **cannot be seen** with naked eyes
- Particles **scatter a beam of light** -- this is called the **Tyndall effect**
- Particles are **stable** -- they do not settle down
- Particles **cannot be separated by filtration** but can be separated by **centrifugation**

### The Tyndall Effect

When a beam of light passes through a colloidal solution, the light gets scattered by the colloidal particles, making the path of light visible. This is called the **Tyndall effect**.

![Tyndall effect: beam of light visible in a colloidal solution but not in a true solution](tyndall-effect-comparison.png)

**Everyday examples of Tyndall effect:**
- When sunlight enters a dark room through a small hole, the dust particles in air scatter the light, making the beam visible
- When sunlight passes through the canopy of a dense forest, tiny water droplets (mist) scatter the light

> **Key idea:** The Tyndall effect is seen in colloids but NOT in true solutions. This is one way to distinguish between them.

### Components of a Colloid

- **Dispersed phase** -- the solute-like component (the particles that are dispersed)
- **Dispersion medium** -- the solvent-like component (the medium in which particles are dispersed)

### Types of Colloids

| Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium | Type | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Liquid | Gas | Aerosol | Fog, clouds, mist |
| Solid | Gas | Aerosol | Smoke |
| Gas | Liquid | Foam | Shaving cream |
| Liquid | Liquid | Emulsion | Milk, face cream |
| Solid | Liquid | Sol | Milk of magnesia, mud |
| Gas | Solid | Foam | Sponge, pumice |
| Liquid | Solid | Gel | Jelly, cheese, butter |
| Solid | Solid | Solid sol | Coloured gemstones, milky glass |

## Comparing Solutions, Suspensions, and Colloids

| Property | Solution | Suspension | Colloid |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Homogeneity** | Homogeneous | Heterogeneous | Appears homogeneous but is heterogeneous |
| **Particle size** | Less than 1 nm | More than 100 nm | Between 1 nm and 100 nm |
| **Visibility** | Not visible | Visible to naked eye | Not visible to naked eye |
| **Tyndall effect** | Not shown | Shows | Shows |
| **Stability** | Stable | Unstable (settles) | Stable |
| **Filtration** | Cannot be filtered | Can be filtered | Cannot be filtered |
| **Example** | Salt water | Muddy water | Milk |

## Separation of Mixtures

Different types of mixtures need different separation techniques. The method chosen depends on the properties of the components.

### Common Separation Techniques

**1. Evaporation** -- Used to separate a solid (solute) dissolved in a liquid (solvent). The liquid evaporates, leaving the solid behind.
- Example: Obtaining salt from sea water

**2. Sublimation** -- Used to separate a sublimable substance (one that turns directly from solid to gas) from a non-sublimable substance.
- Example: Separating ammonium chloride from a mixture of ammonium chloride and salt

**3. Filtration** -- Used to separate an insoluble solid from a liquid.
- Example: Separating tea leaves from tea

**4. Centrifugation** -- Used to separate fine suspended particles from a liquid. The mixture is spun rapidly, and denser particles settle to the bottom.
- Example: Separating cream from milk, separating blood components

**5. Using a Separating Funnel** -- Used to separate two immiscible liquids (liquids that do not mix).
- Example: Separating oil from water

![Separating funnel showing separation of oil and water into two distinct layers](separating-funnel-oil-water.png)

**6. Chromatography** -- Used to separate substances that dissolve in the same solvent but at different rates.
- Example: Separating different pigments from a flower extract, separating dyes in ink

**7. Distillation** -- Used to separate a mixture of two miscible liquids with different boiling points.
- Example: Separating acetone (boiling point 56 degrees C) from water (boiling point 100 degrees C)

![Distillation apparatus setup showing flask, condenser, thermometer, and collection flask](distillation-apparatus-setup.png)

**8. Fractional Distillation** -- Used when the boiling points of two miscible liquids are close (difference less than 25 degrees C).
- Example: Separating different gases from liquid air, separating different fractions of petroleum

**9. Using a Magnet** -- Used to separate magnetic substances from non-magnetic ones.
- Example: Separating iron filings (or iron pins) from sand

**10. Winnowing** -- Used to separate lighter components from heavier ones using wind.
- Example: Separating wheat grains from husk

## Physical and Chemical Changes

### Physical Change

A change in which **no new substance is formed** and the chemical composition remains the same. Only physical properties (shape, size, state) change.

**Examples:**
- Melting of butter
- Boiling of water to form steam
- Dissolving salt in water
- Freezing of water
- Making a fruit salad

### Chemical Change

A change in which **a new substance is formed** with different chemical properties. This is also called a **chemical reaction**.

**Examples:**
- Burning of paper or wood
- Rusting of iron
- Cooking of food
- Growth of a plant
- Digestion of food

> **Key idea:** In physical changes, no new substance is formed. In chemical changes, new substances with different properties are formed.

## Types of Pure Substances

![Classification of matter: pure substances (elements and compounds) and mixtures (homogeneous and heterogeneous)](classification-of-matter-flowchart.png)

Pure substances are classified into **elements** and **compounds**.

### Elements

An **element** is a basic form of matter that **cannot be broken down into simpler substances** by chemical reactions.

There are more than 100 known elements. 92 are naturally occurring; the rest are man-made.

**Classification of elements:**

#### Metals
- Have a **lustre** (shine)
- Are **silvery-grey** or **golden-yellow** in colour
- **Conduct heat and electricity** well
- Are **ductile** (can be drawn into wires)
- Are **malleable** (can be hammered into thin sheets)
- Are **sonorous** (produce a ringing sound when struck)
- Examples: gold, silver, copper, iron, aluminium

**Note:** Mercury is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature.

#### Non-metals
- Display a **variety of colours**
- Are **poor conductors** of heat and electricity
- Are **not lustrous, sonorous, or malleable**
- Examples: hydrogen, oxygen, carbon, nitrogen, sulphur, bromine

**Note:** Bromine is the only non-metal that is liquid at room temperature.

#### Metalloids
- Have properties **intermediate** between metals and non-metals
- Examples: boron, silicon, germanium

> **Key idea:** Most elements are solids. Eleven elements are gases at room temperature. Only two elements are liquids at room temperature -- mercury (metal) and bromine (non-metal).

### Compounds

A **compound** is a substance made of two or more elements that are **chemically combined in a fixed proportion**.

**Examples:**
- **Water (H2O)** -- hydrogen and oxygen in a fixed ratio of 1:8 by mass
- **Carbon dioxide (CO2)** -- carbon and oxygen combined chemically
- **Common salt (NaCl)** -- sodium and chlorine

### Properties of Compounds

- The properties of a compound are **different** from those of its constituent elements
- The composition is **fixed** (always the same ratio of elements)
- Components can only be separated by **chemical or electrochemical reactions**, not by physical methods

> **Key idea:** When iron and sulphur are heated together, they form the compound iron sulphide (FeS), which has completely different properties from both iron and sulphur. Iron is magnetic and sulphur is yellow, but iron sulphide is neither magnetic nor yellow.

---

## Mixtures vs Compounds

| Property | Mixture | Compound |
|---|---|---|
| **Formation** | Components just mix; no new substance formed | Elements react to form a new substance |
| **Composition** | Variable | Fixed |
| **Properties** | Same as constituent substances | Different from constituent elements |
| **Separation** | By physical methods | Only by chemical or electrochemical methods |

---

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Pure substance** -- Made of a single type of particle (element or compound)
- **Mixture** -- Combination of two or more pure substances
- **Solution** -- Homogeneous mixture with particle size less than 1 nm
- **Suspension** -- Heterogeneous mixture with visible particles that settle down
- **Colloid** -- Heterogeneous mixture with particle size between 1 nm and 100 nm
- **Tyndall effect** -- Scattering of light by colloidal particles
- **Solubility** -- Amount of solute needed to form a saturated solution at a given temperature
- **Element** -- Cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical reactions
- **Compound** -- Two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed proportion
- **Alloy** -- Homogeneous mixture of metals (or metal and non-metal)
