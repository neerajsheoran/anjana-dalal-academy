## Pure Substances vs Mixtures

**Kiran:** Papa, when we say "pure milk" at the dairy, it means unadulterated. But our chemistry teacher says milk is not pure. Who is correct?

**Ravi:** Both are correct in their own context. In everyday language, "pure" means free from contamination. In chemistry, "pure" means made of only one type of particle. Milk contains water, fat, proteins, sugar, and minerals -- it is a mixture of many substances. Chemically pure substances include distilled water (only H2O molecules), gold (only gold atoms), and common salt (only NaCl).

**Kiran:** So elements and compounds are pure, and everything else is a mixture?

**Ravi:** Exactly. An element like iron contains only iron atoms. A compound like water contains only water molecules (hydrogen and oxygen combined in a fixed ratio). A mixture combines two or more pure substances without chemical bonding -- the components retain their individual properties and can be separated by physical methods.

**Kiran:** The textbook distinguishes homogeneous and heterogeneous mixtures. How do I tell them apart?

**Ravi:** In a homogeneous mixture, the composition is uniform throughout -- you cannot see separate components. Salt dissolved in water looks the same in every drop. In a heterogeneous mixture, you can see distinct parts -- like oil floating on water or sand mixed with iron filings. The key test: take samples from different parts. If they are identical, it is homogeneous; if different, it is heterogeneous.

## Solutions, Suspensions, and Colloids

**Kiran:** These three types of mixtures seem important for board exams. What are the key differences?

**Ravi:** Think of particle size as the distinguishing factor. In a true solution, particles are smaller than 1 nanometre -- they are individual molecules or ions. They cannot scatter light, cannot be filtered, and the solution is perfectly stable. In a suspension, particles are larger than 100 nm -- visible to the eye, they scatter light, can be filtered, and settle down over time. A colloid sits in between -- particles are 1 to 100 nm, invisible to the naked eye but large enough to scatter light.

**Kiran:** The Tyndall effect distinguishes colloids from solutions, right?

**Ravi:** Yes. When a beam of light passes through a colloid, the particles scatter the light, making the beam visible -- this is the Tyndall effect. You see it when sunlight enters a dark room and the dust particles (a colloid of solid in gas) make the beam visible. Or when car headlights create visible beams in fog (a colloid of liquid droplets in air). True solutions do not show the Tyndall effect.

**Kiran:** Milk is a colloid?

**Ravi:** Milk is an emulsion -- a colloid of liquid fat droplets dispersed in water. Blood is a colloid too. So is ink, butter, and fog. Colloids are everywhere in daily life. The Tyndall effect was studied by the Irish physicist John Tyndall, but Indian scientist C.V. Raman's Nobel Prize-winning work on light scattering is deeply connected to how light interacts with particles of different sizes.

## Separation Techniques

**Kiran:** There are so many separation methods -- evaporation, filtration, distillation, chromatography. How do I choose the right one?

**Ravi:** The method depends on the properties of the components you want to separate. If one component is dissolved in a liquid and you want the solid, use evaporation (like obtaining salt from sea water). If one component is insoluble, use filtration (like removing tea leaves). If both are liquids with different boiling points, use distillation. If both dissolve but at different rates, use chromatography.

**Kiran:** What about separating immiscible liquids like oil and water?

**Ravi:** Use a separating funnel. The denser liquid (water) settles at the bottom. Open the stopcock to drain the water, and the oil remains. This principle is used in petroleum refineries to separate different fractions and in waste water treatment plants across India.

**Kiran:** Chromatography seems fascinating. How does it work?

**Ravi:** Chromatography separates substances based on how differently they travel through a medium. In paper chromatography, you place a drop of ink on filter paper and dip the edge in water. Different dyes in the ink travel at different speeds, separating into distinct bands. This technique is used in forensic science to analyse ink samples, in food testing labs to check for illegal dyes (a real concern in India), and in pharmaceutical quality control.

## Elements, Compounds, and Their Properties

**Kiran:** When iron and sulphur are just mixed, we can separate them with a magnet. But when heated together, they form iron sulphide, which is not magnetic. Why the dramatic change?

**Ravi:** In a mixture, iron and sulphur particles retain their individual properties -- iron is magnetic, sulphur is yellow. No chemical bonds form between them. But when heated, a chemical reaction occurs: iron atoms bond with sulphur atoms to form iron sulphide (FeS). This new compound has entirely different properties -- it is neither magnetic nor yellow. This experiment beautifully illustrates the fundamental difference between a mixture and a compound.

**Kiran:** The textbook lists metals, non-metals, and metalloids. Mercury is a liquid metal and bromine is a liquid non-metal. Those seem unusual.

**Ravi:** They are exceptions that examiners love to test. At room temperature, 11 elements are gases, 2 are liquids (mercury and bromine), and the rest are solids. Mercury's low melting point is why it was historically used in thermometers, though it is now being replaced by safer alternatives. Metalloids like silicon have properties between metals and non-metals -- silicon's semiconductor properties are the foundation of the entire electronics and computer industry.

## Exam Preparation

**Kiran:** What are the must-know topics for the board exam from this chapter?

**Ravi:** Five areas. First, distinguishing pure substances from mixtures with examples. Second, the comparison table for solutions, suspensions, and colloids (particle size, Tyndall effect, stability, filterability). Third, separation techniques and when to use each. Fourth, the difference between elements and compounds, with the iron-sulphur example. Fifth, concentration calculations -- mass percentage and volume percentage. Practice the numerical problems on concentration; they carry good marks and are straightforward once you master the formula.

**Kiran:** Any mnemonics to remember the types of colloids?

**Ravi:** Remember "FELL GAS" for common colloid types: Fog (liquid in gas), Emulsion like milk (liquid in liquid), Latex and paint (solid in liquid), Lather or shaving cream (gas in liquid), Gel (liquid in solid), Aerosol like smoke (solid in gas), Sponge (gas in solid). Associating each type with a familiar Indian household example makes it stick in memory.
