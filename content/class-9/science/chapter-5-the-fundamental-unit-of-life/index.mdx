## Discovery of the Cell

In 1665, **Robert Hooke** observed a thin slice of cork under a simple microscope he had designed himself. He saw tiny box-like compartments that reminded him of the small rooms in a monastery. He called these compartments **cells** (from the Latin word "cella" meaning "a little room").

This was the first time anyone observed that living things are made up of separate units.

### Important Discoveries in Cell Biology

- **Robert Hooke (1665)** -- First observed cells in cork
- **Leeuwenhoek (1674)** -- Discovered free-living cells in pond water using an improved microscope
- **Robert Brown (1831)** -- Discovered the **nucleus** in the cell
- **Purkinje (1839)** -- Coined the term **protoplasm** for the fluid substance of the cell
- **Schleiden (1838) and Schwann (1839)** -- Proposed the **cell theory**: all plants and animals are composed of cells, and the cell is the basic unit of life
- **Virchow (1855)** -- Expanded cell theory: all cells arise from pre-existing cells
- **Electron microscope (1940)** -- Made it possible to observe the detailed structure of the cell

## What are Living Organisms Made Up of?

All living organisms are made up of **cells**. The cell is the **structural and functional unit of life**.

### Unicellular and Multicellular Organisms

- **Unicellular organisms** -- made of a single cell that performs all life functions
  - Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Chlamydomonas, bacteria
- **Multicellular organisms** -- made of many cells, with different cells performing different functions
  - Examples: plants, animals, most fungi

Every multicellular organism starts from a **single cell** that divides repeatedly. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

### Cell Shape and Size

Cells come in different shapes and sizes, depending on their function:
- **Nerve cells** are long and branched (to carry messages over long distances)
- **Red blood cells** are round and disc-shaped (to flow through blood vessels)
- **Muscle cells** are elongated (to contract and produce movement)
- **Amoeba** constantly changes its shape
- **White blood cells (WBCs)** can change shape to engulf bacteria

> **Key idea:** The shape and size of a cell are related to the specific function it performs. Despite these differences, all cells share the same basic organelles.

## Structural Organisation of a Cell

Every cell has three main features:
1. **Plasma membrane** (cell membrane)
2. **Nucleus**
3. **Cytoplasm**

---

## Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane)

The **plasma membrane** is the outermost covering of every cell. It separates the contents of the cell from its external environment.

### Properties of the Plasma Membrane

- Made up of **lipids and proteins**
- It is **selectively permeable** (semi-permeable) -- it allows some substances to pass through while blocking others
- It is **flexible**, which allows the cell to change shape and engulf food (as in Amoeba)
- Can only be seen clearly under an **electron microscope**

### Movement of Substances Across the Membrane

#### Diffusion

**Diffusion** is the movement of a substance from a region of **high concentration** to a region of **low concentration**.

- **CO2** (cellular waste) moves out of the cell by diffusion when its concentration is higher inside than outside
- **O2** enters the cell by diffusion when its concentration is lower inside than outside
- Diffusion plays an important role in **gaseous exchange** between cells and the environment

#### Osmosis

**Osmosis** is the movement of **water molecules** through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of higher water concentration to a region of lower water concentration.

When a cell is placed in different solutions:

- **Hypotonic solution** (more water outside than inside): Water enters the cell by osmosis, and the cell **swells up**
- **Isotonic solution** (equal water concentration): No net movement of water; cell stays the **same size**
- **Hypertonic solution** (less water outside than inside): Water leaves the cell by osmosis, and the cell **shrinks**

**Examples of osmosis:**
- A dried raisin swells when placed in water (water enters by osmosis)
- An egg without its shell swells in pure water and shrinks in concentrated salt solution
- Plant roots absorb water from the soil by osmosis

#### Endocytosis

Some cells, like **Amoeba**, use the flexibility of their cell membrane to **engulf food particles**. This process is called **endocytosis**.

> **Key idea:** The plasma membrane is selectively permeable -- it carefully controls what enters and leaves the cell. Diffusion and osmosis are the main ways substances move across the membrane.

## Cell Wall

**Plant cells** have an additional rigid outer covering called the **cell wall**, located outside the plasma membrane.

### Properties of the Cell Wall

- Made mainly of **cellulose** (a complex carbohydrate)
- Provides **structural strength** and **rigidity** to the plant
- **Freely permeable** -- allows substances to pass through easily
- Protects the cell from bursting in **hypotonic solutions** (dilute media)

### Plasmolysis

When a living plant cell is placed in a **hypertonic solution** (concentrated salt or sugar solution), the cell loses water by osmosis. The cell contents shrink and pull away from the cell wall. This is called **plasmolysis**.

> **Key idea:** Plasmolysis occurs only in living cells, not in dead cells. This is because the selectively permeable plasma membrane works only in living cells.

---

## Nucleus

The **nucleus** is the control centre of the cell. It was discovered by **Robert Brown** in 1831.

### Structure of the Nucleus

- Covered by a **double-layered nuclear membrane** with pores that allow transfer of materials between the nucleus and cytoplasm
- Contains **chromosomes** -- visible as rod-shaped structures when the cell is about to divide
- Chromosomes are made of **DNA** (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) and **proteins**
- **DNA** carries the genetic information needed for inheritance and for building and organising cells
- Functional segments of DNA are called **genes**
- When the cell is not dividing, DNA exists as **chromatin material** (thread-like structures)

### Functions of the Nucleus

- Controls all **chemical activities** of the cell
- Directs **cell development** and maturity
- Plays a central role in **cell division** (reproduction)
- Stores **genetic information** and passes it from parents to offspring

### Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

| Feature | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
|---|---|---|
| **Size** | Small (1-10 micrometres) | Large (5-100 micrometres) |
| **Nuclear region** | Poorly defined, no nuclear membrane (called nucleoid) | Well-defined, surrounded by nuclear membrane |
| **Chromosomes** | Single | More than one |
| **Membrane-bound organelles** | Absent | Present |
| **Examples** | Bacteria | Plants, animals, fungi |

> **Key idea:** Prokaryotic cells (like bacteria) do not have a well-defined nucleus or membrane-bound organelles. Eukaryotic cells (like plant and animal cells) have a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.

---

## Cytoplasm

The **cytoplasm** is the fluid content of the cell enclosed by the plasma membrane. It occupies most of the cell's volume and contains all the cell organelles.

---

## Cell Organelles

Cell organelles are specialised structures within a cell that perform specific functions. In eukaryotic cells, most organelles are **membrane-bound**.

### 1. Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The **ER** is a large network of membrane-bound tubes and sacs extending throughout the cytoplasm.

**Two types:**
- **Rough ER (RER)** -- has **ribosomes** attached to its surface; involved in **protein synthesis**
- **Smooth ER (SER)** -- does not have ribosomes; involved in making **lipids** (fats)

**Functions:**
- **Transport** of materials (especially proteins) within the cell
- **Membrane biogenesis** -- helps build new cell membranes
- **Detoxification** -- SER in liver cells helps detoxify poisons and drugs
- Provides a **structural framework** for biochemical activities

### 2. Golgi Apparatus

First described by **Camillo Golgi**, the Golgi apparatus consists of stacks of flattened membrane-bound sacs called **cisterns** (or cisternae).

**Functions:**
- **Packaging** of materials synthesised in the ER
- **Storage and modification** of products
- **Dispatching** (sending) packaged materials to their targets inside and outside the cell
- Making **complex sugars** from simple sugars
- Formation of **lysosomes**

> **Key idea:** Think of the Golgi apparatus as the "post office" of the cell -- it packages, labels, and sends materials to the right destinations.

### 3. Lysosomes

**Lysosomes** are membrane-bound sacs filled with powerful **digestive enzymes** (made by RER).

**Functions:**
- **Digestion** of foreign materials like bacteria that enter the cell
- Breaking down **worn-out cell organelles**
- Cleaning up cellular waste

Lysosomes are also called **"suicide bags"** of the cell because when a cell is damaged, the lysosomes burst open and their enzymes digest the entire cell.

### 4. Mitochondria

**Mitochondria** are known as the **"powerhouses"** of the cell because they release energy.

**Structure:**
- Have a **double membrane** covering
- Outer membrane is porous
- Inner membrane is **deeply folded** (called cristae), which increases the surface area for energy-producing reactions
- Have their own **DNA and ribosomes** -- can make some of their own proteins

**Function:**
- Release energy in the form of **ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)**, which is the **"energy currency"** of the cell
- The body uses ATP energy for making new compounds and for mechanical work

### 5. Plastids

**Plastids** are found **only in plant cells**. They are of two types:

- **Chromoplasts** (coloured plastids):
  - **Chloroplasts** contain the green pigment **chlorophyll** and are essential for **photosynthesis**
  - Other chromoplasts contain yellow or orange pigments (give colour to flowers and fruits)

- **Leucoplasts** (white/colourless plastids):
  - Store **starch, oils, and proteins**

**Structure of chloroplasts:**
- Have a double membrane
- Contain numerous internal membrane layers embedded in a fluid called **stroma**
- Like mitochondria, they have their own **DNA and ribosomes**

> **Key idea:** Both mitochondria and plastids have their own DNA and ribosomes, which makes them unique among cell organelles. They can make some of their own proteins independently.

### 6. Vacuoles

**Vacuoles** are storage sacs for solid or liquid contents.

- **In animal cells** -- vacuoles are small
- **In plant cells** -- vacuoles are very large, often occupying 50-90% of cell volume

**Functions:**
- In plant cells: filled with **cell sap**, provide **turgidity and rigidity** to the cell, store amino acids, sugars, organic acids, and proteins
- In **Amoeba**: food vacuoles store consumed food
- In some unicellular organisms: contractile vacuoles expel excess water and waste

---

## Plant Cells vs Animal Cells

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---|---|---|
| **Cell wall** | Present (made of cellulose) | Absent |
| **Plastids** | Present (chloroplasts, leucoplasts) | Absent |
| **Vacuoles** | Large central vacuole | Small or absent |
| **Centrosome/Centrioles** | Absent | Present |
| **Shape** | Usually rectangular/fixed | Usually irregular/round |

## Cell Division

New cells are formed for:
- **Growth** of the body
- **Replacing** old, dead, or injured cells
- Forming **gametes** (reproductive cells) for reproduction

### Types of Cell Division

**1. Mitosis** -- The cell (mother cell) divides to form **two identical daughter cells** with the **same number of chromosomes** as the mother cell.
- Used for **growth and repair** of tissues

**2. Meiosis** -- A special type of division that produces **four new cells**, each with **half the number of chromosomes** as the mother cell.
- Used for forming **gametes** (sperm and egg cells)
- Involves **two consecutive divisions**

> **Key idea:** Mitosis maintains the chromosome number (for growth), while meiosis halves the chromosome number (for reproduction).

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Cell** -- The fundamental structural and functional unit of life
- **Plasma membrane** -- Selectively permeable outer covering of the cell
- **Cell wall** -- Rigid outer covering in plant cells, made of cellulose
- **Nucleus** -- Control centre of the cell, contains DNA
- **Cytoplasm** -- Fluid content inside the cell
- **Endoplasmic reticulum** -- Network of membranes for transport and synthesis
- **Golgi apparatus** -- Packaging and dispatching centre of the cell
- **Lysosomes** -- Digestive organelles ("suicide bags")
- **Mitochondria** -- Energy-releasing organelles ("powerhouses")
- **Plastids** -- Found only in plant cells; chloroplasts for photosynthesis
- **Vacuoles** -- Storage sacs; large in plant cells
- **Osmosis** -- Movement of water through a selectively permeable membrane
- **Diffusion** -- Movement from high concentration to low concentration
- **Prokaryotic** -- Cell without a true nucleus (e.g., bacteria)
- **Eukaryotic** -- Cell with a true nucleus (e.g., plant and animal cells)
- **Mitosis** -- Cell division producing two identical daughter cells
- **Meiosis** -- Cell division producing four cells with half the chromosome number
