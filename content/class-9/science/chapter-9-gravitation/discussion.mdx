## The Universal Law of Gravitation

**Kiran:** Papa, Newton saw an apple fall and discovered gravity. Is that story true?

**Ravi:** The apple story is likely simplified, but the insight was real. Newton realised that the force pulling the apple to the ground and the force keeping the Moon in orbit around the Earth are the same force -- gravity. His universal law states that every object attracts every other object with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them: F equals G times M times m divided by d-squared.

**Kiran:** The constant G is very small -- 6.673 times 10 to the power minus 11. What does that mean practically?

**Ravi:** It means gravitational force between everyday objects is negligible. Two 1 kg masses placed 1 metre apart attract each other with a force of only 6.673 times 10 to the power minus 11 Newtons -- far too small to feel. But when one of the masses is the Earth (6 times 10 to the power 24 kg), the force becomes significant. That is why we feel the Earth's gravity but not the gravitational pull of our desk.

**Kiran:** The apple attracts the Earth too, by Newton's Third Law. Why does only the apple move?

**Ravi:** Both the apple and the Earth exert equal forces on each other. But the Earth's mass is so enormous that its acceleration (a equals F divided by M) is negligibly small -- about 10 to the power minus 25 m/s-squared. The apple, with its tiny mass, accelerates at 9.8 m/s-squared. Both accelerate, but the Earth's acceleration is undetectable.

## Acceleration Due to Gravity and Free Fall

**Kiran:** Why is g the same for all objects regardless of mass? If heavier objects have more gravitational force on them, should they not fall faster?

**Ravi:** More gravitational force, yes, but also more inertia (mass). The two effects cancel perfectly. The acceleration g equals GM divided by R-squared -- notice that the object's mass does not appear. Galileo demonstrated this by dropping different masses from the Leaning Tower of Pisa (according to tradition). In vacuum, a feather and a hammer fall at exactly the same rate -- astronauts demonstrated this on the Moon during the Apollo 15 mission.

**Kiran:** So in real life, why does a feather fall slower than a stone?

**Ravi:** Air resistance. The feather has a large surface area relative to its mass, so air drag slows it significantly. The stone, being denser, is barely affected. In a vacuum chamber, they fall together. This distinction between "in vacuum" and "in air" is important for board exams.

**Kiran:** The equations of motion apply to free fall with a equals g?

**Ravi:** Yes. Replace a with g (9.8 m/s-squared) and s with h (height). For an object dropped from rest: v equals gt, h equals half g t-squared, and v-squared equals 2gh. For an object thrown upward: at the highest point, v equals 0, and g is negative (opposing the upward motion). These substitutions are the most common numerical problems in this chapter.

## Mass, Weight, and the Moon

**Kiran:** Mass and weight are used interchangeably in everyday language. What is the scientific difference?

**Ravi:** Mass is the amount of matter -- measured in kilograms, constant everywhere, measured with a beam balance. Weight is the gravitational force on an object -- measured in Newtons, changes with location (because g changes), measured with a spring balance. On the Moon, g is about one-sixth of Earth's g, so your weight on the Moon is one-sixth of your Earth weight, but your mass stays the same.

**Kiran:** An astronaut who weighs 600 N on Earth would weigh only 100 N on the Moon?

**Ravi:** Yes. Their mass is about 61 kg on both Earth and Moon. But weight equals mg, and since g on the Moon is about 1.63 m/s-squared (compared to 9.8 on Earth), weight drops to about 100 N. This is why astronauts can jump much higher on the Moon. India's Chandrayaan missions study the Moon's gravitational field to understand its internal structure.

**Kiran:** Board exam trap -- will a spring balance show the same reading on the Moon?

**Ravi:** No. A spring balance measures weight (force), which depends on g. It will show one-sixth the reading on the Moon. A beam balance compares masses using the same gravitational field for both sides, so it gives the same reading on the Moon as on Earth. This distinction is a favourite exam question.

## Pressure, Buoyancy, and Archimedes' Principle

**Kiran:** Pressure is force per unit area. Why do sharp knives cut better?

**Ravi:** Because the force is concentrated on a smaller area, producing greater pressure. A sharp knife has a very thin edge -- same force, smaller area, higher pressure. Similarly, nails have pointed tips, and camels have broad feet to reduce pressure on soft sand (same weight, larger area, less pressure). Wide shoulder straps on school bags distribute weight over a larger area, reducing pressure.

**Kiran:** Archimedes' Principle says the buoyant force equals the weight of fluid displaced. How does this explain floating?

**Ravi:** An object floats when the buoyant force equals its weight. If the object's density is less than the fluid's density, it displaces enough fluid (by volume) before being fully submerged to generate sufficient buoyant force. Wood floats on water because its density is less than water's. Iron sinks because its density is greater. Ships, despite being made of steel, float because they are hollow -- the overall density (total mass divided by total volume including air) is less than water's density.

**Kiran:** What is relative density?

**Ravi:** Relative density is the ratio of a substance's density to water's density. It has no unit. If relative density is greater than 1, the substance sinks in water; if less than 1, it floats. Gold has a relative density of about 19.3, while cork has about 0.2. Lactometers used by milk inspectors in India work on Archimedes' Principle -- they measure the density of milk to check for adulteration with water.
