## Scientific Concept of Work

In everyday language, "work" means any physical or mental activity. But in science, **work** has a very specific meaning.

**Work is done when a force acts on an object and the object moves in the direction of the force.**

Two conditions must be met for work to be done:
1. A **force** must act on the object
2. The object must **move** (be displaced) in the direction of the force

> **Key idea:** If the force and displacement are in the same direction, positive work is done. If they are in opposite directions, negative work is done. If there is no displacement, or the force is perpendicular to displacement, no work is done.

### Examples

**Work IS done:**
- Pushing a box across the floor (force and displacement in same direction)
- Lifting a book upward (force upward, displacement upward)
- A horse pulling a cart (force in direction of motion)

**Work is NOT done:**
- Pushing a wall (no displacement)
- Carrying a bag while walking on a flat road (force is upward/against gravity, displacement is horizontal -- perpendicular)
- An object moving in a circle with constant speed (centripetal force is perpendicular to displacement)

---

## Formula for Work

**W = F x s**

Where:
- W = work done
- F = force applied (in the direction of displacement)
- s = displacement

- **SI unit of work:** Joule (J)
- **1 Joule** = 1 Newton x 1 metre = 1 N m
- 1 J is the work done when a force of 1 N moves an object through 1 m in the direction of the force

**When force is at an angle to displacement:**

W = F x s x cos(angle)

- If angle = 0 degrees: W = Fs (maximum work)
- If angle = 90 degrees: W = 0 (no work)
- If angle = 180 degrees: W = -Fs (negative work, as in friction)

## Energy

**Energy** is the capacity to do work. An object that has energy can exert a force on another object and do work on it.

- **SI unit of energy:** Joule (J)
- The unit of energy is the same as the unit of work

> **Key idea:** Energy is transferred when work is done. The object doing the work loses energy, and the object on which work is done gains energy.

---

## Forms of Energy

### Kinetic Energy

**Kinetic energy** is the energy possessed by an object due to its **motion**.

**KE = (1/2) x m x v squared**

Where:
- m = mass of the object
- v = velocity of the object

**Key points:**
- A stationary object has zero kinetic energy
- Kinetic energy increases with both mass and velocity
- Kinetic energy is proportional to the **square** of velocity (doubling velocity quadruples KE)

**Example:** A moving car, a flowing river, a flying bird all have kinetic energy.

### Potential Energy

**Potential energy** is the energy possessed by an object due to its **position or configuration**.

#### Gravitational Potential Energy

An object raised to a height above the ground has gravitational potential energy.

**PE = m x g x h**

Where:
- m = mass of the object
- g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s squared)
- h = height above the ground (reference level)

**Example:** A book on a shelf, water stored in a dam, a roller coaster at the top of a hill.

> **Key idea:** The potential energy at a point depends on the reference level chosen. On the ground (reference), PE = 0.

---

## Law of Conservation of Energy

**Energy can neither be created nor destroyed. It can only be transformed from one form to another. The total energy of a system remains constant.**

### Example: Falling Object

When a ball is held at height h:
- PE = mgh, KE = 0
- Total energy = mgh

When the ball is falling at height h' (between top and ground):
- PE = mgh', KE = (1/2)mv squared
- Total energy = PE + KE = mgh (constant)

When the ball reaches the ground:
- PE = 0, KE = (1/2)mv squared = mgh
- Total energy = mgh (same as before)

The potential energy converts to kinetic energy as the object falls, but the **total energy remains constant**.

### Energy Transformations in Daily Life

| Source | Energy Transformation |
|---|---|
| Burning fuel | Chemical energy to heat and light energy |
| Electric bulb | Electrical energy to light and heat energy |
| Photosynthesis | Light energy to chemical energy |
| Hydroelectric dam | Potential energy to kinetic energy to electrical energy |
| Battery | Chemical energy to electrical energy |
| Solar cell | Light energy to electrical energy |

## Rate of Doing Work: Power

**Power** is the rate of doing work (or the rate of energy transfer).

**Power = Work done / Time taken**

**P = W / t**

- **SI unit of power:** Watt (W)
- **1 Watt** = 1 Joule per second = 1 J/s
- **1 kilowatt (kW)** = 1000 W

### Average Power

When different amounts of work are done in different time intervals:

**Average power = Total work done / Total time taken**

### Commercial Unit of Energy

The unit used for measuring electrical energy consumed in homes is the **kilowatt hour (kWh)**.

**1 kWh = 1 kW x 1 hour = 1000 W x 3600 s = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 x 10 to the power 6 J**

1 kWh is also called **1 unit** of electricity.

> **Key idea:** Power tells us how fast work is done. A more powerful engine does the same work in less time. The unit of power (watt) is named after James Watt.

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Work** -- Force x displacement (in the direction of force)
- **Energy** -- Capacity to do work
- **Kinetic energy** -- Energy due to motion
- **Potential energy** -- Energy due to position
- **Law of conservation of energy** -- Total energy is constant; energy cannot be created or destroyed
- **Power** -- Rate of doing work
- **Watt** -- SI unit of power (1 J/s)
- **Kilowatt hour (kWh)** -- Commercial unit of energy (3.6 x 10^6 J)
