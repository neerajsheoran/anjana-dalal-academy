## What Makes an Equation Quadratic

**Kiran:** Papa, sometimes an equation looks cubic but simplifies to a quadratic. How do I handle that in the exam?

**Ravi:** Always simplify first. Expand everything, collect like terms, and check the highest power. If the x cubed terms cancel out and you are left with x squared as the highest power, it is quadratic. The equation the quantity x plus 2 cubed equals x cubed minus 4 looks cubic, but when you expand and cancel x cubed from both sides, you get a clean quadratic.

**Kiran:** And if the x squared terms also cancel?

**Ravi:** Then it is not quadratic — it reduces to linear. The key condition is that the coefficient of x squared must not be zero. The textbook mentions this: a must not equal zero in ax squared plus bx plus c equals zero.

**Kiran:** Our teacher said Sridharacharya derived the quadratic formula in 1025 CE. Was he really Indian?

**Ravi:** Yes, Sridharacharya was an Indian mathematician who gave us the formula we now call the quadratic formula. Even before him, Brahmagupta in the 7th century worked on quadratic equations. India's mathematical heritage in algebra is extraordinary.

## Solving by Factorisation

**Kiran:** When should I use factorisation versus the quadratic formula?

**Ravi:** In the board exam, if the question says "solve by factorisation," you must use that method. Otherwise, either works. Factorisation is faster when the roots are rational numbers. The formula always works but involves more computation.

**Kiran:** For the splitting the middle term method, I need two numbers whose product is ac and sum is b. What if ac is negative?

**Ravi:** Then one of your two numbers will be positive and the other negative. For example, in 2x squared minus 5x plus 3, ac equals 6 and b equals minus 5. You need two numbers with product 6 and sum minus 5 — those are minus 2 and minus 3. Practice 20-30 factorisation problems and the pattern recognition becomes automatic.

**Kiran:** And I should always reject negative roots in real-world problems?

**Ravi:** When the variable represents a physical quantity like length, age, or speed, yes — reject negative roots with a clear statement. Write "Since breadth cannot be negative, x equals minus 12.5 is rejected." This explicit rejection earns marks in the CBSE marking scheme.

## The Quadratic Formula and Discriminant

**Kiran:** The discriminant D equals b squared minus 4ac — I think of it as a traffic signal. Green means go, two real roots. Yellow means caution, one repeated root. Red means stop, no real roots.

**Ravi:** That is a clever analogy. When D is greater than zero, you get two distinct real roots. When D equals zero, both roots are the same — a perfect square situation. When D is less than zero, no real roots exist. The question "find the nature of roots" is typically worth 1-2 marks and requires only computing D.

**Kiran:** What about finding the value of k for which the equation has equal roots?

**Ravi:** Set D equals zero and solve for k. This is a very common 2-3 mark question. Be careful — sometimes one of the k values makes the coefficient of x squared zero, which would make the equation non-quadratic. You must reject that value.

**Kiran:** Like kx times x minus 2 plus 6 equals 0. If k equals zero, it is not quadratic anymore.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Always check that constraint. This subtlety separates a 2-mark answer from a full-marks answer.

## Word Problems

**Kiran:** The word problems in this chapter feel harder than in Chapter 3.

**Ravi:** Because now you are forming quadratic equations, which require more careful setup. The common types are area problems where length times breadth gives a quadratic, age problems where the product of ages leads to a quadratic, consecutive number problems, and speed-distance-time problems where the relationship leads to a quadratic.

**Kiran:** The speed-distance problem where the train takes 3 hours more if it went 8 km per hour slower — that one was tricky.

**Ravi:** The technique is always the same. Let the unknown be x, form an expression using the given relationship, simplify to standard form, solve, and interpret. For that train problem, time equals distance over speed. The condition "3 hours more" gives you 480 over the quantity x minus 8, minus 480 over x, equals 3. Cross-multiply and simplify to get the quadratic.

## Board Exam Tips

**Kiran:** How much does this chapter weigh in the board exam?

**Ravi:** Typically 4-5 marks. You can expect one word problem and one nature-of-roots or find-k-for-equal-roots question. The word problem usually requires forming and solving the equation.

**Kiran:** What mistakes should I avoid?

**Ravi:** First, always write the equation in standard form before solving — ax squared plus bx plus c equals zero. Second, when using the formula, be careful with the sign of b — if b is negative, then minus b is positive. Third, always verify your roots by substituting back. Fourth, in word problems, clearly define the variable, form the equation, solve it, and state the final answer in words. Do not just write "x equals 5" — write "The breadth of the hall is 5 metres."

**Kiran:** Does the quadratic equation connect to any real-world engineering problem?

**Ravi:** Constantly. The trajectory of a projectile — like a ball thrown in cricket — follows a parabolic path described by a quadratic equation. Bridge arches are parabolic. Satellite dish antennas are parabolic reflectors. Even the profit-loss analysis in business often involves quadratic relationships. ISRO's rocket trajectory calculations use quadratic and higher-degree equations extensively. The mathematics you study here is genuinely the language of engineering.
