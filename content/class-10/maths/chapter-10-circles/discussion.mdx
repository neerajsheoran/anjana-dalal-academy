## Tangent and Its Properties

**Kiran:** Papa, a tangent touches the circle at exactly one point. But why can we draw exactly two tangents from an external point?

**Ravi:** Imagine standing outside a circle and looking at it. You can see two edges — the left edge and the right edge. Each line of sight that just grazes the circle is a tangent. From inside the circle, every line through you crosses the circle at two points — no tangent is possible. From a point on the circle, you can touch the circle at that point and go in exactly one direction without crossing it — one tangent.

**Kiran:** The bicycle wheel analogy in the textbook was helpful — the ground is a tangent to the wheel.

**Ravi:** Yes. At any instant, the wheel touches the ground at exactly one point, and the ground is perpendicular to the spoke at that contact point. This is Theorem 10.1 in physical form — the tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact.

## Theorem Proofs

**Kiran:** The proof that the tangent is perpendicular to the radius — will this come in the board exam?

**Ravi:** Theorem 10.1 is a frequently asked proof, worth 3-4 marks. The key argument is that every point on the tangent line other than the point of contact lies outside the circle, so the distance from the centre to any such point is greater than the radius. This makes the radius the shortest distance from the centre to the line, which means it is perpendicular.

**Kiran:** And Theorem 10.2 — the equal tangent lengths theorem?

**Ravi:** This proof uses the RHS congruence criterion. Join the centre to the external point and to both points of contact. You get two right triangles sharing a hypotenuse and each having a radius as one side. By RHS, the triangles are congruent, so the tangent lengths are equal. This proof is also commonly asked in boards.

**Kiran:** The bonus result that OP bisects the angle between the two tangents — is that testable?

**Ravi:** It follows from CPCT after the congruence proof. The CBSE has asked students to prove this as a follow-up. Also, the angle between the two tangents and the angle subtended at the centre by the points of contact are supplementary — they add up to 180 degrees.

## Problem Types

**Kiran:** The concentric circles problem where the chord of the larger circle is tangent to the smaller circle — how do I approach that?

**Ravi:** The chord touches the inner circle, so it is a tangent to the inner circle. The radius to the point of tangency is perpendicular to the chord. And a perpendicular from the centre to a chord bisects the chord. So the point of contact is the midpoint of the chord. This combines the tangent property with a chord property.

**Kiran:** What about the circumscribed quadrilateral property — AB plus CD equals BC plus AD?

**Ravi:** When a quadrilateral circumscribes a circle, each side is a tangent. From each vertex, two tangent segments to the circle are equal. Label them and add up opposite sides — you will find AB plus CD equals BC plus AD. This result leads to a beautiful corollary: if a parallelogram circumscribes a circle, it must be a rhombus.

**Kiran:** Because in a parallelogram opposite sides are equal, so the tangent property forces all sides to be equal?

**Ravi:** Precisely. This chain of reasoning — from the tangent theorem to the circumscribed quadrilateral to the rhombus conclusion — is a favourite multi-step board question.

## Numerical Problems

**Kiran:** The Pythagorean relationship keeps appearing — finding the tangent length when I know the radius and the distance from the centre.

**Ravi:** Yes. Since the tangent, radius, and line from centre to external point form a right triangle, you use the Pythagorean theorem. If the tangent length is 24 cm and the distance from the centre is 25 cm, then the radius is root of 625 minus 576, which is root 49, equals 7 cm.

**Kiran:** Some problems combine tangent properties with similarity of triangles.

**Ravi:** The chord-tangent problem where tangents from both ends of a chord meet at an external point involves constructing the line from the centre to the meeting point. This line is the perpendicular bisector of the chord and the angle bisector of the tangent angle. You can then use similar triangles or the Pythagorean theorem to find the required lengths.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** How is this chapter weighted?

**Ravi:** Typically 3-4 marks. Either a theorem proof or a problem involving tangent lengths and the Pythagorean relationship. Sometimes both appear in the same paper — one as a compulsory question and one as a choice.

**Kiran:** Key things to remember?

**Ravi:** Three points. First, always draw the radius to the point of contact and mark the 90-degree angle — this is your starting point for every problem. Second, from an external point, tangent lengths are equal — use this to set up equations. Third, the angle between two tangents from an external point and the central angle are supplementary. These three facts solve virtually every problem in this chapter.

## Connecting to Advanced Mathematics

**Kiran:** Does this chapter connect to anything in JEE or higher mathematics?

**Ravi:** Yes. In JEE, you study tangent lines to circles in coordinate geometry — the equation of a tangent to a circle at a point, tangent from an external point, and condition for a line to be tangent. All of these use the perpendicularity property you learn here. The concept of tangency extends to curves in calculus — the tangent line to any curve at a point just touches it without crossing.

**Kiran:** So the idea of "touching at one point" generalises beyond circles?

**Ravi:** Beautifully, yes. In differential calculus, the derivative at a point gives the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point. The circle is the simplest case. Indian mathematicians at the Kerala School of Mathematics, particularly Madhava in the 14th century, developed early concepts of calculus centuries before Newton and Leibniz. The tangent concept you study now is a doorway to that magnificent mathematical tradition.
