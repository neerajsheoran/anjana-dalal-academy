## Introduction

You have studied circles in earlier classes -- the definition, properties of chords, arcs, sectors, and segments. In this chapter, we focus on one special concept: **tangents** to a circle.

When a circle and a line are in the same plane, three situations can arise:

1. **No common point** -- the line does not touch the circle at all (non-intersecting line)
2. **Two common points** -- the line cuts through the circle (this line is called a **secant**)
3. **Exactly one common point** -- the line just touches the circle (this line is called a **tangent**)

Think of a bicycle wheel rolling on the ground. The ground touches the wheel at exactly one point at any instant. That point of contact is where the tangent touches the circle.

---

## Tangent to a Circle

A **tangent** to a circle is a line that touches the circle at **exactly one point**. This point is called the **point of contact**.

### Understanding Tangents

Imagine a secant (a line cutting through a circle at two points). As you move the secant parallel to itself, the two intersection points come closer and closer. When they merge into a single point, the secant becomes a tangent.

> **Key idea:** A tangent is a special case of a secant, where the two end points of the chord coincide into one point.

---

## Theorem 1: Tangent is Perpendicular to Radius

**Theorem 10.1:** The tangent at any point of a circle is **perpendicular** to the radius through the point of contact.

### Proof

Given: A circle with centre O and a tangent XY at point P.
To prove: OP is perpendicular to XY.

**Step 1:** Take any point Q on XY other than P.

**Step 2:** Since XY is a tangent, Q must lie **outside** the circle (if Q were inside, XY would be a secant, not a tangent).

**Step 3:** Therefore, OQ > OP (because OP is a radius and Q is outside the circle).

**Step 4:** This is true for **every** point Q on XY except P. So OP is the **shortest distance** from O to the line XY.

**Step 5:** The shortest distance from a point to a line is the perpendicular distance. Therefore, **OP is perpendicular to XY**.

> **Key idea:** At any point on a circle, there is one and only one tangent, and it is always perpendicular to the radius at that point.

### Practical Application

Look at the spokes of a bicycle wheel. Each spoke is along a radius. The ground (tangent) is always at right angles to the spoke that touches the ground.

---

## Number of Tangents from a Point

The number of tangents you can draw depends on **where the point is** relative to the circle:

| Position of Point | Number of Tangents |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Inside** the circle | **0** (no tangent possible) |
| **On** the circle | **1** (exactly one tangent) |
| **Outside** the circle | **2** (exactly two tangents) |

When two tangents are drawn from an external point, the segments from the external point to the points of contact are called the **lengths of the tangents**.

---

## Theorem 2: Equal Tangent Lengths

**Theorem 10.2:** The lengths of tangents drawn from an **external point** to a circle are **equal**.

### Proof

Given: A circle with centre O, an external point P, and tangents PQ and PR to the circle.
To prove: PQ = PR.

**Step 1:** Join OP, OQ, and OR.

**Step 2:** Since PQ is a tangent and OQ is a radius, angle OQP = 90 degrees (by Theorem 10.1). Similarly, angle ORP = 90 degrees.

**Step 3:** In right triangles OQP and ORP:
- OQ = OR (radii of the same circle)
- OP = OP (common side)

**Step 4:** By **RHS congruence**, triangle OQP is congruent to triangle ORP.

**Step 5:** Therefore, **PQ = PR** (by CPCT).

> **Key idea:** From any point outside a circle, you can draw exactly two tangents, and they are always equal in length. Also, the line OP bisects the angle between the two tangents.

### Additional Result

From the proof, we also get: **angle OPQ = angle OPR**. This means OP is the **angle bisector** of the angle QPR (the angle between the two tangents). In other words, the centre of the circle lies on the bisector of the angle between the two tangents.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Concentric Circles

In two concentric circles, prove that the chord of the larger circle which touches the smaller circle is bisected at the point of contact.

**Proof:** Let O be the common centre. Let AB be a chord of the larger circle touching the smaller circle at P.

- AB is a tangent to the smaller circle at P
- OP is the radius to the point of contact
- By Theorem 10.1, OP is perpendicular to AB
- A perpendicular from the centre to a chord **bisects** the chord
- Therefore, **AP = BP**

### Example 2: Angle Between Tangents

Two tangents TP and TQ are drawn from external point T. Prove that angle PTQ = 2 times angle OPQ.

Let angle PTQ = theta.

Since TP = TQ (equal tangent lengths), triangle TPQ is isosceles.
So angle TPQ = angle TQP = (180 - theta)/2 = 90 - theta/2

Also, angle OPT = 90 degrees (tangent perpendicular to radius).

So angle OPQ = angle OPT - angle TPQ = 90 - (90 - theta/2) = **theta/2**

Therefore, angle PTQ = 2 times angle OPQ. Proved!

### Example 3: Finding Tangent Length

From a point Q, the length of the tangent to a circle is 24 cm and the distance of Q from the centre is 25 cm. Find the radius.

Let the radius = r. By Theorem 10.1, the tangent and radius form a right angle.

By Pythagoras theorem: 25^2 = 24^2 + r^2
625 = 576 + r^2
r^2 = 49
**r = 7 cm**

### Example 4: Finding Tangent Length from a Chord

PQ is a chord of length 8 cm in a circle of radius 5 cm. Tangents at P and Q meet at point T. Find TP.

**Step 1:** Join OT. OT is perpendicular to PQ and bisects it (since the triangle TPQ is isosceles).
So PR = QR = 4 cm.

**Step 2:** OR = root(OP^2 - PR^2) = root(25 - 16) = root(9) = 3 cm.

**Step 3:** Using similar triangles TRP and PRO:
TP/PO = RP/RO
TP/5 = 4/3
**TP = 20/3 cm**

## Important Properties for Board Exams

### Tangents at Ends of a Diameter Are Parallel

If tangents are drawn at both ends of a diameter, they are **parallel** to each other (both are perpendicular to the same diameter).

### Circumscribed Quadrilateral Property

If a quadrilateral ABCD is drawn to **circumscribe** a circle (all four sides are tangents to the circle), then:

**AB + CD = AD + BC**

This is because tangent lengths from each vertex to the circle are equal, and when you add up all the sides, the opposite pairs give equal sums.

### Circumscribed Parallelogram Is a Rhombus

If a **parallelogram** circumscribes a circle, it must be a **rhombus**. This is because opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal, and using the property AB + CD = AD + BC along with AB = CD and AD = BC, we get AB = AD, making all sides equal.

### Angle Between Tangents and Angle at Centre Are Supplementary

The angle between two tangents from an external point and the angle subtended at the centre by the line segment joining the points of contact **add up to 180 degrees**.

If angle PTQ = theta, then angle POQ = 180 - theta.

## Chapter Summary

1. A **tangent** to a circle is a line that touches it at exactly one point.

2. **Theorem 10.1:** The tangent at any point of a circle is **perpendicular** to the radius through the point of contact.

3. **Theorem 10.2:** The lengths of tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are **equal**.

4. From a point **inside** a circle, no tangent can be drawn. From a point **on** the circle, exactly one tangent exists. From a point **outside** the circle, exactly two tangents can be drawn.
