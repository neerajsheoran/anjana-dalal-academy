## Introduction

You already know the basic formulas for circles: circumference = 2 pi r and area = pi r^2. In this chapter, we go deeper into finding the areas and lengths associated with **parts** of a circle -- specifically **sectors** and **segments**.

These concepts are used in many real-life situations: the area swept by a clock hand, the grazing area of a horse tied to a post, the area cleaned by a car wiper, and much more.

---

## Recap: Basic Circle Formulas

Before we begin, let us recall:

- **Circumference** of a circle = 2 pi r
- **Area** of a circle = pi r^2

where **r** is the radius.

Unless stated otherwise, use **pi = 22/7** in calculations.

---

## Sector and Segment of a Circle

### What Is a Sector?

A **sector** is the region enclosed by **two radii** and the **arc** between them. Think of it as a "slice" of a circular pizza.

- **Minor sector:** the smaller slice (angle less than 180 degrees)
- **Major sector:** the larger slice (angle greater than 180 degrees)

The angle formed at the centre is called the **angle of the sector** (theta).

Note: Angle of major sector = 360 - theta.

### What Is a Segment?

A **segment** is the region enclosed between a **chord** and the **arc** it cuts off.

- **Minor segment:** the smaller region (on the side of the minor arc)
- **Major segment:** the larger region (on the side of the major arc)

> **Key idea:** A sector is like a pizza slice (bounded by two radii and an arc). A segment is like the region between a chord and the arc (no radii involved).

---

## Area of a Sector

If a complete circle has an angle of 360 degrees and area pi r^2, then a sector with angle theta has:

**Area of sector = (theta / 360) x pi r^2**

### How to Remember

The fraction **(theta / 360)** tells you what fraction of the whole circle the sector represents. Multiply this fraction by the area of the full circle.

### Worked Example 1

Find the area of a sector with radius 6 cm and angle 60 degrees.

Area = (60/360) x (22/7) x 6^2
= (1/6) x (22/7) x 36
= (1/6) x (792/7)
= 132/7
= **18.86 cm^2** (approx.)

### Worked Example 2

The minute hand of a clock is 14 cm long. Find the area swept in 5 minutes.

The minute hand completes 360 degrees in 60 minutes.
In 5 minutes, it sweeps: (5/60) x 360 = 30 degrees.

Area = (30/360) x (22/7) x 14^2
= (1/12) x (22/7) x 196
= (1/12) x 616
= **154/3 cm^2** (approx. 51.33 cm^2)

---

## Length of an Arc

Similarly, the length of the arc corresponding to a sector of angle theta is:

**Length of arc = (theta / 360) x 2 pi r**

### Worked Example

In a circle of radius 21 cm, an arc subtends an angle of 60 degrees. Find the arc length.

Arc length = (60/360) x 2 x (22/7) x 21
= (1/6) x 2 x 22 x 3
= (1/6) x 132
= **22 cm**

---

## Area of a Segment

A segment is the region between a chord and the corresponding arc. To find its area:

**Area of segment = Area of sector - Area of triangle**

The triangle is formed by the two radii and the chord.

> **Key idea:** Segment = Sector minus Triangle. This is the formula you need for all segment problems.

### Finding the Area of the Triangle

For a sector with angle theta and radius r, the triangle formed by the two radii and the chord has area:

**Area of triangle = (1/2) r^2 sin(theta)**

For common angles:
- theta = 60 degrees: Area of triangle = (root(3)/4) r^2
- theta = 90 degrees: Area of triangle = (1/2) r^2
- theta = 120 degrees: Area of triangle = (root(3)/4) r^2

### Worked Example: Segment with 90-Degree Angle

A chord of a circle of radius 10 cm subtends a right angle at the centre. Find the area of the minor segment. (Use pi = 3.14)

**Step 1: Area of sector**
= (90/360) x 3.14 x 10^2
= (1/4) x 314
= 78.5 cm^2

**Step 2: Area of triangle**
= (1/2) x 10 x 10 = 50 cm^2

**Step 3: Area of segment**
= 78.5 - 50 = **28.5 cm^2**

### Worked Example: Segment with 60-Degree Angle

In a circle of radius 21 cm, a chord subtends 60 degrees at the centre. Find the area of the minor segment.

**Step 1: Area of sector**
= (60/360) x (22/7) x 21^2
= (1/6) x (22/7) x 441
= (1/6) x 1386
= 231 cm^2

**Step 2: Area of triangle**
The triangle formed is equilateral (two sides are radii = 21 cm, angle between them = 60 degrees, so it is equilateral).
Area = (root(3)/4) x 21^2 = (root(3)/4) x 441 = (441 root(3))/4 cm^2

Using root(3) = 1.73: Area of triangle = 441 x 1.73 / 4 = 190.73 cm^2

**Step 3: Area of segment**
= 231 - 190.73 = **40.27 cm^2** (approx.)

---

## Area of Major Sector and Major Segment

- **Area of major sector** = Area of circle - Area of minor sector
  = pi r^2 - (theta/360) x pi r^2

- **Area of major segment** = Area of circle - Area of minor segment

## Real-Life Application Problems

### Horse Tied to a Corner

A horse is tied with a 5 m rope to a corner of a square field of side 15 m. The area it can graze is a **quarter circle** (since the corner angle is 90 degrees).

Grazing area = (90/360) x pi x 5^2 = (1/4) x 3.14 x 25 = **19.625 m^2**

If the rope is increased to 10 m:
New area = (1/4) x 3.14 x 100 = 78.5 m^2
Increase = 78.5 - 19.625 = **58.875 m^2**

### Car Wiper

Each wiper blade is 25 cm long and sweeps through 115 degrees. The area cleaned by one blade:

Area = (115/360) x pi x 25^2

Total area for two blades = 2 x (115/360) x 3.14 x 625 = **1254.96 cm^2** (approx.)

### Clock Hand Sweep

The area swept by the minute hand of a clock from 6:00 to 6:20 (20 minutes) with a 14 cm long minute hand:

Angle swept in 20 min = (20/60) x 360 = 120 degrees
Area = (120/360) x (22/7) x 14^2 = (1/3) x 616 = **205.33 cm^2** (approx.)

---

## Combination of Figures

Many board exam problems involve finding areas of regions that combine sectors, segments, and triangles. The approach is:

1. **Break the figure** into recognizable shapes (sectors, triangles, rectangles)
2. **Calculate each area** separately
3. **Add or subtract** as needed

## Chapter Summary

1. **Length of an arc** of a sector with angle theta and radius r:
   = (theta/360) x 2 pi r

2. **Area of a sector** with angle theta and radius r:
   = (theta/360) x pi r^2

3. **Area of a segment** = Area of corresponding sector - Area of corresponding triangle

4. **Area of major sector** = pi r^2 - Area of minor sector

5. **Area of major segment** = pi r^2 - Area of minor segment
