## Sector and Segment

**Kiran:** Papa, I keep confusing sectors and segments. What is the clear difference?

**Ravi:** A sector is like a pizza slice — bounded by two radii and an arc. A segment is the region between a chord and its arc — no radii involved. Think of it this way: cut a circle with two radii and you get a sector. Cut a circle with a straight line (chord) and you get a segment.

**Kiran:** And the formulas use the angle theta at the centre?

**Ravi:** Yes. The fraction theta over 360 tells you what proportion of the full circle the sector represents. Area of a sector is theta over 360 times pi r squared. Arc length is theta over 360 times 2 pi r. These formulas are proportional reasoning — if a full circle is 360 degrees, then theta degrees is theta over 360 of the full circle.

**Kiran:** The minute hand of a clock sweeping for 5 minutes — that is a sector problem?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The minute hand completes 360 degrees in 60 minutes, so in 5 minutes it sweeps 30 degrees. The area swept is a sector with angle 30 degrees and radius equal to the length of the minute hand. ISRO's solar panels on satellites sweep similar arc calculations to maximise sunlight exposure.

## Area of a Segment

**Kiran:** Segment area equals sector area minus triangle area — that makes sense. But how do I find the triangle area?

**Ravi:** For common angles it is straightforward. If theta is 90 degrees, the triangle is right-angled with both legs equal to r, so its area is one-half r squared. If theta is 60 degrees, the triangle formed by the two radii and the chord is equilateral — because two sides are radii of equal length and the angle between them is 60 degrees, making the third side also equal. Its area is root 3 over 4 times r squared.

**Kiran:** What about other angles?

**Ravi:** Use the general formula: area of triangle equals one-half r squared sin theta. But in CBSE Class 10, the questions almost always use 60 or 90 degrees, so you can rely on the specific formulas. The general formula becomes important in Class 11 and JEE.

**Kiran:** And for the major segment, I just subtract the minor segment from the full circle area?

**Ravi:** Yes. Major segment area equals pi r squared minus minor segment area. Similarly, major sector area equals pi r squared minus minor sector area. Some questions ask specifically for the major region, so read carefully.

## Real-World Applications

**Kiran:** The horse tied to a corner of a field — that is a quarter-circle because the corner angle is 90 degrees?

**Ravi:** Yes. The horse can graze in a quarter circle with radius equal to the rope length. If the rope is 5 metres and the field corner is 90 degrees, the grazing area is one-quarter times pi times 25. If the question asks how much more area the horse can graze when the rope is increased to 10 metres, compute both quarter-circle areas and subtract.

**Kiran:** What about the car wiper problem?

**Ravi:** Each wiper sweeps a sector. The angle of sweep and the wiper length are given. Calculate the sector area for one wiper and multiply by two if both wipers are asked about. These application problems make the chapter feel practical and connected to daily life.

## Combination of Shapes

**Kiran:** The toughest problems seem to involve shaded regions where I need to add and subtract areas of different shapes.

**Ravi:** Yes, these combination problems are the most challenging and carry the most marks. The strategy is always the same — break the figure into recognisable shapes like sectors, triangles, rectangles, and semicircles. Calculate each area separately, then add or subtract as needed to get the shaded region.

**Kiran:** Any tips for setting up these problems?

**Ravi:** Draw the figure, identify each component shape, and write out the expression before calculating. For example, "shaded area equals area of semicircle minus area of triangle plus area of sector." Getting the expression right is half the battle. The calculation is just arithmetic after that.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** How much does this chapter weigh?

**Ravi:** Usually 3-5 marks. The questions are computational, so accuracy matters. Always use the value of pi specified in the question — 22 over 7 or 3.14 — and do not mix them up. Show all intermediate steps because CBSE awards step marks.

**Kiran:** Common mistakes?

**Ravi:** Four frequent errors. First, confusing sector and segment formulas. Second, using diameter instead of radius — always halve the diameter first. Third, computing the minor area when the question asks for the major area. Fourth, not converting units — if the answer is asked in square centimetres but the radius is in metres, convert first. Attention to detail wins marks here.

## Connecting to Engineering and Design

**Kiran:** Where are sector and segment calculations used in real life?

**Ravi:** Extensively. Civil engineers calculate the area of circular road curves. Architects design circular windows and arched doorways using sector geometry. The area swept by radar antennas is a sector. Even the design of the Indian Parliament's circular Central Hall involves these calculations.

**Kiran:** The formula for pi — 22 over 7 or 3.14 — is only an approximation. How accurate does pi need to be in practice?

**Ravi:** For CBSE, 22 over 7 or 3.14 is sufficient. But NASA uses 15 digits of pi for interplanetary navigation, and that is enough to calculate the circumference of the observable universe with an error smaller than a hydrogen atom. The Indian mathematician Ramanujan discovered remarkable infinite series for pi that converge extremely rapidly — his formulas are still used in modern computations of pi to trillions of digits.
