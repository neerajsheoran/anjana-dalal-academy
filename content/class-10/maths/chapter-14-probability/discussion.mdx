## Classical vs Experimental Probability

**Kiran:** Papa, in Class 9 we actually tossed coins and counted outcomes. Now we just calculate theoretically?

**Ravi:** Yes. Theoretical or classical probability uses logical reasoning rather than experiments. If a fair coin has two equally likely outcomes, the probability of heads is exactly one-half — you do not need to toss it 1000 times to figure this out. The theoretical approach gives precise answers, while experimental probability gives approximations that improve with more trials.

**Kiran:** But when we toss a coin many times, the experimental probability gets closer to one-half?

**Ravi:** Exactly. This is the Law of Large Numbers — as the number of trials increases, the experimental probability approaches the theoretical probability. Ramanujan and other Indian mathematicians contributed to the deeper mathematical foundations behind this.

## Counting Outcomes Correctly

**Kiran:** When tossing two coins, the outcomes are HH, HT, TH, TT — not just "2 heads, 1 of each, 2 tails." Why does this matter?

**Ravi:** Because HT and TH are different outcomes. The first coin showing heads and the second showing tails is a different physical result from the reverse. If you incorrectly count 3 outcomes instead of 4, every probability you calculate will be wrong. For two dice, the total is 6 times 6 equals 36 outcomes, not 21 or 11.

**Kiran:** For two dice, the pair 3,4 and the pair 4,3 are different?

**Ravi:** Yes. Think of one die as red and the other as blue. Red showing 3 and blue showing 4 is different from red showing 4 and blue showing 3. Always list outcomes as ordered pairs. The sum equals 7 has 6 favourable outcomes: 1 and 6, 2 and 5, 3 and 4, 4 and 3, 5 and 2, 6 and 1. Getting this count right is essential.

## Playing Cards

**Kiran:** The deck of 52 cards with 4 suits, 13 cards each — I need to memorise the structure?

**Ravi:** Yes. Two red suits — hearts and diamonds. Two black suits — spades and clubs. Each suit has ace through 10 plus three face cards: jack, queen, king. So there are 12 face cards total, 26 red cards, 26 black cards, 4 aces, and so on. Board exams love card probability questions because the numbers are well-defined.

**Kiran:** What about "a red king" — that means only hearts king and diamonds king, so 2 out of 52?

**Ravi:** Correct. Probability equals 2 over 52, which simplifies to 1 over 26. Always simplify your fraction. And "not a king" means 52 minus 4 equals 48 favourable outcomes, giving probability 48 over 52 equals 12 over 13.

**Kiran:** The complementary event approach is sometimes faster — P of not E equals 1 minus P of E?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. When the favourable outcomes are hard to count directly, count the unfavourable ones and subtract from 1. For "at least one head in two tosses," instead of listing HH, HT, TH, you can compute 1 minus P of no heads, which is 1 minus one-quarter equals three-quarters.

## Common Problem Types

**Kiran:** Bag problems with coloured balls, numbered discs from 1 to 90, defective items — these are all the same framework?

**Ravi:** Yes. Identify the total number of equally likely outcomes and the number favourable to the event. Divide. For a box with 3 blue, 2 white, and 4 red marbles, total is 9. P of blue equals 3 over 9 equals one-third. Always verify that P of blue plus P of white plus P of red equals 1.

**Kiran:** The numbered disc problems test divisibility concepts — like "what is the probability of drawing a number divisible by 5 from 1 to 90?"

**Ravi:** Right. Count the multiples of 5 between 1 and 90: those are 5, 10, 15, up to 90 — that is 18 numbers. Probability equals 18 over 90 equals one-fifth. Perfect squares, prime numbers, even numbers — all require careful counting. This is where number theory from Chapter 1 connects to probability.

## Board Exam Tips

**Kiran:** How much does this chapter carry?

**Ravi:** Usually 3-5 marks. One or two questions — often a dice problem, a card problem, or a real-life probability situation. Sometimes combined with the assertion-reason format in MCQs.

**Kiran:** Common mistakes?

**Ravi:** Four frequent errors. First, not listing all outcomes for multiple events — two coins have 4 outcomes, not 3. Second, confusing "at least one" with "exactly one." Third, computing a probability greater than 1 or less than 0 — if you get such a value, recheck immediately. Fourth, not simplifying the fraction — 4 over 52 should be written as 1 over 13. Show the total outcomes, the favourable outcomes, and the final simplified probability for full marks.

## Connecting Probability to Real Life

**Kiran:** Is probability used in real-world decisions?

**Ravi:** Everywhere. Weather forecasts use probability — "70 percent chance of rain" means that in similar atmospheric conditions, rain occurs 70 percent of the time. Insurance companies use probability to calculate premiums. Doctors use probability when telling patients about treatment success rates. Even cricket analysts calculate the probability of a team winning based on run rates and wickets.

**Kiran:** India's space missions must involve probability calculations too?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. ISRO engineers calculate the probability of system failures and build redundancies to reduce risk. The Mars Orbiter Mission had to account for probabilities of communication loss, trajectory errors, and engine failures. Every successful mission is a triumph of probability management.

**Kiran:** For JEE and competitive exams, does probability get much harder?

**Ravi:** Yes. JEE introduces conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, random variables, and probability distributions. But the foundation you build here — correctly counting outcomes, using complementary events, and understanding the probability range — remains essential throughout. A strong Class 10 base makes advanced probability much more accessible.
