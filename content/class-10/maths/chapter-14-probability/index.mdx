## Introduction

In Class 9, you studied **experimental (empirical) probability** -- you actually performed experiments (like tossing a coin 100 times) and calculated probability based on the results.

In this chapter, we study **theoretical (classical) probability** -- we calculate probability based on **logical reasoning**, without actually performing the experiment. This is the kind of probability used in board exams and competitive exams.

The study of probability began in the 16th century with the Italian mathematician **J. Cardan**, who wrote the first book on the subject. Later, **Pierre Simon Laplace** (1749-1827) made the greatest contribution to the theory.

---

## Basic Concepts

### Experiment and Outcomes

An **experiment** is an action that produces well-defined results called **outcomes**.

**Examples:**
- Tossing a coin: outcomes are Head (H) and Tail (T)
- Throwing a die: outcomes are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
- Drawing a card from a deck: outcomes are any of the 52 cards

### Equally Likely Outcomes

Outcomes are **equally likely** if each outcome has the same chance of occurring.

- A fair coin: Head and Tail are equally likely
- A fair die: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 are equally likely
- A bag with 4 red and 1 blue ball: drawing a red ball is MORE likely than blue -- these outcomes are **not** equally likely

> **Key idea:** The probability formulas in this chapter apply ONLY when all outcomes are equally likely.

### Event

An **event** is a collection of one or more outcomes.

- "Getting an even number on a die" is an event (outcomes 2, 4, 6)
- "Getting a head on a coin" is an event (outcome H)

An **elementary event** has only one outcome. The sum of probabilities of all elementary events is always **1**.

---

## The Probability Formula

The **theoretical probability** of an event E is:

**P(E) = Number of outcomes favourable to E / Total number of possible outcomes**

This assumes all outcomes are equally likely.

> **Key idea:** Probability is always a fraction -- favourable outcomes divided by total outcomes. It tells us how likely something is to happen.

---

## Important Rules of Probability

### Rule 1: Probability Range

**0 <= P(E) <= 1** for any event E.

- P(E) = 0 means the event is **impossible** (can never happen)
- P(E) = 1 means the event is **certain** (will definitely happen)
- Values between 0 and 1 represent varying degrees of likelihood

### Rule 2: Complementary Events

**P(E) + P(not E) = 1**

or equivalently: **P(not E) = 1 - P(E)**

"Not E" (written as E-bar) is called the **complement** of event E.

**Example:** If the probability of rain is 0.3, then the probability of no rain is 1 - 0.3 = **0.7**.

### Rule 3: Sum of All Elementary Events

The probabilities of all elementary events add up to **1**.

## Worked Examples: Coins

### Example 1: Tossing One Coin

Possible outcomes: {H, T}. Total = 2.

- P(Head) = 1/2
- P(Tail) = 1/2
- P(Head) + P(Tail) = 1. Verified!

### Example 2: Tossing Two Coins

Possible outcomes: {HH, HT, TH, TT}. Total = 4.

- P(at least one head) = 3/4 (favourable: HH, HT, TH)
- P(exactly one head) = 2/4 = 1/2 (favourable: HT, TH)
- P(no head) = 1/4 (favourable: TT)
- P(both heads) = 1/4 (favourable: HH)

### Example 3: Tossing Three Coins

Possible outcomes: {HHH, HHT, HTH, THH, HTT, THT, TTH, TTT}. Total = 8.

- P(all same result) = 2/8 = 1/4 (favourable: HHH, TTT)
- P(not all same) = 1 - 1/4 = **3/4**

## Worked Examples: Dice

### Single Die

Possible outcomes: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. Total = 6.

- P(getting 4) = 1/6
- P(getting an even number) = 3/6 = 1/2 (favourable: 2, 4, 6)
- P(getting a prime number) = 3/6 = 1/2 (favourable: 2, 3, 5)
- P(number greater than 4) = 2/6 = 1/3 (favourable: 5, 6)
- P(number less than 7) = 6/6 = **1** (certain event)
- P(getting 8) = 0/6 = **0** (impossible event)

### Two Dice

When two dice are thrown, each die can show 1-6. Total outcomes = 6 x 6 = **36**.

The 36 outcomes can be listed as ordered pairs (die1, die2):
(1,1), (1,2), ..., (1,6), (2,1), (2,2), ..., (6,6)

**Common questions:**

| Event | Favourable outcomes | Count | Probability |
|-------|-------------------|-------|-------------|
| Sum = 7 | (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1) | 6 | 6/36 = 1/6 |
| Sum = 8 | (2,6), (3,5), (4,4), (5,3), (6,2) | 5 | 5/36 |
| Sum = 2 | (1,1) | 1 | 1/36 |
| Sum = 12 | (6,6) | 1 | 1/36 |
| Sum = 13 | none | 0 | 0 (impossible) |
| Doublet (same number) | (1,1), (2,2), ..., (6,6) | 6 | 6/36 = 1/6 |

## Worked Examples: Playing Cards

A standard deck has **52 cards** divided into **4 suits** of 13 cards each:

- **Spades** (black) and **Clubs** (black)
- **Hearts** (red) and **Diamonds** (red)

Each suit has: Ace, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, Jack, Queen, King

**Face cards** = Jacks + Queens + Kings = 4 + 4 + 4 = **12** face cards

### Probability of Drawing One Card

| Event | Favourable | Probability |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| A king | 4 | 4/52 = 1/13 |
| Not a king | 48 | 48/52 = 12/13 |
| A red card | 26 | 26/52 = 1/2 |
| A face card | 12 | 12/52 = 3/13 |
| A red king | 2 | 2/52 = 1/26 |
| The queen of diamonds | 1 | 1/52 |
| A spade | 13 | 13/52 = 1/4 |
| An ace | 4 | 4/52 = 1/13 |
| A red face card | 6 | 6/52 = 3/26 |
| A black queen | 2 | 2/52 = 1/26 |

## Worked Examples: Balls and Marbles

### Example: Bag of Marbles

A box contains 3 blue, 2 white, and 4 red marbles. One is drawn at random.

Total = 3 + 2 + 4 = 9

- P(white) = 2/9
- P(blue) = 3/9 = 1/3
- P(red) = 4/9

Check: 2/9 + 3/9 + 4/9 = 9/9 = 1. Verified!

### Example: Defective Pens

12 defective pens are mixed with 132 good ones. One pen is drawn at random.

Total = 12 + 132 = 144

- P(good pen) = 132/144 = **11/12**
- P(defective pen) = 12/144 = 1/12

## Worked Examples: Real-Life Situations

### Birthday Problem

Two friends -- what is the probability they have different birthdays?

Assuming 365 days (ignoring leap years):
- Total possibilities for second person's birthday = 365
- Favourable outcomes for different birthday = 364

P(different birthdays) = 364/365
P(same birthday) = 1 - 364/365 = **1/365**

### Defective Bulbs with Replacement Removed

A lot has 20 bulbs, 4 are defective.

First draw: P(defective) = 4/20 = 1/5

If the first bulb drawn is NOT defective and is NOT replaced:
- Remaining bulbs = 19 (4 defective, 15 good)
- P(second bulb is good) = 15/19

### Complementary Events in Games

Sangeeta and Reshma play tennis. P(Sangeeta wins) = 0.62.

P(Reshma wins) = 1 - 0.62 = **0.38**

(Since one of them must win, these are complementary events.)

## Numbers from a Disc/Box

A box contains discs numbered 1 to 90. One is drawn at random.

- P(two-digit number) = 81/90 = 9/10 (numbers 10 to 90 = 81 numbers)
- P(perfect square) = 9/90 = 1/10 (squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81 = 9 numbers)
- P(divisible by 5) = 18/90 = 1/5 (numbers: 5, 10, 15, ..., 90 = 18 numbers)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not listing all outcomes correctly.** For two coins, the outcomes are HH, HT, TH, TT (4 outcomes), NOT "2 heads, 2 tails, 1 of each" (3 outcomes). HT and TH are different!

2. **Confusing "at least one" with "exactly one."** "At least one head" means 1 or more heads, which includes HH, HT, TH. "Exactly one head" means only HT and TH.

3. **Forgetting that probability cannot be negative or greater than 1.** If you get such a value, recheck your calculation.

4. **Not checking if outcomes are equally likely.** The formula only works when all outcomes have the same chance.

## Chapter Summary

1. **Theoretical probability** of event E:
   P(E) = (Number of favourable outcomes) / (Total number of equally likely outcomes)

2. The probability of a **sure event** (certain event) is **1**.

3. The probability of an **impossible event** is **0**.

4. For any event E: **0 <= P(E) <= 1**

5. An **elementary event** has exactly one outcome. The sum of probabilities of all elementary events equals 1.

6. **Complementary events:** P(E) + P(not E) = 1, i.e., **P(not E) = 1 - P(E)**

7. Important total outcomes to remember:
   - 1 coin: 2 outcomes
   - 2 coins: 4 outcomes
   - 3 coins: 8 outcomes
   - 1 die: 6 outcomes
   - 2 dice: 36 outcomes
   - 1 card from deck: 52 outcomes
