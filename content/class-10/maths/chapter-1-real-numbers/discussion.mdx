## The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

**Kiran:** Papa, we already did prime factorisation in earlier classes. Why is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic treated as such a big deal in Class 10?

**Ravi:** Because earlier you just learnt the technique — break a number into primes. Now the theorem says something deeper: every composite number has exactly one unique prime factorisation, regardless of the order. That uniqueness is what makes number theory work.

**Kiran:** How does uniqueness matter practically?

**Ravi:** Think about it this way — modern encryption systems like RSA, which protect every UPI payment you make, rely on the fact that multiplying two large primes is easy, but finding those primes from the product is extremely hard. If factorisation were not unique, the entire security system would collapse. Ramanujan's work on number partitions also builds on this foundation.

**Kiran:** So this theorem is actually behind digital security?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The entire field of cryptography rests on number theory. When you send a message through WhatsApp, the encryption uses prime numbers. Indian mathematicians at ISI Kolkata and IIT research centres contribute to cryptographic algorithms built on these very foundations.

**Kiran:** How is this theorem tested in boards?

**Ravi:** It is a 1-mark theory question that appears almost every year. State the theorem precisely — "Every composite number can be expressed as a product of primes, and this factorisation is unique, apart from the order of factors."

## HCF and LCM by Prime Factorisation

**Kiran:** I get confused about when to pick the smallest power and when to pick the greatest power.

**Ravi:** Here is a simple way to remember — HCF is about what numbers share in common, so you take the smallest power of only the common primes. LCM is about covering everything, so you take the greatest power of all primes from both numbers.

**Kiran:** And the relationship HCF times LCM equals the product of the two numbers — does that always work?

**Ravi:** Only for two numbers. For three numbers, HCF times LCM does not equal the product. This is a common trap in board exams — students assume the formula works universally, but it fails for three or more numbers. Also, the verification step — always check HCF times LCM equals a times b — is a great way to confirm your answer and earn full marks in the CBSE marking scheme.

**Kiran:** What about those word problems where two people go around a circular track?

**Ravi:** Those are LCM problems. When Sonia takes 18 minutes and Ravi takes 12 minutes per round, they meet again at the start after LCM of 18 and 12, which is 36 minutes. ISRO uses similar calculations for satellite orbital periods.

## Proving Irrationality

**Kiran:** The proof that root 2 is irrational uses contradiction. I understand the steps, but how do I write it perfectly in the exam?

**Ravi:** This proof carries 3-4 marks and appears in almost every board exam. Follow this structure precisely. First, assume root 2 is rational, so root 2 equals a over b where a and b are coprime. Second, square both sides to get a squared equals 2b squared. Third, conclude 2 divides a, so write a equals 2c. Fourth, substitute back to get b squared equals 2c squared, meaning 2 divides b as well. Fifth, state the contradiction — both a and b are divisible by 2, but we assumed they were coprime.

**Kiran:** And for root 3, we just replace 2 with 3 everywhere? Or root 5 with 5?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The structure is identical for any prime p. The key theorem you cite is Theorem 1.2 — if a prime p divides a squared, then p divides a. Many students lose marks by not stating this theorem explicitly. Write it out clearly in your answer.

## Irrationality of Expressions

**Kiran:** How do I prove that something like 5 minus root 3 is irrational?

**Ravi:** This is a shorter proof, usually 2 marks. Assume 5 minus root 3 is rational, say equal to a over b. Then root 3 equals 5 minus a over b, which equals a rational number. But we have already proved root 3 is irrational — contradiction. Done.

**Kiran:** So I need to first establish that root 3 is irrational, then use that result?

**Ravi:** Exactly. In the board exam, if the question only asks you to prove 5 minus root 3 is irrational, you can write "Since root 3 is irrational (proved earlier)" and proceed. But if the question says "prove that root 3 is irrational, and hence prove 5 minus root 3 is irrational," then you must write both proofs in full. Read the question carefully — CBSE marking schemes penalise incomplete reasoning.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What are the most common mistakes students make in this chapter?

**Ravi:** Three big ones. First, forgetting to state that a and b are coprime in irrationality proofs — this is essential for the contradiction. Second, confusing HCF and LCM formulas — smallest common powers for HCF, greatest for LCM. Third, applying the product formula HCF times LCM equals a times b to three numbers, where it does not hold.

**Kiran:** How much weightage does this chapter carry?

**Ravi:** Typically 3-5 marks. You can expect one proof question on irrationality and one HCF-LCM problem. Master these two types and this chapter becomes a guaranteed scoring area.

**Kiran:** Does this chapter connect to any competitive exam topics?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Number theory — the study of properties of integers — is one of the most important areas in mathematics. The Fundamental Theorem and Euclid's Division Algorithm are the building blocks for modular arithmetic, which appears in JEE and Olympiad problems. Ramanujan's extraordinary work on partitions and prime numbers all traces back to these foundations. He once said, "An equation means nothing to me unless it expresses a thought of God." Behind every formula here lies a deep structure of numbers waiting to be explored.
