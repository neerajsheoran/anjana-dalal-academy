## The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

You already know that any natural number can be written as a product of its prime factors. For example:

- 2 = 2
- 4 = 2 x 2
- 253 = 11 x 23

Now think about it the other way: if you take any collection of prime numbers and multiply them (allowing repetition), you can produce a huge collection of positive integers. The big question is -- can **every** composite number be produced this way?

The answer is **yes**, and this is precisely what the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states.

> **Key idea:** Every composite number can be expressed (factorised) as a product of primes, and this factorisation is **unique**, apart from the order in which the prime factors occur.

### What Does "Unique" Mean Here?

It means that for any composite number, there is **one and only one** way to write it as a product of primes (if we ignore the order). For example:

- 32760 = 2 x 2 x 2 x 3 x 3 x 5 x 7 x 13 = 2^3 x 3^2 x 5 x 7 x 13

Whether you write it as 2 x 3 x 5 x 7 x 13 x 2 x 3 x 2 or in ascending order, the prime factors and their powers remain the same.

### How to Find Prime Factorisation

Use the **factor tree method**:

1. Start with the number
2. Break it into any two factors
3. Keep breaking each factor until you reach only prime numbers
4. Write the number as a product of all the prime factors

**Example:** Find the prime factorisation of 140.

![Factor tree showing 140 broken down step by step into prime factors 2, 2, 5, and 7](factor-tree-140.png)

- 140 = 2 x 70
- 70 = 2 x 35
- 35 = 5 x 7
- So, **140 = 2 x 2 x 5 x 7 = 2^2 x 5 x 7**

## HCF and LCM Using Prime Factorisation

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic gives us a neat method to find the **HCF** (Highest Common Factor) and **LCM** (Least Common Multiple) of two or more numbers.

### Rules

![Venn diagram showing prime factors of two numbers with common factors in the overlap region for HCF and all factors for LCM](hcf-lcm-venn-diagram.png)

- **HCF** = Product of the **smallest power** of each **common** prime factor
- **LCM** = Product of the **greatest power** of each prime factor involved in the numbers

### Worked Example 1: Find HCF and LCM of 6 and 20

**Step 1:** Prime factorise both numbers.
- 6 = 2^1 x 3^1
- 20 = 2^2 x 5^1

**Step 2:** Find HCF -- take the smallest power of common primes.
- Common prime: 2
- HCF = 2^1 = **2**

**Step 3:** Find LCM -- take the greatest power of all primes.
- LCM = 2^2 x 3^1 x 5^1 = 4 x 3 x 5 = **60**

**Verification:** HCF x LCM = 2 x 60 = 120 = 6 x 20. Verified!

> **Key idea:** For any two positive integers a and b: **HCF(a, b) x LCM(a, b) = a x b**. This relationship is very useful -- if you know one, you can find the other!

### Worked Example 2: Find HCF of 96 and 404, then find their LCM

**Step 1:** Prime factorise.
- 96 = 2^5 x 3
- 404 = 2^2 x 101

**Step 2:** HCF = 2^2 = **4**

**Step 3:** LCM = (96 x 404) / HCF = 38784 / 4 = **9696**

### Worked Example 3: Find HCF and LCM of 6, 72, and 120

**Step 1:** Prime factorise.
- 6 = 2 x 3
- 72 = 2^3 x 3^2
- 120 = 2^3 x 3 x 5

**Step 2:** HCF = 2^1 x 3^1 = **6** (smallest powers of common factors 2 and 3)

**Step 3:** LCM = 2^3 x 3^2 x 5^1 = 8 x 9 x 5 = **360** (greatest powers of all prime factors)

> **Key idea:** For **three** numbers, the formula HCF x LCM = product of the three numbers does **NOT** hold. It only works for two numbers.

## Applications of the Fundamental Theorem

### Can 4^n End with the Digit 0?

Consider the numbers 4^n, where n is a natural number. If 4^n ends with the digit 0, it must be divisible by 5. But:

- 4^n = (2^2)^n = 2^(2n)

The only prime factor is 2. There is **no 5** in the factorisation. By the uniqueness of the Fundamental Theorem, no other prime can appear.

**Therefore, 4^n can never end with the digit zero.**

### Can 6^n End with the Digit 0?

Similarly, 6^n = (2 x 3)^n = 2^n x 3^n. Again, no factor of 5 is present, so **6^n also cannot end with digit zero**.

### Showing Numbers Are Composite

**Example:** Show that 7 x 11 x 13 + 13 is a composite number.

- 7 x 11 x 13 + 13 = 13(7 x 11 + 1) = 13 x 78 = 13 x 2 x 3 x 13

Since this number has factors other than 1 and itself, it is **composite**.

### Circular Path Problem

**Problem:** Sonia takes 18 minutes and Ravi takes 12 minutes to complete one round of a circular sports field. If they start together from the same point and go in the same direction, after how many minutes will they meet again at the starting point?

**Solution:** They will meet at the starting point after **LCM(18, 12)** minutes.

- 18 = 2 x 3^2
- 12 = 2^2 x 3
- LCM = 2^2 x 3^2 = 4 x 9 = **36 minutes**

## Revisiting Irrational Numbers

Recall from Class 9 that a number is called **irrational** if it cannot be written in the form p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not zero.

Some examples of irrational numbers: root 2, root 3, root 15, pi

### Important Theorem

**Theorem 1.2:** Let p be a prime number. If p divides a^2, then p divides a, where a is a positive integer.

This theorem is based on the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic and is used to prove that certain numbers are irrational.

---

## Proving Root 2 Is Irrational

We use a method called **proof by contradiction**.

![Flowchart showing the steps of proof by contradiction for proving root 2 is irrational](proof-by-contradiction-root2-flowchart.png)

**Step 1:** Assume, to the contrary, that root 2 is rational.

**Step 2:** Then we can write root 2 = a/b, where a and b are coprime integers (no common factor other than 1) and b is not zero.

**Step 3:** Squaring both sides: 2 = a^2/b^2, so **a^2 = 2b^2**.

**Step 4:** This means 2 divides a^2. By Theorem 1.2, **2 divides a**. So we can write a = 2c for some integer c.

**Step 5:** Substituting: 2b^2 = (2c)^2 = 4c^2, so **b^2 = 2c^2**.

**Step 6:** This means 2 divides b^2, so **2 divides b**.

**Step 7:** But now both a and b are divisible by 2. This **contradicts** our assumption that a and b are coprime.

**Conclusion:** Our assumption was wrong. Therefore, **root 2 is irrational**.

---

## Proving Root 3 Is Irrational

The proof follows the same structure.

**Step 1:** Assume root 3 is rational. Then root 3 = a/b where a and b are coprime.

**Step 2:** Squaring: 3b^2 = a^2. So 3 divides a^2, which means 3 divides a.

**Step 3:** Let a = 3c. Then 3b^2 = 9c^2, giving b^2 = 3c^2. So 3 divides b.

**Step 4:** Both a and b are divisible by 3 -- contradiction with coprimality.

**Conclusion: root 3 is irrational.**

> **Key idea:** To prove root p is irrational (where p is prime), assume it is rational, derive that p divides both numerator and denominator, and reach a contradiction.

## Irrationality of Expressions Involving Irrational Numbers

### Important Results

- The **sum or difference** of a rational and an irrational number is **irrational**.
- The **product or quotient** of a non-zero rational and an irrational number is **irrational**.

### Worked Example: Prove 5 - root 3 is irrational

**Step 1:** Assume 5 - root 3 is rational. Then 5 - root 3 = a/b for some coprime integers a, b.

**Step 2:** Rearranging: root 3 = 5 - a/b = (5b - a)/b.

**Step 3:** Since a and b are integers, (5b - a)/b is rational. So root 3 would be rational.

**Step 4:** But we proved root 3 is irrational -- **contradiction!**

**Conclusion: 5 - root 3 is irrational.**

### Worked Example: Prove 3 root 2 is irrational

**Step 1:** Assume 3 root 2 is rational. Then 3 root 2 = a/b for coprime integers a, b.

**Step 2:** Rearranging: root 2 = a/(3b).

**Step 3:** Since a, b, and 3 are integers, a/(3b) is rational. So root 2 would be rational.

**Step 4:** But root 2 is irrational -- **contradiction!**

**Conclusion: 3 root 2 is irrational.**

## Chapter Summary

1. **The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic:** Every composite number can be expressed as a product of primes, and this factorisation is unique (apart from the order of factors).

2. **Theorem 1.2:** If p is a prime and p divides a^2, then p divides a (where a is a positive integer).

3. **Irrationality proofs:** Using the Fundamental Theorem and proof by contradiction, we can prove that root 2, root 3, root 5, and similar numbers are irrational.

4. **HCF and LCM by prime factorisation:**
   - HCF = product of smallest powers of common prime factors
   - LCM = product of greatest powers of all prime factors
   - For two numbers a and b: HCF(a, b) x LCM(a, b) = a x b

5. **Key relationships for three numbers p, q, r:**
   - LCM(p, q, r) x HCF(p, q, r) is NOT equal to p x q x r
   - Special formulas exist involving pairwise HCFs and LCMs
