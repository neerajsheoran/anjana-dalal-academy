## Graphical Interpretation

**Kiran:** Papa, how does drawing two lines on a graph tell us whether a system of equations has a solution?

**Ravi:** Every linear equation in two variables represents a straight line. When you have two such equations, you have two lines. If they intersect at one point, that point's coordinates give you the unique solution. If they are the same line, every point on that line is a solution — infinitely many. If they are parallel, they never meet — no solution.

**Kiran:** So "consistent" means the lines meet somewhere?

**Ravi:** Yes. Consistent means at least one solution exists — either unique or infinitely many. Inconsistent means no solution — parallel lines. The ratio test using a1 over a2, b1 over b2, and c1 over c2 lets you determine this without drawing any graph at all.

**Kiran:** That ratio test seems really useful for objective questions.

**Ravi:** Absolutely. In CBSE board MCQs and assertion-reason questions, this test is a quick way to classify the system. If a1 over a2 is not equal to b1 over b2, the lines intersect. If all three ratios are equal, the lines coincide. If the first two ratios are equal but not the third, the lines are parallel.

## Substitution Method

**Kiran:** Between substitution and elimination, which one should I prefer in the exam?

**Ravi:** It depends on the equations. Substitution works best when one variable can be easily expressed in terms of the other — for instance, if one equation is already solved for x, like x equals 3 minus 2y. Just plug that into the second equation and solve.

**Kiran:** What happens if after substitution I get something like 0 equals 0?

**Ravi:** That means the two equations are actually the same line — infinitely many solutions. If you get something impossible like minus 4 equals 0, then there is no solution. These special cases are important for board exams. Write them as conclusions clearly — "The system has infinitely many solutions" or "The system is inconsistent."

**Kiran:** The age problems and digit problems always confuse me. How do I set up the equations?

**Ravi:** The trick is careful translation from English to algebra. For age problems, define variables as present ages and use phrases like "k years ago" by subtracting k. For digit problems, if the tens digit is x and units digit is y, the number is 10x plus y, and the reversed number is 10y plus x. Write each sentence as one equation. Indian civil services exams use similar logical reasoning problems.

## Elimination Method

**Kiran:** In elimination, how do I decide which variable to eliminate?

**Ravi:** Look at the coefficients. If one variable already has the same or easily matchable coefficients in both equations, eliminate that one. For example, if one equation has 3x and the other has 6x, multiply the first by 2 and subtract. Minimise the arithmetic.

**Kiran:** The income-expenditure problem in the textbook where each person saves Rs 2000 — that was a neat application.

**Ravi:** Yes, and such ratio-based problems are favourites in CBSE boards. The key is setting up the equations correctly using the given ratios. If incomes are in ratio 9 to 7, write them as 9x and 7x, not 9 and 7. Similarly for expenditures. The saving condition gives you two equations in two unknowns.

## Equations Reducible to Linear Form

**Kiran:** Some questions have 1 over x and 1 over y in them. How are those linear?

**Ravi:** They are not linear in x and y, but if you substitute u equals 1 over x and v equals 1 over y, they become linear in u and v. Solve for u and v first, then convert back. This substitution technique appears in JEE as well, so learning it thoroughly now gives you a head start.

**Kiran:** Are there other substitutions I should watch for?

**Ravi:** Yes. Sometimes the equations involve expressions like 1 over the quantity x plus y or 1 over the quantity x minus y. Substitute those entire expressions as new variables. The CBSE board has asked such problems in previous years — 2019 and 2023 both had questions of this type.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What is the typical marks distribution for this chapter?

**Ravi:** Usually 3-5 marks. You can expect either a word problem requiring you to form and solve equations, or a graphical question asking you to plot two lines and find the solution. For graphical questions, use a sharp pencil, take an appropriate scale, and plot at least three points per line for accuracy.

**Kiran:** Any common mistakes to watch for?

**Ravi:** Three frequent errors. First, sign mistakes during elimination — be very careful when subtracting equations. Second, forgetting to verify the solution by substituting back into both original equations — this verification step earns marks. Third, in word problems, not stating what your variables represent at the start. Always write "Let x equal..." and "Let y equal..." before forming equations. CBSE examiners look for this.

## Connecting to Higher Mathematics

**Kiran:** Systems of equations seem very fundamental. Do they become more complex later?

**Ravi:** In Class 12, you will solve systems of three equations in three variables using matrices and determinants. In engineering, systems of hundreds or thousands of equations are solved using computers — this is called linear algebra, and it powers everything from Google's search engine to structural analysis of bridges. The two-variable system you master here is the foundation for all of that.

**Kiran:** India's IT industry must use these methods extensively?

**Ravi:** Daily. Machine learning algorithms at companies like TCS, Infosys, and Indian startups solve massive systems of linear equations to train models that recognise speech, translate languages, and recommend products. The abstract skills of setting up and solving equations translate directly into technological capability. Master the fundamentals now and the applications will follow naturally.
