## Introduction

Imagine this: Akhila goes to a village fair. She wants to ride the Giant Wheel and play Hoopla. The number of times she played Hoopla is half the number of rides on the Giant Wheel. Each ride costs Rs 3 and each game of Hoopla costs Rs 4. She spent Rs 20 in total. How do you find out how many rides she took and how many games she played?

You can set up two equations:
- y = x/2 (Hoopla games are half the rides)
- 3x + 4y = 20 (total cost)

This is a **pair of linear equations in two variables**. In this chapter, we learn how to solve such pairs using graphical and algebraic methods.

### What Is a Pair of Linear Equations?

The general form of a pair of linear equations in two variables x and y is:

- **a1x + b1y + c1 = 0**
- **a2x + b2y + c2 = 0**

where a1, a2, b1, b2, c1, c2 are real numbers such that a1^2 + b1^2 is not zero and a2^2 + b2^2 is not zero.

A **solution** of such a pair is a pair of values (x, y) that satisfies both equations simultaneously.

---

## Graphical Method of Solution

When you draw the graphs of two linear equations on the same coordinate plane, each equation represents a **straight line**. Three cases can arise:

![Three cases of pair of linear equations: intersecting lines (one solution), coincident lines (infinite solutions), and parallel lines (no solution)](linear-equations-three-cases-graph.png)

### Case 1: The Lines Intersect at One Point
- The pair has a **unique solution** (the coordinates of the intersection point)
- The pair is called **consistent**

### Case 2: The Lines Coincide (are the same line)
- The pair has **infinitely many solutions**
- Every point on the line is a solution
- The pair is called **dependent** (and consistent)

### Case 3: The Lines Are Parallel
- The pair has **no solution**
- The pair is called **inconsistent**

> **Key idea:** The nature of the solution depends entirely on how the two lines relate to each other -- do they cross, overlap, or run parallel?

---

## Comparing Ratios to Determine the Type of Solution

For the pair of equations a1x + b1y + c1 = 0 and a2x + b2y + c2 = 0, you can determine the type of solution **without drawing graphs** by comparing the ratios of coefficients:

| Condition | Lines | Solution |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| a1/a2 is not equal to b1/b2 | Intersecting | Unique solution (consistent) |
| a1/a2 = b1/b2 = c1/c2 | Coincident | Infinitely many solutions (dependent, consistent) |
| a1/a2 = b1/b2 but not equal to c1/c2 | Parallel | No solution (inconsistent) |

### Worked Example

Check whether the following pairs of equations are consistent, inconsistent, or dependent:

**(i) x - 2y = 0 and 3x + 4y - 20 = 0**

- a1/a2 = 1/3, b1/b2 = -2/4 = -1/2
- Since 1/3 is not equal to -1/2, the lines **intersect** -- unique solution (consistent)

**(ii) 2x + 3y - 9 = 0 and 4x + 6y - 18 = 0**

- a1/a2 = 2/4 = 1/2, b1/b2 = 3/6 = 1/2, c1/c2 = -9/-18 = 1/2
- Since all ratios are equal, the lines **coincide** -- infinitely many solutions (dependent)

**(iii) x + 2y - 4 = 0 and 2x + 4y - 12 = 0**

- a1/a2 = 1/2, b1/b2 = 2/4 = 1/2, c1/c2 = -4/-12 = 1/3
- Since a1/a2 = b1/b2 but not equal to c1/c2, the lines are **parallel** -- no solution (inconsistent)

## Solving Graphically -- Step by Step

To solve a pair of linear equations graphically:

1. **Find at least two solutions** (points) for each equation by substituting values of x
2. **Plot these points** on graph paper
3. **Draw straight lines** through the points for each equation
4. **Read the coordinates** of the point of intersection -- that is your solution

### Worked Example

Solve graphically: x + 3y = 6 and 2x - 3y = 12

For x + 3y = 6: when x = 0, y = 2; when x = 6, y = 0

For 2x - 3y = 12: when x = 0, y = -4; when x = 3, y = -2

Plotting these points and drawing the lines, they intersect at **(6, 0)**.

![Graph showing two straight lines x + 3y = 6 and 2x - 3y = 12 intersecting at the point (6, 0)](graphical-solution-intersection-point.png)

So the solution is **x = 6, y = 0**.

## Algebraic Methods of Solving

The graphical method is not always practical, especially when the solution involves fractions or decimals. Algebraic methods are more accurate. We study two main methods:

1. **Substitution Method**
2. **Elimination Method**

---

## Substitution Method

### Steps

**Step 1:** From either equation, express one variable in terms of the other (pick the simpler equation).

**Step 2:** Substitute this expression into the other equation. This gives a single equation in one variable.

**Step 3:** Solve this equation to find the value of that variable.

**Step 4:** Substitute back to find the value of the other variable.

### Worked Example 1

Solve: 7x - 15y = 2 ... (1) and x + 2y = 3 ... (2)

**Step 1:** From equation (2): x = 3 - 2y

**Step 2:** Substitute in equation (1):
7(3 - 2y) - 15y = 2
21 - 14y - 15y = 2
-29y = -19
**y = 19/29**

**Step 3:** Substitute back:
x = 3 - 2(19/29) = 3 - 38/29 = (87 - 38)/29 = **49/29**

**Solution: x = 49/29, y = 19/29**

### Worked Example 2: Age Problem

Aftab tells his daughter, "Seven years ago, I was seven times as old as you were then. Also, three years from now, I shall be three times as old as you will be." Find their present ages.

Let Aftab's age = s years, daughter's age = t years.

**Equation 1:** s - 7 = 7(t - 7), i.e., s - 7t + 42 = 0
**Equation 2:** s + 3 = 3(t + 3), i.e., s - 3t = 6

From equation (2): s = 3t + 6

Substituting in equation (1): (3t + 6) - 7t + 42 = 0
-4t + 48 = 0, so **t = 12**

Then s = 3(12) + 6 = **42**

**Aftab is 42 years old and his daughter is 12 years old.**

### Special Cases in Substitution

**Case 1: Infinitely many solutions**

If after substitution you get a **true statement** like 18 = 18 (with no variable), the equations are dependent and have infinitely many solutions.

**Example:** 2x + 3y = 9 and 4x + 6y = 18. The second equation is just double the first. After substitution, you get 18 = 18, which is always true.

**Case 2: No solution**

If after substitution you get a **false statement** like -4 = 0, the equations are inconsistent and have no solution.

**Example:** x + 2y - 4 = 0 and 2x + 4y - 12 = 0. After substitution, you get 8 - 12 = 0, i.e., -4 = 0, which is false.

## Elimination Method

In this method, we eliminate one variable by making its coefficients equal in both equations, then adding or subtracting.

### Steps

**Step 1:** Multiply one or both equations by suitable numbers to make the coefficients of one variable (x or y) **numerically equal**.

**Step 2:** **Add or subtract** the equations to eliminate that variable.

**Step 3:** Solve the resulting equation in one variable.

**Step 4:** Substitute back to find the other variable.

### Worked Example 1: Income and Expenditure

The ratio of incomes of two persons is 9:7 and the ratio of their expenditures is 4:3. Each saves Rs 2000 per month. Find their monthly incomes.

Let incomes be Rs 9x and Rs 7x, expenditures be Rs 4y and Rs 3y.

**Equation 1:** 9x - 4y = 2000
**Equation 2:** 7x - 3y = 2000

**Step 1:** Multiply equation (1) by 3 and equation (2) by 4:
- 27x - 12y = 6000 ... (3)
- 28x - 12y = 8000 ... (4)

**Step 2:** Subtract (3) from (4):
28x - 27x = 8000 - 6000
**x = 2000**

**Step 3:** Substitute in equation (1):
9(2000) - 4y = 2000
18000 - 4y = 2000
4y = 16000
**y = 4000**

**Monthly incomes: Rs 18,000 and Rs 14,000**

**Verification:** 18000:14000 = 9:7. Expenditures: 16000:12000 = 4:3. Each saves Rs 2000. Correct!

### Worked Example 2: Reversing Digits

The sum of a two-digit number and the number obtained by reversing the digits is 66. If the digits differ by 2, find the number.

Let the tens digit = x, units digit = y.

Original number = 10x + y. Reversed number = 10y + x.

**Equation 1:** (10x + y) + (10y + x) = 66, which gives 11(x + y) = 66, so **x + y = 6**

**Equation 2:** x - y = 2 (or y - x = 2)

**Case 1:** x + y = 6 and x - y = 2
Adding: 2x = 8, so x = 4, y = 2. Number = **42**

**Case 2:** x + y = 6 and y - x = 2
Adding: 2y = 8, so y = 4, x = 2. Number = **24**

Both **42 and 24** are valid answers. Verification: 42 + 24 = 66 and 4 - 2 = 2.

## Word Problems -- Setting Up Equations

Here are some common types of word problems and how to set them up:

### Type 1: Age Problems
- "A was n times as old as B, k years ago" becomes: (A - k) = n(B - k)

### Type 2: Number Problems
- Two-digit number with tens digit x and units digit y = 10x + y
- Reversing the digits gives: 10y + x

### Type 3: Cost Problems
- "m items of type A and n items of type B cost Rs P" becomes: ma + nb = P

### Type 4: Fraction Problems
- If the fraction is x/y, conditions like "adding 2 to numerator and denominator gives 9/11" become: (x + 2)/(y + 2) = 9/11

### Type 5: Speed, Distance, Time Problems
- Use the formula: Distance = Speed x Time
- Upstream speed = boat speed - stream speed
- Downstream speed = boat speed + stream speed

> **Key idea:** The trick to solving word problems is translating the English sentences into mathematical equations. Read the problem carefully, define your variables, and set up the equations step by step.

## Equations Reducible to Linear Form

Some equations are not linear at first glance but can be **transformed** into linear equations.

**Example:** Solve 2/x + 3/y = 13 and 5/x - 4/y = -2

These are not linear in x and y. But if we substitute **u = 1/x** and **v = 1/y**, we get:

- 2u + 3v = 13
- 5u - 4v = -2

Now solve using elimination or substitution to find u and v, then convert back to x and y.

## Chapter Summary

1. A pair of linear equations in two variables can be solved by:
   - **Graphical method** (plotting lines and finding the intersection)
   - **Substitution method** (express one variable in terms of the other)
   - **Elimination method** (make coefficients equal and add/subtract)

2. **Graphically**, two linear equations can represent:
   - **Intersecting lines** -- unique solution (consistent)
   - **Coincident lines** -- infinitely many solutions (dependent, consistent)
   - **Parallel lines** -- no solution (inconsistent)

3. **Quick test using ratios** for a1x + b1y + c1 = 0 and a2x + b2y + c2 = 0:
   - a1/a2 is not equal to b1/b2 -- unique solution
   - a1/a2 = b1/b2 = c1/c2 -- infinitely many solutions
   - a1/a2 = b1/b2 but not equal to c1/c2 -- no solution

4. Many real-world problems involving ages, costs, digits, speeds, and fractions can be modelled as pairs of linear equations and solved using these methods.
