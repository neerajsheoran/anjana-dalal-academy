## Similar vs Congruent Figures

**Kiran:** Papa, congruent figures are the same shape and size, and similar figures are the same shape but possibly different sizes. So all congruent figures are similar, but not the other way around?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Similarity is a broader concept. Every passport photo is congruent — same size, same image. But a passport photo and a poster of the same picture are similar — same shape, different sizes. Architects use similarity when they make scale models of buildings. A 1:100 model of a bridge is similar to the actual bridge.

**Kiran:** For polygons, we need both equal angles and proportional sides. But for triangles, just one condition is enough?

**Ravi:** Yes, and this is what makes triangles special. For general polygons, a rectangle and a square both have 90-degree angles but are not similar because the sides are not proportional. But for triangles, if the angles match, the sides automatically become proportional. This is the AA criterion.

## Basic Proportionality Theorem

**Kiran:** BPT says that a line parallel to one side of a triangle divides the other two sides in the same ratio. How often does the proof come in boards?

**Ravi:** The proof is a standard 4-5 mark question. Learn it thoroughly — draw the figure, construct perpendiculars, use the equal-area argument, and conclude. The converse is equally important: if a line divides two sides proportionally, it is parallel to the third side.

**Kiran:** The proof uses the fact that triangles with the same base between the same parallels have equal areas. That connects back to Class 9 geometry.

**Ravi:** Good observation. Mathematics builds layer upon layer. BPT is attributed to Thales, the Greek mathematician, but Indian texts also contain similar proportionality results. In engineering, this theorem is used in computer graphics for texture mapping — when a 3D image is projected onto a 2D screen, proportional division is everywhere.

**Kiran:** What are the typical numerical problems based on BPT?

**Ravi:** Given that DE is parallel to BC in triangle ABC with specific lengths for AD, DB, and AE, find EC. Apply AD over DB equals AE over EC directly. Sometimes the problem gives you the full sides AB and AC instead, so compute AD and AE carefully.

## Criteria for Similarity

**Kiran:** AA, SSS, SAS — which one is used most often in problems?

**Ravi:** AA is by far the most commonly used, because showing two angles equal is usually the easiest. Remember, since angles in a triangle sum to 180 degrees, showing two pairs of equal angles automatically ensures the third pair is also equal. So AAA reduces to AA.

**Kiran:** For SSS similarity, I just check if the three ratios of corresponding sides are equal?

**Ravi:** Yes. And for SAS, you need one pair of equal angles with the including sides proportional. The order of vertices in the similarity statement matters — triangle ABC similar to triangle PQR means A corresponds to P, B to Q, C to R. Getting the correspondence wrong is a common exam error.

**Kiran:** The shadow problems are interesting — the lamp, the girl, and her shadow form similar triangles.

**Ravi:** Those are classic applications. The sun's rays are effectively parallel, so the triangles formed by any two objects and their shadows are similar. Eratosthenes used this very principle to estimate the circumference of the Earth over 2000 years ago.

## Areas of Similar Triangles and Pythagoras Theorem

**Kiran:** The ratio of areas equals the square of the ratio of sides — this seems important.

**Ravi:** Extremely important. If two similar triangles have sides in ratio 2 to 3, their areas are in ratio 4 to 9. This extends to altitudes, medians, and angle bisectors — the area ratio equals the square of any corresponding linear measurement ratio.

**Kiran:** And the Pythagoras theorem proof using similarity — is that expected in boards?

**Ravi:** The proof is a potential 5-mark question. The key idea is drawing the altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse, which creates two smaller triangles, each similar to the original. From these similarities, you derive AB squared equals AD times AC and BC squared equals CD times AC. Adding gives the theorem.

**Kiran:** The converse is also testable — if the square of one side equals the sum of squares of the other two, then the triangle is right-angled.

**Ravi:** Yes. Use the converse to check whether a triangle with given sides is right-angled. For sides 5, 12, 13: check if 13 squared equals 5 squared plus 12 squared. Since 169 equals 25 plus 144, yes — it is a right triangle. This type of verification problem is a board exam favourite.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** This chapter carries heavy marks. What should I focus on?

**Ravi:** Typically 5-8 marks. The proof of BPT is a near-certain question — learn it step by step with a clear diagram. Problems on AA similarity with numerical side calculations are common. The Pythagoras theorem proof using similarity and its converse for verifying right triangles both appear regularly.

**Kiran:** What mistakes should I avoid?

**Ravi:** Three key errors. First, getting the vertex correspondence wrong in similarity statements — triangle ABC similar to triangle DEF is not the same as triangle ABC similar to triangle EDF. Second, forgetting to square the ratio when computing areas of similar triangles. Third, not drawing a clear figure with all given information labelled. A well-drawn figure earns marks even before you begin solving.

**Kiran:** Does this chapter connect to other chapters?

**Ravi:** Strongly. The Pythagoras theorem appears in coordinate geometry for the distance formula, in trigonometry for deriving identities, and in circles for tangent-length calculations. Similarity connects to the area theorem used in constructions. Mastering Chapter 6 strengthens your entire geometry foundation. Bhaskara, the great Indian mathematician, gave an elegant proof of the Pythagoras theorem using just a single diagram and the word "Behold!" — that is the beauty of mathematical reasoning.
