## Introduction

In Class 9, you studied **congruence** of triangles -- two figures are congruent if they have the same shape AND the same size. In this chapter, we go a step further to study **similarity** -- two figures that have the same shape but not necessarily the same size.

Think about how the heights of mountains like Mount Everest or the distance to the Moon have been measured. These cannot be measured directly with a tape! They were calculated using the principle of **similarity of figures** and indirect measurements.

---

## Similar Figures

Two figures are **similar** if they have the same shape, but may differ in size.

### Examples of Similar Figures

- All **circles** are similar (they all have the same shape -- round)
- All **squares** are similar
- All **equilateral triangles** are similar

> **Key idea:** All congruent figures are similar, but all similar figures are NOT necessarily congruent. Congruence is a special case of similarity where the size is also the same.

### When Are Two Polygons Similar?

Two polygons of the same number of sides are similar if:
1. Their **corresponding angles are equal**, AND
2. Their **corresponding sides are in the same ratio** (proportion)

Both conditions must be satisfied. For example:
- A **square** and a **rectangle** have equal angles (all 90 degrees) but sides are not in the same ratio -- so they are NOT similar
- A **square** and a **rhombus** have sides in the same ratio but angles may not be equal -- so they are NOT similar

**Note:** For triangles, a special rule applies -- we do not need BOTH conditions. Either one is sufficient (as we shall see below).

---

## Basic Proportionality Theorem (Thales' Theorem)

This is one of the most important theorems in the chapter.

**Theorem (BPT):** If a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle, it divides the other two sides **in the same ratio**.

In triangle ABC, if DE is drawn parallel to BC such that D lies on AB and E lies on AC, then:

![Triangle ABC with line DE parallel to BC, where D is on AB and E is on AC, showing proportional segments AD, DB, AE, EC](basic-proportionality-theorem-diagram.png)

**AD/DB = AE/EC**

### Proof Outline

1. Draw DE parallel to BC in triangle ABC
2. Join BE and CD
3. Draw DM perpendicular to AC and EN perpendicular to AB
4. Using the fact that triangles BDE and CDE have equal areas (same base DE, between same parallels DE and BC)
5. Show that Area(ADE)/Area(BDE) = AD/DB and Area(ADE)/Area(CDE) = AE/EC
6. Since Area(BDE) = Area(CDE), we get **AD/DB = AE/EC**

### Converse of BPT

If a line divides any two sides of a triangle **in the same ratio**, then the line is **parallel** to the third side.

> **Key idea:** BPT and its converse are the foundation for many problems in this chapter. If you see a line parallel to one side of a triangle, immediately think of BPT.

### Worked Example

In triangle ABC, DE is parallel to BC with D on AB and E on AC. If AD = 4, DB = 5, and AE = 8, find EC.

By BPT: AD/DB = AE/EC
4/5 = 8/EC
EC = 8 x 5/4 = **10**

## Similarity of Triangles

For triangles, we have special criteria that make checking similarity easier than for general polygons.

### Criteria for Similarity of Triangles

#### 1. AAA Similarity Criterion

If in two triangles, **all three corresponding angles are equal**, then the triangles are similar.

Since the sum of angles in a triangle is always 180 degrees, if two angles are equal, the third automatically becomes equal. So we get:

#### 2. AA Similarity Criterion

![Two triangles with corresponding angles marked equal, showing similar triangles with proportional sides](aa-similarity-criterion-diagram.png)

If in two triangles, **two angles of one are equal to two angles of the other**, then the triangles are similar.

> **Key idea:** AA criterion is the most commonly used similarity test. If you can show any two pairs of angles are equal, the triangles are similar!

#### 3. SSS Similarity Criterion

If in two triangles, the **corresponding sides are in the same ratio**, then the triangles are similar.

That is, if AB/PQ = BC/QR = CA/RP, then triangle ABC ~ triangle PQR.

#### 4. SAS Similarity Criterion

If **one angle** of a triangle is equal to one angle of another triangle, and the **sides including these angles are in the same ratio** (proportional), then the triangles are similar.

That is, if angle A = angle P and AB/PQ = AC/PR, then triangle ABC ~ triangle PQR.

---

## How to Write Similarity

When we write **triangle ABC ~ triangle PQR**, the order of vertices matters! It tells us which vertices correspond to each other:

- A corresponds to P
- B corresponds to Q
- C corresponds to R

This means:
- Angle A = Angle P, Angle B = Angle Q, Angle C = Angle R
- AB/PQ = BC/QR = CA/RP

---

## Worked Examples on Similarity

### Example 1: Using AA Criterion

In triangles ABC and DEF, angle A = angle D = 50 degrees and angle B = angle E = 60 degrees. Are the triangles similar?

Since two pairs of angles are equal, by **AA criterion**, triangle ABC ~ triangle DEF.

Also, angle C = angle F = 180 - 50 - 60 = 70 degrees.

### Example 2: Shadow Problem

A girl of height 90 cm walks away from a lamp-post (height 3.6 m) at 1.2 m/s. Find the length of her shadow after 4 seconds.

**Step 1:** Distance walked = 1.2 x 4 = 4.8 m

**Step 2:** Let shadow length = x metres.

**Step 3:** The lamp-post triangle and the girl-shadow triangle are similar (AA criterion -- both have a right angle and share the angle at the shadow tip).

**Step 4:** (4.8 + x)/x = 3.6/0.9 = 4

**Step 5:** 4.8 + x = 4x, so 3x = 4.8, giving **x = 1.6 m**

### Example 3: Pole and Tower Shadow

A vertical pole of 6 m casts a shadow of 4 m. At the same time, a tower casts a shadow of 28 m. Find the height of the tower.

The triangles formed by the pole-shadow and tower-shadow are similar (same angle of elevation of the sun).

Height of tower / 28 = 6 / 4
Height of tower = 6 x 28 / 4 = **42 m**

## Areas of Similar Triangles

**Theorem:** The ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the **square of the ratio of their corresponding sides**.

If triangle ABC ~ triangle PQR, then:

**Area(ABC) / Area(PQR) = (AB/PQ)^2 = (BC/QR)^2 = (CA/RP)^2**

### Worked Example

If triangle ABC ~ triangle DEF and AB/DE = 3/5, find the ratio of their areas.

Area(ABC)/Area(DEF) = (AB/DE)^2 = (3/5)^2 = **9/25**

### Also Applies to:

- Ratio of squares of corresponding **altitudes**
- Ratio of squares of corresponding **medians**
- Ratio of squares of corresponding **angle bisectors**

> **Key idea:** When dealing with areas of similar triangles, always think "square of the ratio." If sides are in ratio 2:3, areas are in ratio 4:9.

## Pythagoras Theorem

This is perhaps the most famous theorem in all of mathematics.

**Theorem:** In a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

![Right triangle ABC with squares drawn on each side, showing that the area of the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the areas of the squares on the other two sides](pythagoras-theorem-visual-proof.png)

If triangle ABC is right-angled at B, then:

**AC^2 = AB^2 + BC^2**

### Proof Using Similarity

1. Draw BD perpendicular to AC (where angle B = 90 degrees)
2. In triangle ADB and triangle ABC:
   - Angle A is common
   - Angle ADB = Angle ABC = 90 degrees
   - So triangle ADB ~ triangle ABC (AA)
   - This gives: AD/AB = AB/AC, i.e., **AB^2 = AD x AC** ... (1)

3. Similarly, triangle BDC ~ triangle ABC
   - This gives: CD/BC = BC/AC, i.e., **BC^2 = CD x AC** ... (2)

4. Adding (1) and (2):
   AB^2 + BC^2 = AD x AC + CD x AC = AC(AD + CD) = AC x AC = **AC^2**

### Converse of Pythagoras Theorem

If in a triangle, the square of one side equals the sum of squares of the other two sides, then the angle opposite the first side is a **right angle**.

That is, if AC^2 = AB^2 + BC^2, then angle B = 90 degrees.

> **Key idea:** Pythagoras theorem works ONLY for right-angled triangles. The converse helps you CHECK whether a triangle is right-angled.

### Worked Example: Is It a Right Triangle?

A triangle has sides 5 cm, 12 cm, and 13 cm. Is it right-angled?

Check: 13^2 = 169. And 5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169.

Since 13^2 = 5^2 + 12^2, by the converse of Pythagoras theorem, the triangle is right-angled at the vertex opposite the 13 cm side.

### Worked Example: Ladder Problem

A ladder 10 m long reaches a window 8 m above the ground. How far is the foot of the ladder from the base of the wall?

Let the distance from wall = x.

By Pythagoras theorem: 10^2 = 8^2 + x^2
100 = 64 + x^2
x^2 = 36
**x = 6 m**

## Important Results Related to Pythagoras Theorem

1. In an **obtuse triangle** ABC with angle B obtuse, if AD is perpendicular to CB produced:
   AC^2 = AB^2 + BC^2 + 2BC x BD

2. In an **acute triangle** ABC, if AD is perpendicular to BC:
   AC^2 = AB^2 + BC^2 - 2BC x BD

## Chapter Summary

1. Two figures having the same shape but not necessarily the same size are called **similar** figures.

2. All congruent figures are similar but the converse is not true.

3. Two polygons are similar if (i) corresponding angles are equal AND (ii) corresponding sides are in the same ratio.

4. **Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT):** If a line is parallel to one side of a triangle, it divides the other two sides in the same ratio.

5. **Converse of BPT:** If a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, the line is parallel to the third side.

6. **Similarity criteria for triangles:**
   - **AA:** Two angles of one triangle equal to two angles of another
   - **SSS:** Corresponding sides in the same ratio
   - **SAS:** One angle equal and including sides in the same ratio

7. The ratio of **areas** of two similar triangles equals the **square** of the ratio of their corresponding sides.

8. **Pythagoras Theorem:** In a right triangle, hypotenuse^2 = base^2 + height^2.

9. **Converse of Pythagoras Theorem:** If the square of one side equals the sum of squares of the other two sides, the triangle is right-angled.
