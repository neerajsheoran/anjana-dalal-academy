## Introduction

In Class 9, you learned that to locate a point on a plane, we need a pair of **coordinate axes**. The distance of a point from the y-axis is called its **x-coordinate (abscissa)**, and the distance from the x-axis is called its **y-coordinate (ordinate)**.

In this chapter, we will learn three important concepts:

1. **Distance Formula** -- how to find the distance between two points
2. **Section Formula** -- how to find the coordinates of a point that divides a line segment in a given ratio
3. **Area of a Triangle** -- using coordinates of the three vertices

Coordinate geometry is widely used in physics, engineering, navigation, and even art!

---

## Distance Formula

### The Problem

A town B is located 36 km east and 15 km north of town A. What is the straight-line distance from A to B?

Using Pythagoras theorem: Distance = root(36^2 + 15^2) = root(1296 + 225) = root(1521) = **39 km**

### The General Formula

The distance between two points **P(x1, y1)** and **Q(x2, y2)** is:

**PQ = root[(x2 - x1)^2 + (y2 - y1)^2]**

This is called the **distance formula**. It is derived from the Pythagoras theorem.

### Special Cases

- Distance of a point **P(x, y) from the origin** O(0, 0): **OP = root(x^2 + y^2)**
- Distance between two points on the **x-axis**, say (x1, 0) and (x2, 0): **|x2 - x1|**
- Distance between two points on the **y-axis**, say (0, y1) and (0, y2): **|y2 - y1|**

> **Key idea:** The distance formula is just the Pythagoras theorem applied to coordinate geometry. The horizontal and vertical differences between the points form the two legs of a right triangle, and the distance is the hypotenuse.

## Worked Examples on Distance Formula

### Example 1: Distance Between Two Points

Find the distance between A(3, 2) and B(-2, -3).

AB = root[(3 - (-2))^2 + (2 - (-3))^2]
= root[(5)^2 + (5)^2]
= root[25 + 25]
= root(50)
= **5 root(2) units** (approximately 7.07 units)

### Example 2: Proving a Square

Show that A(1, 7), B(4, 2), C(-1, -1), D(-4, 4) form a square.

- AB = root(9 + 25) = root(34)
- BC = root(25 + 9) = root(34)
- CD = root(9 + 25) = root(34)
- DA = root(25 + 9) = root(34)
- AC = root(4 + 64) = root(68) (diagonal)
- BD = root(64 + 4) = root(68) (diagonal)

All four sides are equal (root 34) and both diagonals are equal (root 68). Therefore, ABCD is a **square**.

### Example 3: Collinearity

Are A(3, 1), B(6, 4), and C(8, 6) collinear (lying on the same line)?

- AB = root(9 + 9) = root(18) = 3 root(2)
- BC = root(4 + 4) = root(8) = 2 root(2)
- AC = root(25 + 25) = root(50) = 5 root(2)

Since AB + BC = 3 root(2) + 2 root(2) = 5 root(2) = AC, the points are **collinear**.

> **Key idea:** Three points A, B, C are collinear if AB + BC = AC (or any such combination where the sum of two distances equals the third).

### Example 4: Equidistant Points

Find a relation between x and y such that (x, y) is equidistant from (7, 1) and (3, 5).

Set the distances equal:
(x - 7)^2 + (y - 1)^2 = (x - 3)^2 + (y - 5)^2

Expanding and simplifying:
x^2 - 14x + 49 + y^2 - 2y + 1 = x^2 - 6x + 9 + y^2 - 10y + 25

-14x - 2y + 50 = -6x - 10y + 34

-8x + 8y = -16

**x - y = 2**

This is the equation of the perpendicular bisector of the line joining (7, 1) and (3, 5).

## Applications of Distance Formula

You can use the distance formula to:

1. **Find the type of triangle** formed by three points (equilateral, isosceles, right-angled)
2. **Find the type of quadrilateral** (square, rectangle, rhombus, parallelogram)
3. **Check collinearity** of three points
4. **Find points on an axis** equidistant from two given points

### How to Identify Quadrilaterals

| Shape | Condition |
|-------|-----------|
| Parallelogram | Opposite sides equal |
| Rectangle | Opposite sides equal AND diagonals equal |
| Rhombus | All four sides equal |
| Square | All four sides equal AND diagonals equal |

## Section Formula

### The Problem

Suppose a telephone company wants to place a relay tower P between towns A and B such that the distance from B is twice the distance from A. If A is the origin and B is at (36, 15), where should P be placed?

P divides AB in the ratio 1:2.

### The General Formula

If point **P(x, y)** divides the line segment joining **A(x1, y1)** and **B(x2, y2)** internally in the ratio **m1 : m2**, then:

**x = (m1 x x2 + m2 x x1) / (m1 + m2)**

**y = (m1 x y2 + m2 x y1) / (m1 + m2)**

This is called the **section formula**.

> **Key idea:** Think of it as a weighted average. The point closer to B gets more "weight" from B's coordinates, and vice versa.

### Applying to the Relay Tower Problem

P divides AB in ratio 1:2, where A(0, 0) and B(36, 15).

x = (1 x 36 + 2 x 0) / (1 + 2) = 36/3 = **12**
y = (1 x 15 + 2 x 0) / (1 + 2) = 15/3 = **5**

The relay tower should be at **P(12, 5)**.

## Mid-Point Formula

A special case of the section formula is when the point divides the segment in the ratio **1:1** (i.e., it is the **mid-point**).

The mid-point of the line segment joining **A(x1, y1)** and **B(x2, y2)** is:

**M = ((x1 + x2)/2, (y1 + y2)/2)**

### Worked Example

Find the mid-point of A(4, -6) and B(-2, 8).

M = ((4 + (-2))/2, (-6 + 8)/2) = (2/2, 2/2) = **(1, 1)**

## Worked Examples on Section Formula

### Example 1

Find the coordinates of the point which divides the line segment joining A(1, -2) and B(-3, 4) in the ratio 2:3.

x = (2(-3) + 3(1)) / (2 + 3) = (-6 + 3)/5 = **-3/5**
y = (2(4) + 3(-2)) / (2 + 3) = (8 - 6)/5 = **2/5**

The point is **(-3/5, 2/5)**.

### Example 2

Find the ratio in which the point (-1, 6) divides the line segment joining (-3, 10) and (6, -8).

Let the ratio be k:1.

x-coordinate: -1 = (6k + (-3)) / (k + 1)
-k - 1 = 6k - 3
-7k = -2
**k = 2/7**

So the ratio is **2:7**.

Verify with y-coordinate: y = (2(-8)/7 + 10) / (2/7 + 1) = (-16/7 + 10) / (9/7) = (54/7) / (9/7) = 6. Correct!

### Example 3: Trisection Points

Find the trisection points of the line segment joining A(2, -2) and B(-7, 4).

The trisection points divide AB into three equal parts. Let them be P and Q.

- P divides AB in ratio 1:2:
  P = ((1(-7) + 2(2))/3, (1(4) + 2(-2))/3) = (-3/3, 0/3) = **(-1, 0)**

- Q divides AB in ratio 2:1:
  Q = ((2(-7) + 1(2))/3, (2(4) + 1(-2))/3) = (-12/3, 6/3) = **(-4, 2)**

## Area of a Triangle

If you know the coordinates of the three vertices of a triangle, you can find its area using a formula.

### The Formula

Area of a triangle with vertices **A(x1, y1)**, **B(x2, y2)**, and **C(x3, y3)** is:

**Area = (1/2) |x1(y2 - y3) + x2(y3 - y1) + x3(y1 - y2)|**

We take the **absolute value** because area is always positive.

> **Key idea:** If the area comes out to be zero, it means the three points are **collinear** (lying on the same straight line).

### Worked Example 1

Find the area of the triangle with vertices A(1, -1), B(-4, 6), C(-3, -5).

Area = (1/2) |1(6 - (-5)) + (-4)((-5) - (-1)) + (-3)((-1) - 6)|
= (1/2) |1(11) + (-4)(-4) + (-3)(-7)|
= (1/2) |11 + 16 + 21|
= (1/2) |48|
= **24 square units**

### Worked Example 2: Checking Collinearity

Are the points (1, 5), (2, 3), and (-2, -11) collinear?

Area = (1/2) |1(3 - (-11)) + 2((-11) - 5) + (-2)(5 - 3)|
= (1/2) |1(14) + 2(-16) + (-2)(2)|
= (1/2) |14 - 32 - 4|
= (1/2) |-22|
= 11

Since the area is NOT zero, the points are **not collinear**.

### Worked Example 3: Finding Unknown Vertices

Find the value of k if the points A(2, 3), B(4, k), and C(6, -3) are collinear.

For collinearity, area = 0:

0 = (1/2) |2(k - (-3)) + 4((-3) - 3) + 6(3 - k)|
0 = |2(k + 3) + 4(-6) + 6(3 - k)|
0 = |2k + 6 - 24 + 18 - 6k|
0 = |-4k|
**k = 0**

## Area of a Quadrilateral

To find the area of a quadrilateral with given vertices, divide it into two triangles using a diagonal and add their areas.

**Area of quadrilateral ABCD = Area of triangle ABC + Area of triangle ACD**

## Chapter Summary

1. **Distance Formula:** The distance between P(x1, y1) and Q(x2, y2) is:
   PQ = root[(x2 - x1)^2 + (y2 - y1)^2]

2. **Section Formula:** The point P dividing the segment joining A(x1, y1) and B(x2, y2) in ratio m1:m2 has coordinates:
   ((m1.x2 + m2.x1)/(m1 + m2), (m1.y2 + m2.y1)/(m1 + m2))

3. **Mid-point Formula:** Mid-point of A(x1, y1) and B(x2, y2) is:
   ((x1 + x2)/2, (y1 + y2)/2)

4. **Area of a Triangle** with vertices (x1, y1), (x2, y2), (x3, y3):
   Area = (1/2)|x1(y2 - y3) + x2(y3 - y1) + x3(y1 - y2)|

5. Three points are **collinear** if the area of the triangle formed by them is zero.
