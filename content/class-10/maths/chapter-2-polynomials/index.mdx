## Introduction

In Class 9, you studied polynomials in one variable and their degrees. Let us quickly recall the basics before going deeper.

If p(x) is a polynomial in x, the **highest power of x** in p(x) is called the **degree** of the polynomial. For example:

- **4x + 2** has degree 1
- **2y^2 - 3y + 4** has degree 2
- **5x^3 - 4x^2 + x - 2** has degree 3

Expressions like 1/(x+1) or 1/root(x+2) are **not** polynomials.

### Types of Polynomials by Degree

| Degree | Name | General Form | Examples |
|--------|------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Linear | ax + b | 2x - 3, 3x + 5 |
| 2 | Quadratic | ax^2 + bx + c | x^2 - 3x + 2, 5v^2 - 3 |
| 3 | Cubic | ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d | 2x^3 - x + 1, x^3 |

The word **quadratic** comes from "quadrate" meaning "square".

In this chapter, we explore the relationship between the **zeroes** of a polynomial and its **coefficients**, and also study the geometrical meaning of zeroes.

## Zeroes of a Polynomial

If p(x) is a polynomial and k is any real number, the value obtained by replacing x with k is called the **value of p(x) at x = k**, written as **p(k)**.

A real number k is called a **zero** of a polynomial p(x) if **p(k) = 0**.

### Example: Finding Zeroes

Consider p(x) = x^2 - 3x - 4.

- p(-1) = (-1)^2 - 3(-1) - 4 = 1 + 3 - 4 = **0**
- p(4) = (4)^2 - 3(4) - 4 = 16 - 12 - 4 = **0**

Since p(-1) = 0 and p(4) = 0, the zeroes of this polynomial are **-1 and 4**.

### Zero of a Linear Polynomial

For a linear polynomial p(x) = ax + b, the zero is:

**k = -b/a = -(Constant term) / (Coefficient of x)**

For example, the zero of 2x + 3 is -3/2.

## Geometrical Meaning of Zeroes

### For Linear Polynomials

The graph of y = ax + b is a **straight line**. It intersects the x-axis at exactly one point, namely (-b/a, 0). The x-coordinate of this point is the zero of the polynomial.

**Example:** The graph of y = 2x + 3 is a straight line that crosses the x-axis at (-3/2, 0). So the zero of 2x + 3 is -3/2.

### For Quadratic Polynomials

The graph of y = ax^2 + bx + c is a **parabola** (a U-shaped curve):

- If **a > 0**, the parabola opens **upwards** (like a cup)
- If **a &lt; 0**, the parabola opens **downwards** (like an inverted cup)

> **Key idea:** The zeroes of a quadratic polynomial are the x-coordinates of the points where the parabola intersects the x-axis.

Three cases are possible:

**Case 1: Two distinct zeroes** -- The parabola crosses the x-axis at two different points. The polynomial has two distinct zeroes.

**Case 2: One zero (repeated)** -- The parabola just touches the x-axis at one point. The polynomial has two equal zeroes (one repeated zero).

**Case 3: No real zeroes** -- The parabola does not cross or touch the x-axis at all. The polynomial has no real zeroes.

### For Cubic Polynomials

The graph of a cubic polynomial can cross the x-axis at **at most 3 points**.

**Example:** For y = x^3 - 4x:
- At x = -2: y = -8 + 8 = 0
- At x = 0: y = 0
- At x = 2: y = 8 - 8 = 0

The zeroes are -2, 0, and 2 (three distinct zeroes).

> **Key idea:** A polynomial of degree n has **at most n zeroes**. This is a fundamental rule. A linear polynomial has at most 1 zero, a quadratic has at most 2, and a cubic has at most 3.

## Relationship Between Zeroes and Coefficients of a Quadratic Polynomial

This is one of the most important topics in this chapter and is frequently asked in board exams.

If **alpha** and **beta** are the zeroes of the quadratic polynomial **ax^2 + bx + c** (where a is not zero), then:

- **Sum of zeroes** = alpha + beta = **-b/a** = -(Coefficient of x) / (Coefficient of x^2)
- **Product of zeroes** = alpha x beta = **c/a** = (Constant term) / (Coefficient of x^2)

> **Key idea:** The zeroes of a quadratic polynomial are directly related to its coefficients through these two simple formulas. Memorise them well!

### How to Derive This

If alpha and beta are zeroes of ax^2 + bx + c, then (x - alpha) and (x - beta) are its factors:

ax^2 + bx + c = k(x - alpha)(x - beta) = k[x^2 - (alpha + beta)x + alpha x beta]

Comparing coefficients:
- a = k
- b = -k(alpha + beta), so alpha + beta = -b/a
- c = k(alpha x beta), so alpha x beta = c/a

## Worked Examples on Zeroes and Coefficients

### Example 1: Find zeroes and verify the relationship

**Polynomial:** x^2 + 7x + 10

**Step 1:** Factorise by splitting the middle term.
- x^2 + 7x + 10 = x^2 + 5x + 2x + 10 = x(x + 5) + 2(x + 5) = (x + 2)(x + 5)

**Step 2:** Find zeroes.
- x + 2 = 0 gives x = -2
- x + 5 = 0 gives x = -5
- Zeroes are **-2 and -5**

**Step 3:** Verify the relationships.
- Sum of zeroes = -2 + (-5) = -7 = -(7)/1 = -b/a. Verified!
- Product of zeroes = (-2)(-5) = 10 = 10/1 = c/a. Verified!

### Example 2: Find zeroes of x^2 - 3

**Step 1:** Use the identity a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b).
- x^2 - 3 = (x - root 3)(x + root 3)

**Step 2:** Zeroes are **root 3** and **-root 3**.

**Step 3:** Verify.
- Sum = root 3 + (-root 3) = 0 = -(0)/1 = -b/a. Verified!
- Product = (root 3)(-root 3) = -3 = -3/1 = c/a. Verified!

### Example 3: Find zeroes of 2x^2 - 8x + 6

**Step 1:** Factorise.
- 2x^2 - 8x + 6 = 2x^2 - 6x - 2x + 6 = 2x(x - 3) - 2(x - 3) = (2x - 2)(x - 3) = 2(x - 1)(x - 3)

**Step 2:** Zeroes are **1 and 3**.

**Step 3:** Verify.
- Sum = 1 + 3 = 4 = -(-8)/2 = 8/2 = 4. Verified!
- Product = 1 x 3 = 3 = 6/2 = 3. Verified!

## Finding a Quadratic Polynomial from Given Zeroes

If you know the sum and product of zeroes, you can write the quadratic polynomial.

### Formula

If S = sum of zeroes and P = product of zeroes, then the quadratic polynomial is:

**x^2 - Sx + P**

(or any non-zero constant multiple of it: k(x^2 - Sx + P))

### Example: Sum = -3, Product = 2

The polynomial is x^2 - (-3)x + 2 = **x^2 + 3x + 2**.

You can verify: the zeroes of x^2 + 3x + 2 = (x + 1)(x + 2) are -1 and -2. Their sum is -3 and product is 2. Correct!

### More Examples

| Sum of zeroes | Product of zeroes | Quadratic polynomial |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 1/4 | -1 | x^2 - (1/4)x - 1 or 4x^2 - x - 4 |
| root 2 | 1/3 | x^2 - root(2)x + 1/3 or 3x^2 - 3root(2)x + 1 |
| 0 | root 5 | x^2 + root 5 |
| 1 | 1 | x^2 - x + 1 |

## Relationship Between Zeroes and Coefficients of a Cubic Polynomial

If **alpha, beta, and gamma** are the zeroes of the cubic polynomial **ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d**, then:

- **Sum of zeroes:** alpha + beta + gamma = **-b/a**
- **Sum of products taken two at a time:** alpha x beta + beta x gamma + gamma x alpha = **c/a**
- **Product of all three zeroes:** alpha x beta x gamma = **-d/a**

> **Key idea:** For a cubic polynomial, there are three relationships connecting the three zeroes to the four coefficients. Notice how the signs alternate: -b/a, +c/a, -d/a.

### Worked Example

**Verify** that 3, -1, and -1/3 are zeroes of p(x) = 3x^3 - 5x^2 - 11x - 3.

**Step 1:** Check each value.
- p(3) = 3(27) - 5(9) - 11(3) - 3 = 81 - 45 - 33 - 3 = 0
- p(-1) = 3(-1) - 5(1) - 11(-1) - 3 = -3 - 5 + 11 - 3 = 0
- p(-1/3) = 3(-1/27) - 5(1/9) - 11(-1/3) - 3 = -1/9 - 5/9 + 11/3 - 3 = 0

All three give zero, so they are indeed zeroes.

**Step 2:** Verify the relationships (a = 3, b = -5, c = -11, d = -3).

- Sum = 3 + (-1) + (-1/3) = 2 - 1/3 = 5/3 = -(-5)/3 = -b/a. Verified!
- Sum of products in pairs = 3(-1) + (-1)(-1/3) + (-1/3)(3) = -3 + 1/3 - 1 = -11/3 = c/a. Verified!
- Product = 3 x (-1) x (-1/3) = 1 = -(-3)/3 = -d/a. Verified!

## Important Tips for Board Exams

1. **Always verify** the relationship between zeroes and coefficients after finding the zeroes.

2. To factorise quadratic polynomials, use the **splitting the middle term** method:
   - For ax^2 + bx + c, find two numbers whose product is a x c and sum is b
   - Split the middle term using these two numbers
   - Factor by grouping

3. To find a quadratic polynomial from given zeroes alpha and beta, use:
   - p(x) = x^2 - (alpha + beta)x + (alpha x beta)

4. Remember the maximum number of zeroes:
   - Linear (degree 1): at most **1** zero
   - Quadratic (degree 2): at most **2** zeroes
   - Cubic (degree 3): at most **3** zeroes
   - Degree n: at most **n** zeroes

## Chapter Summary

1. Polynomials of degrees 1, 2, and 3 are called **linear**, **quadratic**, and **cubic** polynomials respectively.

2. A quadratic polynomial in x with real coefficients is of the form **ax^2 + bx + c**, where a, b, c are real numbers and a is not zero.

3. The **zeroes** of a polynomial p(x) are the x-coordinates of the points where the graph of y = p(x) intersects the x-axis.

4. A quadratic polynomial can have **at most 2 zeroes** and a cubic polynomial can have **at most 3 zeroes**.

5. If alpha and beta are zeroes of ax^2 + bx + c, then:
   - alpha + beta = -b/a
   - alpha x beta = c/a

6. If alpha, beta, and gamma are zeroes of ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d, then:
   - alpha + beta + gamma = -b/a
   - alpha.beta + beta.gamma + gamma.alpha = c/a
   - alpha.beta.gamma = -d/a
