## Angles of Elevation and Depression

**Kiran:** Papa, angle of elevation is when I look up, and angle of depression is when I look down. But they are both measured from the horizontal, not the vertical?

**Ravi:** Correct. This is a crucial distinction. The angle of elevation is between the horizontal line from your eye and the line of sight going upward. The angle of depression is between the horizontal and the line of sight going downward. Both are measured from the horizontal. Students who measure from the vertical get the wrong answer.

**Kiran:** And the angle of elevation from point A looking up at point B equals the angle of depression from point B looking down at point A?

**Ravi:** Yes, because the two horizontals are parallel, and the line of sight acts as a transversal. The angles are alternate interior angles. This relationship is used constantly in problems involving buildings, towers, and cliffs.

**Kiran:** How did people actually use this in history?

**Ravi:** Ancient Indian mathematicians like Aryabhata used the concepts of trigonometry to estimate astronomical distances. Surveyors in the Great Trigonometric Survey of India, led by George Everest in the 19th century, used these exact methods to map the Indian subcontinent and measure the height of Mount Everest. The mountain is named after him precisely because of this trigonometric work.

## Problem-Solving Strategy

**Kiran:** I always struggle with knowing which trigonometric ratio to use in a problem.

**Ravi:** Here is a simple rule. Identify which two sides of the right triangle are involved — the side you know and the side you want. If both are non-hypotenuse sides, use tan. If one is the hypotenuse and the other is the opposite side, use sin. If one is the hypotenuse and the other is the adjacent side, use cos. In about 80 percent of board exam problems, tan is the right choice because you deal with height and horizontal distance.

**Kiran:** And I should always draw a diagram first?

**Ravi:** Always. A well-labelled diagram is worth marks by itself. Draw the ground as a horizontal line, the object as a vertical line, mark the angle, and identify the right triangle. Without a diagram, you risk setting up the wrong equation.

## Single-Triangle Problems

**Kiran:** The tower problem where I stand 15 metres away and the angle of elevation is 60 degrees — that is straightforward. tan 60 equals height over 15, so height equals 15 root 3.

**Ravi:** Good. Now notice the observer height problem. When an observer is 1.5 metres tall and the angle is measured from her eyes, the total height of the object is the calculated value plus 1.5 metres. Many students forget to add the observer's height.

**Kiran:** Oh, so the right triangle is formed from the observer's eyes to the top of the object, not from the ground?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The base of the triangle is at eye level, not ground level. After computing the height above the eyes, add the observer's height to get the full height. This subtlety appears frequently in board exams and catches careless students.

## Two-Triangle Problems

**Kiran:** The two-building problem and the shadow problem — those involve setting up two equations simultaneously?

**Ravi:** Yes. Typically, one angle gives you one equation involving the unknown height and distance, and the second angle gives you another equation. Eliminate the distance variable to find the height, or vice versa. The shadow problem where the shadow is 40 metres longer at 30 degrees than at 60 degrees is a classic — you set up h equals x root 3 from the first condition and h equals the quantity x plus 40 over root 3 from the second, then solve.

**Kiran:** Rationalisation comes up a lot in these problems.

**Ravi:** Yes, because expressions like 8 root 3 over the quantity root 3 minus 1 need to be rationalised. Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate — root 3 plus 1. This is a mechanical step but must be done correctly. Board examiners expect the final answer in simplified, rationalised form.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** How many marks does this chapter carry?

**Ravi:** Usually 3-5 marks, often combined with Chapter 8 in the trigonometry unit. The most common question is a single diagram with one or two angles, requiring you to find height and distance. Previous year questions frequently involve towers, buildings, lighthouses, or flying objects.

**Kiran:** What mistakes should I be most careful about?

**Ravi:** Five common errors. First, measuring the angle from the vertical instead of the horizontal. Second, forgetting to add the observer's height. Third, using the wrong trigonometric ratio — always check opposite, adjacent, hypotenuse. Fourth, not rationalising the denominator. Fifth, writing tan 30 equals root 3 instead of 1 over root 3 — always double-check your standard values. A systematic diagram and clear labelling will prevent most of these errors.

## Connecting to Real Engineering

**Kiran:** Do engineers still use these methods today, or has technology replaced them?

**Ravi:** The principles are exactly the same — technology just makes the measurements more precise. Theodolites and total stations used by surveyors are sophisticated angle-measuring instruments based on the same trigonometry. When NHAI plans a new highway, surveyors use angles of elevation and depression to map the terrain. When ISRO tracks a satellite, the angle calculations are fundamentally trigonometric.

**Kiran:** The Great Trigonometric Survey of India mapped the entire subcontinent using these methods?

**Ravi:** Yes. Starting in 1802, the survey took decades and covered thousands of kilometres. Radhanath Sikdar, an Indian mathematician working on the survey, was the first to calculate that Peak XV — later named Mount Everest — was the tallest mountain in the world. He did this using precisely the kind of trigonometric calculations you are studying now.

**Kiran:** That is inspiring — a Class 10 concept was used to measure the world's tallest mountain.

**Ravi:** And that is why understanding the fundamentals deeply matters more than memorising formulas. The formulas are tools, but the understanding is power. Master this chapter and you carry a skill that has literally changed how we see the world.
