## Introduction

In the previous chapter, you learnt about trigonometric ratios. Now we will see how trigonometry is used in real life to find **heights and distances** that cannot be measured directly.

How do engineers measure the height of a building, the width of a river, or the distance of a ship from a lighthouse? They use trigonometry! This chapter is all about applying the ratios you learnt in Chapter 8 to solve practical problems.

---

## Key Terminology

### Line of Sight

The **line of sight** is the line drawn from the eye of an observer to the point on the object being viewed.

### Angle of Elevation

When you look **upward** at an object (like the top of a building or a flying kite), the angle between the **horizontal** and your line of sight is called the **angle of elevation**.

Think of it this way: you raise your head to look up, and the angle your eyes make with the horizontal is the angle of elevation.

### Angle of Depression

![Diagram showing angle of elevation (looking up from ground to building top) and angle of depression (looking down from building top to ground) with horizontal lines and line of sight](angle-of-elevation-depression-diagram.png)

When you look **downward** at an object (like a boat from a cliff or a car from a tower), the angle between the **horizontal** and your line of sight is called the **angle of depression**.

Think of it this way: you lower your head to look down, and the angle your eyes make with the horizontal is the angle of depression.

> **Key idea:** The angle of elevation and the angle of depression are always measured from the **horizontal line**, never from the vertical. Also, the angle of elevation from point A to point B equals the angle of depression from point B to point A (alternate angles).

## How to Solve Heights and Distances Problems

### Step-by-Step Approach

1. **Draw a clear diagram** showing all the given information
2. **Identify the right triangle(s)** in the diagram
3. **Mark** the known sides and angles
4. **Choose the appropriate trigonometric ratio** (sin, cos, or tan) based on which sides are known and which are needed
5. **Solve** the equation to find the unknown

### Which Ratio to Use?

| You know | You need to find | Use |
|----------|-----------------|-----|
| Opposite and Hypotenuse | - | sin |
| Adjacent and Hypotenuse | - | cos |
| Opposite and Adjacent | - | tan |

> **Key idea:** In most problems, **tan** is the most commonly used ratio because you typically know or need the height (opposite) and the horizontal distance (adjacent).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Height of a Tower

From a point on the ground, 15 m away from the foot of a tower, the angle of elevation of the top is 60 degrees. Find the height of the tower.

**Diagram:** Right triangle with base = 15 m, angle of elevation = 60 degrees, height = ?

**Solution:**
tan 60 = height / 15
root(3) = height / 15
Height = 15 root(3) m (approximately 25.98 m)

### Example 2: Ladder Problem

An electrician needs to reach a point 3.7 m below the top of a 5 m pole. Her ladder makes an angle of 60 degrees with the ground. Find the length of the ladder and how far from the pole she should place it.

**Height to reach:** 5 - 1.3 = 3.7 m

**Finding ladder length:**
sin 60 = 3.7 / ladder length
root(3)/2 = 3.7 / ladder length
Ladder length = 3.7 x 2 / root(3) = **4.28 m** (approx.)

**Finding distance from pole:**
cot 60 = distance / 3.7
1/root(3) = distance / 3.7
Distance = 3.7 / root(3) = **2.14 m** (approx.)

### Example 3: Observer and Chimney

An observer 1.5 m tall stands 28.5 m away from a chimney. The angle of elevation of the chimney top from her eyes is 45 degrees. Find the chimney's height.

**Important:** The angle is from the observer's **eyes**, not from the ground.

tan 45 = AE / 28.5, where AE is the height above the observer's eye level
1 = AE / 28.5
AE = 28.5 m

Total chimney height = AE + observer's height = 28.5 + 1.5 = **30 m**

### Example 4: Two Angles of Elevation (Flag on Building)

![Right triangle diagram showing a building with a flagstaff on top, observer at point P, with two angles of elevation 30 degrees to the building top and 45 degrees to the flag top](two-angles-of-elevation-flag-building.png)

From a point P, the angle of elevation of a 10 m building is 30 degrees. A flagstaff on top of the building has angle of elevation 45 degrees from the same point. Find the length of the flagstaff and distance from the building.

**Step 1:** Find distance from building.
tan 30 = 10 / distance
1/root(3) = 10 / distance
Distance = 10 root(3) = **17.32 m**

**Step 2:** Find flagstaff length.
Let flagstaff = x. Total height = (10 + x).
tan 45 = (10 + x) / (10 root(3))
1 = (10 + x) / (10 root(3))
10 + x = 10 root(3)
x = 10(root(3) - 1) = 10(1.732 - 1) = **7.32 m**

## Shadow Problems

### Example 5: Tower Shadow

A tower's shadow is 40 m longer when the sun's altitude is 30 degrees than when it is 60 degrees. Find the tower's height.

Let height = h, shorter shadow = x.

**When sun's altitude is 60 degrees:**
tan 60 = h/x, so root(3) = h/x, giving h = x root(3) ... (1)

**When sun's altitude is 30 degrees:**
tan 30 = h/(x + 40), so 1/root(3) = h/(x + 40) ... (2)

Substituting h = x root(3) from (1) into (2):
x root(3) / root(3) = (x + 40)/1... wait, let's redo:
From (2): h = (x + 40)/root(3)
From (1): x root(3) = (x + 40)/root(3)
3x = x + 40
2x = 40, so x = 20

h = 20 root(3) = **34.64 m** (approx.)

## Two Buildings / Angle of Depression Problems

### Example 6: Two Buildings

From the top of a multi-storeyed building, the angles of depression of the top and bottom of an 8 m building are 30 degrees and 45 degrees respectively. Find the height of the taller building and the distance between them.

Let the height of the taller building = h, and distance between buildings = d.

**From bottom:** tan 45 = h/d, so d = h ... (1)

**From top of shorter building:** The height difference = (h - 8)
tan 30 = (h - 8)/d
1/root(3) = (h - 8)/d ... (2)

Substituting d = h from (1):
1/root(3) = (h - 8)/h
h = root(3)(h - 8) = root(3)h - 8 root(3)
h(root(3) - 1) = 8 root(3)
h = 8 root(3) / (root(3) - 1)

Rationalising: h = 8 root(3)(root(3) + 1) / (3 - 1) = 8(3 + root(3)) / 2 = **4(3 + root(3))** m

### Example 7: Bridge Over a River

From a bridge 3 m above the river, the angles of depression of opposite banks are 30 degrees and 45 degrees. Find the width of the river.

**Left bank:** tan 30 = 3/d1, so d1 = 3 root(3) m
**Right bank:** tan 45 = 3/d2, so d2 = 3 m

Width = d1 + d2 = 3 root(3) + 3 = **3(root(3) + 1) m**

## Common Problem Types for Board Exams

### Type 1: Simple Height Finding
Given: distance from base and angle of elevation.
Find: height using tan.

### Type 2: Two Angles of Elevation from Same Point
Given: two objects at different heights, same observation point.
Find: height of each or distance.

### Type 3: Moving Observer
Given: angles of elevation at two different distances.
Find: height or the distance moved.

### Type 4: Angle of Depression Problems
Given: height and angle of depression.
Find: horizontal distance.

### Type 5: Two Buildings
Given: angles of depression from one building to top and bottom of another.
Find: heights and distances.

## Important Tips

1. Always draw a **clear, labelled diagram** before solving.

2. Remember that the **angle of depression** from point A equals the **angle of elevation** from the ground point (alternate interior angles with parallel horizontals).

3. When the observer's height is given (e.g., "a 1.5 m tall boy"), the angle is from the **eyes**, so you need to add the observer's height to the calculated value.

4. Common values you should know by heart:
   - tan 30 = 1/root(3)
   - tan 45 = 1
   - tan 60 = root(3)
   - root(3) = 1.732 (approximately)

5. If a problem involves **two right triangles**, set up equations from each and solve simultaneously.

## Chapter Summary

1. The **line of sight** is the line from the observer's eye to the object being viewed.

2. The **angle of elevation** is formed when we look upward -- it is the angle between the line of sight and the horizontal.

3. The **angle of depression** is formed when we look downward -- it is the angle between the line of sight and the horizontal.

4. Heights and distances of objects can be calculated using **trigonometric ratios** in right triangles formed by the line of sight, the horizontal, and the vertical.
