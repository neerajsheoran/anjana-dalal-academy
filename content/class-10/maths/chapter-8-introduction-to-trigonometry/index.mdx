## Introduction

Imagine you are looking at the top of Qutub Minar. A right triangle can be formed between you, the base of the Minar, and its top. Can you find the height of the Minar without measuring it directly? Yes -- using **trigonometry**!

The word **trigonometry** comes from the Greek words:
- **tri** = three
- **gon** = sides
- **metron** = measure

Trigonometry is the study of relationships between the sides and angles of a triangle. It was first developed by ancient civilizations in Egypt, Babylon, and India. The Indian mathematician **Aryabhata** (476-550 CE) was one of the first to use the concept of "sine" in the way we use it today.

---

## Trigonometric Ratios

Consider a right triangle ABC, right-angled at B. For the acute angle A:

![Right triangle ABC with angle A labeled, showing opposite side (BC), adjacent side (AB), and hypotenuse (AC)](right-triangle-sides-labeled.png)

- The side **opposite** to angle A is **BC**
- The side **adjacent** to angle A is **AB**
- The **hypotenuse** (longest side, opposite the right angle) is **AC**

### The Six Trigonometric Ratios

| Ratio | Definition | Formula |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **sin A** | opposite / hypotenuse | BC / AC |
| **cos A** | adjacent / hypotenuse | AB / AC |
| **tan A** | opposite / adjacent | BC / AB |
| **cosec A** | hypotenuse / opposite | AC / BC |
| **sec A** | hypotenuse / adjacent | AC / AB |
| **cot A** | adjacent / opposite | AB / BC |

### Reciprocal Relationships

- **cosec A = 1 / sin A**
- **sec A = 1 / cos A**
- **cot A = 1 / tan A**

### Quotient Relationships

- **tan A = sin A / cos A**
- **cot A = cos A / sin A**

> **Key idea:** "sin A" does not mean "sin" multiplied by "A". It is a single notation meaning "the sine of angle A". Separated from the angle, "sin" has no meaning by itself.

---

## Finding All Ratios from One

If you know any one trigonometric ratio, you can find all the others using the Pythagoras theorem.

### Worked Example

Given sin A = 1/3, find all other trigonometric ratios.

**Step 1:** sin A = opposite/hypotenuse = BC/AC = 1/3

Let BC = k, then AC = 3k.

**Step 2:** Find AB using Pythagoras theorem:
AB^2 = AC^2 - BC^2 = 9k^2 - k^2 = 8k^2
AB = 2 root(2) k

**Step 3:** Calculate all ratios:
- cos A = AB/AC = 2 root(2) k / 3k = **2 root(2) / 3**
- tan A = BC/AB = k / 2 root(2) k = **1 / 2 root(2)**
- cosec A = 1/sin A = **3**
- sec A = 1/cos A = **3 / 2 root(2)**
- cot A = 1/tan A = **2 root(2)**

> **Key idea:** The trigonometric ratios depend ONLY on the angle, not on the size of the triangle. If you take a bigger or smaller right triangle with the same angle A, the ratios will remain the same (because of similarity of triangles).

## Trigonometric Ratios of Specific Angles

These values must be memorised for the board exam.

### Values for 0, 30, 45, 60, and 90 Degrees

| Angle A | 0 degrees | 30 degrees | 45 degrees | 60 degrees | 90 degrees |
|---------|-----------|------------|------------|------------|------------|
| **sin A** | 0 | 1/2 | 1/root(2) | root(3)/2 | 1 |
| **cos A** | 1 | root(3)/2 | 1/root(2) | 1/2 | 0 |
| **tan A** | 0 | 1/root(3) | 1 | root(3) | not defined |
| **cosec A** | not defined | 2 | root(2) | 2/root(3) | 1 |
| **sec A** | 1 | 2/root(3) | root(2) | 2 | not defined |
| **cot A** | not defined | root(3) | 1 | 1/root(3) | 0 |

### How to Remember: Trick for sin Values

For sin 0, sin 30, sin 45, sin 60, sin 90, think of the pattern:

root(0)/2, root(1)/2, root(2)/2, root(3)/2, root(4)/2

= 0, 1/2, 1/root(2), root(3)/2, 1

For **cos**, the values are the same but in **reverse order**.

### Deriving Values for 45 Degrees

Take an isosceles right triangle with equal sides = 1.
Hypotenuse = root(1^2 + 1^2) = root(2).

- sin 45 = 1/root(2)
- cos 45 = 1/root(2)
- tan 45 = 1

### Deriving Values for 30 and 60 Degrees

![Equilateral triangle with side 2 split into two right triangles by a perpendicular, showing 30-60-90 triangle with sides 1, root 3, and 2](equilateral-triangle-30-60-derivation.png)

Take an equilateral triangle with side 2. Draw a perpendicular from one vertex to the opposite side, creating two right triangles. Each has:
- Hypotenuse = 2, base = 1, height = root(3)

For the 30-degree angle:
- sin 30 = 1/2
- cos 30 = root(3)/2

For the 60-degree angle:
- sin 60 = root(3)/2
- cos 60 = 1/2

## Important Observations

1. The value of **sin A** always lies between **0 and 1** (inclusive) for 0 &lt;= A &lt;= 90.
2. The value of **cos A** always lies between **0 and 1** (inclusive) for 0 &lt;= A &lt;= 90.
3. **sin A increases** from 0 to 1 as A increases from 0 to 90 degrees.
4. **cos A decreases** from 1 to 0 as A increases from 0 to 90 degrees.
5. **tan A increases** from 0 towards infinity as A increases from 0 to 90 degrees.

## Trigonometric Ratios of Complementary Angles

Two angles are **complementary** if their sum is 90 degrees. In a right triangle with angle A, the other acute angle is (90 - A).

### The Complementary Angle Relationships

- **sin(90 - A) = cos A**
- **cos(90 - A) = sin A**
- **tan(90 - A) = cot A**
- **cot(90 - A) = tan A**
- **sec(90 - A) = cosec A**
- **cosec(90 - A) = sec A**

> **Key idea:** The trigonometric ratio of an angle equals the co-ratio of its complement. sin and cos are complementary; tan and cot are complementary; sec and cosec are complementary.

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** Evaluate tan 65 / cot 25.

Since 65 + 25 = 90, they are complementary.
tan 65 = tan(90 - 25) = cot 25

So tan 65 / cot 25 = cot 25 / cot 25 = **1**

**Example 2:** If sin A = cos B, find A + B.

sin A = cos B = sin(90 - B)
So A = 90 - B
**A + B = 90 degrees**

**Example 3:** Express cot 85 + cos 75 in terms of ratios of angles between 0 and 45 degrees.

cot 85 = cot(90 - 5) = tan 5
cos 75 = cos(90 - 15) = sin 15

So the answer is **tan 5 + sin 15**

## Trigonometric Identities

An equation involving trigonometric ratios that is true for **all** values of the angle is called a **trigonometric identity**.

### The Three Fundamental Identities

**Identity 1:** **sin^2 A + cos^2 A = 1**

This follows directly from the Pythagoras theorem:
In triangle ABC, BC^2 + AB^2 = AC^2
Dividing by AC^2: (BC/AC)^2 + (AB/AC)^2 = 1
So sin^2 A + cos^2 A = 1

**Identity 2:** **1 + tan^2 A = sec^2 A** (valid for 0 &lt;= A &lt; 90)

Dividing the Pythagoras equation by AB^2:
(BC/AB)^2 + 1 = (AC/AB)^2
tan^2 A + 1 = sec^2 A

**Identity 3:** **1 + cot^2 A = cosec^2 A** (valid for 0 &lt; A &lt;= 90)

Dividing the Pythagoras equation by BC^2:
1 + (AB/BC)^2 = (AC/BC)^2
1 + cot^2 A = cosec^2 A

> **Key idea:** All three identities come from the Pythagoras theorem. They are extremely useful in simplifying trigonometric expressions and proving other identities.

### Useful Rearrangements

From Identity 1:
- sin^2 A = 1 - cos^2 A
- cos^2 A = 1 - sin^2 A

From Identity 2:
- tan^2 A = sec^2 A - 1
- sec^2 A - tan^2 A = 1

From Identity 3:
- cot^2 A = cosec^2 A - 1
- cosec^2 A - cot^2 A = 1

## Proving Trigonometric Identities

### Tips for Proving Identities

1. Start with the more complicated side (usually LHS)
2. Convert everything to **sin** and **cos** if stuck
3. Use the three fundamental identities
4. Look for opportunities to **factorise** or use algebraic identities like a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)

### Worked Example 1: Prove that (1 - cos^2 A) cosec^2 A = 1

LHS = (1 - cos^2 A) cosec^2 A
= sin^2 A x (1/sin^2 A) [using Identity 1]
= 1 = RHS

### Worked Example 2: Prove that (1 + cot^2 A)/(1 + tan^2 A) = cot^2 A

LHS = cosec^2 A / sec^2 A [using Identities 2 and 3]
= (1/sin^2 A) / (1/cos^2 A)
= cos^2 A / sin^2 A
= cot^2 A = RHS

### Worked Example 3: Prove that (sin A + cosec A)^2 + (cos A + sec A)^2 = 7 + tan^2 A + cot^2 A

LHS = sin^2 A + 2 sin A cosec A + cosec^2 A + cos^2 A + 2 cos A sec A + sec^2 A
= (sin^2 A + cos^2 A) + 2(1) + cosec^2 A + 2(1) + sec^2 A
= 1 + 2 + (1 + cot^2 A) + 2 + (1 + tan^2 A)
= 7 + tan^2 A + cot^2 A = RHS

## Chapter Summary

1. **Trigonometric ratios** of angle A in a right triangle:
   - sin A = opposite/hypotenuse, cos A = adjacent/hypotenuse, tan A = opposite/adjacent

2. **Reciprocal ratios:** cosec A = 1/sin A, sec A = 1/cos A, cot A = 1/tan A

3. **Quotient relations:** tan A = sin A/cos A, cot A = cos A/sin A

4. If one ratio is known, all others can be determined using the Pythagoras theorem.

5. **Standard values** for 0, 30, 45, 60, and 90 degrees must be memorised.

6. sin A and cos A never exceed 1; sec A and cosec A are always >= 1.

7. **Complementary angle relations:** sin(90-A) = cos A, cos(90-A) = sin A, etc.

8. **Three fundamental identities:**
   - sin^2 A + cos^2 A = 1
   - 1 + tan^2 A = sec^2 A
   - 1 + cot^2 A = cosec^2 A
