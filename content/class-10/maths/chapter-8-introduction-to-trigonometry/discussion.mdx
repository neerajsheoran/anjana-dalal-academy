## The Six Trigonometric Ratios

**Kiran:** Papa, I keep mixing up which ratio is which. sin, cos, tan, and then their reciprocals — how do I keep them straight?

**Ravi:** Use the mnemonic "SOH-CAH-TOA." Sin equals Opposite over Hypotenuse. Cos equals Adjacent over Hypotenuse. Tan equals Opposite over Adjacent. For the reciprocals, cosec is the reciprocal of sin, sec is the reciprocal of cos, and cot is the reciprocal of tan.

**Kiran:** And "opposite" and "adjacent" change depending on which angle I am looking at?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The hypotenuse is always the side opposite the right angle — it never changes. But opposite and adjacent are relative to the acute angle you are considering. If you switch to the other acute angle, opposite and adjacent swap. This is actually why the complementary angle formulas work — sin of an angle equals cos of its complement.

**Kiran:** Aryabhata worked on trigonometry, right?

**Ravi:** Yes. Aryabhata, in the 5th century CE, introduced the concept of "ardha-jya" which became our modern sine function. Indian mathematicians were pioneers of trigonometry. The word "sine" itself derives from a long chain of translations of the Sanskrit word "jya." This is a matter of genuine pride.

## Standard Angle Values

**Kiran:** The table of values for 0, 30, 45, 60, and 90 degrees — I need to memorise the whole thing?

**Ravi:** You only need to memorise the sine row. Use the pattern: root 0 over 2, root 1 over 2, root 2 over 2, root 3 over 2, root 4 over 2. That gives you 0, one-half, 1 over root 2, root 3 over 2, and 1. The cosine values are the same row in reverse order. And tan equals sin divided by cos, so you can compute those on the spot.

**Kiran:** That pattern is really elegant. And for cosec, sec, cot — just take reciprocals?

**Ravi:** Yes. But remember that sin 0 is zero, so cosec 0 is undefined — you cannot divide by zero. Similarly, cos 90 is zero, so sec 90 and tan 90 are undefined. The board exam sometimes tests whether you know which values are undefined.

**Kiran:** How do I derive the 30-60 values if I forget the table?

**Ravi:** Start with an equilateral triangle of side 2. Drop a perpendicular from one vertex to the opposite side. You get a right triangle with hypotenuse 2, base 1, and height root 3. The 30-degree angle is at the top, the 60-degree angle is at the base. Read off the ratios directly.

## Complementary Angles

**Kiran:** sin of 90 minus A equals cos A — this means sin 60 equals cos 30?

**Ravi:** Yes. And tan 65 equals cot 25, sec 70 equals cosec 20, and so on. Whenever an angle and its complement appear in the same expression, use these identities to simplify. A common board exam question is to evaluate an expression like sin 50 over cos 40 — since 50 and 40 are complementary, sin 50 equals cos 40, so the answer is 1.

**Kiran:** What about expressing cot 85 in terms of angles less than 45?

**Ravi:** cot 85 equals cot of 90 minus 5, which equals tan 5. Similarly, cos 75 equals sin 15. These conversions are standard 1-mark questions. The rule is simple — the "co" in cosine, cotangent, and cosecant stands for "complement."

## Trigonometric Identities

**Kiran:** The three identities — sin squared A plus cos squared A equals 1, 1 plus tan squared A equals sec squared A, and 1 plus cot squared A equals cosec squared A — they all come from Pythagoras?

**Ravi:** Yes. Each one is the Pythagorean theorem divided by a different side squared. Divide by the hypotenuse squared to get the first. Divide by the adjacent side squared to get the second. Divide by the opposite side squared to get the third. They are the same theorem in three disguises.

**Kiran:** For proving identities in the exam, should I work on both sides or just one?

**Ravi:** Best practice is to start with the more complex side and simplify it to match the simpler side. If you are stuck, convert everything to sin and cos — that is a universally effective strategy. Also look for algebraic identities like a squared minus b squared equals a minus b times a plus b.

**Kiran:** The identity proofs carry good marks in boards?

**Ravi:** Yes, typically 3-4 marks. Write each step clearly with the identity or formula you are using. CBSE examiners award step marks, so even a partially correct proof earns partial credit. Practice at least 15-20 identity proofs before the exam.

## Board Exam Tips

**Kiran:** What are the most tested question types?

**Ravi:** Four types dominate. First, evaluating expressions using standard values — like find sin 60 cos 30 plus sin 30 cos 60. Second, proving identities — usually one 3-4 mark question. Third, complementary angle simplification. Fourth, finding all ratios when one is given.

**Kiran:** Any mistakes to watch for?

**Ravi:** Do not write sin A as sin times A — it is a single function notation. Do not confuse sin squared A, which means the quantity sin A whole squared, with sin of A squared. And always specify the angle — sin by itself has no meaning. These notational errors cost marks unnecessarily.

**Kiran:** How does this chapter connect to JEE and higher mathematics?

**Ravi:** Trigonometry is one of the most heavily weighted topics in JEE Mathematics. In Class 11, you will extend trigonometric ratios to all angles using the unit circle, study trigonometric equations, and learn inverse trigonometric functions. In Class 12, trigonometric substitutions appear in calculus. The identities and standard values you memorise now are used throughout. Aryabhata's original work on sine tables laid the groundwork for everything you study here — a tradition of mathematical excellence spanning fifteen centuries.
