## Introduction

In earlier classes, you learnt how to find the surface areas and volumes of basic solids -- cuboids, cylinders, cones, and spheres. In real life, however, most objects are **combinations** of these basic shapes.

For example:
- A **truck container** is a cylinder with two hemispherical ends
- A **test tube** is a cylinder with a hemispherical bottom
- An **ice cream cone** with a scoop is a cone with a hemispherical top
- A **gulab jamun** is a cylinder with two hemispherical ends

![Examples of combined solids: truck container (cylinder with hemispheres), test tube (cylinder with hemisphere), ice cream cone (cone with hemisphere)](combined-solids-examples.png)

In this chapter, we learn how to find the surface areas and volumes of such **combined solids**.

---

## Recap: Formulas for Basic Solids

### Cuboid (length l, breadth b, height h)
- **TSA** = 2(lb + bh + hl)
- **Volume** = l x b x h

### Cylinder (radius r, height h)
- **CSA** = 2 pi r h
- **TSA** = 2 pi r(r + h)
- **Volume** = pi r^2 h

### Cone (radius r, height h, slant height l)
- **CSA** = pi r l
- **TSA** = pi r(r + l)
- **Volume** = (1/3) pi r^2 h
- **Slant height** l = root(r^2 + h^2)

### Sphere (radius r)
- **SA** = 4 pi r^2
- **Volume** = (4/3) pi r^3

### Hemisphere (radius r)
- **CSA** = 2 pi r^2
- **TSA** = 3 pi r^2
- **Volume** = (2/3) pi r^3

> **Key idea:** Keep these formulas at your fingertips. Every problem in this chapter uses these basic formulas in combination.

## Surface Area of Combination of Solids

When two or more basic solids are combined, the total surface area of the new object is the **sum of the curved (or flat) surfaces that are visible**.

### Important Rule

When two solids are joined, the surfaces at the **junction are hidden** and should NOT be counted. Only count the surfaces that are **exposed**.

### Worked Example 1: Playing Top (Lattu)

A playing top is shaped like a cone topped by a hemisphere. Total height = 5 cm, diameter = 3.5 cm.

**Step 1:** Identify the surfaces.
- CSA of the hemisphere (the dome on top)
- CSA of the cone (the body)
- The flat circular base where they join is **hidden**

**Step 2:** Calculate.
- Radius r = 3.5/2 = 1.75 cm
- Height of hemisphere = r = 1.75 cm
- Height of cone = 5 - 1.75 = 3.25 cm
- Slant height of cone l = root(1.75^2 + 3.25^2) = root(3.0625 + 10.5625) = root(13.625) = 3.69 cm (approx.)
- CSA of hemisphere = 2 pi r^2 = 2 x (22/7) x 1.75^2 = 19.25 cm^2
- CSA of cone = pi r l = (22/7) x 1.75 x 3.69 = 20.27 cm^2 (approx.)

**Total surface area = 19.25 + 20.27 = 39.52 cm^2** (approx.)

### Worked Example 2: Medicine Capsule

A capsule is a cylinder with hemispherical ends. Length = 14 mm, diameter = 5 mm.

- Radius r = 2.5 mm
- Length of cylindrical part = 14 - 2(2.5) = 9 mm
- CSA of cylinder = 2 pi r h = 2 x pi x 2.5 x 9
- CSA of 2 hemispheres = 2 x 2 pi r^2 = 4 pi r^2 = 4 pi x 6.25

Total surface area = 2 pi x 2.5 x 9 + 4 pi x 6.25 = pi(45 + 25) = **70 pi mm^2** = 220 mm^2 (approx.)

## Volume of Combination of Solids

The volume of a combined solid is simply the **sum of the volumes** of its individual parts.

### Worked Example 1: Toy (Cone on Hemisphere)

A toy is made by mounting a cone on a hemisphere. Radius = 1 cm, height of cone = 1 cm.

Volume = Volume of cone + Volume of hemisphere
= (1/3) pi r^2 h + (2/3) pi r^3
= (1/3) pi (1)^2 (1) + (2/3) pi (1)^3
= pi/3 + 2pi/3
= **pi cm^3** (= 3.14 cm^3)

### Worked Example 2: Gulab Jamun

A gulab jamun is shaped like a cylinder with hemispherical ends. Length = 5 cm, diameter = 2.8 cm.

- Radius r = 1.4 cm
- Length of cylindrical part = 5 - 2(1.4) = 2.2 cm

Volume of one gulab jamun = pi r^2 h + 2 x (2/3) pi r^3
= pi(1.4)^2(2.2) + (4/3) pi (1.4)^3
= pi x 1.96 x 2.2 + (4/3) pi x 2.744
= pi(4.312 + 3.659)
= **25.05 cm^3** (approx.)

For 45 gulab jamuns, sugar syrup at 30% of volume:
= 45 x 25.05 x 0.30 = **338.18 cm^3** (approx.)

## Conversion of One Solid into Another

When a solid is **melted** and **recast** into another shape, the **volume remains the same**. Only the shape changes.

> **Key idea:** In conversion problems, equate the volumes of the original and new solids.

### Worked Example 1: Spherical Balls from a Cylinder

A metallic sphere of radius 4.2 cm is melted and recast into a cylinder of radius 6 cm. Find the height of the cylinder.

Volume of sphere = Volume of cylinder
(4/3) pi (4.2)^3 = pi (6)^2 h
(4/3)(74.088) = 36h
98.784 = 36h
**h = 2.744 cm**

### Worked Example 2: How Many Coins from a Cuboid?

A metallic cuboid (15 cm x 11 cm x 5 cm) is melted into circular coins of diameter 1.4 cm and thickness 0.2 cm.

Volume of cuboid = 15 x 11 x 5 = 825 cm^3

Volume of one coin = pi x (0.7)^2 x 0.2 = (22/7) x 0.49 x 0.2 = 0.308 cm^3

Number of coins = 825 / 0.308 = **2678 coins** (approx.)

### Worked Example 3: Water Flow in a Pipe

Water flows through a cylindrical pipe of internal diameter 2 cm at 0.7 m/s into a cylindrical tank of diameter 40 cm. How long will it take to raise the water level by 24 cm?

Volume of water needed = pi x (20)^2 x 24 cm^3
Volume flowing per second = pi x (1)^2 x 70 cm^3 (0.7 m = 70 cm)

Time = pi x 400 x 24 / (pi x 70) = 9600/70 = **137.14 seconds** (approx.)

## Frustum of a Cone

![Cone being cut by a plane parallel to its base, producing a frustum with labeled dimensions r1 (larger radius), r2 (smaller radius), h (height), and l (slant height)](frustum-of-cone-labeled.png)

When a cone is cut by a plane parallel to its base, the part between the base and the cutting plane is called a **frustum**.

Think of a bucket, a flower pot, or the lower portion of a lampshade -- these are all frustums.

### Dimensions of a Frustum

A frustum has:
- **r1** = radius of the larger base (bottom)
- **r2** = radius of the smaller base (top)
- **h** = height (perpendicular distance between the two bases)
- **l** = slant height = root(h^2 + (r1 - r2)^2)

### Formulas for a Frustum

- **CSA** = pi (r1 + r2) l
- **TSA** = pi (r1 + r2) l + pi r1^2 + pi r2^2
- **Volume** = (1/3) pi h (r1^2 + r2^2 + r1 x r2)

### Worked Example: The Bucket

A bucket is in the form of a frustum with top diameter 30 cm, bottom diameter 20 cm, and height 35 cm. Find its capacity.

r1 = 15 cm, r2 = 10 cm, h = 35 cm

Volume = (1/3) x (22/7) x 35 x (15^2 + 10^2 + 15 x 10)
= (1/3) x (22/7) x 35 x (225 + 100 + 150)
= (1/3) x (22/7) x 35 x 475
= (22 x 5 x 475) / 3
= **24583.33 cm^3** (approx.)

Converting to litres: 24583.33 / 1000 = **24.58 litres** (approx.)

## Tips for Board Exam Problems

1. **Draw a diagram** of the solid and label all dimensions clearly.

2. **Identify which basic solids** make up the combination.

3. For **surface area**: add only the **visible** surfaces (exclude joined faces).

4. For **volume**: add the volumes of all parts.

5. For **conversion problems**: set volume of original = volume of new shape.

6. **Always check units**: if the answer is asked in litres, convert cm^3 to litres (1 litre = 1000 cm^3).

7. Remember: **1 m^3 = 1000 litres** and **1 cm^3 = 1 ml**.

## Chapter Summary

1. The **surface area** of a combined solid is found by adding the surface areas of individual visible surfaces.

2. The **volume** of a combined solid is the sum of volumes of its individual parts.

3. When a solid is melted and recast, **volume is conserved**.

4. **Frustum of a cone** formulas:
   - CSA = pi(r1 + r2)l
   - TSA = pi(r1 + r2)l + pi r1^2 + pi r2^2
   - Volume = (1/3) pi h(r1^2 + r2^2 + r1 r2)
   - Slant height l = root(h^2 + (r1 - r2)^2)
