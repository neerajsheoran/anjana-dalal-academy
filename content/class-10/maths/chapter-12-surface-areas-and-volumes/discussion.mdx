## Combination of Solids

**Kiran:** Papa, almost every object around us is a combination of basic shapes. A pencil is a cylinder plus a cone. A capsule is a cylinder plus two hemispheres.

**Ravi:** Exactly. This chapter teaches you to see real objects as combinations of cuboids, cylinders, cones, spheres, and hemispheres. A gulab jamun is a cylinder with hemispherical ends. An ice cream cone with a scoop is a cone with a hemisphere on top. Even a rocket has a conical nose, a cylindrical body, and possibly hemispherical fuel tanks.

**Kiran:** ISRO's rockets must involve these calculations constantly.

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Engineers at ISRO calculate the volume of fuel tanks, the surface area for heat shielding, and the material needed for each component — all using the formulas from this chapter. When Chandrayaan's lander was designed, every surface and volume had to be computed precisely.

## Surface Area of Combined Solids

**Kiran:** When I join a cone on top of a hemisphere, do I add both total surface areas?

![Cone joined on top of a hemisphere, with the hidden circular junction area crossed out, showing only the visible CSA of each part](combined-solid-hidden-surface.png)

**Ravi:** No, and this is the most important concept. When two solids are joined, the surfaces at the junction are hidden and should not be counted. For a cone on a hemisphere, the flat circular base of the cone and the flat circular base of the hemisphere are joined together — both are hidden. You count only the curved surface area of the cone plus the curved surface area of the hemisphere.

**Kiran:** So the total surface area is CSA of cone plus CSA of hemisphere?

**Ravi:** Yes. The general rule is: add up all the surfaces that are visible from the outside. The joined surfaces are internal and invisible. Students who add total surface areas without subtracting the hidden parts lose marks consistently.

**Kiran:** What if the radii at the junction are different?

**Ravi:** That typically does not happen in standard problems — the joining surfaces have the same radius. But if they differ, you would need to account for an annular ring at the junction. CBSE problems keep the radii equal at joints.

## Volume of Combined Solids and Conversion Problems

**Kiran:** For volume, I just add the individual volumes — no subtraction needed?

**Ravi:** Correct. Unlike surface area, volume is additive. The volume of a cone on a hemisphere is simply the volume of the cone plus the volume of the hemisphere. There are no hidden volumes.

**Kiran:** The conversion problems — where a solid is melted and recast — rely on volume conservation?

**Ravi:** Yes. When a metallic sphere is melted and recast into a cylinder, the volume of the sphere equals the volume of the cylinder. Set them equal and solve for the unknown dimension. Similarly, when you melt a cuboid into coins, divide the cuboid volume by one coin's volume to find the number of coins.

**Kiran:** The water flow problem where water flows through a pipe into a tank — that is also volume conservation?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The volume of water flowing per second through the pipe equals pi times the pipe radius squared times the flow speed. The volume needed to fill the tank to a certain height equals pi times the tank radius squared times the height. Set the total flow equal to the required volume and solve for time.

## Frustum of a Cone

**Kiran:** A frustum looks like a bucket — a cone with the top sliced off?

**Ravi:** Yes. When you cut a cone by a plane parallel to its base, the portion between the base and the cut is a frustum. Buckets, lampshades, and flower pots are all frustums. The key formulas involve both radii — r1 for the larger base and r2 for the smaller base.

**Kiran:** The volume formula looks complicated — one-third pi h times the quantity r1 squared plus r2 squared plus r1 times r2.

**Ravi:** It is derived by subtracting the smaller cone from the larger cone. You do not need to memorise the derivation for boards, but understanding it helps you remember the formula. Notice the three terms inside — r1 squared, r2 squared, and r1 times r2. If r2 becomes zero, you get the standard cone formula.

**Kiran:** The slant height formula is root of h squared plus the quantity r1 minus r2 squared?

**Ravi:** Yes. It is analogous to the cone's slant height formula but with r1 minus r2 replacing r. This is needed for the curved surface area calculation.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What types of problems appear most frequently?

**Ravi:** Three main types. First, find the surface area or volume of a combined solid — 4-5 marks. Second, conversion problems where one solid is melted into another — 3-4 marks. Third, frustum problems involving buckets or containers — 3-4 marks. This chapter carries significant weight, typically 5-6 marks.

**Kiran:** Mistakes to avoid?

**Ravi:** First, using diameter instead of radius — halve every diameter. Second, forgetting to exclude hidden surfaces in TSA calculations. Third, unit conversion errors — 1 litre equals 1000 cubic centimetres, 1 cubic metre equals 1000 litres. Fourth, calculation errors with pi — keep pi as 22 over 7 throughout and simplify fractions at the end. Write the formula, substitute, and compute step by step.

**Kiran:** Is this chapter useful beyond the exam?

**Ravi:** Very much. Civil engineers calculate the volume of water tanks, the surface area for painting buildings, and the material needed for cylindrical pillars. Pharmaceutical companies calculate capsule volumes for drug dosing. Food companies design packaging using these exact formulas. India's rapid urbanisation means architects and engineers use these calculations daily for every building, bridge, and water tank in the country. The practical value of this chapter extends far beyond the classroom.
