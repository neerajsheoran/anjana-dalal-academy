## The Human Eye

The **human eye** is a remarkable optical instrument that enables us to see the colourful world around us. It is roughly spherical in shape, about 2.3 cm in diameter.

![Labelled diagram of the human eye showing cornea, iris, pupil, eye lens, ciliary muscles, retina (with rod and cone cells), optic nerve, and vitreous humour](human-eye-structure-labeled.png)

### Structure of the Eye

- **Cornea** — transparent, curved front surface that refracts most of the light entering the eye
- **Iris** — coloured, muscular diaphragm behind the cornea that controls the size of the pupil
- **Pupil** — the dark circular opening in the iris through which light enters; it dilates in dim light and constricts in bright light
- **Eye lens** — a transparent, biconvex, crystalline lens that fine-focuses light onto the retina. Its focal length can be adjusted by **ciliary muscles**
- **Retina** — the light-sensitive screen at the back of the eye containing rod cells (dim light/peripheral vision) and cone cells (colour vision/bright light)
- **Optic nerve** — carries electrical signals from the retina to the brain

The image formed on the retina is **real and inverted**. The brain interprets it as erect.

> **Key idea:** The eye lens focuses light onto the retina, forming a real, inverted image. The iris controls the amount of light entering through the pupil.

## Power of Accommodation

The ability of the eye lens to change its focal length to focus on objects at different distances is called the **power of accommodation**.

- For **distant objects:** Ciliary muscles relax, the lens becomes thin and flat, and focal length increases
- For **near objects:** Ciliary muscles contract, the lens becomes thick and bulgy, and focal length decreases

The **least distance of distinct vision** (near point) for a normal eye is about **25 cm**. The **far point** is at **infinity**.

> **Key idea:** The ciliary muscles change the curvature and focal length of the eye lens to focus on objects at various distances. This is the power of accommodation.

## Defects of Vision and Their Correction

![Two eye defects side by side: myopia (image forms in front of retina, corrected by concave lens) and hypermetropia (image forms behind retina, corrected by convex lens)](myopia-hypermetropia-correction.png)

### Myopia (Short-sightedness/Near-sightedness)

A person can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects clearly. The image of distant objects forms **in front of the retina**.

**Causes:** Excessive curvature of the eye lens, or elongation of the eyeball.
**Correction:** Using a **concave lens** (diverging lens) of appropriate power that diverges the incoming rays so that the image falls on the retina.

### Hypermetropia (Long-sightedness/Far-sightedness)

A person can see distant objects clearly but cannot see nearby objects clearly. The image of nearby objects forms **behind the retina**.

**Causes:** Focal length of eye lens is too long, or the eyeball is too short.
**Correction:** Using a **convex lens** (converging lens) of appropriate power.

### Presbyopia

With ageing, the ciliary muscles weaken and the eye lens becomes less flexible, reducing the power of accommodation. The near point gradually moves further away.

**Correction:** Using **bifocal lenses** — concave for upper part (distance vision) and convex for lower part (near vision).

> **Key idea:** Myopia is corrected with concave lenses, hypermetropia with convex lenses, and presbyopia with bifocal lenses.

## Refraction of Light Through a Prism

When a ray of white light passes through a glass **prism**, it gets refracted twice (at entry and exit surfaces) and bends towards the base. The angle between the incident ray and the emergent ray is called the **angle of deviation**.

### Dispersion of White Light

A prism splits white light into its constituent colours — a band called the **spectrum**: **Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red** (VIBGYOR).

![White light entering a glass prism and splitting into the spectrum (VIBGYOR): violet bends most at the bottom and red bends least at the top](prism-dispersion-white-light-spectrum.png)

This splitting is called **dispersion**. It occurs because the prism refracts different colours by different amounts — **violet** is refracted the most and **red** the least.

**Newton's experiment:** Newton recombined the spectrum using a second inverted prism to show that white light is composed of seven colours.

> **Key idea:** A prism disperses white light into seven colours (VIBGYOR) because different colours have different speeds in glass and are refracted by different amounts.

## Atmospheric Refraction

The earth's atmosphere has varying densities — denser near the surface, rarer higher up. Light from distant objects passes through these layers and bends gradually, causing several phenomena:

### Twinkling of Stars

Stars appear to twinkle because starlight passes through layers of the atmosphere with varying refractive indices. The continuous change in atmospheric conditions causes the apparent position and brightness of stars to fluctuate, making them twinkle.

### Why Planets Do Not Twinkle

Planets are much closer to Earth than stars and appear as extended sources of light (not point sources). The fluctuations from different parts of the planet's disc average out, so planets appear steady.

### Advanced Sunrise and Delayed Sunset

The Sun appears about **2 minutes before actual sunrise** and about **2 minutes after actual sunset** because atmospheric refraction bends sunlight so that it reaches us even when the Sun is slightly below the horizon.

> **Key idea:** Atmospheric refraction causes twinkling of stars, steadiness of planets, and the apparent advance of sunrise and delay of sunset.

## Scattering of Light

When sunlight enters the atmosphere, fine particles scatter different colours differently. This is called **scattering of light**.

### Tyndall Effect

The scattering of light by colloidal particles is called the **Tyndall effect**. It makes the path of a beam of light visible through a colloidal solution or through smoke/mist.

### Why Is the Sky Blue?

Fine molecules in the atmosphere scatter shorter wavelengths (blue) much more than longer wavelengths (red). This is called **Rayleigh scattering**. The scattered blue light reaches our eyes from all directions, making the sky appear blue.

### Why Does the Sky Appear Dark from Space?

In outer space, there is no atmosphere to scatter light. Without scattering, the sky appears **dark** (black) to astronauts.

### Why Is the Sun Reddish at Sunrise and Sunset?

At sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels through a much thicker layer of the atmosphere. Most of the blue light is scattered away, and only the longer wavelength red light reaches our eyes, making the Sun appear reddish.

> **Key idea:** Scattering of shorter wavelengths (blue) by atmospheric molecules makes the sky blue. At sunrise/sunset, the longer path through the atmosphere scatters away blue, leaving red.
