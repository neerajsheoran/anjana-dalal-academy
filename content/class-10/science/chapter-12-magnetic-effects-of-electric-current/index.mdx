## Introduction

In 1820, Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a compass needle gets deflected when placed near a current-carrying conductor. This showed that **electricity and magnetism are related** — electric current produces a magnetic field around it.

> **Key idea:** A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it, establishing the link between electricity and magnetism.

---

## Magnetic Field and Field Lines

A **magnetic field** is a region around a magnet (or current-carrying conductor) where a magnetic force can be experienced. It is represented by **magnetic field lines**.

Properties of magnetic field lines:
- They emerge from the **north pole** and merge at the **south pole** (outside the magnet)
- Inside the magnet, they run from south to north
- They are **closed curves** (continuous loops)
- They **never intersect** each other
- The **closeness** of field lines indicates the **strength** of the field (closer = stronger)

> **Key idea:** Magnetic field lines show the direction and strength of the magnetic field. They are closed loops that never cross each other.

---

## Magnetic Field Due to a Current-Carrying Conductor

### Straight Conductor

A current-carrying straight conductor produces a magnetic field in the form of **concentric circles** around it. The field strength decreases with distance from the conductor.

**Right-Hand Thumb Rule:** If you grip the conductor with your right hand such that the thumb points in the direction of current, the curled fingers indicate the direction of the magnetic field lines.

### Circular Loop (Coil)

A current-carrying circular loop produces a magnetic field that resembles a bar magnet at large distances. At the centre of the loop, the field lines are nearly straight and parallel, giving a nearly uniform field.

Every point on the wire carrying current contributes to the magnetic field at the centre. The field strength at the centre depends on the **number of turns**, the **current**, and the **radius** of the coil.

### Solenoid

A **solenoid** is a coil of many circular turns wound closely. When carrying current, it produces a magnetic field similar to a **bar magnet** — with a north and south pole at either end.

Inside the solenoid, the field is **uniform** (parallel and equally spaced field lines). The strength depends on the number of turns, current, and the core material.

An **electromagnet** is a solenoid with a soft iron core inside, which greatly increases the magnetic field strength. Electromagnets can be switched on/off and their strength can be varied — advantages over permanent magnets.

> **Key idea:** A solenoid behaves like a bar magnet. Adding a soft iron core creates a powerful electromagnet whose strength can be controlled.

## Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a **force**. This is the principle behind **electric motors**.

**Fleming's Left-Hand Rule:** Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the left hand mutually perpendicular to each other:
- **Forefinger** → direction of the magnetic **Field** (B)
- **Middle finger** → direction of the **Current** (I)
- **Thumb** → direction of the **Force/Motion** (F)

The force is maximum when the current is **perpendicular** to the magnetic field and zero when they are **parallel**.

> **Key idea:** A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force whose direction is given by Fleming's left-hand rule. This principle is used in electric motors.

## Electric Motor

An **electric motor** converts **electrical energy into mechanical energy** (rotational motion).

**Principle:** A current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a force that makes it rotate.

**Components:**
- **Armature coil** — rectangular coil of insulated copper wire
- **Field magnets** — permanent magnets providing the magnetic field
- **Split ring commutator** — reverses the direction of current every half rotation to keep the coil rotating in the same direction
- **Brushes** — carbon brushes maintain electrical contact with the rotating commutator

**Uses:** Electric fans, washing machines, water pumps, mixers, computer hard drives.

> **Key idea:** An electric motor uses the force on a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field to produce rotation. The split ring commutator reverses current direction every half turn.

## Electromagnetic Induction

**Electromagnetic induction** is the process of generating an electric current (or EMF) by changing the magnetic field around a conductor. Discovered by **Michael Faraday**.

When a magnet is moved towards or away from a coil, or when a current-carrying coil is moved near another coil, an electric current is **induced** in the coil. This current is called an **induced current**.

**Conditions for induction:**
- There must be **relative motion** between the magnet and the coil (or change in magnetic field)
- The induced current **direction reverses** when the direction of motion is reversed

**Fleming's Right-Hand Rule:** Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the right hand mutually perpendicular:
- **Forefinger** → direction of the magnetic **Field**
- **Thumb** → direction of **Motion** of the conductor
- **Middle finger** → direction of the **Induced current**

> **Key idea:** A changing magnetic field induces an electric current in a conductor. The direction of induced current is given by Fleming's right-hand rule.

## Electric Generator

An **electric generator** converts **mechanical energy into electrical energy** — the reverse of a motor.

**Principle:** Electromagnetic induction — when a coil rotates in a magnetic field, the changing magnetic flux induces an EMF/current.

### Types
- **AC generator:** Produces **alternating current** (current that reverses direction periodically). Uses slip rings.
- **DC generator:** Produces **direct current** (current that flows in one direction). Uses a split ring commutator.

In India, AC changes direction **100 times per second** (frequency = 50 Hz). The advantage of AC is that it can be easily **stepped up** (increased voltage) or **stepped down** using transformers for efficient long-distance transmission.

> **Key idea:** Generators convert mechanical energy to electrical energy using electromagnetic induction. AC generators use slip rings, DC generators use split ring commutators.

## Domestic Electric Circuits

In India, the supply voltage is **220 V AC** at **50 Hz**. The domestic circuit has three wires:

- **Live wire (red)** — carries current at high potential
- **Neutral wire (black)** — at roughly zero potential
- **Earth wire (green)** — connected to the ground for safety

### Safety Devices

- **Fuse** — a thin wire of low melting point that melts and breaks the circuit when current exceeds the safe limit
- **MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker)** — automatically switches off when current exceeds limits
- **Earthing** — prevents electric shock by providing a low-resistance path to ground

**Short circuit** occurs when live and neutral wires come in direct contact, causing excessive current. Overloading occurs when too many appliances draw current simultaneously.

> **Key idea:** Domestic circuits use three wires (live, neutral, earth) with safety devices (fuse, MCB, earthing) to prevent damage from short circuits and overloading.
