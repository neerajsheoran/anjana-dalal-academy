## Introduction

Living organisms respond to changes in their environment through controlled movements. These responses require systems for **control and coordination**. In animals, this is achieved through the **nervous system** and the **endocrine (hormonal) system**. In plants, coordination is achieved through **hormones** (phytohormones).

> **Key idea:** Control and coordination ensure that organisms respond appropriately to environmental changes through nervous impulses (fast, precise) and hormonal signals (slower, longer-lasting).

---

## Animals — Nervous System

### Nerve Cells (Neurons)

The **neuron** (nerve cell) is the functional unit of the nervous system. Its structure includes:

![Labelled diagram of a neuron showing dendrites, cell body with nucleus, axon covered with myelin sheath, and nerve endings (axon terminals) at the synapse](neuron-structure-labeled.png)

- **Dendrite** — short, branched projections that receive information (stimuli) and create an impulse
- **Cell body (cyton)** — contains the nucleus; processes the impulse
- **Axon** — long fibre that transmits the impulse away from the cell body
- **Nerve ending** — terminal branches of the axon that pass the impulse to the next neuron or to a muscle/gland

Information from the environment is detected by specialised tips of nerve cells called **receptors**, located in sense organs:
- **Gustatory receptors** — detect taste
- **Olfactory receptors** — detect smell
- **Photoreceptors** — detect light (in eyes)

### Transmission of Nerve Impulse

The impulse travels as an **electrochemical signal**. At the dendrite, a chemical reaction creates an electrical impulse that travels through the cell body and along the axon. At the junction between two neurons (called a **synapse**), the impulse triggers the release of **chemical substances (neurotransmitters)** that cross the gap and start a new impulse in the next neuron.

> **Key idea:** Neurons transmit information as electrochemical impulses. At synapses, chemical neurotransmitters bridge the gap between neurons to continue the signal.

## Reflex Actions and Reflex Arc

A **reflex action** is a rapid, automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that does not involve conscious thought. Examples include withdrawing your hand from a hot object, blinking when something comes close to your eyes.

The pathway of a reflex action is called a **reflex arc**:

![Reflex arc diagram showing the pathway: receptor in skin detects stimulus, sensory neuron carries signal to spinal cord, relay neuron processes it, motor neuron sends response to effector muscle](reflex-arc-pathway.png)

**Receptor → Sensory (afferent) neuron → Spinal cord (relay neuron) → Motor (efferent) neuron → Effector (muscle/gland)**

Reflex actions are controlled by the **spinal cord**, not the brain, which is why they are so fast. The brain is only informed of the action after it has occurred.

The reflex arc allows organisms to respond to danger quickly without waiting for the brain to process the information.

> **Key idea:** Reflex actions are rapid, involuntary responses controlled by the spinal cord through reflex arcs. They protect the body from harm by responding faster than conscious thought allows.

## Human Brain

The **brain** is the main coordinating centre of the body, protected by the **cranium** (skull) and surrounded by a fluid-filled membrane for shock absorption.

![Diagram of human brain in side view showing cerebrum (fore-brain), cerebellum (hind-brain), medulla oblongata, pons, and spinal cord, with functions labeled for each region](human-brain-parts-labeled.png)

### Parts of the Brain

**Fore-brain:**
- **Cerebrum** — the largest part; responsible for thinking, memory, intelligence, voluntary actions, and interpreting sensory information (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch). Different areas control different functions.

**Mid-brain:**
- Controls involuntary reflex movements of the head, neck, and trunk

**Hind-brain:**
- **Cerebellum** — controls posture, balance, and precise voluntary movements (e.g., walking, riding a bicycle, picking up objects)
- **Medulla oblongata (Medulla)** — controls involuntary actions like blood pressure, salivation, vomiting, breathing, and heart rate. It connects the brain to the spinal cord.
- **Pons** — relays signals between the cerebrum and cerebellum

### Spinal Cord

The **spinal cord** is a cylindrical structure running through the vertebral column. It:
- Acts as a pathway between the brain and the rest of the body
- Controls reflex actions
- A spinal cord injury can disrupt both reflex actions and signals between the brain and body parts below the injury

> **Key idea:** The brain has three main regions — fore-brain (thinking, sensory processing), mid-brain (reflexes), and hind-brain (balance, involuntary actions). The spinal cord controls reflex actions and relays signals.

---

## Types of Actions

- **Voluntary actions** — consciously controlled by the cerebrum (e.g., writing, talking, walking)
- **Involuntary actions** — not under conscious control, managed by the medulla (e.g., heartbeat, breathing, peristalsis)
- **Reflex actions** — automatic, rapid responses controlled by the spinal cord (e.g., jerking hand away from flame)

**Difference between involuntary and reflex actions:** Involuntary actions are controlled by the brain (medulla), while reflex actions are controlled by the spinal cord. Both are not under conscious control.

> **Key idea:** Voluntary actions involve the cerebrum; involuntary actions involve the medulla; reflex actions involve the spinal cord.

## Coordination in Plants

Plants do not have a nervous system, but they respond to stimuli through **movements** controlled by **plant hormones (phytohormones)**.

### Types of Plant Movements

**Independent of growth (Nastic movements):**
- The sensitive plant (**Mimosa pudica**) folds its leaves when touched. This is caused by changes in **water content** (turgor pressure) in cells at the base of the leaf, not by growth. The cells lose water and shrink, causing the leaf to droop.

**Dependent on growth (Tropic movements):**
- These are directional movements in response to stimuli. They occur due to unequal distribution of plant hormones causing differential growth.

### Tropisms

- **Phototropism** — growth towards or away from light. Shoots show **positive phototropism** (grow towards light); roots show **negative phototropism**.
- **Geotropism (Gravitropism)** — response to gravity. Roots show **positive geotropism** (grow downward); shoots show **negative geotropism**.
- **Chemotropism** — growth towards or away from chemicals. Example: pollen tube grows towards the ovule.
- **Hydrotropism** — growth towards water. Roots show positive hydrotropism.
- **Thigmotropism** — growth in response to touch. Example: tendrils of pea plants coil around a support.

### Plant Hormones

| Hormone | Function |
|---|---|
| **Auxin** | Promotes cell elongation; causes phototropism and geotropism; synthesised at shoot tip |
| **Gibberellins** | Promote stem growth and flowering |
| **Cytokinins** | Promote cell division; present in areas of active division (fruits, seeds) |
| **Abscisic acid (ABA)** | Inhibits growth; causes wilting of leaves (stress hormone) |
| **Ethylene** | Promotes fruit ripening |

> **Key idea:** Plants coordinate responses using hormones. Auxin causes phototropism by accumulating on the shaded side, promoting cell elongation there and causing the plant to bend towards light.

## Hormones in Animals (Endocrine System)

The **endocrine system** consists of glands that secrete **hormones** directly into the blood. Hormones are chemical messengers that regulate specific body functions.

### Important Endocrine Glands and Hormones

| Gland | Hormone | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Pituitary** (master gland) | Growth hormone | Regulates growth and development of the body |
| **Thyroid** | Thyroxin | Regulates metabolism of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats |
| **Pancreas** | Insulin | Lowers blood sugar level by converting glucose to glycogen |
| **Adrenal** | Adrenaline | Prepares body for emergency ("fight or flight") — increases heart rate, blood pressure, breathing rate |
| **Testes** (males) | Testosterone | Development of male sex organs and secondary sexual characteristics |
| **Ovaries** (females) | Oestrogen | Development of female sex organs; regulates menstrual cycle |

### Important Points

- **Iodine** is essential for synthesis of thyroxin. Iodine deficiency causes **goitre** (swollen neck).
- **Insulin** deficiency causes **diabetes** (high blood sugar).
- **Growth hormone** deficiency in childhood leads to **dwarfism**; excess leads to **gigantism**.
- **Adrenaline** is the "emergency hormone" — it prepares the body for fight or flight.

### Feedback Mechanism

Hormones are secreted in precise quantities, regulated by **feedback mechanisms**. For example, when blood sugar rises, the pancreas detects it and secretes more insulin. As blood sugar falls, insulin secretion reduces.

### Nervous vs. Hormonal Control

| Feature | Nervous System | Hormonal System |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Fast (milliseconds) | Slow (minutes to hours) |
| Duration | Short-lived | Long-lasting |
| Transmission | Electrical impulses along nerves | Chemical hormones through blood |
| Target | Specific muscle/gland | May affect multiple organs |
| Nature | Precise, localised | General, widespread |

> **Key idea:** The endocrine system uses hormones as chemical messengers for slower, longer-lasting coordination. Feedback mechanisms maintain hormone levels. Both nervous and hormonal systems work together for effective control and coordination.
