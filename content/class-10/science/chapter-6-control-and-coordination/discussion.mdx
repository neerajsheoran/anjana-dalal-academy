## The Nervous System

**Kiran:** Papa, the neuron is the basic unit of the nervous system. How does it actually transmit a signal?

**Ravi:** The signal travels as an electrochemical impulse. When a receptor detects a stimulus, it triggers a chemical change at the dendrite tip that creates an electrical impulse. This impulse travels through the cell body and along the axon. At the end of the axon, the electrical signal causes the release of chemical neurotransmitters that cross the tiny gap called a synapse and trigger an impulse in the next neuron.

**Kiran:** So the synapse is where electrical becomes chemical and then electrical again?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The synapse acts as a one-way gate — signals can only cross in one direction, from the axon terminal of one neuron to the dendrite of the next. This ensures information flows in the correct direction. Understanding synaptic transmission is fundamental for NEET and also for fields like neuroscience and artificial intelligence, where Indian researchers at IIT and IISC are making significant contributions.

## Reflex Actions

**Kiran:** When I touch a hot object, I pull my hand back before I even feel the pain. How does that work?

**Ravi:** That is a reflex action. The signal travels from the receptor in your hand through a sensory neuron to the spinal cord, where a relay neuron connects it to a motor neuron that signals your arm muscles to contract — all without involving the brain. The entire pathway is called a reflex arc. The brain is informed after the action is already complete.

**Kiran:** So reflexes bypass the brain for speed?

**Ravi:** Yes. If the signal had to travel all the way to the brain, be processed, and then send a command back, the delay could cause serious injury. The spinal cord handles it directly. This is why a person with a spinal injury may lose reflexes below the injury site even if their brain is functioning normally.

**Kiran:** The reflex arc diagram — receptor, sensory neuron, relay neuron, motor neuron, effector — is that commonly asked?

**Ravi:** Very commonly. Practice drawing and labelling it. Also know the difference between a reflex action, which is involuntary, and a voluntary action, which involves the brain's conscious decision.

## The Brain and Its Parts

**Kiran:** The brain has three main parts — forebrain, midbrain, and hindbrain. What does each control?

**Ravi:** The forebrain contains the cerebrum, which is the centre of intelligence, memory, thought, and voluntary actions. Different regions of the cerebrum process different information — hearing, seeing, speaking, thinking. The midbrain connects the forebrain to the hindbrain. The hindbrain includes the cerebellum, which controls balance and coordination, the pons, which relays signals, and the medulla oblongata, which controls involuntary functions like breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure.

**Kiran:** The medulla controls things we do not think about — breathing, heartbeat. What happens if it is damaged?

**Ravi:** It can be fatal because these are essential life functions. The brain is protected by the skull, three membranes called meninges, and cerebrospinal fluid that acts as a shock absorber. Despite all this protection, head injuries remain a serious medical concern. Homi Bhabha National Institute in Mumbai conducts advanced neuroscience research relevant to this.

## Hormonal Control

**Kiran:** The endocrine system uses hormones instead of electrical signals. How is this different from the nervous system?

**Ravi:** Nervous control is fast, precise, and short-lived — an electrical impulse travels along specific neurons to specific targets. Hormonal control is slower, broader, and longer-lasting — hormones travel through the blood and affect all cells that have the right receptors. Think of the nervous system as a phone call to one person and the endocrine system as a radio broadcast to everyone tuned to that frequency.

**Kiran:** Adrenaline during a scary situation — that is hormonal?

**Ravi:** Yes. The adrenal glands release adrenaline, which increases heart rate, breathing rate, and blood flow to muscles — preparing the body for fight or flight. Other important hormones include insulin from the pancreas, which controls blood sugar; thyroxine from the thyroid, which controls metabolism; and growth hormone from the pituitary.

**Kiran:** Diabetes is caused by insufficient insulin?

**Ravi:** Type 1 diabetes results from the pancreas not producing enough insulin. Type 2 involves cells becoming resistant to insulin. India has one of the highest diabetes rates in the world — over 70 million people — making this a critical public health issue. Understanding the hormonal basis is essential for medical sciences.

## Plant Hormones

**Kiran:** Plants do not have a nervous system, so they rely entirely on hormones for coordination?

**Ravi:** Yes. Auxin promotes cell elongation and is responsible for phototropism — bending towards light. Gibberellins promote stem growth. Cytokinins promote cell division. Abscisic acid inhibits growth and causes wilting of leaves. These hormones work together to coordinate plant responses to light, gravity, and water.

**Kiran:** The bending of a plant towards light — auxin accumulates on the shaded side, causing more growth there, so the plant bends towards light?

**Ravi:** Precisely. This was demonstrated elegantly in experiments by Darwin and later Went. For CBSE, know the five plant hormones and their functions, and be able to explain phototropism and geotropism using auxin redistribution.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What are the high-frequency questions from this chapter?

**Ravi:** The reflex arc diagram with labels is asked almost every year — 3 marks. The difference between nervous and hormonal control — 2-3 marks. Functions of different parts of the brain — 2-3 marks. Plant hormones and tropic movements — 2-3 marks. This chapter typically carries 5-6 marks across the biology section.

**Kiran:** For NEET, which concepts are most foundational?

**Ravi:** Synaptic transmission, neurotransmitters, and the distinction between sensory and motor neurons are tested heavily in NEET. The endocrine system expands enormously in Class 12 — hypothalamus, pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas, and reproductive hormones. Building a clear mental map of nervous versus hormonal coordination now makes Class 12 biology significantly easier. Indian medical researchers at AIIMS and NIMHANS conduct cutting-edge neuroscience research that builds on exactly these foundations.
