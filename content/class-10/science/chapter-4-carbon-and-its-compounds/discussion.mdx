## The Covalent Bond

**Kiran:** Papa, carbon has 4 valence electrons. It cannot gain 4 or lose 4 — so it shares. That is covalent bonding?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Gaining 4 electrons would require too much energy against nuclear repulsion, and losing 4 would also need enormous energy. Sharing is the energy-efficient solution. Carbon forms 4 covalent bonds, allowing it to bond with other carbon atoms and with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and halogens. This versatility is why carbon forms millions of compounds.

**Kiran:** Single, double, and triple bonds — how do these differ in strength?

**Ravi:** A single bond shares one electron pair, a double bond shares two, and a triple bond shares three. Triple bonds are the strongest and shortest — nitrogen gas with its triple bond is very stable, which is why it makes up 78 percent of our atmosphere without reacting easily. The carbon-carbon single bond in ethane, double bond in ethene, and triple bond in ethyne illustrate this beautifully.

**Kiran:** Properties of covalent compounds — low melting points and poor conductors. Why?

**Ravi:** The bonds within molecules are strong, but the forces between molecules are weak. So molecules separate easily when heated — hence low melting points. And since no ions are formed, there are no charged particles to carry electricity. Diamond is an exception because every atom is bonded in an infinite 3D network — no individual molecules exist.

## Catenation and Tetravalency

**Kiran:** Catenation — carbon bonding to itself to form chains and rings. Is this unique to carbon?

**Ravi:** Silicon also shows some catenation, but carbon is far superior because the C-C bond is very strong due to carbon's small atomic size. Carbon forms straight chains, branched chains, and rings. This is why organic chemistry — the chemistry of carbon — has more compounds than the rest of chemistry combined.

**Kiran:** Saturated and unsaturated compounds — what is the practical difference?

**Ravi:** Saturated compounds like methane and ethane have only single bonds — they are stable and less reactive. Unsaturated compounds like ethene and ethyne have double or triple bonds — they are more reactive and undergo addition reactions. In the kitchen, saturated fats are solid at room temperature, while unsaturated fats are liquid — oils. Hydrogenation of vegetable oils to make vanaspati ghee converts unsaturated compounds to saturated ones.

**Kiran:** That connects chemistry to nutrition.

**Ravi:** Indeed. And for NEET aspirants, understanding the difference between saturated and unsaturated fats is fundamental to biochemistry.

## Homologous Series and Nomenclature

**Kiran:** A homologous series — each member differs by CH2 from the next. Methane, ethane, propane, butane — the pattern is clear.

**Ravi:** Yes, and the IUPAC naming system follows a logical pattern. The prefix tells you the number of carbons — meth for 1, eth for 2, prop for 3, but for 4, pent for 5. The suffix tells you the type of bond — ane for single, ene for double, yne for triple. For functional groups, ol for alcohol, al for aldehyde, one for ketone, and oic acid for carboxylic acid.

**Kiran:** So CH3OH is methanol — one carbon with an OH group?

**Ravi:** Correct. And CH3COOH is ethanoic acid — two carbons with a COOH group. The naming system is systematic and predictable, unlike the old common names. Board exams test both IUPAC and common names — ethanol and ethyl alcohol, ethanoic acid and acetic acid, methanal and formaldehyde.

## Chemical Properties and Reactions

**Kiran:** Combustion, oxidation, addition, substitution — which reactions are most important for boards?

**Ravi:** All four. Combustion of carbon compounds produces CO2 and H2O — this is why fossil fuels work. Oxidation of ethanol to ethanoic acid using alkaline KMnO4 is a frequently tested reaction. Addition reactions convert unsaturated to saturated compounds — hydrogen adding across a double bond. Substitution in saturated compounds — chlorine replacing hydrogen in methane in the presence of sunlight.

**Kiran:** Ethanol and ethanoic acid — the textbook gives these special attention.

**Ravi:** Because they are the most commonly encountered organic compounds. Ethanol is used as a fuel additive — India blends up to 20 percent ethanol with petrol. Ethanoic acid is vinegar. The reactions of these two compounds — esterification, reaction with sodium, reaction with sodium carbonate — are board exam favourites.

**Kiran:** Soap and detergent — those are also carbon compounds?

**Ravi:** Yes. Soap is sodium or potassium salt of a long-chain fatty acid. It has a hydrophilic head that attracts water and a hydrophobic tail that attracts grease. In hard water, soap forms scum because calcium and magnesium ions precipitate the soap. Detergents work even in hard water because they do not form insoluble precipitates.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** This is a long chapter. What carries the most marks?

**Ravi:** Typically 5-6 marks. Focus on nomenclature, electron-dot structures of simple molecules like methane and ethene, properties and reactions of ethanol and ethanoic acid, and the difference between soap and detergent. Drawing structures and writing balanced equations are essential skills here.

**Kiran:** What mistakes should I avoid?

**Ravi:** First, confusing structural formulae with molecular formulae — always draw bonds clearly. Second, wrong nomenclature — count carbons carefully. Third, not balancing combustion equations. Fourth, mixing up addition and substitution reactions — addition occurs in unsaturated compounds, substitution in saturated ones. Practice drawing at least 10 structural formulae before the exam.

**Kiran:** For NEET and JEE, carbon chemistry becomes organic chemistry — a major subject?

**Ravi:** The largest single subject in both exams. Organic chemistry in Classes 11 and 12 covers reaction mechanisms, named reactions, functional group transformations, and stereochemistry. Everything builds on the concepts of covalent bonding, functional groups, and nomenclature that you learn here. India's pharmaceutical industry — based in Hyderabad, Mumbai, and Ahmedabad — is built entirely on organic chemistry. A strong Class 10 foundation makes the transition to advanced organic chemistry much smoother.
