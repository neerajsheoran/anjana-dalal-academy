## Introduction

Carbon is an element of immense significance both in its elemental form and in the combined form. Food, clothes, medicines, books, and all living structures are based on this versatile element. Despite the earth's crust having only **0.02% carbon** (in minerals like carbonates, coal, and petroleum) and the atmosphere having **0.03% carbon dioxide**, carbon forms an astonishingly large number of compounds — estimated to be in the millions.

> **Key idea:** Carbon is the basis of all organic chemistry and life itself. The unique bonding properties of carbon allow it to form a vast number of diverse compounds.

---

## Bonding in Carbon — The Covalent Bond

Unlike ionic compounds, most carbon compounds are **poor conductors of electricity** and have **low melting and boiling points**. This indicates that the bonding in carbon compounds does not produce ions — instead, atoms share electrons.

Carbon has **4 electrons** in its outermost shell (atomic number 6). It cannot easily gain 4 electrons (to form C⁴⁻) or lose 4 electrons (to form C⁴⁺). Instead, carbon achieves noble gas configuration by **sharing** its valence electrons with other atoms.

### Covalent Bonds

Bonds formed by the **sharing of electron pairs** between two atoms are called **covalent bonds**.

![Electron dot structures showing single bond (H2), double bond (O2), and triple bond (N2) with shared electron pairs between atoms](covalent-bond-types-electron-dot.png)

- **Single bond:** One shared pair of electrons (e.g., H—H in H₂, C—H in CH₄)
- **Double bond:** Two shared pairs of electrons (e.g., O=O in O₂, C=C in ethene)
- **Triple bond:** Three shared pairs of electrons (e.g., N≡N in N₂, C≡C in ethyne)

**Properties of covalent compounds:**
- Low melting and boiling points (weak intermolecular forces)
- Generally poor conductors of electricity (no ions formed)
- Strong bonds within molecules but weak forces between molecules

### Allotropes of Carbon

Carbon exists in different forms called **allotropes**:

![Three allotropes of carbon: diamond (tetrahedral 3D network), graphite (hexagonal layered sheets), and fullerene C60 (spherical cage structure)](carbon-allotropes-diamond-graphite-fullerene.png)

- **Diamond:** Each carbon atom bonded to four others in a rigid 3D structure. Hardest natural substance. Does not conduct electricity.
- **Graphite:** Each carbon atom bonded to three others in hexagonal layers. Layers slide over each other (smooth and slippery). Conducts electricity.
- **Fullerenes:** Carbon atoms arranged in hollow structures. C-60 (Buckminsterfullerene) has atoms arranged like a football.

> **Key idea:** Carbon forms covalent bonds by sharing electrons. Covalent compounds have low melting points and poor electrical conductivity. Carbon allotropes (diamond, graphite, fullerene) have vastly different physical properties despite identical chemical composition.

## Versatile Nature of Carbon

Two properties make carbon uniquely versatile:

### 1. Catenation

**Catenation** is the unique ability of carbon to form bonds with other carbon atoms, creating long chains, branched chains, and rings. Carbon-carbon bonds are very strong and stable due to carbon's small atomic size.

No other element exhibits catenation to the extent seen in carbon. Silicon can form chains of only 7-8 atoms, and those compounds are very reactive.

### 2. Tetravalency

Carbon has a **valency of 4**, so it can bond with four other atoms of carbon or other elements. This allows carbon to form compounds with oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, sulphur, chlorine, and many other elements.

> **Key idea:** Catenation (ability to form C-C chains) and tetravalency (bonding with four atoms) together explain why carbon forms millions of compounds.

## Saturated and Unsaturated Carbon Compounds

### Saturated Compounds (Alkanes)

Compounds where carbon atoms are linked only by **single bonds** are called **saturated compounds** or **alkanes**. They are generally not very reactive.

General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ₊₂**

Examples: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈), Butane (C₄H₁₀)

### Unsaturated Compounds

Compounds containing **double bonds** (alkenes) or **triple bonds** (alkynes) are called **unsaturated compounds**. They are more reactive than saturated compounds.

- **Alkenes** (double bond): General formula **CₙH₂ₙ** — e.g., Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆)
- **Alkynes** (triple bond): General formula **CₙH₂ₙ₋₂** — e.g., Ethyne (C₂H₂), Propyne (C₃H₄)

Compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen are called **hydrocarbons**.

---

## Chains, Branches, and Rings

Carbon compounds can exist as:
- **Straight chains** (e.g., butane)
- **Branched chains** (e.g., isobutane)
- **Rings** (e.g., cyclohexane C₆H₁₂, benzene C₆H₆)

### Structural Isomers

Compounds with the **same molecular formula** but **different structures** are called **structural isomers**. For example, butane (C₄H₁₀) has two isomers: n-butane (straight chain) and isobutane (branched chain).

> **Key idea:** Carbon compounds can form straight chains, branched chains, and rings. Compounds with the same formula but different structures are structural isomers.

## Functional Groups

In hydrocarbons, hydrogen atoms can be replaced by other atoms or groups called **heteroatoms** (like Cl, Br, O, N, S). Groups containing heteroatoms that determine the chemical properties of a compound are called **functional groups**.

Important functional groups:

| Functional Group | Class of Compound | Example |
|---|---|---|
| —Cl, —Br | Haloalkane | Chloromethane, Bromopentane |
| —OH | Alcohol | Methanol, Ethanol |
| —CHO | Aldehyde | Methanal, Ethanal |
| —CO— | Ketone | Propanone, Butanone |
| —COOH | Carboxylic acid | Methanoic acid, Ethanoic acid |

> **Key idea:** Functional groups determine the chemical properties of carbon compounds, regardless of the carbon chain length.

---

## Homologous Series

A **homologous series** is a series of compounds with the same functional group, where successive members differ by a **—CH₂—** unit (molecular mass difference of 14 u).

Properties of a homologous series:
- Same general formula
- Same functional group
- Similar chemical properties
- Gradual change in physical properties (melting point, boiling point) with increasing molecular mass
- Successive members differ by CH₂ (14 u)

Example — Alcohol series: CH₃OH, C₂H₅OH, C₃H₇OH, C₄H₉OH

## Nomenclature of Carbon Compounds

Naming carbon compounds follows the IUPAC system:

1. **Identify the number of carbon atoms** — determines the root name (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-)
2. **Identify the functional group** — determines the suffix or prefix
3. **For suffix starting with a vowel**, drop the final 'e' from the root name
4. **For unsaturated compounds**, replace '-ane' with '-ene' (double bond) or '-yne' (triple bond)

Examples: Propanone (3C + ketone), Ethanol (2C + alcohol), Propene (3C + double bond)

> **Key idea:** IUPAC nomenclature uses the carbon chain length as the root name and the functional group as a prefix or suffix to systematically name carbon compounds.

## Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

### Combustion

Carbon and its compounds burn in oxygen to release **heat and light** (oxidation reactions):
- C + O₂ → CO₂ + heat and light
- CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + heat and light

**Saturated hydrocarbons** burn with a **clean blue flame**, while **unsaturated hydrocarbons** burn with a **yellow sooty flame** (due to incomplete combustion).

A flame is produced only when gaseous substances burn. Coal/charcoal may glow red without producing a flame.

### Oxidation

Alcohols can be converted to **carboxylic acids** using oxidising agents like **alkaline potassium permanganate** (KMnO₄) or **acidified potassium dichromate** (K₂Cr₂O₇).

Example: Ethanol → Ethanoic acid (addition of oxygen)

### Addition Reaction

**Unsaturated hydrocarbons** add hydrogen in the presence of catalysts (palladium or nickel) to form **saturated hydrocarbons**. This is called **hydrogenation**.

This process is used industrially to convert **vegetable oils** (unsaturated, liquid) into **fats/ghee** (saturated, solid).

### Substitution Reaction

**Saturated hydrocarbons** react with chlorine in the presence of **sunlight** — chlorine replaces hydrogen atoms one by one:

CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (in sunlight)

> **Key idea:** Carbon compounds undergo combustion (burning), oxidation (alcohol to acid), addition reactions (unsaturated compounds + H₂), and substitution reactions (saturated compounds + Cl₂).

## Ethanol — Properties and Reactions

**Ethanol (C₂H₅OH)** is a liquid at room temperature, commonly called alcohol. It is the active ingredient in alcoholic drinks, used as a solvent in medicines (tincture iodine, cough syrups), and is miscible with water in all proportions.

### Reactions of Ethanol

**With sodium:** 2Na + 2C₂H₅OH → 2C₂H₅ONa (sodium ethoxide) + H₂

**Dehydration:** Heating ethanol at 443 K with concentrated H₂SO₄ removes water to form ethene:
C₂H₅OH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O

**Health effects:** Small quantities cause drunkenness; pure ethanol (absolute alcohol) can be lethal. Methanol is even more dangerous — small quantities can cause blindness and death.

**Denatured alcohol:** Industrial ethanol made unfit for drinking by adding methanol and blue dye.

## Ethanoic Acid — Properties and Reactions

**Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH)** — commonly called acetic acid — is a carboxylic acid. A 5-8% solution in water is called **vinegar**. Pure ethanoic acid freezes at 290 K (16.6°C) and is called **glacial acetic acid**.

Carboxylic acids are **weak acids** (partially ionised), unlike mineral acids like HCl.

### Reactions of Ethanoic Acid

**Esterification:** Ethanoic acid + Ethanol → Ester + Water (in the presence of acid catalyst). Esters have sweet smells — used in perfumes and flavourings.

**Saponification:** Ester + NaOH → Alcohol + Sodium salt of acid. This reaction is used to make **soap**.

**With bases:** CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O

**With carbonates/hydrogencarbonates:** 2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂

> **Key idea:** Ethanol and ethanoic acid are commercially important carbon compounds. Ethanoic acid undergoes esterification with alcohols, and the reverse (saponification) is used to make soap.

## Soaps and Detergents

### Soaps

**Soaps** are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids (fatty acids). A soap molecule has two ends:
- **Hydrophilic end** (ionic) — attracts water
- **Hydrophobic end** (hydrocarbon tail) — attracts oil/grease

### Cleaning Action — Micelle Formation

![Cross-section of a micelle: soap molecules arranged in a sphere with hydrophobic tails pointing inward around an oil droplet and hydrophilic heads facing outward into the water](micelle-formation-soap-cleaning.png)

When soap is added to water, the hydrophobic tails cluster together around oil droplets while the hydrophilic ends face outward into the water. This forms structures called **micelles**. The oil/dirt is trapped inside the micelle and can be washed away with water.

Agitation (beating, scrubbing, or machine washing) helps dislodge the oily dirt and form micelles.

### Hard Water Problem

In **hard water** (containing Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions), soap reacts with these ions to form an insoluble precipitate (scum), reducing its cleaning ability and wasting soap.

### Detergents

**Detergents** are sodium salts of sulphonic acids or ammonium salts. They have the same cleaning mechanism as soaps but do **not form insoluble precipitates** with hard water ions. Hence, detergents remain effective in hard water.

You **cannot** test for hard water using a detergent (since detergents work equally well in hard and soft water). Only soap can distinguish hard water from soft water.

> **Key idea:** Soaps clean by forming micelles that trap oily dirt. Soaps are ineffective in hard water (form scum), but detergents work well in both hard and soft water.
