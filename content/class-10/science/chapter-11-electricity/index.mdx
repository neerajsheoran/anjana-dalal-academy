## Electric Current and Circuit

**Electric current** is the flow of electric charge (electrons) through a conductor. By convention, the direction of current is taken opposite to the flow of electrons (from positive to negative terminal).

**Electric current (I) = Charge (Q) / Time (t)**, measured in **amperes (A)**. 1 A = 1 C/s.

An **electric circuit** is a continuous closed path for current flow, consisting of a source (battery/cell), conducting wires, a switch, and electrical components.

An **ammeter** measures current and is connected in **series** in the circuit.

> **Key idea:** Electric current is the rate of flow of charge. It flows from the positive to the negative terminal of a battery in a closed circuit.

---

## Electric Potential and Potential Difference

**Electric potential difference** between two points is the work done to move a unit charge from one point to the other.

**V = W/Q**, measured in **volts (V)**. 1 V = 1 J/C.

A **voltmeter** measures potential difference and is connected in **parallel** across the component.

> **Key idea:** Potential difference drives the flow of current through a circuit. A voltmeter is connected in parallel to measure it.

---

## Ohm's Law

**Ohm's Law** states that the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided the temperature remains constant.

**V = IR**

Where R is the **resistance** of the conductor, measured in **ohms (Ω)**.

Resistance is the opposition offered by a conductor to the flow of current. Good conductors have low resistance; insulators have very high resistance.

---

## Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance of a conductor depends on:

1. **Length (l):** R is directly proportional to length. Longer wire = more resistance.
2. **Area of cross-section (A):** R is inversely proportional to area. Thicker wire = less resistance.
3. **Material:** Different materials have different resistivities.
4. **Temperature:** Resistance generally increases with temperature for metals.

**R = ρl/A**, where ρ (rho) is the **resistivity** of the material, measured in Ω·m.

**Resistivity** is a characteristic property of the material — it does not change with the dimensions of the conductor but varies with temperature.

Metals like copper and aluminium have **low resistivity** (good conductors). Alloys like nichrome and manganin have **high resistivity** (used in heating elements). Rubber and glass have very high resistivity (insulators).

> **Key idea:** Resistance depends on length, cross-sectional area, and resistivity of the material. R = ρl/A.

## Combination of Resistors

### Series Combination

Resistors connected end-to-end so that the same current flows through each:

**R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...**

- Current is the **same** through each resistor
- Potential difference **divides** among resistors (V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃)
- Total resistance is always **greater** than the largest individual resistance

### Parallel Combination

Resistors connected between the same two points:

**1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...**

- Potential difference is the **same** across each resistor
- Current **divides** among resistors (I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃)
- Total resistance is always **less** than the smallest individual resistance

> **Key idea:** In series, resistances add up and current is constant. In parallel, reciprocals of resistances add up and voltage is constant.

## Heating Effect of Electric Current

When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted to **heat energy**. This is called the **heating effect** of electric current (Joule heating).

**Heat produced: H = I²Rt** (Joule's law of heating)

Also: H = VIt = V²t/R

### Applications
- Electric heaters, toasters, irons, kettles
- Electric bulbs (filament glows white-hot)
- Electric fuses (thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit when current exceeds safe limits)

### Why Alloys for Heating Devices?
Alloys like nichrome have **high resistivity** (produce more heat) and **high melting points** (do not melt easily). They also do not oxidise easily at high temperatures.

### Why Tungsten for Lamp Filaments?
Tungsten has a very **high melting point** (3380°C), allowing it to glow white-hot without melting. It also has high resistivity.

> **Key idea:** Heat produced in a conductor is proportional to I², R, and t (H = I²Rt). Alloys with high resistivity and melting points are used in heating devices.

## Electric Power

**Electric power** is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed.

**P = VI = I²R = V²/R**, measured in **watts (W)**

1 watt = 1 volt × 1 ampere

The commercial unit of electrical energy is the **kilowatt-hour (kWh)**, commonly called a "unit."

**1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J**

### Domestic Electric Circuits

In homes, appliances are connected in **parallel** (not series) because:
- Each appliance gets the **full supply voltage** (220 V)
- Each appliance can be **independently switched** on or off
- If one appliance fails, others continue to work
- Parallel connection provides **lower total resistance**, allowing sufficient current

> **Key idea:** Electric power (P = VI) is measured in watts. Domestic appliances are connected in parallel to ensure each gets full voltage and can operate independently.
