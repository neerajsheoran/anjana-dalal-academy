## Electric Current and Ohm's Law

**Kiran:** Papa, electric current is the flow of electrons, but we say current flows from positive to negative. Why the opposite convention?

**Ravi:** Historically, before electrons were discovered, scientists assumed current flowed from positive to negative. By the time J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897, the convention was already established. So we stick with conventional current direction — positive to negative — even though electrons actually flow the other way. This does not affect calculations at all.

**Kiran:** Ohm's Law — V equals IR — seems straightforward. But it only works when temperature is constant?

**Ravi:** Correct. Ohm's Law states that current is directly proportional to voltage at constant temperature. The constant of proportionality is resistance. Materials that obey Ohm's Law are called ohmic conductors — metals are the best examples. LEDs and diodes do not obey Ohm's Law — their resistance changes with voltage. For CBSE, focus on ohmic conductors.

**Kiran:** The ammeter goes in series and the voltmeter goes in parallel — why?

**Ravi:** An ammeter measures current flowing through a component, so it must be in the path of the current — in series. A voltmeter measures the potential difference across a component, so it must be connected across it — in parallel. An ammeter has very low resistance to avoid affecting the current, and a voltmeter has very high resistance to avoid drawing current from the circuit.

## Resistance and Resistivity

**Kiran:** Resistance depends on length, area, material, and temperature. The formula R equals rho times l over A — rho is resistivity?

**Ravi:** Yes. Resistivity is a property of the material itself — copper has low resistivity, making it a good conductor, while rubber has extremely high resistivity, making it an insulator. Nichrome has relatively high resistivity and is used in heating elements. The resistivity of a material changes with temperature — for metals, it increases; for semiconductors like silicon, it decreases.

**Kiran:** India's semiconductor industry in Bengaluru works with silicon — understanding resistivity is fundamental there?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Silicon has a resistivity between conductors and insulators, which is why it is called a semiconductor. By adding tiny amounts of impurities — a process called doping — engineers can precisely control its resistivity. This is the basis of every chip in your phone and computer.

## Series and Parallel Circuits

**Kiran:** In series, the total resistance is the sum. In parallel, it is the reciprocal of the sum of reciprocals. Why are household circuits wired in parallel?

**Ravi:** Three reasons. First, in parallel, each device gets the full voltage from the power supply. In series, the voltage divides. Second, in parallel, if one device fails, others continue working. In series, one failure breaks the entire circuit. Third, in parallel, you can add devices without affecting the resistance seen by other devices. This is why your house has multiple parallel branches for different rooms.

**Kiran:** The total resistance in parallel is always less than the smallest individual resistance?

**Ravi:** Yes, always. If you connect two 6-ohm resistors in parallel, the total is 3 ohms. Adding more paths for current reduces the overall resistance. This is intuitive — more paths mean easier flow, like adding more lanes to a highway reduces traffic congestion.

**Kiran:** For numerical problems, how do I approach complex circuits?

**Ravi:** Break the circuit into series and parallel sections. Simplify step by step — combine parallel resistors first, then add series resistors. Redraw the simplified circuit at each step. This systematic approach works for any circuit in the CBSE syllabus.

## Heating Effect of Current

**Kiran:** The heating effect — H equals I squared R t — is why electric heaters and fuses work?

**Ravi:** Yes. When current flows through a resistance, electrical energy is converted to heat. The heat produced depends on the square of the current, the resistance, and the time. This is Joule's law of heating. Electric heaters use high-resistance nichrome wire to generate heat. Fuses use this principle for safety — when excessive current flows, the fuse wire heats up and melts, breaking the circuit.

**Kiran:** Electric power — P equals VI equals I squared R equals V squared over R. The unit is watt?

**Ravi:** Yes. The electricity bill charges you in kilowatt-hours — 1 kWh is the energy consumed by a 1000-watt device running for 1 hour. In India, the cost per unit varies by state and consumption slab. Calculating the monthly electricity cost of appliances is a practical application question that CBSE includes.

**Kiran:** An 80-watt fan running for 10 hours a day for 30 days — that would be 24 kWh per month?

**Ravi:** Exactly. 80 times 10 times 30 equals 24000 watt-hours, which is 24 kWh or 24 units. If the rate is Rs 5 per unit, the cost is Rs 120 per month. This kind of calculation is both a board exam question and a practical life skill.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** This chapter carries heavy marks. What should I prioritise?

**Ravi:** Typically 5-8 marks. Numerical problems on Ohm's Law, series-parallel circuits, and power-energy calculations are the highest-scoring areas. Know the formulas, the sign conventions for current direction, and how to draw circuit diagrams with proper symbols. Conceptual questions on why household circuits are parallel and how fuses work are also common.

**Kiran:** Common errors?

**Ravi:** First, using the wrong formula in parallel versus series combinations. Second, forgetting to convert units — kilowatts to watts, hours to seconds. Third, not drawing clear circuit diagrams. Fourth, confusing resistance and resistivity. Practice 15-20 numerical problems covering different circuit configurations before the exam.

**Kiran:** India's push for electric vehicles and solar energy — does this chapter relate to that?

**Ravi:** Directly. Electric vehicles use battery circuits with cells in series and parallel to achieve the right voltage and capacity. Solar panels are connected in series-parallel combinations to provide the required output. India's National Electric Mobility Mission and the International Solar Alliance, headquartered in Gurugram, both rely on engineers who understand these circuit principles. The physics of electricity you study now is at the heart of India's clean energy future.
