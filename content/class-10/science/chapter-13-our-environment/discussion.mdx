## Ecosystems and Food Chains

**Kiran:** Papa, an ecosystem has biotic and abiotic components interacting together. The food chain shows how energy flows from one organism to another. But in reality, organisms eat many different things — so it is more of a food web?

**Ravi:** Exactly. A food chain is a simplified linear pathway — grass to deer to lion. But in nature, a deer also eats shrubs and fruits, and lions eat zebras and buffaloes too. When you connect all these food chains, you get a food web — a complex, interconnected network. Disrupting one species in a food web can have cascading effects throughout the ecosystem.

**Kiran:** The trophic levels — producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, tertiary consumers. Energy decreases at each level?

**Ravi:** Yes, and this is governed by the 10 percent law. Only about 10 percent of the energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The rest is lost as heat during respiration, used for life processes, or lost as waste. This is why food chains rarely have more than 4-5 trophic levels — there simply is not enough energy to sustain more.

**Kiran:** So vegetarians are more energy-efficient than non-vegetarians?

**Ravi:** From a trophic perspective, yes. When you eat plants directly, you are at the second trophic level. When you eat animals that ate plants, you are at the third level — 90 percent of the energy was already lost. This is why producing 1 kg of meat requires about 10 kg of grain. India's large vegetarian population is actually quite energy-efficient in ecological terms.

## Biological Magnification

**Kiran:** Pesticides like DDT accumulate at higher trophic levels — biological magnification. How does this work?

**Ravi:** Non-biodegradable chemicals cannot be broken down by organisms. When a small organism absorbs a tiny amount of pesticide, and a larger organism eats thousands of those small organisms, the pesticide accumulates — the concentration increases at each trophic level. A hawk at the top of the food chain may have pesticide concentrations millions of times higher than the water it drinks.

**Kiran:** DDT was banned in many countries because of this?

**Ravi:** Yes. Rachel Carson's book "Silent Spring" in 1962 exposed the devastating effects of DDT on bird populations — their eggshells became so thin they broke before hatching. India banned DDT for agricultural use but still allows limited use for malaria control in some regions. The debate between disease prevention and environmental protection is a real policy challenge.

**Kiran:** This concept connects biology to environmental science and public policy.

**Ravi:** Indeed. For NEET, biological magnification is a frequently tested concept. For competitive exams in environmental science and civil services, understanding the ecological consequences of pollutants is essential.

## Ozone Layer Depletion

**Kiran:** The ozone layer in the stratosphere protects us from ultraviolet radiation. CFCs from refrigerators and air conditioners destroy it?

**Ravi:** Yes. Ozone is O3 — three atoms of oxygen. In the stratosphere, UV radiation breaks O2 into oxygen atoms, which combine with O2 to form O3. This ozone absorbs harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) release chlorine atoms in the stratosphere that catalytically destroy ozone molecules — one chlorine atom can destroy thousands of ozone molecules.

**Kiran:** The Montreal Protocol banned CFCs?

**Ravi:** Yes, in 1987. India signed the Montreal Protocol and has been phasing out ozone-depleting substances. The ozone hole over Antarctica has been slowly recovering since then — a rare success story in international environmental cooperation. India transitioned from CFC-based refrigerants to HFC-based ones, though HFCs contribute to global warming, so further transitions are needed.

**Kiran:** UNEP recognised India's efforts in phasing out CFCs?

**Ravi:** Yes. India received the Ozone Award from UNEP for its efforts. Understanding the science behind ozone depletion empowers citizens to make informed choices and support effective environmental policies.

## Waste Management

**Kiran:** Biodegradable waste decomposes naturally, but non-biodegradable waste persists in the environment for centuries?

**Ravi:** Correct. Vegetable peels, paper, and cotton are biodegradable — bacteria and fungi break them down. But plastics, glass, and certain pesticides are non-biodegradable — they accumulate in the environment. India generates about 62 million tonnes of solid waste annually, and improper disposal is a major environmental challenge.

**Kiran:** The 3 Rs — reduce, reuse, recycle — are practical solutions?

**Ravi:** Yes. Reduce means using less — carry your own bag instead of taking a plastic one. Reuse means using items again — glass jars can store spices. Recycle means processing waste into new materials — aluminium cans can be melted and reformed. India's Swachh Bharat Mission has promoted waste segregation, but individual awareness and action remain critical.

**Kiran:** The difference between the impact of biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste is a common exam question?

**Ravi:** Very common. Know 3-4 examples of each and their environmental impact. Also understand how decomposers play a crucial role in recycling nutrients — without them, dead organisms would pile up and nutrients would be locked away from new life.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What are the key exam topics?

**Ravi:** The 10 percent law and energy flow through trophic levels — 2-3 marks. Biological magnification — 2-3 marks. Ozone depletion and the role of CFCs — 2-3 marks. Differences between biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste — 1-2 marks. This chapter is relatively shorter but conceptually rich. Diagrams of food chains and food webs should be practised.

**Kiran:** For competitive exams, this connects to environmental studies?

**Ravi:** Yes. For NEET, ecological concepts from this chapter form the basis of the Ecology unit in Class 12. For UPSC and other civil services exams, understanding ecosystems, pollution, and environmental policy is directly relevant. Homi Bhabha's vision of using science for India's development included environmental sustainability — a value worth carrying forward.
