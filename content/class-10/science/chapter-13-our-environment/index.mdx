## Ecosystem and Its Components

An **ecosystem** consists of all the living organisms (biotic components) in an area interacting with each other and with the non-living (abiotic) components of the environment.

**Biotic components** include all living organisms — plants, animals, microorganisms and human beings.

**Abiotic components** include physical factors like temperature, rainfall, wind, soil and minerals.

Examples of ecosystems:
- **Natural ecosystems** — forests, ponds, lakes, deserts, grasslands
- **Artificial (human-made) ecosystems** — gardens, crop fields, aquariums

Based on how organisms obtain their sustenance, they are grouped as:
- **Producers (autotrophs)** — green plants and certain bacteria that make food by photosynthesis using sunlight
- **Consumers (heterotrophs)** — organisms that depend on producers directly or indirectly. They are classified as **herbivores** (plant-eaters), **carnivores** (meat-eaters), **omnivores** (eat both) and **parasites**
- **Decomposers** — bacteria and fungi that break down dead remains and waste products of organisms into simple inorganic substances that are returned to the soil

> **Key idea:** An ecosystem has biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components. Organisms are classified as producers, consumers, or decomposers based on how they obtain food.

## Food Chains and Food Webs

A **food chain** is a series of organisms in which each organism feeds on the one before it, transferring energy from one level to the next.

Each step or level of the food chain forms a **trophic level**:
- **First trophic level** — Producers (autotrophs/green plants)
- **Second trophic level** — Primary consumers (herbivores)
- **Third trophic level** — Secondary consumers (small carnivores)
- **Fourth trophic level** — Tertiary consumers (large carnivores)

Example: Grass → Deer → Lion

In nature, organisms are usually eaten by more than one type of organism, and each organism may eat several different things. This creates a network of interconnected food chains called a **food web**.

> **Key idea:** A food chain shows the transfer of energy from producers to consumers at successive trophic levels. Interconnected food chains form a food web.

## Flow of Energy in an Ecosystem

The flow of energy in an ecosystem follows important rules:

1. **Green plants capture about 1%** of the energy of sunlight that falls on their leaves and convert it into food energy
2. **10% law** — on average, only **10%** of the energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next level. The rest is lost as heat to the environment, used in digestion, and used for life processes
3. **Energy flow is unidirectional** — energy captured by autotrophs does not revert back to the Sun, and energy passed to herbivores does not return to the autotrophs
4. **Energy diminishes progressively** at each trophic level — this is why food chains generally have only **three or four trophic levels**
5. There are generally a **greater number of individuals at lower trophic levels** — the greatest number is of producers

> **Key idea:** Only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. Energy flow is unidirectional and decreases at each level, limiting food chains to 3-4 steps.

## Biological Magnification

**Biological magnification** is the phenomenon in which harmful non-biodegradable chemicals (like pesticides) accumulate progressively at each trophic level of a food chain.

How it happens:
- Pesticides and other chemicals used on crops are washed into soil or water bodies
- Plants absorb these chemicals from the soil, and aquatic organisms take them up from water
- Since these chemicals are **non-biodegradable**, they are not broken down or excreted
- At each trophic level, the concentration of these chemicals **increases** because organisms at higher levels consume many organisms from lower levels
- **Humans**, being at the top of most food chains, accumulate the **maximum concentration** of these harmful chemicals

This is why food grains, vegetables, fruits, and meat may contain pesticide residues that cannot always be removed by washing.

> **Key idea:** Non-biodegradable pesticides accumulate at increasing concentrations through the food chain (biological magnification). Humans at the top of food chains are most affected.

## Ozone Layer and Its Depletion

**Ozone (O3)** is a molecule made up of three atoms of oxygen. While regular oxygen (O2) is essential for life, ozone is a deadly poison at ground level. However, in the upper atmosphere, ozone performs a vital function — it **shields the Earth** from harmful **ultraviolet (UV) radiation** from the Sun, which can cause skin cancer and other damage to organisms.

**Formation of ozone:**
- High-energy UV radiation splits molecular oxygen (O2) into free oxygen atoms (O)
- These free oxygen atoms combine with O2 to form ozone (O3)
- O2 → O + O (UV radiation splits O2)
- O + O2 → O3 (ozone is formed)

**Ozone depletion:**
- The ozone layer began to thin sharply in the **1980s**
- This was linked to synthetic chemicals called **chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)**, used as refrigerants and in fire extinguishers
- CFCs released into the atmosphere break down ozone molecules faster than they are formed
- In **1987**, the **United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)** forged an agreement to freeze CFC production at 1986 levels
- CFC-free refrigerators are now mandatory worldwide

> **Key idea:** The ozone layer protects Earth from harmful UV radiation. CFCs deplete the ozone layer. International agreements have been made to limit CFC emissions and protect the ozone layer.

## Waste Management — Biodegradable and Non-biodegradable

Substances can be classified based on whether biological processes can break them down:

**Biodegradable substances** — substances that can be broken down by biological processes (bacteria, fungi, enzymes). Examples: vegetable peels, paper, cotton cloth, wood, food waste.

**Non-biodegradable substances** — substances that cannot be broken down by biological processes. They persist in the environment for long periods or may harm ecosystem members. Examples: plastics, polythene bags, metal cans, glass, synthetic fibres, pesticides.

### Why the distinction matters

- Enzymes are **specific in their action** — specific enzymes break down specific substances. Human-made materials like plastics do not have corresponding enzymes in nature
- Non-biodegradable wastes can **block drains**, **contaminate soil and water**, harm animals that ingest them, and persist for hundreds of years
- Biodegradable wastes, if not managed properly, can also cause problems — producing **foul smell**, becoming **breeding grounds for disease-causing organisms**, and releasing harmful gases

### Managing waste

- Separate biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste
- **Reduce** the use of non-biodegradable materials
- **Reuse** and **recycle** materials wherever possible
- Use environment-friendly practices like carrying cloth bags, composting kitchen waste, and avoiding single-use plastics

> **Key idea:** Biodegradable substances are broken down by biological processes; non-biodegradable substances persist in the environment. Proper waste management through reduce, reuse, and recycle is essential for environmental protection.
