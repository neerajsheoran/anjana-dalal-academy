## Introduction — Why Do Organisms Reproduce?

**Reproduction** is the biological process by which organisms produce new individuals of their kind. Unlike other life processes (nutrition, respiration, etc.), reproduction is not essential for the survival of an individual organism, but it is essential for the survival of the **species**.

Reproduction involves the creation of a **DNA copy** and additional cellular apparatus. The DNA copying mechanism introduces slight **variations** each time, which is important for the survival of the species in changing environments.

> **Key idea:** Reproduction ensures the continuation of a species. DNA copying during reproduction creates variations that help species survive environmental changes.

---

## Modes of Reproduction

### Asexual Reproduction

**Asexual reproduction** involves only one parent organism. It produces offspring that are genetically identical (or nearly identical) to the parent. It occurs in lower organisms.

**Fission:**
- **Binary fission:** The organism splits into two equal halves. Example: **Amoeba** (can divide in any plane), **Leishmania** (divides along a definite axis)
- **Multiple fission:** The organism divides into many daughter cells simultaneously. Example: **Plasmodium** (malaria parasite)

**Fragmentation:** The organism breaks into two or more pieces, each growing into a complete organism. Example: **Spirogyra** (a filamentous alga)

**Regeneration:** The ability of an organism to grow a complete body from a cut body part using specialised cells. Example: **Hydra**, **Planaria**. Note: Regeneration is not the same as reproduction because it cannot occur from every piece of the body — it requires organised, specialised cells.

**Budding:** A small outgrowth (bud) develops on the parent body, grows, and eventually detaches to form a new individual. Example: **Hydra**, **Yeast**

**Vegetative Propagation:** New plants grow from vegetative parts (roots, stems, leaves) without seeds. Examples:
- **Stem:** Potato (eyes/buds), sugarcane, rose (stem cuttings)
- **Roots:** Sweet potato, dahlia
- **Leaves:** Bryophyllum (buds on leaf margins grow into new plants)

Advantages of vegetative propagation: Plants can bear flowers and fruits earlier; plants that have lost the ability to produce seeds can reproduce; genetic uniformity is maintained.

**Spore Formation:** Organisms produce spores in a **sporangium**. These spores are enclosed in thick walls for protection. Under favourable conditions, spores germinate into new organisms. Example: **Rhizopus** (bread mould)

> **Key idea:** Asexual reproduction creates offspring from a single parent with minimal genetic variation. Methods include fission, fragmentation, regeneration, budding, vegetative propagation, and spore formation.

---

## Sexual Reproduction

**Sexual reproduction** involves two parents — male and female — who produce specialised **germ cells (gametes)** that fuse to form a new individual. It creates greater **genetic variation** because DNA from two individuals combines.

### Why the Variation?

DNA copies generated during reproduction are not perfectly identical. These variations may be beneficial, harmful, or neutral. Over time, variations that provide survival advantages are selected by nature (natural selection), helping the species adapt to changing environments.

> **Key idea:** Sexual reproduction generates greater variation by combining DNA from two parents. These variations are crucial for evolution and species survival in changing environments.

## Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants

### Flower Structure

A flower has four main parts:
- **Sepals** — green, outermost protective parts (collectively: calyx)
- **Petals** — colourful, attract pollinators (collectively: corolla)
- **Stamens** — male reproductive organs (anther + filament). The **anther** produces **pollen grains** (male gametes).
- **Pistil/Carpel** — female reproductive organ consisting of **stigma** (receives pollen), **style** (tube connecting stigma to ovary), and **ovary** (contains **ovules** with female gametes)

A flower with both stamens and pistil is called **bisexual** (e.g., Hibiscus, mustard). A flower with only one is called **unisexual** (e.g., papaya, watermelon).

### Pollination

**Pollination** is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma of a flower.
- **Self-pollination:** Pollen transferred to the stigma of the same flower
- **Cross-pollination:** Pollen transferred to the stigma of another flower of the same species (by wind, water, insects, birds)

### Fertilisation

After pollination, the pollen grain germinates on the stigma and grows a **pollen tube** through the style to reach the ovule. The male gamete travels down the pollen tube and fuses with the female gamete (egg) in the ovule. This fusion is called **fertilisation**.

After fertilisation:
- The **zygote** develops into an **embryo**
- The **ovule** develops into a **seed**
- The **ovary** develops into a **fruit**

> **Key idea:** In flowering plants, pollination (transfer of pollen) is followed by fertilisation (fusion of male and female gametes). The ovule becomes the seed and the ovary becomes the fruit.

## Reproduction in Human Beings

### Puberty

**Puberty** is the period when the reproductive system matures and becomes functional.

Changes in boys: growth of facial hair, deepening of voice, growth of body hair, broadening of shoulders
Changes in girls: enlargement of breasts, widening of hips, growth of body hair, onset of menstruation

These changes are controlled by hormones — **testosterone** in males and **oestrogen** in females.

### Male Reproductive System

- **Testes** — produce sperms (male gametes) and secrete testosterone. Located outside the abdomen in the **scrotum** (sperm formation requires a lower temperature).
- **Vas deferens** — tube that carries sperms from testes to the urethra
- **Seminal vesicles and prostate gland** — add secretions (seminal fluid) that provide nutrition and ease transport of sperms
- **Urethra** — common passage for sperms and urine
- **Penis** — organ for delivering sperms into the female body

### Female Reproductive System

- **Ovaries** — produce eggs (female gametes) and secrete oestrogen. One egg is released every month (ovulation).
- **Fallopian tubes (Oviducts)** — carry the egg from ovary to uterus; site of fertilisation
- **Uterus (Womb)** — elastic, muscular organ where the embryo develops
- **Vagina** — receives sperms during intercourse; birth canal

### Fertilisation and Development

Fertilisation occurs in the **fallopian tube**. The fertilised egg (zygote) divides to form an **embryo**, which implants in the thick, blood-rich lining of the uterus. The embryo develops organs and is then called a **foetus**.

The **placenta** provides nutrition — a disc-shaped tissue with villi embedded in the uterine wall. Glucose and oxygen pass from the mother's blood to the embryo, and waste products move in the reverse direction.

Development takes approximately **nine months**. Birth occurs through rhythmic contractions of uterine muscles.

### Menstruation

If the egg is not fertilised, the thickened uterine lining breaks down and is shed through the vagina as blood and mucus. This is called **menstruation** and occurs roughly every month, lasting 2-8 days.

> **Key idea:** The male reproductive system produces sperms and delivers them. The female reproductive system produces eggs, provides a site for fertilisation, and nourishes the developing embryo. Menstruation occurs when the egg is not fertilised.

## Reproductive Health

**Contraceptive methods** to prevent pregnancy include:
1. **Mechanical barriers** — condoms (also prevent STDs), diaphragms
2. **Hormonal methods** — oral contraceptive pills (prevent egg release; may have side effects)
3. **Intrauterine devices** — Copper-T, loop (placed in uterus; may cause side effects)
4. **Surgical methods** — vasectomy (blocking vas deferens in males), tubectomy (blocking fallopian tubes in females)

**Sexually transmitted diseases (STDs):** Gonorrhoea, syphilis (bacterial), warts, HIV-AIDS (viral). Condoms help prevent transmission.

The copper-T prevents pregnancy but does **not** protect against sexually transmitted diseases.

> **Key idea:** Various contraceptive methods exist to prevent unwanted pregnancies. Only barrier methods (condoms) provide some protection against sexually transmitted diseases.
