## Introduction to Chemical Reactions

In everyday life, we observe numerous changes taking place around us. Milk left at room temperature during summers turns sour, an iron nail left exposed to humid atmosphere acquires a reddish-brown coating, grapes get fermented, and food gets digested in our body. In all these situations, the nature and identity of the initial substances change. Whenever such a **chemical change** occurs, we say that a **chemical reaction** has taken place.

Several observations help us determine whether a chemical reaction has occurred:

- **Change in state** (solid to liquid, liquid to gas, etc.)
- **Change in colour**
- **Evolution of a gas**
- **Change in temperature**

For example, when a **magnesium ribbon** is cleaned with sandpaper and burnt in air, it burns with a dazzling white flame and changes into a white powder called **magnesium oxide**. This is a chemical reaction between magnesium and oxygen.

> **Key idea:** A chemical reaction involves the transformation of one or more substances (reactants) into new substances (products), accompanied by observable changes such as colour change, gas evolution, temperature change, or change of state.

## Chemical Equations

A **chemical equation** is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction. It shows the reactants and products of a reaction using chemical formulae.

### Word Equations

The simplest way to represent a chemical reaction is through a **word-equation**:

**Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide**

The substances that undergo change (magnesium and oxygen) are called **reactants**, written on the left-hand side (LHS). The new substances formed (magnesium oxide) are called **products**, written on the right-hand side (RHS). An arrow pointing from reactants to products indicates the direction of the reaction.

### Chemical Formulae Equations

Word-equations can be made more concise by using **chemical formulae**:

**Mg + O₂ → MgO**

This is called a **skeletal chemical equation** since it may not be balanced.

> **Key idea:** A chemical equation uses symbols and formulae to represent a chemical reaction, with reactants on the left and products on the right, separated by an arrow.

## Balanced Chemical Equations

According to the **law of conservation of mass**, mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. This means the total number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides of the equation. An equation that satisfies this condition is called a **balanced chemical equation**.

### Steps to Balance a Chemical Equation (Hit-and-Trial Method)

1. **Write the skeletal equation** with correct formulae of reactants and products.
2. **List the number of atoms** of each element on both sides.
3. **Start balancing** with the compound containing the maximum number of atoms, selecting the element with the highest count.
4. **Use coefficients** (whole numbers placed before formulae) to equalise atoms. Never alter the chemical formulae themselves.
5. **Check all elements** are balanced.
6. **Add physical state symbols**: (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, (g) for gas, and (aq) for aqueous solution.

**Example:** Balancing the reaction of iron with steam:

- Skeletal equation: Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
- Balanced equation: 3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) → Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g)

Reaction conditions such as temperature, pressure, or catalyst may be indicated above or below the arrow.

> **Key idea:** A balanced chemical equation has an equal number of atoms of each element on both sides, satisfying the law of conservation of mass. Use the hit-and-trial method to balance equations by adjusting coefficients.

## Types of Chemical Reactions

### Combination Reaction

A **combination reaction** is one in which two or more substances combine to form a single product.

- **Calcium oxide (quick lime) + Water → Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime)**
  CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) + Heat

- **Burning of coal:** C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)

- **Formation of water:** 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

The reaction of quick lime with water is highly **exothermic** (releases heat). Slaked lime is used for **whitewashing** walls, where it slowly reacts with CO₂ in air to form calcium carbonate, giving walls a shiny finish.

> **Key idea:** In a combination reaction, two or more reactants combine to form a single product. Many combination reactions are exothermic.

---

### Decomposition Reaction

A **decomposition reaction** is the opposite of a combination reaction. A single substance breaks down to produce two or more simpler substances.

**Thermal decomposition** (by heat):
- 2FeSO₄(s) → Fe₂O₃(s) + SO₂(g) + SO₃(g)
  (Ferrous sulphate crystals change from green to brown)
- CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
  (Limestone to quick lime — important in cement manufacture)
- 2Pb(NO₃)₂(s) → 2PbO(s) + 4NO₂(g) + O₂(g)
  (Lead nitrate produces brown fumes of nitrogen dioxide)

**Electrolytic decomposition** (by electricity):
- 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
  (Electrolysis of water produces hydrogen and oxygen in a 2:1 ratio)

**Photolytic decomposition** (by light):
- 2AgCl(s) → 2Ag(s) + Cl₂(g)
  (Silver chloride turns grey in sunlight — used in black and white photography)
- 2AgBr(s) → 2Ag(s) + Br₂(g)

Decomposition reactions require energy (heat, light, or electricity) and are therefore **endothermic reactions**.

> **Key idea:** Decomposition reactions break down a single compound into two or more simpler substances. They require energy input and are endothermic in nature.

---

### Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

**Exothermic reactions** release energy (usually as heat) along with the formation of products:
- Burning of natural gas: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g)
- Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆(aq) + 6O₂(aq) → 6CO₂(aq) + 6H₂O(l) + energy
- Decomposition of vegetable matter into compost

**Endothermic reactions** absorb energy from the surroundings:
- Decomposition of calcium carbonate by heating
- Decomposition of silver chloride by light
- Mixing barium hydroxide with ammonium chloride (temperature drops)

> **Key idea:** Exothermic reactions release heat energy, while endothermic reactions absorb energy. Respiration is an important exothermic process in living organisms.

---

### Displacement Reaction

A **displacement reaction** occurs when a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.

- **Iron displaces copper:** Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
  (The blue colour of copper sulphate fades, and a brownish coating of copper appears on the iron nail)

- **Zinc displaces copper:** Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)

- **Lead displaces copper:** Pb(s) + CuCl₂(aq) → PbCl₂(aq) + Cu(s)

Zinc and lead are more reactive than copper, so they can displace copper from its compounds.

> **Key idea:** In a displacement reaction, a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.

---

### Double Displacement Reaction

A **double displacement reaction** involves an exchange of ions between two reactants to form two new compounds.

**Na₂SO₄(aq) + BaCl₂(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2NaCl(aq)**

When sodium sulphate and barium chloride solutions are mixed, a white **precipitate** of barium sulphate is formed. Any reaction that produces a precipitate is called a **precipitation reaction**.

Similarly, mixing lead nitrate solution with potassium iodide solution produces a yellow precipitate of lead iodide.

> **Key idea:** In a double displacement reaction, ions are exchanged between two compounds. When an insoluble product (precipitate) forms, it is also called a precipitation reaction.

## Oxidation and Reduction (Redox Reactions)

### Oxidation

**Oxidation** is defined as:
- The **gain of oxygen** by a substance, OR
- The **loss of hydrogen** by a substance.

Example: When copper powder is heated in air, it gains oxygen to form black copper(II) oxide:
2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO

### Reduction

**Reduction** is defined as:
- The **loss of oxygen** by a substance, OR
- The **gain of hydrogen** by a substance.

Example: When hydrogen gas is passed over heated copper oxide, the oxide loses oxygen:
CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O

### Redox Reactions

In the above reaction, CuO is **reduced** (loses oxygen) while H₂ is **oxidised** (gains oxygen). Since oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously, such reactions are called **oxidation-reduction reactions** or **redox reactions**.

Other examples:
- ZnO + C → Zn + CO (C is oxidised, ZnO is reduced)
- MnO₂ + 4HCl → MnCl₂ + 2H₂O + Cl₂ (HCl is oxidised, MnO₂ is reduced)

> **Key idea:** Oxidation is the gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen. Reduction is the loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen. Reactions where both occur simultaneously are called redox reactions.

## Effects of Oxidation in Everyday Life

### Corrosion

**Corrosion** is the process by which metals are gradually eaten away by the action of air, moisture, and acids on their surface.

- **Iron** acquires a reddish-brown coating called **rust** (iron oxide).
- **Copper** develops a green coating (basic copper carbonate).
- **Silver** gets a black coating (silver sulphide).

Corrosion causes enormous damage to buildings, bridges, ships, and other metal structures. Painting, oiling, greasing, galvanising, chrome plating, and alloying are methods used to prevent corrosion.

### Rancidity

**Rancidity** is the oxidation of fats and oils in food, causing unpleasant smell and taste changes.

Methods to prevent rancidity:
- Adding **antioxidants** to food
- Storing food in **airtight containers**
- Flushing food packages with **nitrogen gas** (e.g., chips packets)
- Refrigeration

> **Key idea:** Corrosion is the deterioration of metals due to oxidation by environmental substances. Rancidity is the oxidation of fats and oils in food. Both are everyday effects of oxidation reactions.
