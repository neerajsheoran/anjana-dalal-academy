## Identifying Chemical Reactions

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I know a chemical reaction has actually occurred? Dissolving sugar in water looks like a change, but it is not a chemical reaction.

**Ravi:** Good question. Look for these indicators — a change in colour, evolution of a gas, change in temperature, formation of a precipitate, or a change in state that cannot be easily reversed. Dissolving sugar is a physical change because you can get the sugar back by evaporating the water. But when magnesium burns in air with a dazzling white flame and becomes magnesium oxide — that is irreversible. New bonds are formed and broken.

**Kiran:** The burning of a magnesium ribbon is spectacular. Does industry use magnesium?

**Ravi:** Magnesium is used in fireworks — those bright white sparklers during Diwali contain magnesium. It is also used in lightweight alloys for aircraft. India's Hindustan Aeronautics Limited uses magnesium alloys in helicopter components. Understanding such reactions is the foundation of materials science.

## Balancing Chemical Equations

**Kiran:** The law of conservation of mass says atoms cannot appear or disappear. So balancing is just making sure every atom on the left appears on the right?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Use the hit-and-trial method. Start with the most complex molecule, count each element on both sides, and adjust coefficients until balanced. Never change the chemical formula — only the coefficients. For example, Fe plus H2O gives Fe3O4 plus H2. Balancing gives 3Fe plus 4H2O gives Fe3O4 plus 4H2.

**Kiran:** The state symbols — s, l, g, aq — are those mandatory in board answers?

**Ravi:** Yes. CBSE marking schemes specifically award marks for correct state symbols. The symbol s means solid, l means liquid, g means gas, and aq means dissolved in water. Also, an upward arrow indicates gas evolution and a downward arrow indicates precipitation. Writing these shows conceptual clarity.

**Kiran:** What about conditions like "heat" or "catalyst" written above the arrow?

**Ravi:** Always include them when given. MnO2 as a catalyst in the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide, sunlight in the decomposition of silver chloride, electricity in electrolysis — these conditions are part of the complete equation.

## Types of Chemical Reactions

**Kiran:** Combination, decomposition, displacement, double displacement, oxidation-reduction — how do I quickly identify the type?

**Ravi:** Use this framework. If two or more reactants form one product, it is combination. If one reactant breaks into two or more products, it is decomposition. If an element displaces another element from a compound, it is displacement. If two compounds exchange ions, it is double displacement. And if oxygen or hydrogen is gained or lost, think redox.

**Kiran:** The activity series determines which metal displaces which?

**Ravi:** Yes. A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive one. Zinc displaces copper from copper sulphate solution because zinc is more reactive. But copper cannot displace zinc. This is why copper vessels develop a green coating — the copper reacts with atmospheric gases, not displacing them.

**Kiran:** Decomposition reactions that need heat are called thermal decomposition. What about those that need light?

**Ravi:** Those are photochemical decomposition. Silver chloride turning grey in sunlight is a classic example — it decomposes to silver and chlorine. This is the basis of black-and-white photography. And electrolytic decomposition uses electricity — electrolysis of water into hydrogen and oxygen is the industrial method used for producing hydrogen fuel.

## Oxidation and Reduction

**Kiran:** Oxidation is gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen, and reduction is the opposite. They always occur together?

**Ravi:** Always. That is why they are called redox reactions. In the reaction CuO plus H2 gives Cu plus H2O, copper oxide is reduced because it loses oxygen, and hydrogen is oxidised because it gains oxygen. The substance that gets reduced is the oxidising agent, and the substance that gets oxidised is the reducing agent.

**Kiran:** Corrosion and rancidity — are those also oxidation?

**Ravi:** Yes. Rusting of iron is oxidation of iron by oxygen in the presence of moisture — the product is hydrated iron oxide, which is reddish-brown. Rancidity is oxidation of fats and oils in food, making them taste bad. Antioxidants like BHA and BHT are added to packaged food to prevent this. Nitrogen gas flushing in chip packets serves the same purpose.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What carries the most marks in this chapter?

**Ravi:** Balancing equations and identifying reaction types together account for most of the marks — typically 3-5 marks. One common question gives you a word equation and asks you to write the balanced chemical equation with state symbols. Another asks you to identify the type of reaction from a given equation.

**Kiran:** Common mistakes?

**Ravi:** Three main ones. First, writing wrong chemical formulae — memorise the valencies and formulae of common compounds. Second, forgetting state symbols. Third, changing subscripts instead of coefficients while balancing. The subscript is part of the formula and must not be altered. Only the coefficient in front of the formula changes during balancing.

## Connecting to Industry and Research

**Kiran:** Are these types of reactions used in Indian industry?

**Ravi:** Every single type. Combination reactions are used in cement manufacturing — CaO plus SiO2 gives calcium silicate. Decomposition reactions are used in the Bhopal gas tragedy's chemical plant and in petroleum refining. Displacement reactions are the basis of metal extraction at Tata Steel. Redox reactions power every battery in every electric vehicle being manufactured in India today.

**Kiran:** Understanding reaction types helps predict what will happen when chemicals interact?

**Ravi:** That is precisely the power of classification. When you know the type, you can predict the products. A chemist at a pharmaceutical company in Hyderabad uses these principles daily to design drug synthesis routes. For NEET, recognising reaction types quickly is essential for organic chemistry in Class 12. Build this skill now and it pays dividends for years.
