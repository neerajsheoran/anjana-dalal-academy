## What Are Life Processes?

Living organisms are well-organised structures that constantly need repair and maintenance to survive. The processes that together perform this maintenance job are called **life processes**. These include **nutrition**, **respiration**, **transportation**, and **excretion**. These processes must continue even when an organism is not performing any visible activity — even while sleeping.

Movement at the molecular level is a defining characteristic of life. Organisms must constantly move molecules around to maintain their organised structures against the tendency of the environment to break down order.

> **Key idea:** Life processes are the essential maintenance functions — nutrition, respiration, transport, and excretion — that keep organisms alive by providing energy, raw materials, and waste removal.

## Nutrition

**Nutrition** is the process of taking in food (a source of energy) from outside the body and using it for growth, repair, and energy.

### Autotrophic Nutrition

**Autotrophs** (like green plants and some bacteria) make their own food from simple inorganic substances using external energy sources. The main process is **photosynthesis**.

**Photosynthesis:** 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → (sunlight, chlorophyll) → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

The events during photosynthesis:
1. **Absorption** of light energy by **chlorophyll** (in chloroplasts).
2. **Conversion** of light energy to chemical energy and **splitting of water** molecules into hydrogen and oxygen (light reaction).
3. **Reduction** of carbon dioxide to **carbohydrates** (dark reaction).

**Stomata** — tiny pores on leaf surfaces — allow gas exchange for photosynthesis. **Guard cells** control the opening and closing of stomata to regulate water loss. Desert plants take up CO₂ at night and use it during the day.

Carbohydrates not immediately used are stored as **starch**. Plants also need nitrogen (from nitrates/nitrites in soil), phosphorus, iron, and magnesium as raw materials.

### Heterotrophic Nutrition

**Heterotrophs** depend on other organisms for nutrition. Types include:
- **Saprophytic:** Break down food outside the body, then absorb it (e.g., fungi — bread moulds, yeast, mushrooms).
- **Parasitic:** Derive nutrition from a host without killing it (e.g., cuscuta/amar-bel, ticks, lice, tapeworms).
- **Holozoic:** Take in whole food and break it down inside the body (e.g., Amoeba, humans).

**Amoeba** uses temporary finger-like extensions called **pseudopodia** to engulf food particles, forming a **food vacuole** where digestion occurs.

**Paramoecium** uses **cilia** covering its cell surface to move food to a specific spot for ingestion.

> **Key idea:** Autotrophs make their own food through photosynthesis using CO₂, water, sunlight, and chlorophyll. Heterotrophs depend on other organisms for their nutrition.

## Nutrition in Human Beings

The human **alimentary canal** is a long tube extending from the mouth to the anus with specialised regions for different functions.

### Digestion Process

1. **Mouth:** Food is crushed by teeth and mixed with **saliva** from salivary glands. Saliva contains the enzyme **salivary amylase** which breaks down **starch** into **sugar** (maltose). The tongue helps in mixing and swallowing.

2. **Oesophagus (Food pipe):** Food is pushed down by wave-like contractions called **peristalsis** (contraction and relaxation of muscles along the gut wall).

3. **Stomach:** A thick-walled muscular bag. Gastric glands in the stomach wall secrete:
   - **Hydrochloric acid** — creates an acidic medium (kills bacteria, activates enzymes)
   - **Pepsin** — a protein-digesting enzyme
   - **Mucus** — protects the stomach lining from acid

4. **Small intestine:** The longest part of the alimentary canal. Receives secretions from the **liver** (bile juice) and **pancreas** (pancreatic juice):
   - **Bile juice** (from liver, stored in gall bladder) — makes the medium alkaline and emulsifies fats (breaks large fat globules into smaller ones)
   - **Pancreatic juice** — contains enzymes (trypsin for proteins, lipase for fats, amylase for starch)
   - **Intestinal juice** — completes digestion of proteins, carbohydrates, and fats

   The inner lining of the small intestine has finger-like projections called **villi** which increase the surface area for absorption. Villi are richly supplied with blood vessels for efficient absorption of digested food.

5. **Large intestine:** Absorbs water and some salts from undigested food. The remaining waste is expelled through the **anus**.

> **Key idea:** The human digestive system progressively breaks down complex food molecules using mechanical processes (chewing, peristalsis) and chemical processes (enzymes, bile) into simple absorbable forms. Villi in the small intestine maximise absorption.

## Respiration

**Respiration** is the process of breaking down food (glucose) to release energy in the form of **ATP** (adenosine triphosphate). ATP is the energy currency of cells.

### Breakdown of Glucose

Glucose is first broken down in the **cytoplasm** into a 3-carbon molecule called **pyruvate**. After that, the pathway depends on oxygen availability:

**Aerobic respiration** (with oxygen, in mitochondria):
Pyruvate → CO₂ + H₂O + Energy (large amount — 38 ATP)

**Anaerobic respiration** (without oxygen):
- In **yeast** (fermentation): Pyruvate → Ethanol + CO₂ + Energy (less)
- In **muscles** (during vigorous exercise): Pyruvate → Lactic acid + Energy (less)

The buildup of **lactic acid** in muscles during heavy exercise causes **cramps**.

Aerobic respiration produces **much more energy** than anaerobic respiration because glucose is completely broken down.

### Human Respiratory System

The human respiratory system consists of:
- **Nostrils** — filter and warm incoming air; hair and mucus trap dust
- **Pharynx and larynx** — air passages
- **Trachea (windpipe)** — rings of cartilage prevent collapse
- **Bronchi** — two branches entering the lungs
- **Bronchioles** — smaller branches within the lungs
- **Alveoli** — balloon-like structures at the end of bronchioles

**Alveoli** provide an enormous surface area for gas exchange. They have thin walls and are richly supplied with blood capillaries. Oxygen diffuses into the blood, and CO₂ diffuses out from the blood into the alveoli.

**Breathing mechanism:** The diaphragm and rib muscles contract (inhalation) to expand the chest cavity, drawing air in. They relax (exhalation) to push air out.

**Haemoglobin** in red blood cells carries oxygen. A deficiency of haemoglobin leads to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity, causing breathlessness and fatigue.

> **Key idea:** Respiration breaks down glucose to release energy as ATP. Aerobic respiration (in mitochondria) releases much more energy than anaerobic respiration. In humans, alveoli in the lungs provide a large surface area for efficient gas exchange.

## Transportation in Human Beings

The **circulatory system** in humans consists of the **heart**, **blood**, and **blood vessels**.

### Blood

**Blood** is a fluid connective tissue consisting of:
- **Plasma** — liquid portion (straw-coloured) that carries dissolved nutrients, CO₂, nitrogenous wastes
- **Red blood cells (RBCs)** — contain haemoglobin to transport oxygen
- **White blood cells (WBCs)** — fight infections
- **Platelets** — help in blood clotting at wound sites

### Blood Vessels

- **Arteries** — carry blood away from the heart; thick elastic walls; no valves
- **Veins** — carry blood towards the heart; thin walls; have valves to prevent backflow
- **Capillaries** — thin-walled; allow exchange of materials between blood and tissues

### Heart and Double Circulation

The human heart has **four chambers**: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, and left ventricle. Valves ensure blood flows in one direction.

**Double circulation:** Blood passes through the heart twice in one complete cycle:
1. **Pulmonary circulation:** Right ventricle → Lungs (for oxygenation) → Left atrium
2. **Systemic circulation:** Left ventricle → Body organs → Right atrium

Separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood is essential in **mammals and birds** because they maintain constant body temperature (warm-blooded). This requires more energy, hence more efficient oxygen supply — which is achieved by complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

**Blood pressure:** The force exerted by blood on artery walls. Normal: 120/80 mm Hg (systolic/diastolic).

**Lymph** (tissue fluid) is a yellowish fluid similar to plasma that drains into lymphatic capillaries and eventually returns to the blood. It carries digested fats and helps in disease defence.

> **Key idea:** The human circulatory system uses double circulation to efficiently supply oxygenated blood to the body. Separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood is crucial for warm-blooded animals.

## Transportation in Plants

Plants have a **vascular system** consisting of **xylem** and **phloem** for transport.

### Transport of Water and Minerals (Xylem)

**Xylem** vessels and tracheids form a continuous system of water-conducting channels from roots to leaves.

- **Root pressure:** Roots actively take up ions, creating a concentration difference that draws water in by osmosis. This creates an upward push.
- **Transpiration pull:** Evaporation of water from leaf stomata creates a suction force that pulls water up through xylem vessels. This is the major driving force during the day.

**Transpiration** (loss of water vapour from aerial plant parts) helps in:
- Absorption and upward movement of water and minerals
- Temperature regulation in plants

### Transport of Food (Phloem)

**Phloem** transports the products of photosynthesis (**translocation**) from leaves to other parts of the plant.

- Translocation occurs in **sieve tubes** with the help of **companion cells**.
- This process requires **energy** (from ATP).
- Sucrose is loaded into phloem, increasing osmotic pressure, which draws water in and moves the food material.
- Transport occurs in both upward and downward directions based on the plant's needs.

> **Key idea:** Xylem transports water and minerals upward using root pressure and transpiration pull. Phloem transports food (products of photosynthesis) using energy-dependent translocation.

## Excretion

**Excretion** is the removal of harmful metabolic waste products from the body.

### Excretion in Human Beings

The **excretory system** includes:
- A pair of **kidneys** (in the abdomen)
- A pair of **ureters**
- **Urinary bladder**
- **Urethra**

**Nephron** — the basic filtration unit of the kidney. Each kidney has millions of nephrons. A nephron consists of:
- **Bowman's capsule** — cup-shaped structure surrounding a cluster of capillaries (glomerulus)
- **Tubular part** — reabsorbs useful substances like glucose, amino acids, salts, and water

Urine formation involves:
1. **Filtration** — blood is filtered in the glomerulus; filtrate enters Bowman's capsule
2. **Selective reabsorption** — useful substances are reabsorbed in the tubule
3. **Collection** — urine flows through ureters to the urinary bladder

The amount of water reabsorbed depends on how much excess water the body has and how much waste needs to be dissolved.

**Artificial kidney (Haemodialysis):** In kidney failure, an artificial kidney removes nitrogenous waste from blood through dialysis using semi-permeable tubes suspended in dialysing fluid.

### Excretion in Plants

Plants use different strategies for excretion:
- **Oxygen** (waste from photosynthesis) and **CO₂** (waste from respiration) are removed through stomata.
- **Excess water** is removed by transpiration.
- Waste products are stored in **cellular vacuoles**, **leaves that fall off**, or as **resins and gums** in old xylem.
- Some wastes are excreted into the soil.

> **Key idea:** The kidneys filter blood through millions of nephrons, selectively reabsorbing useful substances and excreting waste as urine. Plants excrete wastes through stomata, transpiration, leaf fall, and storage in vacuoles.
