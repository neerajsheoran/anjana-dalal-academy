## Nutrition — Autotrophic and Heterotrophic

**Kiran:** Papa, photosynthesis seems simple — CO2 plus water plus sunlight gives glucose and oxygen. But the actual process is more complex?

**Ravi:** Much more complex. There are two main stages. The light reaction happens in the thylakoids of chloroplasts — chlorophyll absorbs light, water molecules are split into hydrogen and oxygen (photolysis), and ATP and NADPH are produced. The dark reaction (Calvin cycle) happens in the stroma — CO2 is reduced to glucose using the ATP and NADPH from the light reaction.

**Kiran:** The stomata play a critical role — they let CO2 in and O2 out?

**Ravi:** Yes. Guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata. During the day, stomata are generally open for gas exchange. At night, most plants close their stomata to conserve water. Desert plants like cacti have a special adaptation — they open stomata at night to take in CO2 and store it, then use it for photosynthesis during the day. This is called CAM photosynthesis.

**Kiran:** Heterotrophic nutrition — saprophytic, parasitic, and holozoic. Amoeba uses pseudopodia?

**Ravi:** Yes. Amoeba engulfs food using temporary finger-like extensions called pseudopodia, forming a food vacuole. Paramoecium uses cilia. These are classic CBSE diagram questions — practice drawing both. For NEET, understanding the different modes of nutrition is foundational for biology.

## Human Digestive System

**Kiran:** The digestive system breaks down food mechanically and chemically. What happens at each stage?

**Ravi:** In the mouth, teeth physically break food while salivary amylase begins starch digestion. In the stomach, hydrochloric acid creates an acidic environment for pepsin to digest proteins. The stomach's mucus lining protects it from its own acid. In the small intestine, bile from the liver emulsifies fats, and pancreatic enzymes complete the digestion of proteins, carbohydrates, and fats.

**Kiran:** Absorption happens in the small intestine through villi?

**Ravi:** Yes. The inner surface of the small intestine has millions of finger-like projections called villi, which greatly increase the surface area for absorption. Each villus has a network of blood capillaries and a lacteal for absorbing nutrients. The large intestine absorbs water and forms solid waste.

**Kiran:** Why is the small intestine so long — about 6 metres?

**Ravi:** To maximise both digestion time and absorption surface area. The combination of length, villi, and microvilli gives the small intestine an effective surface area equivalent to a tennis court. This is a favourite CBSE question — "How is the small intestine designed for efficient absorption?"

## Respiration

**Kiran:** Respiration is not the same as breathing?

**Ravi:** Important distinction. Breathing is the physical process of inhaling and exhaling air. Respiration is the chemical process of breaking down glucose to release energy. Cellular respiration happens in the mitochondria. Aerobic respiration uses oxygen and produces CO2, water, and 38 ATP molecules per glucose. Anaerobic respiration occurs without oxygen — in yeast it produces ethanol and CO2 (fermentation), and in our muscles during intense exercise it produces lactic acid.

**Kiran:** That is why muscles ache after heavy exercise — lactic acid buildup?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The oxygen debt is repaid when you breathe heavily after exercise, and the lactic acid is broken down. Athletes train to improve their aerobic capacity so they can delay the switch to anaerobic respiration.

**Kiran:** The energy equation — C6H12O6 plus 6O2 gives 6CO2 plus 6H2O plus energy. This is the reverse of photosynthesis?

**Ravi:** Essentially, yes. Photosynthesis stores energy in glucose, and respiration releases it. Together, they form a beautiful cycle that sustains life on Earth.

## Transportation and Excretion

**Kiran:** The human circulatory system has a double circulation — blood passes through the heart twice in one complete circuit?

**Ravi:** Yes. The right side of the heart pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation), and the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic circulation). This separation ensures efficient oxygen delivery. The four chambers — two atria and two ventricles — with valves prevent backflow.

**Kiran:** In plants, xylem carries water upward and phloem carries food downward?

**Ravi:** Xylem transports water and minerals from roots to leaves using transpiration pull — it is a one-way, passive process in dead cells. Phloem transports sucrose and other products of photosynthesis from leaves to other parts — it is bidirectional and uses living cells with active transport. The distinction between xylem and phloem is a high-frequency board question.

**Kiran:** Excretion — the kidneys filter blood and produce urine. The nephron is the functional unit?

**Ravi:** Yes. Each kidney has about one million nephrons. Blood enters through the glomerulus, gets filtered in Bowman's capsule, and useful substances like glucose and amino acids are reabsorbed in the tubular region. The remaining waste with water forms urine. Dialysis machines replicate nephron function for patients with kidney failure — a technology developed from understanding this biology.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** This is one of the longest chapters. How should I prepare?

**Ravi:** Focus on diagrams — the human digestive system, heart, nephron, and stomata are commonly asked for labelling. Learn the functions of each organ and enzyme. Know the differences between aerobic and anaerobic respiration, arteries and veins, xylem and phloem. Practice 3-mark and 5-mark questions that ask for process explanations with diagrams.

**Kiran:** For NEET preparation, which parts of this chapter matter most?

**Ravi:** All of it. This chapter is essentially human physiology at a basic level. NEET expands on every topic here — digestive enzymes, cardiac cycle, nephron function, transpiration. Building a strong foundation now saves enormous time in Class 11 and 12 biology.
