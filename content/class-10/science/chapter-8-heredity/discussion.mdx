## Mendel's Experiments

**Kiran:** Papa, Mendel worked with pea plants in the 1860s, but his work was ignored for decades. Why is he called the Father of Genetics?

**Ravi:** Mendel's genius was in his systematic, mathematical approach. He chose pea plants because they had clearly contrasting traits — tall versus short, round versus wrinkled seeds, purple versus white flowers. He carefully controlled pollination and counted the offspring in each generation. His discovery that traits are inherited in specific ratios — 3:1 in the F2 generation — was revolutionary. His work was rediscovered in 1900 by three scientists independently.

**Kiran:** The monohybrid cross — TT crossed with tt gives all Tt in F1, and then Tt crossed with Tt gives TT, Tt, Tt, tt in F2. The 3:1 phenotypic ratio.

**Ravi:** Correct. The genotypic ratio is 1:2:1 — one TT, two Tt, one tt. The key insight is that traits are determined by pairs of factors, which we now call genes. Each parent contributes one factor to the offspring. The dominant trait masks the recessive trait in heterozygous individuals.

**Kiran:** So tallness is dominant over shortness in pea plants. Does dominance always work this way?

**Ravi:** In Mendel's examples, yes. But in nature, there are cases of incomplete dominance and codominance, which you will study in Class 12. For CBSE Class 10, focus on Mendel's results with complete dominance.

## Dihybrid Cross

**Kiran:** The dihybrid cross considers two traits simultaneously. The F2 ratio is 9:3:3:1?

**Ravi:** Yes. Mendel crossed plants with round yellow seeds (RRYY) with wrinkled green seeds (rryy). The F1 generation was all round yellow (RrYy). In F2, he got 9 round yellow, 3 round green, 3 wrinkled yellow, and 1 wrinkled green — the 9:3:3:1 ratio.

**Kiran:** This shows that the two traits are inherited independently?

**Ravi:** Exactly. This is Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment — genes for different traits are inherited independently of each other, provided they are on different chromosomes. The 9:3:3:1 ratio is a direct consequence of two independent 3:1 ratios combined.

**Kiran:** Writing the Punnett square for a dihybrid cross with 16 boxes — that is commonly asked?

**Ravi:** Yes, it is a standard 5-mark question. Practice drawing the 4 by 4 Punnett square, filling in all 16 genotypes, and then counting the phenotypic ratios. Take care with the gamete combinations — RY, Ry, rY, ry for each parent.

## Sex Determination

**Kiran:** In humans, sex is determined by the X and Y chromosomes. Females are XX and males are XY. So the father determines the sex of the child?

**Ravi:** Yes. The mother always contributes an X chromosome. The father can contribute either X or Y — if X, the child is female (XX); if Y, the child is male (XY). The probability is 50-50 for each. This is an important concept both scientifically and socially, given that sex-selective practices have been a serious issue in India.

**Kiran:** The Pre-Conception and Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques Act bans sex determination tests for this reason?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The PCPNDT Act makes it illegal to use ultrasound or other techniques to determine the sex of an unborn child. Understanding the science empowers you to counter unscientific beliefs. No parent, diet, or ritual can "choose" the sex of a child — it is purely a matter of which sperm fertilises the egg.

**Kiran:** Some organisms have different sex determination systems?

**Ravi:** Yes. In birds, females are ZW and males are ZZ — the opposite of humans. Some organisms determine sex by environmental factors — temperature in certain reptiles. For CBSE, focus on the human XX-XY system.

## Evolution

**Kiran:** Heredity and evolution are connected — accumulated variations over generations lead to new species?

**Ravi:** Yes. When populations are separated geographically, they accumulate different variations. Over long periods, these differences become significant enough that the populations can no longer interbreed — speciation has occurred. Darwin's finches on the Galapagos Islands are a classic example.

**Kiran:** Homologous organs like the human hand, whale flipper, and bat wing — same structure, different functions. Analogous organs like bird wings and insect wings — different structure, same function.

**Ravi:** That distinction is critical for CBSE. Homologous organs indicate common ancestry — evidence for evolution. Analogous organs show convergent evolution — different organisms evolving similar solutions to similar problems. Fossils provide direct evidence of organisms that lived millions of years ago.

**Kiran:** India has important fossil sites?

**Ravi:** Yes. The Siwalik Hills have yielded fossils of ancient elephants and horses. The Deccan Traps preserve fossils from the time of the dinosaur extinction. Indian palaeontologists contribute significantly to understanding evolutionary history.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What are the key questions from this chapter?

**Ravi:** The monohybrid cross with Punnett square is almost guaranteed — 3-5 marks. Sex determination in humans is a favourite 2-3 mark question. Differences between homologous and analogous organs appear frequently. And the dihybrid cross ratio of 9:3:3:1 is tested in both MCQ and short answer formats.

**Kiran:** For NEET, this chapter expands significantly?

**Ravi:** Enormously. Mendel's laws, linkage, crossing over, chromosomal theory of inheritance, blood group genetics, sex-linked inheritance, Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium — all build on these Class 10 foundations. Mastering Mendel's crosses now creates a strong platform for Class 12 genetics.
