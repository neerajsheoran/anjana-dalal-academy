## Introduction — Accumulation of Variation During Reproduction

We have learnt that reproduction involves DNA copying, and this process is not perfectly accurate. The small differences (variations) that arise during reproduction are the basis of **heredity** — the passing of traits from parents to offspring.

Not all variations are equally important. Some may give a survival advantage, while others may not. Over many generations, the accumulation of variations can lead to significant differences between organisms, driving **evolution**.

> **Key idea:** Variations arise during DNA copying in reproduction. These variations accumulate over generations and are the basis of heredity and evolution.

## Heredity — Mendel's Contributions

**Gregor Johann Mendel** (1822-1884), an Austrian monk, is known as the "Father of Genetics." He performed systematic experiments with garden pea plants (*Pisum sativum*) and discovered the fundamental laws of heredity.

### Why Pea Plants?

Pea plants were ideal because they:
- Have many clearly contrasting traits (tall/short, round/wrinkled seeds, etc.)
- Can self-pollinate (allowing controlled crosses)
- Have a short generation time
- Produce many offspring

### Mendel's Monohybrid Cross

Mendel crossed plants with contrasting traits for a single character. For example, tall plants (TT) crossed with short plants (tt):

![Mendel's monohybrid cross: tall (TT) crossed with short (tt) producing all tall F1 (Tt), then F1 self-pollinated producing F2 in 3:1 ratio (TT, Tt, Tt, tt) shown in a Punnett square](mendel-monohybrid-cross-punnett-square.png)

**F₁ generation:** All plants were tall. The tall trait was expressed, and the short trait seemed to disappear. Mendel called the expressed trait **dominant** and the hidden trait **recessive**.

**F₂ generation:** When F₁ plants were self-pollinated, both tall and short plants appeared in a ratio of approximately **3:1** (3 tall : 1 short).

This showed that:
- The trait that appears in F₁ is **dominant** (tallness)
- The trait that remains hidden in F₁ but reappears in F₂ is **recessive** (shortness)
- Both traits are inherited, but the recessive trait is masked by the dominant one in F₁

### Genotype and Phenotype

- **Genotype** — the genetic makeup (e.g., TT, Tt, tt)
- **Phenotype** — the observable trait (e.g., tall or short)
- **Homozygous** — both copies of the gene are identical (TT or tt)
- **Heterozygous** — the two copies are different (Tt)

In F₂: TT (tall) : Tt (tall) : tt (short) = 1:2:1 genotypic ratio = 3:1 phenotypic ratio

> **Key idea:** Mendel's experiments showed that traits are controlled by pairs of factors (genes). Dominant traits are expressed when at least one dominant allele is present. Recessive traits are expressed only when both alleles are recessive.

## Dihybrid Cross — Independent Inheritance of Traits

Mendel also studied the inheritance of **two traits simultaneously** (dihybrid cross). He crossed plants with round, yellow seeds (RRYY) with plants having wrinkled, green seeds (rryy).

**F₁ generation:** All plants had round, yellow seeds (both dominant).

**F₂ generation:** Four types appeared in the ratio **9:3:3:1**:
- 9 Round, Yellow
- 3 Round, Green
- 3 Wrinkled, Yellow
- 1 Wrinkled, Green

New combinations (round green and wrinkled yellow) appeared, showing that the two traits are **inherited independently**. This is Mendel's **Law of Independent Assortment**.

> **Key idea:** Different traits are inherited independently of each other. This independent assortment creates new combinations of traits in the offspring.

## How Are These Traits Expressed?

Genes carry information for making **proteins**. Proteins control cellular processes and determine traits. For example, a gene may code for an enzyme that produces a specific pigment determining plant height or seed colour.

If a gene has a change (mutation) that produces an inefficient enzyme, the trait may change. In a heterozygous organism (Tt), one copy makes enough functional protein to produce the dominant phenotype.

### Genes and Chromosomes

- Each cell has **two copies** of each gene — one from each parent.
- Genes are located on **chromosomes** — separate, independent pieces of DNA.
- Each cell has two copies of each chromosome (diploid).
- **Germ cells (gametes)** have only **one copy** of each chromosome (haploid).
- When two gametes fuse during fertilisation, the diploid number is restored.

This explains why traits can be inherited independently — genes on different chromosomes assort independently into gametes.

> **Key idea:** Traits are expressed through proteins coded by genes. Genes are located on chromosomes. Germ cells carry half the chromosome number, and fertilisation restores the full number.

## Sex Determination

The sex of a newborn is determined differently in different species:
- Some reptiles: determined by the **temperature** at which eggs develop
- Snails: can change sex (not genetically determined)
- **Human beings:** genetically determined by **sex chromosomes**

![Sex determination chart showing father (XY) and mother (XX) with all possible combinations: XX (girl) and XY (boy), each with 50% probability](sex-determination-xx-xy-chart.png)

### Sex Determination in Humans

Humans have **23 pairs of chromosomes** — 22 pairs of **autosomes** and 1 pair of **sex chromosomes**.

- **Females:** XX (two X chromosomes)
- **Males:** XY (one X and one shorter Y chromosome)

All children inherit an **X chromosome** from their mother. The **father's contribution** determines the sex:
- If the father contributes an **X**, the child is a **girl (XX)**
- If the father contributes a **Y**, the child is a **boy (XY)**

The probability of having a boy or girl is **50:50** (1:1).

> **Key idea:** In humans, sex is determined by sex chromosomes. The father's sperm determines the sex of the child — X-bearing sperm produces a girl, Y-bearing sperm produces a boy.
