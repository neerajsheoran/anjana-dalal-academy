## Introduction

Light enables us to see objects. An object reflects light that falls on it, and this reflected light, when received by our eyes, allows us to see. Light travels in **straight lines** (rectilinear propagation), which is demonstrated by the sharp shadows cast by opaque objects.

> **Key idea:** Light travels in straight lines. We see objects because they reflect light into our eyes.

---

## Reflection of Light

When light falls on a polished surface (like a mirror), it bounces back. This is called **reflection**. The **laws of reflection** state:

1. The **angle of incidence** equals the **angle of reflection** (∠i = ∠r)
2. The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the surface at the point of incidence all lie in the **same plane**

A **plane mirror** forms an image that is virtual, erect, of the same size as the object, laterally inverted, and at the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front.

---

## Spherical Mirrors

**Spherical mirrors** have reflecting surfaces that form part of a sphere.

![Concave and convex mirrors side by side, showing concave mirror (reflecting surface curves inward, converges parallel rays to focus F) and convex mirror (reflecting surface curves outward, diverges parallel rays appearing to come from focus F behind mirror)](concave-convex-mirror-comparison.png)

### Types
- **Concave mirror:** Reflecting surface curves inward (like the inside of a spoon). Converges light.
- **Convex mirror:** Reflecting surface curves outward (like the back of a spoon). Diverges light.

### Key Terms
- **Pole (P):** Centre of the reflecting surface
- **Centre of curvature (C):** Centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part
- **Radius of curvature (R):** Radius of the sphere
- **Principal axis:** Line passing through P and C
- **Principal focus (F):** Point where parallel rays converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex)
- **Focal length (f):** Distance between P and F. **f = R/2**

![Ray diagram showing image formation by a concave mirror when the object is placed between C and F: two rays from the object reflect and converge beyond C to form a real, inverted, enlarged image](concave-mirror-ray-diagram-beyond-c.png)

### Image Formation by Concave Mirror

| Object Position | Image Position | Image Nature | Image Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At F | Real, inverted | Highly diminished (point) |
| Beyond C | Between F and C | Real, inverted | Diminished |
| At C | At C | Real, inverted | Same size |
| Between C and F | Beyond C | Real, inverted | Enlarged |
| At F | At infinity | Real, inverted | Highly enlarged |
| Between F and P | Behind mirror | Virtual, erect | Enlarged |

### Image Formation by Convex Mirror

A convex mirror always forms **virtual, erect, and diminished** images, regardless of object position. The image is formed behind the mirror between P and F.

### Uses
- **Concave mirrors:** Shaving/makeup mirrors, dentist mirrors, headlights, solar furnaces, reflectors in torches
- **Convex mirrors:** Rear-view/side mirrors of vehicles (give wider field of view and erect image)

> **Key idea:** Concave mirrors converge light and can form real or virtual images. Convex mirrors always form virtual, erect, diminished images with a wider field of view.

## Mirror Formula and Magnification

### Mirror Formula
**1/v + 1/u = 1/f**

Where: v = image distance, u = object distance, f = focal length

### Sign Convention (New Cartesian)
- All distances are measured from the pole of the mirror
- Distances in the direction of incident light are positive; opposite direction negative
- Heights above the principal axis are positive; below are negative

### Magnification
**m = h'/h = -v/u**

Where h' = image height, h = object height. Positive m means erect image; negative m means inverted image.

> **Key idea:** The mirror formula (1/v + 1/u = 1/f) relates object distance, image distance, and focal length. Magnification gives the ratio of image height to object height.

## Refraction of Light

**Refraction** is the bending of light as it passes from one transparent medium to another. This bending occurs because light travels at different speeds in different media.

### Laws of Refraction
1. The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal all lie in the **same plane**
2. **Snell's Law:** The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is constant for a given pair of media. This constant is the **refractive index** (n).

**n = sin i / sin r = speed of light in medium 1 / speed of light in medium 2**

![Refraction of light: ray bending towards the normal when passing from air (rarer) to glass (denser), and bending away from the normal when passing from glass to air](refraction-rarer-to-denser-medium.png)

- When light travels from a rarer to a denser medium (e.g., air to glass), it bends **towards** the normal
- When light travels from a denser to a rarer medium (e.g., glass to air), it bends **away from** the normal

The **refractive index** of a medium indicates how much the speed of light is reduced in that medium compared to vacuum. A higher refractive index means a denser medium (optically).

> **Key idea:** Refraction occurs because light changes speed when entering a different medium. Light bends towards the normal when entering a denser medium and away when entering a rarer medium.

## Spherical Lenses

### Types
- **Convex lens (Converging lens):** Thicker in the middle; converges parallel rays to a focus
- **Concave lens (Diverging lens):** Thinner in the middle; diverges parallel rays so they appear to come from a focus

### Key Terms
- **Optical centre (O):** Centre of the lens; light passes through without deviation
- **Principal focus (F):** Point where parallel rays converge (convex) or appear to diverge from (concave)
- **Focal length (f):** Distance from optical centre to principal focus

![Ray diagram showing image formation by a convex lens when object is placed beyond 2F1: two rays refract through the lens and converge between F2 and 2F2 to form a real, inverted, diminished image](convex-lens-ray-diagram.png)

### Image Formation by Convex Lens

| Object Position | Image Position | Image Nature | Image Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At F₂ | Real, inverted | Highly diminished (point) |
| Beyond 2F₁ | Between F₂ and 2F₂ | Real, inverted | Diminished |
| At 2F₁ | At 2F₂ | Real, inverted | Same size |
| Between F₁ and 2F₁ | Beyond 2F₂ | Real, inverted | Enlarged |
| At F₁ | At infinity | Real, inverted | Highly enlarged |
| Between F₁ and O | Same side as object | Virtual, erect | Enlarged |

### Image Formation by Concave Lens

A concave lens always forms **virtual, erect, and diminished** images on the same side as the object.

---

## Lens Formula and Power

### Lens Formula
**1/v - 1/u = 1/f**

### Magnification
**m = h'/h = v/u**

### Power of a Lens
**P = 1/f** (where f is in metres, P is in dioptres, D)

- Convex lens: positive power (positive focal length)
- Concave lens: negative power (negative focal length)

> **Key idea:** The lens formula (1/v - 1/u = 1/f) relates image distance, object distance, and focal length. Power of a lens (in dioptres) is the reciprocal of focal length (in metres).
