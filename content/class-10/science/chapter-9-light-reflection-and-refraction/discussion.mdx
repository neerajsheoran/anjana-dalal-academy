## Reflection and Spherical Mirrors

**Kiran:** Papa, we use mirrors every day, but the physics behind them is quite involved. Concave mirrors converge light and convex mirrors diverge it — why is that?

**Ravi:** It is about the geometry of the reflecting surface. A concave mirror curves inward, so parallel rays hitting different parts of the surface all reflect towards a common point — the focus. A convex mirror curves outward, so parallel rays diverge after reflection, appearing to come from a virtual focus behind the mirror.

**Kiran:** The image formation table for concave mirrors has six cases depending on where the object is placed. I need to memorise all six?

**Ravi:** Yes, and understand the pattern. As the object moves from infinity towards the mirror, the image starts at the focus, moves outward, and eventually becomes virtual and enlarged when the object is between the focus and the pole. For convex mirrors, the image is always virtual, erect, and diminished — regardless of object position. This makes convex mirrors ideal for rear-view mirrors in vehicles.

**Kiran:** The mirror formula — 1 over v plus 1 over u equals 1 over f — and the sign convention. Those are confusing.

**Ravi:** The New Cartesian Sign Convention takes the pole as the origin, the principal axis as the x-axis, and measures all distances along the direction of incident light as positive. So for a concave mirror, u is negative, f is negative, and v can be positive or negative depending on the image position. Always apply the sign convention before substituting into the formula. Practice at least 10 numerical problems to build confidence.

## Refraction and Snell's Law

**Kiran:** Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another. Why does light bend?

**Ravi:** Because light travels at different speeds in different media. When light enters a denser medium like glass from air, it slows down and bends towards the normal. When it goes from glass to air, it speeds up and bends away from the normal. The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is constant for a given pair of media — this is Snell's Law.

**Kiran:** The refractive index — n equals speed of light in vacuum divided by speed of light in the medium?

**Ravi:** Yes. For glass, n is about 1.5; for water, about 1.33; for diamond, about 2.42. The higher the refractive index, the more the light slows down and bends. Diamond's high refractive index, combined with its dispersion properties, is what gives it that spectacular sparkle.

**Kiran:** Real-life examples of refraction — the swimming pool appearing shallower, a pencil appearing bent in water?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The apparent depth is always less than the actual depth when viewed from above. This is important in physics and also in everyday safety — a pool that looks 1.5 metres deep might actually be 2 metres deep. Architects and civil engineers account for refractive effects in designing underwater structures.

## Lenses

**Kiran:** Convex lenses converge light and concave lenses diverge light. The image formation rules are similar to mirrors but with different sign conventions?

**Ravi:** Correct. For a convex lens, the sign convention is that distances measured in the direction of incident light are positive. The focal length of a convex lens is positive, and for a concave lens it is negative. The lens formula is the same structure as the mirror formula: 1 over v minus 1 over u equals 1 over f.

**Kiran:** Magnification for lenses — m equals v over u, and for mirrors — m equals negative v over u. Why the difference?

**Ravi:** The sign conventions differ between mirrors and lenses, which introduces the negative sign for mirrors. For lenses, a positive magnification means an erect image and a negative magnification means an inverted image. Always check the sign of your answer to verify the nature of the image.

**Kiran:** Power of a lens — P equals 1 over f in metres, measured in dioptres. An ophthalmologist prescribes lenses in dioptres?

**Ravi:** Yes. If your eye doctor prescribes a lens of power minus 2 dioptres, that is a concave lens with focal length minus 50 cm — used for correcting myopia. A positive dioptre lens is convex, used for hypermetropia. The dioptre system was developed for practical convenience in eye care.

## Ray Diagrams

**Kiran:** Drawing ray diagrams is important for the exam. What rules should I follow?

![Three standard ray diagram rules for a concave mirror: ray parallel to axis reflects through F, ray through F reflects parallel to axis, and ray through C reflects back on itself](three-ray-rules-concave-mirror.png)

**Ravi:** For mirrors: a ray parallel to the principal axis reflects through the focus; a ray through the focus reflects parallel to the axis; a ray through the centre of curvature reflects back on itself. For lenses: a ray parallel to the axis refracts through the focus; a ray through the optical centre passes undeviated; a ray through the focus refracts parallel to the axis. Use at least two of these three rules to locate the image.

**Kiran:** Should I use a ruler and protractor?

**Ravi:** A ruler for straight lines, yes. Protractors are not needed for ray diagrams. Label the focus, centre of curvature, object, and image clearly. CBSE awards marks for neat, correctly labelled ray diagrams even if the final numerical answer has a small error.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** This is a high-marks chapter. What should I focus on?

**Ravi:** Expect 5-8 marks. Numerical problems using the mirror and lens formulae are almost guaranteed — practice with the sign convention. Ray diagram questions are common — both for mirrors and lenses. Conceptual questions on real versus virtual images, and applications of mirrors and lenses in daily life, also appear. C.V. Raman's Nobel Prize-winning work on light scattering reminds us of India's deep contributions to optics.

**Kiran:** Biggest mistakes?

**Ravi:** Wrong sign convention is the number one error. Always draw a quick diagram, mark the sign convention directions, and assign signs before substituting. Second, confusing the mirror and lens formulae — the mirror formula has a plus sign between 1 over v and 1 over u, while the lens formula has a minus sign. Third, not stating whether the image is real or virtual, erect or inverted, magnified or diminished. Complete characterisation of the image earns full marks.

**Kiran:** Does this chapter connect to modern technology?

**Ravi:** Enormously. Cameras in smartphones use miniature convex lenses. Telescopes at Indian astronomical observatories like the one in Hanle, Ladakh, use combinations of concave mirrors and lenses. Fibre optic cables — which carry India's internet traffic — work on the principle of total internal reflection. Laser technology, used in eye surgery and industrial cutting, is also rooted in optics. The principles of reflection and refraction you study here are the foundation of all optical technology.
