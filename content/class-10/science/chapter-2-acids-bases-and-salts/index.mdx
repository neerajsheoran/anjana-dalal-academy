## Introduction

The sour taste of food is due to **acids** and the bitter taste is due to **bases** present in them. Acids turn blue litmus red, while bases turn red litmus blue. **Litmus** is a natural indicator ‚ÄĒ a purple dye extracted from lichen (a plant of division Thallophyta). Other natural indicators include turmeric, red cabbage leaves, and coloured petals of flowers like Hydrangea, Petunia, and Geranium.

**Synthetic indicators** such as methyl orange and phenolphthalein are also used to test for acids and bases. Additionally, some substances whose odour changes in acidic or basic media are called **olfactory indicators** (e.g., onion, vanilla essence, clove oil).

> **Key idea:** Indicators are substances that show different colours (or odours) in acidic and basic solutions, helping us identify the nature of a substance without tasting it.

## Chemical Properties of Acids and Bases

### Reaction of Acids with Metals

When an acid reacts with a metal, **hydrogen gas** is evolved and a **salt** is formed.

**Acid + Metal ‚Üí Salt + Hydrogen gas**

For example, when dilute sulphuric acid reacts with zinc granules, zinc sulphate and hydrogen gas are produced. The hydrogen gas burns with a pop sound when a burning candle is brought near it.

### Reaction of Bases with Metals

Some metals also react with bases to produce hydrogen gas and a salt. For example:

**2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) ‚Üí Na‚āāZnO‚āā(s) + H‚āā(g)**
(Sodium zincate)

However, such reactions are not possible with all metals.

> **Key idea:** Both acids and bases can react with certain metals to produce hydrogen gas, though the salts formed differ.

---

### Reaction of Acids with Metal Carbonates and Hydrogencarbonates

All metal **carbonates** and **hydrogencarbonates** react with acids to give the corresponding salt, carbon dioxide, and water.

**Metal carbonate/Metal hydrogencarbonate + Acid ‚Üí Salt + CO‚āā + Water**

Examples:
- Na‚āāCO‚āÉ(s) + 2HCl(aq) ‚Üí 2NaCl(aq) + H‚āāO(l) + CO‚āā(g)
- NaHCO‚āÉ(s) + HCl(aq) ‚Üí NaCl(aq) + H‚āāO(l) + CO‚āā(g)

The CO‚āā gas produced turns **lime water milky** due to the formation of calcium carbonate:

Ca(OH)‚āā(aq) + CO‚āā(g) ‚Üí CaCO‚āÉ(s) + H‚āāO(l)

On passing excess CO‚āā, the calcium carbonate dissolves to form soluble calcium hydrogencarbonate:

CaCO‚āÉ(s) + H‚āāO(l) + CO‚āā(g) ‚Üí Ca(HCO‚āÉ)‚āā(aq)

> **Key idea:** The reaction of acids with carbonates and hydrogencarbonates always produces carbon dioxide gas, which can be tested by passing it through lime water (turns milky).

---

### Neutralisation Reaction

When an **acid** reacts with a **base**, a **salt** and **water** are formed. This is called a **neutralisation reaction**.

**Base + Acid ‚Üí Salt + Water**

Example: NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) ‚Üí NaCl(aq) + H‚āāO(l)

The effect of a base is nullified by an acid and vice-versa.

### Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids

**Metallic oxides** react with acids to give salts and water, similar to the reaction of a base with an acid. Therefore, metallic oxides are called **basic oxides**.

**Metal oxide + Acid ‚Üí Salt + Water**

Example: CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) ‚Üí CuCl‚āā(aq) + H‚āāO(l)

### Reaction of Non-metallic Oxides with Bases

**Non-metallic oxides** react with bases to produce salts and water, similar to how acids react with bases. Therefore, non-metallic oxides are **acidic in nature**.

Example: Ca(OH)‚āā(aq) + CO‚āā(g) ‚Üí CaCO‚āÉ(s) + H‚āāO(l)

> **Key idea:** Metallic oxides are basic in nature, while non-metallic oxides are acidic in nature.

## What Do All Acids and All Bases Have in Common?

### Acids in Aqueous Solution

All acids produce **hydrogen ions H‚Āļ(aq)** (or hydronium ions H‚āÉO‚Āļ) when dissolved in water. These H‚Āļ ions are responsible for the acidic properties of all acids. Compounds like glucose and alcohol contain hydrogen but do not show acidic behaviour because they do not produce H‚Āļ ions in solution.

HCl + H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚āÉO‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ

Hydrogen ions cannot exist alone and always combine with water molecules to form **hydronium ions** (H‚āÉO‚Āļ).

Acids in aqueous solution **conduct electricity** because of the presence of ions that carry the electric current.

### Bases in Aqueous Solution

All bases produce **hydroxide ions OH‚ĀĽ(aq)** when dissolved in water. Bases that are soluble in water are called **alkalis**.

NaOH(s) ‚Üí Na‚Āļ(aq) + OH‚ĀĽ(aq)

### Dry HCl vs. HCl Solution

Dry HCl gas does not change the colour of dry litmus paper because HCl can produce H‚Āļ ions only in the presence of water. Without water, no ions are formed and hence no acidic behaviour is observed.

> **Key idea:** Acids produce H‚Āļ(aq) ions and bases produce OH‚ĀĽ(aq) ions in aqueous solutions. The acidic or basic character of a substance is due to these ions.

## Dilution of Acids and Bases

The process of mixing an acid or a base with water is highly **exothermic**. The heat generated can cause the mixture to splash if not done carefully.

**Important rule:** Always add **acid to water** slowly with constant stirring, never add water to a concentrated acid. Adding water to acid can cause the mixture to splash due to the large amount of heat released.

**Dilution** decreases the concentration of ions (H‚āÉO‚Āļ or OH‚ĀĽ) per unit volume.

> **Key idea:** When diluting an acid, always add acid to water, not water to acid. This prevents dangerous splashing caused by the exothermic nature of the process.

## pH Scale ‚ÄĒ Strength of Acids and Bases

The **pH scale** measures the hydrogen ion concentration in a solution. The term pH stands for "potenz" (German for power). The scale ranges from **0 to 14**:

- **pH = 7**: Neutral solution
- **pH < 7**: Acidic solution (lower pH = stronger acid)
- **pH > 7**: Basic/alkaline solution (higher pH = stronger base)

A **universal indicator** is a mixture of several indicators that shows different colours at different pH values.

**Strong acids** (like HCl, H‚āāSO‚āĄ) produce more H‚Āļ ions and have very low pH values. **Weak acids** (like acetic acid, citric acid) produce fewer H‚Āļ ions and have pH closer to 7.

### Importance of pH in Everyday Life

**Our body and pH:** The human body works within the pH range of 7.0 to 7.8. Living organisms can survive only within a narrow range of pH.

**Acid rain:** When the pH of rain water drops below 5.6, it is called acid rain. It damages aquatic life and buildings.

**Soil pH:** Plants require a specific pH range for healthy growth. Farmers treat acidic soil with quick lime, slaked lime, or chalk to neutralise excess acid.

**Digestion:** The stomach produces hydrochloric acid for digestion. During indigestion, excess acid causes pain, which is relieved by **antacids** (bases like magnesium hydroxide ‚ÄĒ Milk of Magnesia).

**Tooth decay:** Tooth enamel (calcium hydroxyapatite) corrodes when pH in the mouth falls below 5.5. Bacteria produce acids from food particles. Using basic toothpaste neutralises the excess acid.

**Self-defence in nature:** Bee stings inject acid (causing pain), which can be relieved by applying baking soda (a mild base). Nettle leaves inject methanoic acid through stinging hair.

> **Key idea:** The pH scale (0-14) quantitatively measures the acidity or basicity of a solution. pH plays a critical role in biological processes, agriculture, digestion, and dental health.

## Salts

### Family of Salts

Salts are ionic compounds formed by the reaction of acids with bases. Salts having the same positive radical (cation) or the same negative radical (anion) belong to the same **family of salts**.

For example: NaCl and Na‚āāSO‚āĄ belong to the sodium salt family. NaCl and KCl belong to the chloride salt family.

### pH of Salts

- Salts of **strong acid + strong base** are **neutral** (pH = 7). Example: NaCl
- Salts of **strong acid + weak base** are **acidic** (pH < 7). Example: NH‚āĄCl
- Salts of **weak acid + strong base** are **basic** (pH > 7). Example: Na‚āāCO‚āÉ

> **Key idea:** The pH of a salt depends on the strength of the acid and base from which it is formed.

## Chemicals from Common Salt

**Sodium chloride (NaCl)** ‚ÄĒ common salt ‚ÄĒ is an important raw material for making many useful chemicals.

### Sodium Hydroxide (Chlor-Alkali Process)

When electricity is passed through brine (aqueous NaCl), it decomposes:

**2NaCl(aq) + 2H‚āāO(l) ‚Üí 2NaOH(aq) + Cl‚āā(g) + H‚āā(g)**

This is called the **chlor-alkali process** (chlor for chlorine, alkali for sodium hydroxide). Chlorine is released at the anode, hydrogen at the cathode, and NaOH forms near the cathode.

### Bleaching Powder

**Bleaching powder** ‚ÄĒ Ca(ClO)‚āā ‚ÄĒ is produced by the action of chlorine on dry slaked lime:

2Ca(OH)‚āā + 2Cl‚āā ‚Üí Ca(ClO)‚āā + CaCl‚āā + 2H‚āāO

Uses: bleaching cotton and linen in textile industry, bleaching wood pulp in paper factories, as an oxidising agent, and for disinfecting drinking water.

### Baking Soda (Sodium Hydrogencarbonate)

**Baking soda (NaHCO‚āÉ)** is a mild, non-corrosive basic salt.

On heating: 2NaHCO‚āÉ ‚Üí Na‚āāCO‚āÉ + H‚āāO + CO‚āā

Uses:
- Making **baking powder** (baking soda + tartaric acid) ‚ÄĒ the CO‚āā released makes bread/cake soft and spongy
- As an ingredient in **antacids** to neutralise excess stomach acid
- In **soda-acid fire extinguishers**

### Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate Decahydrate)

**Washing soda (Na‚āāCO‚āÉ.10H‚āāO)** is obtained by recrystallisation of sodium carbonate:

Na‚āāCO‚āÉ + 10H‚āāO ‚Üí Na‚āāCO‚āÉ.10H‚āāO

Uses: in glass, soap, and paper industries; manufacture of borax; as a cleaning agent; for removing permanent hardness of water.

> **Key idea:** Common salt is the starting material for making sodium hydroxide (chlor-alkali process), bleaching powder, baking soda, and washing soda ‚ÄĒ all industrially important chemicals.

## Water of Crystallisation

**Water of crystallisation** is the fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt.

- **Copper sulphate**: CuSO‚āĄ.5H‚āāO (blue crystals; turns white on heating as water is lost; blue colour returns when water is added back)
- **Gypsum**: CaSO‚āĄ.2H‚āāO

### Plaster of Paris

When **gypsum** is heated at 373 K, it loses water molecules and becomes **calcium sulphate hemihydrate** ‚ÄĒ known as **Plaster of Paris**:

CaSO‚āĄ.2H‚āāO ‚Üí CaSO‚āĄ.¬ĹH‚āāO + 1¬ĹH‚āāO

Plaster of Paris is a white powder that, when mixed with water, sets into a hard solid mass (gypsum again):

CaSO‚āĄ.¬ĹH‚āāO + 1¬ĹH‚āāO ‚Üí CaSO‚āĄ.2H‚āāO

Uses: making casts for fractured bones, making toys, decorative materials, and smoothing surfaces.

Plaster of Paris must be stored in **moisture-proof containers** because it can absorb moisture from the atmosphere and set into hard gypsum, becoming useless.

> **Key idea:** Water of crystallisation is chemically bonded water in crystal structure. Plaster of Paris (CaSO‚āĄ.¬ĹH‚āāO) hardens by absorbing water to re-form gypsum, which is why it must be kept dry.
