## Understanding Acids and Bases

**Kiran:** Papa, our teacher said we should never taste chemicals to test if they are acidic or basic. So how were acids and bases first identified?

**Ravi:** Historically, the sour taste of lemon and vinegar identified acids, and the bitter, soapy feel of bases was noticed with substances like baking soda and soap. But in the laboratory, we use indicators. Litmus, a natural indicator from lichen, turns red in acid and blue in base. Turmeric turns reddish-brown in a base — that is why a turmeric stain on a white shirt turns red when washed with soap.

**Kiran:** Phenolphthalein and methyl orange are synthetic indicators. When would I use each?

**Ravi:** Phenolphthalein is colourless in acid and turns pink in base. Methyl orange is red in acid and yellow in base. In titrations for competitive exams, the choice of indicator depends on the strength of the acid and base involved. For CBSE, knowing the colour changes is sufficient.

## Chemical Properties

**Kiran:** Acids react with metals to give hydrogen gas. Is that why zinc granules fizz in dilute sulphuric acid?

**Ravi:** Yes. The reaction is Zn plus H2SO4 gives ZnSO4 plus H2. The hydrogen gas burns with a pop sound when a burning splint is brought near it — that is the standard test for hydrogen. Importantly, not all metals react with acids. Gold and platinum are unreactive — which is why gold ornaments do not corrode.

**Kiran:** And the reaction of acids with metal carbonates gives CO2, which turns lime water milky?

**Ravi:** Correct. This is the test for CO2. When you add dilute HCl to baking soda, you get NaCl, water, and CO2. Pass the gas through lime water — it turns milky due to formation of CaCO3. If you pass excess CO2, the milkiness disappears because soluble Ca(HCO3)2 forms. This reversal is a favourite CBSE question.

**Kiran:** Neutralisation — acid plus base gives salt plus water. Is this always exothermic?

**Ravi:** Yes, neutralisation is always exothermic. The pH moves towards 7. In our stomachs, excess acid causes acidity, and we take antacids like milk of magnesia, which is Mg(OH)2, to neutralise it. Farmers add quick lime to acidic soil to neutralise it before planting.

## The pH Scale

**Kiran:** pH ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral. Why is this scale so important?

**Ravi:** Because pH affects virtually every chemical and biological process. Our blood maintains a pH between 7.35 and 7.45 — even a small deviation can be fatal. Our stomach operates at pH around 1.2 — extremely acidic, necessary for digestion. Acid rain has a pH below 5.6 and damages the Taj Mahal's marble — the CaCO3 reacts with sulphuric acid in the rain.

**Kiran:** So the yellowing of the Taj Mahal is a chemistry problem?

**Ravi:** Indeed. The Supreme Court of India ordered industries near Agra to switch to cleaner fuels to reduce SO2 emissions, which cause acid rain. This is where chemistry meets environmental policy. For NEET preparation, understanding pH in biological systems is fundamental.

**Kiran:** The pH of common substances — I need to know these for the exam?

**Ravi:** Know the key ones: stomach acid is about 1, lemon juice about 2, vinegar about 3, pure water is 7, baking soda about 8.5, milk of magnesia about 10, and NaOH solution about 14. The more you know, the better you can handle application-based questions.

## Important Salts

**Kiran:** Common salt, baking soda, washing soda, Plaster of Paris, bleaching powder — these are all salts derived from NaCl?

**Ravi:** Most of them trace back to NaCl or related compounds. Baking soda is NaHCO3, used in fire extinguishers and cooking. Washing soda is Na2CO3 dot 10H2O, used in glass manufacturing and water softening. Bleaching powder is CaOCl2, made from chlorine and slaked lime, used in water purification. Plaster of Paris is CaSO4 dot half H2O, used for setting broken bones and making moulds.

**Kiran:** The water of crystallisation concept — these salts hold water molecules in their crystal structure?

**Ravi:** Yes. CuSO4 dot 5H2O is blue, but when heated, it loses water and turns white. Adding water back restores the blue colour. This reversible colour change is a classic board exam demonstration. The specific number of water molecules — 5 for copper sulphate, 10 for washing soda — must be memorised.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** How much does this chapter weigh?

**Ravi:** Typically 5-6 marks across the chemistry section. Expect questions on reactions of acids and bases, the pH scale, and important salts with their uses. Equations with state symbols are essential.

**Kiran:** What should I focus on most?

**Ravi:** Three things. First, write balanced equations for every reaction discussed — acid plus metal, acid plus carbonate, neutralisation, preparation of salts. Second, know the pH scale thoroughly with examples. Third, memorise the chemical formulae, preparation methods, and uses of the five important salts. CBSE frequently asks "write one use each" type questions.

## Everyday Chemistry

**Kiran:** Baking soda in cooking, antacids for acidity, bleaching powder for water purification — chemistry is everywhere in the kitchen and medicine cabinet.

**Ravi:** Indeed. When your mother adds baking soda to dosa batter, the NaHCO3 reacts with the mild acids in the fermented batter, releasing CO2 gas that makes the dosa fluffy. When you brush your teeth, the toothpaste is mildly basic to neutralise the acids produced by bacteria. Even the chlorination of water in your municipal supply uses bleaching powder chemistry.

**Kiran:** For NEET, does this chapter connect to biochemistry?

**Ravi:** Directly. The pH of blood, stomach, and intestinal fluids determines enzyme activity. Enzymes work optimally at specific pH values — pepsin at pH 2, trypsin at pH 8. Buffer solutions that maintain pH stability are essential in biological systems. Understanding pH now builds the foundation for biochemistry in higher classes. India's pharmaceutical industry — the third largest by volume in the world — relies on acid-base chemistry in drug formulation and quality control.
