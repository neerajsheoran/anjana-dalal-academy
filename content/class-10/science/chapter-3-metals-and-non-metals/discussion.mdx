## Physical Properties

**Kiran:** Papa, metals are lustrous, malleable, ductile, and good conductors. But there are so many exceptions — mercury is liquid, sodium is soft, lead is a poor conductor. How do I handle these in the exam?

**Ravi:** The trick is to learn the general properties first and then memorise the exceptions as a separate list. CBSE loves to test exceptions — "name a metal that is liquid at room temperature" (mercury), "name a non-metal that conducts electricity" (graphite), "name a non-metal that is lustrous" (iodine). These are 1-mark questions that appear every year.

**Kiran:** Gold can be beaten into sheets so thin that light passes through them. That is malleability?

**Ravi:** Yes. Gold is the most malleable metal — one gram can be hammered into a sheet covering about one square metre. Silver is the best conductor of heat and electricity, but we use copper and aluminium in wiring because they are much cheaper. India is one of the largest consumers of copper wire for its expanding electrical grid.

**Kiran:** Allotropes of carbon — diamond and graphite have the same atoms but such different properties.

**Ravi:** Because the arrangement of atoms differs. In diamond, each carbon bonds to four others in a 3D network — extremely rigid and hard. In graphite, each carbon bonds to three others in flat layers that slide over each other — hence graphite is soft and slippery. This demonstrates that properties depend not just on composition but on structure.

## Chemical Properties and Reactivity Series

**Kiran:** The reactivity series — K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Ni, Sn, Pb, H, Cu, Hg, Ag, Au, Pt — I need to memorise this order?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Use the mnemonic "King Navigates Calmly Making All Zones Friendly, Nicely Snapping Pairs, Having Conquered Haughty Aggressive Gold Parties." The reactivity series determines which displacement reactions occur, how metals are extracted, and how they react with water and acids.

**Kiran:** Metals above hydrogen in the series react with dilute acids to give hydrogen gas, and metals below hydrogen do not?

**Ravi:** Correct. Copper, silver, and gold do not react with dilute HCl or H2SO4 because they are below hydrogen. This is why copper vessels can hold acidic foods like tamarind chutney, but iron kadhai cannot.

**Kiran:** What about reaction with water?

**Ravi:** Potassium and sodium react vigorously — even explosively — with cold water. Calcium reacts with warm water. Magnesium reacts with hot water. Aluminium and iron react only with steam. Copper, silver, and gold do not react with water at all. This gradient of reactivity is directly tested in boards.

## Extraction of Metals

**Kiran:** The method of extraction depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series?

**Ravi:** Yes. Highly reactive metals like sodium and aluminium are extracted by electrolytic reduction — they cannot be reduced by carbon. Medium-reactive metals like iron and zinc are extracted by reducing their oxides with carbon or coke. This is the basis of the iron blast furnace. Less reactive metals like copper and mercury can be obtained by simply heating their ores.

**Kiran:** India's aluminium industry at NALCO in Odisha uses electrolysis?

**Ravi:** Yes, NALCO is one of Asia's largest integrated aluminium complexes. The Hall-Heroult process electrolyses alumina dissolved in cryolite. This requires enormous electricity, which is why aluminium plants are located near power stations. Understanding extraction methods connects chemistry to India's industrial geography.

**Kiran:** Corrosion prevention — galvanisation, painting, oiling, alloying — which is most important for boards?

**Ravi:** All are important, but galvanisation and alloying appear most often. Galvanisation is coating iron with zinc — zinc corrodes preferentially, protecting the iron. Alloying changes the properties of metals — stainless steel contains iron, chromium, and nickel, making it corrosion-resistant. Bronze, brass, and solder are other important alloys to know.

## Ionic and Covalent Bonding

**Kiran:** Metals and non-metals form ionic bonds by transferring electrons. How is this different from covalent bonding?

**Ravi:** In ionic bonding, a metal loses electrons and a non-metal gains them, forming positive and negative ions that attract each other. NaCl is the classic example — sodium loses one electron, chlorine gains it. In covalent bonding, atoms share electrons — this happens between non-metals. Ionic compounds have high melting points and conduct electricity when dissolved or molten. Covalent compounds generally do not.

**Kiran:** The formation of NaCl from sodium and chlorine — writing the electron-dot structure is a board question?

**Ravi:** Yes, frequently. Draw the outer shell electrons for both atoms, show the transfer with an arrow, and indicate the charges on the resulting ions. Then show the electrostatic attraction. This diagram is worth 2-3 marks.

## Board Exam Strategy

**Kiran:** What carries the most marks here?

**Ravi:** This chapter is heavily weighted — often 5-8 marks. Key topics are the reactivity series, extraction methods, ionic bond formation, and properties of ionic compounds. Questions on corrosion prevention and alloys are also common.

**Kiran:** What should I prioritise?

**Ravi:** Memorise the reactivity series thoroughly, learn the extraction methods for each category, and practice drawing electron-dot structures for NaCl and MgO. Know at least five differences between ionic and covalent compounds. And always relate extraction methods to the reactivity series — this logical connection impresses examiners.

**Kiran:** For NEET, which parts of this chapter are most important?

**Ravi:** The bonding concepts — ionic versus covalent — are fundamental for chemistry in NEET. Understanding how electron transfer creates ions, and how ionic compounds behave differently from covalent compounds, is essential for physical chemistry and organic chemistry in Classes 11 and 12. India's steel, aluminium, and copper industries all employ chemists who apply these principles daily in refining and quality control. The science you study now is directly applicable to India's industrial growth.
