## Physical Properties of Metals

Elements can be classified as **metals** or **non-metals** based on their physical and chemical properties. Metals exhibit several characteristic physical properties:

- **Metallic lustre:** Metals have a shining surface when freshly cleaned. This property is called metallic lustre.
- **Hardness:** Most metals are hard, though hardness varies. Alkali metals (lithium, sodium, potassium) are so soft they can be cut with a knife.
- **Malleability:** Metals can be beaten into thin sheets. Gold and silver are the most malleable metals.
- **Ductility:** Metals can be drawn into thin wires. Gold is the most ductile metal — about 2 km of wire can be drawn from one gram of gold.
- **Conductivity (Heat):** Metals are good conductors of heat. Silver and copper are the best conductors of heat. Lead and mercury are poor conductors.
- **Conductivity (Electricity):** Metals are good conductors of electricity, which is why copper and aluminium wires are used in electrical circuits.
- **Sonority:** Metals produce a ringing sound when struck against a hard surface. This is why school bells are made of metals.
- **State:** All metals are solids at room temperature except **mercury**, which is a liquid.

> **Key idea:** Metals are generally hard, lustrous, malleable, ductile, sonorous, and good conductors of heat and electricity. Exceptions exist — mercury is liquid, sodium is soft, and lead is a poor conductor of heat.

## Physical Properties of Non-metals

**Non-metals** have properties opposite to metals:

- Non-metals are generally **not lustrous** (exception: iodine is lustrous).
- Non-metals are **not malleable or ductile** — they are brittle.
- Non-metals are **poor conductors** of heat and electricity (exception: graphite conducts electricity).
- Non-metals are either **solids or gases** at room temperature (exception: bromine is a liquid).
- Non-metals do not produce a ringing sound — they are **not sonorous**.

**Carbon** exists in different forms called **allotropes**:
- **Diamond** — the hardest natural substance with very high melting and boiling points.
- **Graphite** — a conductor of electricity, used in pencil leads.

> **Key idea:** Non-metals are generally brittle, dull, and poor conductors. Exceptions include iodine (lustrous), graphite (conducts electricity), and diamond (extremely hard).

## Chemical Properties of Metals

### Reaction of Metals with Oxygen

Almost all metals combine with oxygen to form **metal oxides**, which are generally **basic** in nature.

**Metal + Oxygen → Metal oxide**

Examples:
- 2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO (black copper oxide)
- 4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃

Some metal oxides like **aluminium oxide** and **zinc oxide** are **amphoteric** — they react with both acids and bases:
- Al₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂O
- Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO₂ + H₂O

Different metals show different reactivities towards oxygen:
- **Potassium and sodium** react so vigorously that they catch fire in open air (stored in kerosene oil).
- **Magnesium, aluminium, zinc, lead** develop a protective oxide layer.
- **Iron filings** burn vigorously in flame, but iron does not burn on heating.
- **Copper** gets coated with black CuO but does not burn.
- **Silver and gold** do not react with oxygen even at high temperatures.

> **Key idea:** Metal oxides are basic in nature. Amphoteric oxides (like Al₂O₃ and ZnO) show both acidic and basic behaviour. Metals vary greatly in their reactivity with oxygen.

### Reaction of Metals with Water

Metals react with water to produce a **metal oxide** (or metal hydroxide) and **hydrogen gas**.

**Metal + Water → Metal oxide + Hydrogen**

The reactivity varies significantly:
- **Potassium and sodium** react violently with cold water, producing so much heat that the hydrogen catches fire.
  - 2K(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2KOH(aq) + H₂(g) + heat
  - 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g) + heat
- **Calcium** reacts with cold water but less violently. It floats because H₂ bubbles stick to its surface.
  - Ca(s) + 2H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) + H₂(g)
- **Magnesium** reacts with hot water but not cold water.
- **Aluminium, iron, zinc** react with steam but not cold or hot water.
  - 3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) → Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g)
- **Lead, copper, silver, gold** do not react with water at all.

### Reaction of Metals with Dilute Acids

**Metal + Dilute acid → Salt + Hydrogen gas**

Reactivity order: Mg > Al > Zn > Fe. Copper does not react with dilute HCl.

**Note:** Hydrogen gas is not evolved when a metal reacts with **nitric acid** because HNO₃ is a strong oxidising agent that oxidises H₂ to water.

**Aqua regia** (3 parts concentrated HCl + 1 part concentrated HNO₃) can dissolve gold and platinum.

### Reaction with Salt Solutions (Displacement)

A more reactive metal can **displace** a less reactive metal from its salt solution:

**Metal A + Salt solution of B → Salt solution of A + Metal B**

Example: Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)

> **Key idea:** The reactivity of metals decreases in the order: K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au. This is called the reactivity series or activity series.

## The Reactivity Series

The **reactivity series** (or activity series) lists metals in decreasing order of reactivity:

![Reactivity series of metals arranged vertically from most reactive (potassium) at top to least reactive (gold) at bottom, with hydrogen marked as the dividing line](reactivity-series-metals.png)

**K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > [H] > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au**

- Metals above hydrogen can displace hydrogen from dilute acids.
- A more reactive metal can displace a less reactive metal from its salt solution.
- Metals at the top (K, Na, Ca) are extremely reactive and are never found free in nature.
- Metals at the bottom (Cu, Ag, Au) are least reactive and can be found in free state.

> **Key idea:** The reactivity series helps predict whether a displacement reaction will occur and guides the method used for extracting a metal from its ore.

## How Do Metals and Non-metals React? — Ionic Bonding

Metals and non-metals react by the **transfer of electrons**. Metals lose electrons to form **cations** (positive ions), while non-metals gain electrons to form **anions** (negative ions).

![Diagram showing electron transfer from sodium atom to chlorine atom: sodium loses one electron to become Na+ cation, chlorine gains one electron to become Cl- anion, forming ionic bond in NaCl](ionic-bond-formation-nacl.png)

**Example — Formation of NaCl:**
- Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (sodium loses 1 electron)
- Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (chlorine gains 1 electron)
- Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are held together by strong electrostatic forces of attraction.

**Example — Formation of MgCl₂:**
- Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (magnesium loses 2 electrons)
- Each Cl atom gains 1 electron → 2Cl⁻

Compounds formed by transfer of electrons are called **ionic compounds** or **electrovalent compounds**.

### Properties of Ionic Compounds

1. **Physical nature:** Solid, hard, and brittle.
2. **High melting and boiling points:** Due to strong electrostatic forces between ions.
3. **Solubility:** Soluble in water but insoluble in organic solvents like kerosene and petrol.
4. **Electrical conductivity:** Conduct electricity in molten state and in aqueous solution (due to free-moving ions), but not in solid state (ions are fixed in rigid structure).

> **Key idea:** Ionic compounds are formed by electron transfer from metals to non-metals. They have high melting points, are soluble in water, and conduct electricity when molten or dissolved.

## Occurrence and Extraction of Metals

### Minerals and Ores

- **Minerals:** Elements or compounds that occur naturally in the earth's crust.
- **Ores:** Minerals from which metals can be profitably extracted.
- **Gangue:** Impurities (soil, sand, etc.) present in the ore.
- **Enrichment of ore:** The process of removing gangue from the ore before extraction.

### Extraction Based on Reactivity

**Metals of low reactivity** (Cu, Hg, Ag, Au):
- Found in free state or as sulphide/oxide ores.
- Extracted by heating (reduction by heat alone).
- Example: 2HgS(s) + 3O₂(g) → 2HgO(s) + 2SO₂(g), then 2HgO(s) → 2Hg(l) + O₂(g)

**Metals of medium reactivity** (Zn, Fe, Pb):
- Found as oxides, sulphides, or carbonates.
- **Roasting:** Heating sulphide ores strongly in excess air to convert to oxide.
  - 2ZnS(s) + 3O₂(g) → 2ZnO(s) + 2SO₂(g)
- **Calcination:** Heating carbonate ores in limited air to convert to oxide.
  - ZnCO₃(s) → ZnO(s) + CO₂(g)
- **Reduction with carbon:** ZnO(s) + C(s) → Zn(s) + CO(g)

**Thermit reaction:** Fe₂O₃(s) + 2Al(s) → 2Fe(l) + Al₂O₃(s) + Heat
This highly exothermic displacement reaction is used to join railway tracks.

**Metals of high reactivity** (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al):
- Cannot be reduced by carbon.
- Obtained by **electrolytic reduction** of their molten compounds.
- Example: Electrolysis of molten NaCl — Na deposited at cathode, Cl₂ at anode.

![Electrolytic refining setup showing impure metal anode on the left, pure metal cathode on the right, metal salt solution as electrolyte, and anode mud settling at the bottom](electrolytic-refining-setup.png)

### Refining of Metals — Electrolytic Refining

- **Anode:** Impure metal
- **Cathode:** Thin strip of pure metal
- **Electrolyte:** Solution of the metal salt
- Pure metal from anode dissolves and deposits on cathode.
- Insoluble impurities settle as **anode mud**.

> **Key idea:** The method of extraction depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series: heat alone for low-reactivity metals, carbon reduction for medium, and electrolysis for highly reactive metals.

## Corrosion and Its Prevention

**Corrosion** is the gradual destruction of metals by chemical reaction with their environment (moisture, acids, gases).

- **Iron:** Reddish-brown rust (hydrated iron oxide) — requires both air and water.
- **Silver:** Black coating (silver sulphide) from reaction with sulphur in air.
- **Copper:** Green coating (basic copper carbonate) from reaction with moist CO₂.

### Prevention Methods

- **Painting, oiling, greasing** — creates a barrier against air and moisture.
- **Galvanisation** — coating iron/steel with a thin layer of zinc. Protects even if the coating is broken.
- **Chrome plating and anodising** — protective metallic or oxide coatings.
- **Alloying** — mixing metals to improve properties.

### Alloys

An **alloy** is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal. Alloys have different properties from their constituent metals:

- **Iron + Carbon (0.05%)** = hard, strong steel
- **Iron + Nickel + Chromium** = stainless steel (hard, does not rust)
- **Copper + Zinc** = brass
- **Copper + Tin** = bronze
- **Lead + Tin** = solder (low melting point, used for welding)
- **Pure gold (24 carat)** is too soft for jewellery; 22 carat gold = 22 parts gold + 2 parts copper or silver.

If mercury is one of the metals, the alloy is called an **amalgam**.

Alloys generally have **lower electrical conductivity** and **lower melting points** than pure metals.

> **Key idea:** Corrosion can be prevented by painting, galvanising, or alloying. Alloys combine the desirable properties of different metals and are widely used in everyday life and industry.
