We use mathematical phrases every day without realising it -- "I need 3 packets of biscuits at Rs 25 each" is really the expression 3 x 25. In this chapter, we will learn how to read, write, and evaluate arithmetic expressions correctly, using brackets and the concept of terms.

## Simple Expressions

An **arithmetic expression** is a mathematical phrase that uses numbers and operations like +, -, x, and /. For example:

- 13 + 2 (read as "13 plus 2" or "the sum of 13 and 2")
- 20 - 4 (read as "20 minus 4" or "the difference of 20 and 4")
- 12 x 5 (read as "12 times 5" or "the product of 12 and 5")
- 18 / 3 (read as "18 divided by 3" or "the quotient of 18 and 3")

Every arithmetic expression has a **value** -- the number it evaluates to. For example, the value of 13 + 2 is **15**. We write this using the equality sign: **13 + 2 = 15**.

### Different Expressions, Same Value

Multiple expressions can have the same value. For example, all of these equal 12:

- 10 + 2
- 15 - 3
- 3 x 4
- 24 / 2

### Comparing Expressions

Just as we compare numbers using =, < and >, we can compare expressions based on their values:

- 10 + 2 **>** 7 + 1 (because 12 > 8)
- 13 - 2 **<** 4 x 3 (because 11 < 12)

> **Smart Comparison:** You do not always need to calculate exact values to compare. For example, consider 1023 + 125 vs 1022 + 128. The second number starts with 1 less (1022 vs 1023), but gains 3 more (128 vs 125). So the second expression is 2 more. No need to add large numbers!

## Reading and Evaluating Complex Expressions

When an expression has more than one operation, **the order in which we evaluate matters**. Consider this situation:

Mallesh brought 30 marbles. Arun brought 5 bags with 4 marbles in each bag. How many marbles did they bring in total?

Mallesh wrote: **30 + 5 x 4**

- If we add first: (30 + 5) x 4 = 35 x 4 = 140 (Wrong!)
- If we multiply first: 30 + (5 x 4) = 30 + 20 = 50 (Correct!)

Just as punctuation marks remove confusion in language, **brackets** and the concept of **terms** remove confusion in mathematics.

## Brackets in Expressions

**Brackets** tell us which part of an expression to evaluate first.

**Example:** Irfan bought biscuits for Rs 15 and toor dal for Rs 56. He paid Rs 100. What change does he get?

- Without brackets: 100 - 15 + 56 = 141 (Absurd -- more than he paid!)
- With brackets: 100 - (15 + 56) = 100 - 71 = **29** (Correct!)

> **Rule:** When evaluating an expression with brackets, always evaluate the expression **inside the brackets first**, then perform the remaining operations.

## Terms in Expressions

**Terms** are the parts of an expression separated by the **+** sign. To find the terms, we first convert all subtractions into additions of negative numbers.

For example:
- In **12 + 7**, the terms are **12** and **7**
- In **83 - 14**, we rewrite as 83 + (-14), so the terms are **83** and **-14**
- In **6 x 5 + 3**, the terms are **6 x 5** and **3**

Notice that 6 x 5 is a **single term** because it does not contain a + sign within it.

### How to Evaluate Using Terms

1. Identify all the terms of the expression
2. Evaluate each term (perform any multiplication or division within each term)
3. Add all the evaluated terms together

**Example:** Evaluate 30 + 5 x 4

| Step | Working |
|------|---------|
| Identify terms | 30 and 5 x 4 |
| Evaluate each term | 30 = 30, and 5 x 4 = 20 |
| Add all terms | 30 + 20 = **50** |

**Example:** Evaluate 5 x (3 + 2) + 7 x 8 + 3

| Step | Working |
|------|---------|
| Identify terms | 5 x (3 + 2), 7 x 8, and 3 |
| Evaluate each term | 5 x 5 = 25, 7 x 8 = 56, and 3 = 3 |
| Add all terms | 25 + 56 + 3 = **84** |

## Swapping and Grouping

### The Commutative Property of Addition

In mathematics, **swapping the order of terms does not change the sum**. This is called the **commutative property of addition**.

- 6 + (-4) = 2
- (-4) + 6 = 2

Both give the same answer!

This works for any number of terms, including negative numbers:

**Term 1 + Term 2 = Term 2 + Term 1**

### The Associative Property of Addition

**Grouping terms differently does not change the sum.** This is called the **associative property of addition**.

For example, with the expression (-7) + 10 + (-11):
- Adding first two, then third: [(-7) + 10] + (-11) = 3 + (-11) = -8
- Adding last two, then first: (-7) + [10 + (-11)] = (-7) + (-1) = -8
- Adding first and last, then second: [(-7) + (-11)] + 10 = (-18) + 10 = -8

All three give the same answer: **-8**.

> **Practical Use:** If Manasa adds a long list of numbers and forgets one, she does not need to start over. She can simply add the forgotten number to her existing sum. This works because of the associative property!

### Swapping in Everyday Life

Swapping order does not always work in daily life. You can put on your hat and shoes in any order and look the same. But you must put on socks **before** shoes -- the order matters! In addition, however, the order never matters.

## Removing Brackets -- Part I

### Brackets Preceded by a Minus Sign

When you remove brackets that come after a **minus sign**, all terms inside **change their signs**.

**Example:** 200 - (40 + 3)

To subtract 43 from 200, we can subtract 40 first and then subtract 3:
- 200 - 40 - 3 = 157

So: **200 - (40 + 3) = 200 - 40 - 3**

Notice: +40 became -40, and +3 became -3.

**Example:** 500 - (250 - 100)

- 500 - (250 - 100) = 500 - 150 = 350
- This equals: 500 - 250 + 100 = 350

Notice: +250 became -250, and -100 became +100.

> **Key Rule:** When removing brackets preceded by a **minus sign**, the signs of **all terms inside** the brackets get flipped (+ becomes - and - becomes +).

### Brackets Preceded by a Plus Sign

When you remove brackets that come after a **plus sign**, the terms inside **keep their signs unchanged**.

**Example:** 28 + (35 - 10) = 28 + 35 - 10 = 53

The signs of 35 and -10 remain the same.

### Tinker the Terms

What happens when we change the value of one term in an expression?

| Expression | Value | Reasoning |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| 53 + (-16) | 37 | Given |
| **54** + (-16) | **38** | 54 is one more than 53, so value is 1 more |
| **52** + (-16) | **36** | 52 is one less than 53, so value is 1 less |
| 53 + (**-15**) | **38** | -15 is one more than -16, so value is 1 more |
| 53 + (**-17**) | **36** | -17 is one less than -16, so value is 1 less |

## Removing Brackets -- Part II (The Distributive Property)

### Multiplying a Number by a Sum

Lhamo and Norbu each ordered a vegetable cutlet (Rs 43) and a rasgulla (Rs 24). The total cost is:

**2 x (43 + 24) = 2 x 43 + 2 x 24 = 86 + 48 = 134**

This is the **distributive property**: multiplying a number by a sum is the same as multiplying the number by each part and then adding.

### Multiplying a Number by a Difference

Similarly, for differences:

**(14 - 6) x 10 = 14 x 10 - 6 x 10 = 140 - 60 = 80**

> **The Distributive Property in Words:** The multiple of a sum (or difference) is the same as the sum (or difference) of the multiples.

### Clever Calculations Using the Distributive Property

The distributive property makes many calculations much faster:

**Example:** Find 97 x 25

97 x 25 = (100 - 3) x 25 = 100 x 25 - 3 x 25 = 2500 - 75 = **2425**

**Example:** Find 63 x 18, given that 53 x 18 = 954

63 x 18 = (53 + 10) x 18 = 53 x 18 + 10 x 18 = 954 + 180 = **1134**

### The Distributive Property with Common Factors

When two terms share a common factor, we can factor it out:

**4 x 5 + 3 x 5 = (4 + 3) x 5 = 7 x 5 = 35**

This works because both terms are multiples of 5.

Similarly: **10 x 98 + 3 x 98 = (10 + 3) x 98 = 13 x 98**

## Practical Applications

### Writing Expressions for Real Situations

**Paying with coins and notes:**
To pay Rs 432, you could use:
- 4 notes of Rs 100 + 1 note of Rs 20 + 1 note of Rs 10 + 2 coins of Rs 1
- Expression: 4 x 100 + 1 x 20 + 1 x 10 + 2 x 1 = 432

**Metro tickets:**
A metro ticket costs Rs 40 for adults and Rs 20 for children. For 4 adults and 3 children:
- Expression: 4 x 40 + 3 x 20 = 160 + 60 = Rs 220

**Monthly savings:**
Binu earns Rs 20,000 per month. She spends Rs 5,000 on rent, Rs 5,000 on food, and Rs 2,000 on other expenses. Her annual savings:
- Expression: 12 x [20,000 - (5,000 + 5,000 + 2,000)] = 12 x 8,000 = Rs 96,000

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Arithmetic expression** | A mathematical phrase using numbers and operations (+, -, x, /) |
| **Value of an expression** | The number the expression evaluates to |
| **Terms** | Parts of an expression separated by the + sign (after converting all subtractions to additions) |
| **Brackets** | Symbols ( ) used to indicate which part of an expression should be evaluated first |
| **Commutative property** | Swapping the order of terms in addition does not change the sum |
| **Associative property** | Grouping terms differently in addition does not change the sum |
| **Distributive property** | Multiplying a number by a sum (or difference) equals the sum (or difference) of the individual products |
| **Inverse** | The number with the opposite sign; the inverse of 14 is -14, and the inverse of -14 is 14 |
