## Why Order Matters

**Kiran:** Papa, my friend and I both solved 30 + 5 x 4 and got different answers. I got 50 and she got 140. Who is right?

**Ravi:** You are! The confusion is about the order of operations. In the expression 30 + 5 x 4, the multiplication 5 x 4 must be done first, giving 20. Then 30 + 20 equals 50.

**Kiran:** But why does multiplication come before addition?

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. Mallesh brought 30 marbles, and Arun brought 5 bags with 4 marbles each. The total is 30 plus 20, which is 50. If we added 30 and 5 first, we would get 35 bags of 4 marbles, which is 140 -- a completely wrong story. The concept of terms helps us avoid this confusion.

**Kiran:** So the terms in 30 + 5 x 4 are 30 and 5 x 4?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Terms are separated by the plus sign. Each term is evaluated first, then all terms are added. This is why brackets exist too -- they tell us explicitly what to compute first.

## Brackets Save the Day

**Kiran:** Papa, Irfan bought biscuits for Rs 15 and dal for Rs 56, then paid Rs 100. I wrote 100 - 15 + 56 and got Rs 141. That is more than he paid!

**Ravi:** The issue is that you need brackets. The correct expression is 100 - (15 + 56). Without brackets, the minus sign applies only to 15, and then 56 gets added. With brackets, you first calculate 15 + 56 = 71, then subtract from 100 to get Rs 29.

**Kiran:** So brackets group things that belong together?

**Ravi:** Yes, like punctuation in language. "Let us eat, Grandma" and "Let us eat Grandma" have very different meanings! Brackets in maths serve the same purpose -- they remove ambiguity. When you see brackets, always evaluate the inside first.

**Kiran:** And when I remove brackets preceded by a minus sign, all signs inside flip?

**Ravi:** Correct. 200 - (40 + 3) becomes 200 - 40 - 3. But 200 + (40 + 3) becomes 200 + 40 + 3 with no sign changes. This is a rule worth memorising for exams.

## Swapping and Grouping

**Kiran:** Papa, does it matter what order I add numbers in?

**Ravi:** Not at all! This is the commutative property. Whether you add 6 + (-4) or (-4) + 6, you get 2 either way. Think of it like putting on your hat and shoes -- the order does not matter for the final result.

**Kiran:** And grouping?

**Ravi:** The associative property says you can group terms however you like. For (-7) + 10 + (-11), you can add any two first and then the third. The answer is always -8. This is practically useful -- if you are adding a long list of numbers and forget one, just add it at the end. No need to start over.

**Kiran:** Like when Amma calculates the grocery bill and remembers she forgot to add the price of dahi?

**Ravi:** Exactly. She just adds it to the running total. Mathematics supports that because of the associative property.

## The Distributive Property

**Kiran:** Papa, I need to calculate 97 x 25 for homework. That seems hard to do mentally.

**Ravi:** Break 97 into 100 - 3. Then 97 x 25 = (100 - 3) x 25 = 100 x 25 - 3 x 25 = 2500 - 75 = 2425. This is the distributive property in action.

**Kiran:** So I can split a number to make the multiplication easier?

**Ravi:** Yes. It works the other way too. If you know that 53 x 18 = 954, you can find 63 x 18 by writing it as (53 + 10) x 18 = 954 + 180 = 1134. You leverage what you already know.

**Kiran:** This is like how the sabzi-wala at the market quickly calculates prices! He probably does 4 kg at Rs 48 per kg as 4 x 50 minus 4 x 2.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The distributive property is not just a textbook concept -- it is mental maths that people use every day, from shopkeepers to engineers. Master it and you will solve problems much faster in exams.

## Real-World Expressions

**Kiran:** Can we write real-life situations as expressions?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Suppose our family takes the metro. An adult ticket is Rs 40 and a child ticket is Rs 20. For 4 adults and 3 children, the total is 4 x 40 + 3 x 20 = 160 + 60 = Rs 220.

**Kiran:** And if Binu aunty earns Rs 20,000 per month, spends Rs 5,000 on rent, Rs 5,000 on food, and Rs 2,000 on other things, her annual savings would be...

**Ravi:** 12 x (20,000 - 5,000 - 5,000 - 2,000) = 12 x 8,000 = Rs 96,000. See how we used brackets to first find the monthly savings, and then multiplied by 12?

**Kiran:** Maths really is just a way to describe what happens in real life!

**Ravi:** That is the whole point. An arithmetic expression is a compact way to capture a real situation. Learn to translate between words and expressions, and you will never find word problems difficult.
