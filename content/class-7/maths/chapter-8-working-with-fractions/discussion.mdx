## Multiplying Fractions

**Kiran:** Papa, Aaron walks 3 km in an hour. His tortoise walks only 1/4 km in an hour. How far does the tortoise go in 3 hours?

**Ravi:** Simple -- 3 times 1/4. You are repeating 1/4 three times: 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 3/4 km. To multiply a whole number by a fraction, multiply the whole number by the numerator and keep the denominator.

**Kiran:** What about multiplying two fractions, like 1/2 times 1/4?

**Ravi:** Imagine the tortoise walks 1/4 km in an hour. In half an hour, it covers half of 1/4. Visualise a square -- shade 1/4 of it vertically, then take 1/2 of that by dividing horizontally. The overlap is 1/8 of the square. So 1/2 times 1/4 = 1/8.

**Kiran:** And the general rule?

**Ravi:** Multiply numerators together and denominators together. For 2/3 times 4/5: (2 times 4) over (3 times 5) = 8/15. A useful tip: simplify before multiplying. In 5/6 times 3/10, cancel 5 with 10 and 3 with 6, getting 1/2 times 1/2 = 1/4.

## Fraction of a Quantity

**Kiran:** Papa, when the teacher says "find 2/3 of 60," what does "of" mean?

**Ravi:** "Of" means multiplication! So 2/3 of 60 = 2/3 times 60 = (2 times 60) / 3 = 120/3 = 40.

**Kiran:** A school has 720 students and 5/8 are girls. So the number of girls is 5/8 of 720?

**Ravi:** Yes. 5/8 times 720 = (5 times 720) / 8 = 3600/8 = 450 girls. Notice something important -- when you multiply by a proper fraction (less than 1), the result is smaller than the original. 450 is less than 720.

**Kiran:** That makes sense. Taking a fraction of something means taking a part of it, so the result should be smaller.

**Ravi:** Exactly. But multiplying by an improper fraction (greater than 1) makes the result larger. 4 times 3/2 = 6, which is bigger than 4. Understanding this helps you check if your answers are reasonable.

## Dividing Fractions

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I divide by a fraction? For example, how many pieces of 1/2 metre can I cut from a 3-metre rope?

**Ravi:** Think about it practically. Each metre gives you 2 pieces of half a metre. So 3 metres give 3 times 2 = 6 pieces. Mathematically, 3 divided by 1/2 = 3 times 2 = 6.

**Kiran:** So dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The reciprocal of a/b is b/a -- you flip it upside down. Dividing by 1/2 is the same as multiplying by 2/1 = 2. For any fraction: a/b divided by c/d = a/b times d/c.

**Kiran:** Let me try: 3/4 divided by 1/8. That is 3/4 times 8/1 = 24/4 = 6. So I can cut 6 pieces of 1/8 metre from 3/4 metre of ribbon!

**Ravi:** Perfect. And a number multiplied by its reciprocal always gives 1. For example, 3/4 times 4/3 = 12/12 = 1. This property is what makes the division rule work.

## Mixed Numbers and Real Problems

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I multiply 2 and 1/3 by 1 and 1/2?

**Ravi:** First convert to improper fractions. 2 and 1/3 = 7/3, and 1 and 1/2 = 3/2. Now multiply: 7/3 times 3/2 = 21/6 = 7/2 = 3 and 1/2.

**Kiran:** And for word problems?

**Ravi:** Here is one. A car travels 24 and 1/2 km on 3 and 1/2 litres of petrol. What is the mileage?

**Kiran:** Mileage = distance divided by fuel. So 49/2 divided by 7/2 = 49/2 times 2/7 = 98/14 = 7 km per litre.

**Ravi:** Excellent! Notice how the 2 in the numerator and denominator cancelled out. Always look for simplification opportunities. In exams, this saves time and reduces errors.

## Why These Rules Work

**Kiran:** Papa, these fraction rules feel like magic. Why does "multiply numerators and denominators" actually work?

**Ravi:** Think of 2/3 times 4/5 visually. Draw a square. Divide it into 5 columns and shade 4 of them for 4/5. Then divide it into 3 rows and shade 2 of them for 2/3. The overlap is 8 small rectangles out of 15 total -- that is 8/15.

**Kiran:** So the denominator of the product tells me the total number of small parts, and the numerator tells me how many are in the overlap?

**Ravi:** Precisely. The total parts = 3 times 5 = 15, and the overlapping parts = 2 times 4 = 8. This visual proof shows why the rule works. Similarly, the division rule works because dividing by c/d asks "how many groups of c/d fit into a/b?" -- which is the same as multiplying a/b by the reciprocal d/c.

**Kiran:** Understanding why the rules work makes them much easier to remember.

**Ravi:** That is the difference between memorising and learning. When you understand the reason, you will never forget the rule.
