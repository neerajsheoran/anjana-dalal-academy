## What Does Congruence Mean?

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher says two figures are congruent if they are "geometric twins." What does that mean exactly?

**Ravi:** Two figures are congruent if one can be placed exactly over the other so they match perfectly -- same shape and same size. You can rotate or flip one figure, but it must fit exactly on the other.

**Kiran:** So two circles with the same radius are congruent?

**Ravi:** Yes. And two rectangles with the same length and breadth are congruent. But two rectangles that are 3 by 5 and 4 by 4 are NOT congruent, even though their perimeters might be similar.

**Kiran:** What about two triangles? How do I check if they are congruent?

**Ravi:** That is the big question of this chapter. A triangle has 6 measurements -- 3 sides and 3 angles. But you do not need all 6 to guarantee congruence. Certain combinations of just 3 measurements are enough.

---

## SSS -- Three Sides Are Enough

**Kiran:** If I know all three sides of two triangles are equal, are they definitely congruent?

**Ravi:** Yes, this is the SSS condition -- Side-Side-Side. When you construct a triangle with given side lengths using a compass, the shape is completely determined. The two arcs from the base endpoints can only intersect in ways that produce congruent triangles.

**Kiran:** Can you give me an example?

**Ravi:** Triangle ABC has sides AB = 6 cm, BC = 5 cm, CA = 3.5 cm. Triangle DEJ has DE = 3.5 cm, EJ = 5 cm, JD = 6 cm. All three pairs of sides match (in some order), so the triangles are congruent by SSS.

**Kiran:** What about the order of vertices? Does that matter?

**Ravi:** Very much! When we write "triangle ABC is congruent to triangle XYZ," we mean A corresponds to X, B to Y, C to Z. So AB = XY, BC = YZ, and AC = XZ. Getting the correspondence wrong is a common exam mistake.

---

## SAS and the Included Angle

**Kiran:** Is knowing two sides and one angle enough?

**Ravi:** Only if the angle is the included angle -- the angle between the two known sides. This is the SAS condition. If AB = XY, AC = XZ, and angle A = angle X, the triangles are congruent.

**Kiran:** What if the angle is not between the two sides?

**Ravi:** Then it is the SSA case, which is ambiguous. With two sides and a non-included angle, you might get two different valid triangles. The compass arc can hit the other line at two different points.

**Kiran:** So SSA is unreliable but SAS is guaranteed?

**Ravi:** Exactly. In exams, always check whether the given angle is the included angle. If it is, use SAS confidently. If it is not, be cautious -- SSA does not guarantee congruence unless the triangle is right-angled, which gives us the special RHS condition.

---

## ASA, AAS, and RHS

**Kiran:** What about knowing two angles and a side?

**Ravi:** If the side is between the two known angles, that is ASA -- Angle-Side-Angle. If the side is not between them, that is AAS -- Angle-Angle-Side. Both guarantee congruence!

**Kiran:** Why does AAS work if the side is not included?

**Ravi:** Because if you know two angles of a triangle, the third is automatically determined (since all three add to 180 degrees). So AAS information converts to ASA information. Knowing two angles and any side completely determines the triangle.

**Kiran:** And what about AAA -- all three angles equal?

**Ravi:** Three equal angles do NOT guarantee congruence. You can have two triangles with angles 30, 70, and 80 degrees but different sizes. They have the same shape but different sizes -- those are called similar triangles, which you will study later.

**Kiran:** So angles determine shape but not size, while sides determine size?

**Ravi:** That is an excellent summary. For congruence, you need information about both shape and size.

---

## Using Congruence to Prove Facts

**Kiran:** Papa, how is congruence useful beyond just checking if triangles match?

**Ravi:** Congruence lets you prove that certain sides or angles must be equal. For example, in a rectangle ABCD, the diagonal BD creates two triangles: ABD and CDB. Since AB = CD, AD = CB, and BD is common, the triangles are congruent by SSS.

**Kiran:** So the diagonal splits a rectangle into two identical triangles?

**Ravi:** Yes. And because corresponding parts of congruent triangles are equal, you can deduce that angle ABD = angle CDB, which tells you something about the geometry of rectangles.

**Kiran:** So congruence is a tool for discovering new facts from known ones -- like proofs in the parallel lines chapter?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Geometry builds layer by layer. You use properties of parallel lines to prove angle relationships, angle relationships to prove the angle sum property of triangles, and congruence conditions to prove that specific triangles match. Each result becomes a stepping stone for the next.
