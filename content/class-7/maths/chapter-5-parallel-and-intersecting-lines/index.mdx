Look around your classroom -- the edges of a table, the lines on a notebook, the frame of the door. Lines are everywhere! In this chapter, we will explore what happens when lines meet (or do not meet), and discover interesting properties about the angles they form.

## Across the Line

When two lines on a flat surface (a **plane**) meet at a point, we say they **intersect** each other. The point where they meet is called the **point of intersection**.

![Two intersecting lines forming four angles labelled a, b, c, d at the point of intersection](intersecting-lines-four-angles.png)

When two lines intersect, they form **four angles** at the point of intersection.

### Linear Pairs

Two adjacent angles formed by intersecting lines always add up to **180 degrees** because together they form a straight angle. Such pairs are called **linear pairs**.

If angle **a** = 120 degrees:
- a + b = 180 degrees, so b = 60 degrees
- b + c = 180 degrees, so c = 120 degrees
- c + d = 180 degrees, so d = 60 degrees

### Vertically Opposite Angles

The angles that are opposite to each other at the point of intersection are called **vertically opposite angles**. In the example above:
- a and c are vertically opposite (both 120 degrees)
- b and d are vertically opposite (both 60 degrees)

> **Important Property:** Vertically opposite angles are **always equal**. This is true for any pair of intersecting lines, and we can prove it using the fact that linear pairs add up to 180 degrees.

**Proof:** Since a + b = 180 degrees and b + c = 180 degrees, it follows that a = c. Similarly, since a + b = 180 degrees and a + d = 180 degrees, it follows that b = d.

## Perpendicular Lines

When two lines intersect such that **all four angles are equal**, each angle must be **90 degrees** (since 4 x 90 = 360).

**Perpendicular lines** are lines that intersect at right angles (90 degrees). We mark perpendicular lines with a small square symbol at the point of intersection.

## Parallel Lines

Some lines, no matter how far we extend them, will **never meet**. Such lines are called **parallel lines**.

![Parallel lines with arrow marks and perpendicular lines with a square symbol at the intersection](parallel-perpendicular-lines.png)

**Parallel lines** are lines that lie on the same plane and do not intersect, no matter how far they are extended in either direction.

**Examples of parallel lines in everyday life:**
- Opposite edges of a table or notebook
- Railway tracks
- Horizontal lines on ruled paper
- Opposite sides of a door frame

> **Important:** The lines must lie on the same plane to be called parallel. A line on a table and a line on a wall may never meet, but they are not called parallel because they are not on the same plane.

### Notation

- Parallel lines are marked with **arrow marks** (>) on them
- If there are multiple sets of parallel lines, the second set uses double arrows (>>)
- Perpendicular lines are marked with a **small square** at the intersection point

## Paper Folding Activities

Parallel and perpendicular lines can be created through paper folding:

1. The **opposite edges** of a square sheet are parallel to each other
2. The **adjacent edges** are perpendicular to each other
3. Folding the sheet horizontally in half creates a new line **parallel** to the top and bottom edges
4. Each additional horizontal fold doubles the number of parallel lines
5. A vertical fold creates a line **perpendicular** to the horizontal lines

## Transversals

A **transversal** is a line that intersects two or more lines at distinct points.

![Transversal crossing two parallel lines with all 8 angles labelled showing corresponding and alternate angle pairs](transversal-parallel-lines-angles.png)

When a transversal intersects two lines, it creates **8 angles** in total -- 4 at each point of intersection.

```
     1  2          line l
     4  3
                   transversal t
     5  6          line m
     8  7
```

Since vertically opposite angles are equal (giving us 4 pairs), there are at most **4 distinct angle measures** among the 8 angles.

## Corresponding Angles

When a transversal crosses two lines, angles that are in the **same position** at each intersection are called **corresponding angles**.

In the diagram above, the corresponding angle pairs are:
- Angle 1 and Angle 5
- Angle 2 and Angle 6
- Angle 3 and Angle 7
- Angle 4 and Angle 8

### The Corresponding Angles Property

> **When a transversal intersects parallel lines**, the corresponding angles are **always equal**.

Conversely, if the corresponding angles formed by a transversal on two lines are equal, then the two lines **must be parallel**.

If the lines are **not parallel**, the corresponding angles formed by a transversal can **never** be equal.

## Alternate Angles

**Alternate angles** are pairs of angles that are on **opposite sides** of the transversal and between the two lines.

In the standard labelling:
- Angle d and Angle f are alternate angles
- Angle c and Angle e are alternate angles

### Why Alternate Angles Are Equal (for Parallel Lines)

We can prove this using what we already know:
1. Angle f = Angle b (corresponding angles, since lines are parallel)
2. Angle b = Angle d (vertically opposite angles)
3. Therefore, Angle f = Angle d

> **Alternate angles formed by a transversal intersecting parallel lines are always equal.**

## Finding All Angles

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines and you know **just one angle**, you can find all eight angles.

**Example:** If angle 6 = 135 degrees:

| Angle | Measure | Reason |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Angle 6 | 135 degrees | Given |
| Angle 2 | 135 degrees | Corresponding angle of 6 |
| Angle 8 | 135 degrees | Vertically opposite to 6 |
| Angle 4 | 135 degrees | Corresponding angle of 8 |
| Angle 5 | 45 degrees | Linear pair with 6 (180 - 135) |
| Angle 1 | 45 degrees | Corresponding angle of 5 |
| Angle 7 | 45 degrees | Vertically opposite to 5 |
| Angle 3 | 45 degrees | Corresponding angle of 7 |

> **Key insight:** When a transversal crosses parallel lines, there are only **two distinct angle values**, and they always add up to **180 degrees**.

## Checking if Lines Are Parallel

To determine whether two lines are parallel:
1. Draw a transversal across both lines
2. Measure the corresponding angles (or alternate angles)
3. If corresponding angles are **equal**, the lines are parallel
4. If they are **not equal**, the lines are not parallel

**Example:** If angle a = 120 degrees and angle f = 70 degrees, are the lines parallel?

No! For the lines to be parallel, the corresponding angles must be equal. Since 120 does not equal 70, the lines are **not parallel**.

## Drawing Parallel Lines

![Step-by-step diagram of drawing parallel lines using a set square and ruler](drawing-parallel-lines-set-square.png)

### Using a Set Square and Ruler

1. Draw a line **l** with a ruler
2. Place a set square so that one of its edges lies along line l
3. Place a ruler along another edge of the set square
4. Slide the set square along the ruler to a new position
5. Draw a new line along the same edge of the set square

The two lines are parallel because the corresponding angles (both 90 degrees) are equal.

### Through Paper Folding

To draw a line parallel to line **l** passing through point **A**:
1. Fold a line **t** perpendicular to **l** that passes through A
2. Fold another line **m** perpendicular to **t** that passes through A
3. Lines **l** and **m** are parallel (both are perpendicular to t)

## Interior Angles on the Same Side

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines, the **co-interior angles** (also called same-side interior angles) are angles that are between the two lines and on the same side of the transversal.

Co-interior angles always add up to **180 degrees**.

## Measurements and Geometry

When you measure angles with a protractor, your measurements might not be perfectly exact -- a linear pair might not add up to exactly 180 degrees, or vertically opposite angles might differ slightly. This is because of:
- Measurement errors from the protractor
- The thickness of drawn lines (ideal geometric lines have zero thickness)

In geometry, we work with **ideal** lines and angles, arriving at properties through **reasoning and proof** rather than measurement alone.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Intersecting lines** | Lines that meet at a point on a plane |
| **Linear pair** | Two adjacent angles formed by intersecting lines that add up to 180 degrees |
| **Vertically opposite angles** | Opposite angles formed at the point of intersection; they are always equal |
| **Perpendicular lines** | Lines that intersect at right angles (90 degrees) |
| **Parallel lines** | Lines on the same plane that never meet, no matter how far extended |
| **Transversal** | A line that intersects two or more lines at distinct points |
| **Corresponding angles** | Angles in the same position at each intersection point of a transversal; equal when lines are parallel |
| **Alternate angles** | Angles on opposite sides of the transversal between two lines; equal when lines are parallel |
| **Proof** | A logical justification that a mathematical statement is always true |
