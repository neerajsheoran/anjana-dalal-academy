## Lines Meeting and Not Meeting

**Kiran:** Papa, I was looking at railway tracks and wondering -- the two rails never seem to meet. But the road crossing them cuts across both. Are these different types of lines?

**Ravi:** Excellent observation! The two rails are parallel lines -- they lie on the same plane and never intersect no matter how far you extend them. The road crossing them is a transversal -- a line that intersects two or more lines at different points.

**Kiran:** And when two lines do meet?

**Ravi:** They form four angles at the point of intersection. The adjacent angles always add up to 180 degrees -- these are called linear pairs. And the opposite angles are always equal -- those are called vertically opposite angles.

**Kiran:** So if one angle is 120 degrees, I can find all four?

**Ravi:** Yes. The adjacent angle is 180 - 120 = 60 degrees. The vertically opposite angle is also 120 degrees. And the fourth is 60 degrees. Just one angle determines all four.

---

## Perpendicular Lines

**Kiran:** Papa, what if all four angles at the intersection are equal?

**Ravi:** Then each angle must be 90 degrees, since 4 times 90 is 360. Such lines are called perpendicular lines. We mark them with a small square symbol at the intersection.

**Kiran:** Like the edges of my notebook? The horizontal lines are perpendicular to the vertical margin line.

**Ravi:** Exactly. And adjacent edges of a door frame, or the crossbar and upright of a cricket stump -- all perpendicular. Now here is a key idea: parallel lines must be on the same plane. A line on the floor and a line on the wall may never meet, but they are not parallel because they are not on the same plane.

**Kiran:** That is like two roads on different levels of a flyover -- they do not intersect but they are not parallel either.

**Ravi:** Perfect analogy. Geometry requires precision in definitions.

---

## Transversals and Angle Pairs

**Kiran:** When a transversal crosses two lines, how many angles does it create?

**Ravi:** Eight angles -- four at each intersection point. But here is the elegant part: using vertically opposite angles, there are only four distinct angle measures among those eight. And if the two lines are parallel, there are only two distinct values, and they always add up to 180 degrees.

**Kiran:** So knowing just one angle gives me all eight?

**Ravi:** Exactly. If angle 6 is 135 degrees, then its corresponding angle, its vertically opposite angle, and the corresponding angle of that vertically opposite angle are all 135 degrees. The remaining four are each 180 - 135 = 45 degrees.

**Kiran:** What are corresponding angles exactly?

**Ravi:** Angles in the same position at each intersection. Think of two identical traffic intersections on the same road -- the top-right angle at the first intersection corresponds to the top-right angle at the second. When the lines are parallel, corresponding angles are always equal.

---

## Alternate Angles and Proofs

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher mentioned alternate angles. How are they different from corresponding angles?

**Ravi:** Alternate angles are on opposite sides of the transversal and between the two lines. They form a Z-shape or S-shape pattern. When the lines are parallel, alternate angles are equal too.

**Kiran:** Can we prove that without just accepting it?

**Ravi:** Yes, and this is beautiful. We know corresponding angles are equal for parallel lines, and we know vertically opposite angles are equal. If angle f equals angle b (corresponding), and angle b equals angle d (vertically opposite), then angle f equals angle d. Since f and d are alternate angles, we have proved they are equal using what we already knew!

**Kiran:** So we built new knowledge from old knowledge. That is how mathematics works?

**Ravi:** Exactly. A proof is a chain of logical steps. You start from things you know are true and arrive at something new that must also be true. This is why geometry values proof over measurement -- a protractor might give small errors, but a proof is always exact.

---

## Testing for Parallel Lines

**Kiran:** Can I use these angle properties to check if two lines are parallel?

**Ravi:** Yes! Draw a transversal across both lines and measure the corresponding angles. If they are equal, the lines are parallel. If not, the lines are not parallel.

**Kiran:** So the property works both ways -- parallel lines give equal corresponding angles, and equal corresponding angles mean the lines must be parallel?

**Ravi:** Correct. This is called a necessary and sufficient condition. And you can also use alternate angles or co-interior angles for the same test. Co-interior angles -- the ones on the same side between the lines -- should add up to 180 degrees for parallel lines.

**Kiran:** This is useful for construction too, right? Like making sure shelves are parallel to the floor?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Architects and carpenters use these principles daily. A set square and ruler can draw parallel lines precisely because they maintain equal corresponding angles. Ancient Indian builders used similar geometric principles to construct perfectly aligned temple walls and water channels.
