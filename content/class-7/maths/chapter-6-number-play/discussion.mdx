## The Power of Parity

**Kiran:** Papa, my friend challenged me: pick exactly 5 cards from 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15, 17 that add up to 30. I tried for an hour and could not do it!

**Ravi:** That is because it is impossible. And parity proves it. All nine cards have odd numbers. The sum of 5 odd numbers is always odd, because odd + odd = even, so 4 odds give even, and even + odd = odd. Since 30 is even, you can never get it from 5 odd numbers.

**Kiran:** So instead of trying every combination, I can just check if the parity works?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Parity is a powerful tool for proving impossibility. Here is another one: Martin and Maria were born exactly one year apart. Can the sum of their ages ever be 112?

**Kiran:** Their ages are consecutive numbers, so one is even and one is odd. Even + odd = odd. But 112 is even. So... no, it is impossible!

**Ravi:** Brilliant. You just proved something without checking a single specific case. That is the elegance of mathematical reasoning.

## Magic Squares

**Kiran:** Papa, I saw a magic square in the chapter where every row, column, and diagonal adds to the same number. How does that work?

**Ravi:** For a 3 by 3 magic square using numbers 1 to 9, the total of all numbers is 45. Since there are 3 rows with equal sums, the magic sum is 45 divided by 3, which is 15. The centre must be 5, and through logical reasoning, you can place all nine numbers.

**Kiran:** Can we make magic squares with other numbers?

**Ravi:** Yes! Replace 5 with any number m. The general form uses m-4, m-3, m-2, m-1, m, m+1, m+2, m+3, m+4. For 9 consecutive numbers centred at 25, the magic sum would be 3 times 25 = 75.

**Kiran:** That is amazing. Is this a modern discovery?

**Ravi:** Not at all! The earliest recorded 4 by 4 magic square is from the Parshvanath Jain Temple in Khajuraho, India, dating back to the 10th century. It is called the Chautis Yantra because its magic sum is 34. India has a rich history of recreational mathematics.

## The 1089 Trick

**Kiran:** Papa, pick any 3-digit number where the first and last digits differ by at least 2.

**Ravi:** Let me try 523.

**Kiran:** Reverse it: 325. Subtract the smaller from the larger: 523 - 325 = 198. Reverse 198 to get 891. Now add: 198 + 891 = 1089!

**Ravi:** Interesting! Let me try another. 741. Reversed: 147. Subtract: 741 - 147 = 594. Reverse: 495. Add: 594 + 495 = 1089 again!

**Kiran:** It always gives 1089!

**Ravi:** This is a wonderful example of how number patterns work. The structure of our place value system guarantees this result. When you subtract a 3-digit number from its reverse, the middle digit of the result is always 9, and the first and last digits always add to 9. So the result plus its reverse always gives 1089.

**Kiran:** Maths can feel like magic when you understand the patterns behind it!

## Divisibility Tests

**Kiran:** Papa, is there a quick way to check if 4518 is divisible by 9 without actually dividing?

**Ravi:** Add the digits: 4 + 5 + 1 + 8 = 18. Since 18 is divisible by 9, so is 4518. This is the divisibility test for 9.

**Kiran:** Are there similar tests for other numbers?

**Ravi:** Yes! For 2: check if the last digit is even. For 5: check if the last digit is 0 or 5. For 3: check if the digit sum is divisible by 3. For 4: check if the last two digits form a number divisible by 4. For 11: compute the alternating sum of digits.

**Kiran:** Let me try 11. For 82918: 8 - 1 + 9 - 2 + 8 = 22. Since 22 is divisible by 11, the number is divisible by 11!

**Ravi:** Perfect. These tests are incredibly useful in exams when you need to factorise numbers or simplify fractions quickly. They save enormous time compared to long division.

## Palindromes and Digit Sums

**Kiran:** Papa, what is a palindrome in maths?

**Ravi:** A number that reads the same forwards and backwards -- like 121, 1331, or 45654. And here is a fun experiment: take 48, reverse it to get 84, and add: 48 + 84 = 132. Not a palindrome yet. Reverse 132 to get 231, add: 132 + 231 = 363. That is a palindrome!

**Kiran:** So many numbers can be turned into palindromes by repeatedly reversing and adding?

**Ravi:** Yes, most numbers eventually become palindromes through this process. The digit sum is another interesting concept -- you add all digits of a number, and if the result has more than one digit, you add again until you get a single digit. The digit sum of 789 is 7 + 8 + 9 = 24, then 2 + 4 = 6.

**Kiran:** Numbers really do have hidden patterns everywhere!

**Ravi:** That is why this chapter is called Number Play. Mathematics is not just about calculation -- it is about discovering patterns, making conjectures, and proving them. The more you explore, the more surprises you find.
