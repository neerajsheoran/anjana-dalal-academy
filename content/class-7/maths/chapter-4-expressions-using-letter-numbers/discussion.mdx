## Letters as Numbers

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we use letters like "a" and "x" in maths? Letters belong in English, not in equations!

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. Shabnam is 3 years older than Aftab. If Aftab is 4, Shabnam is 7. If Aftab is 10, Shabnam is 13. Instead of writing this relationship for every possible age, we write s = a + 3, where "a" stands for Aftab's age and "s" for Shabnam's.

**Kiran:** So the letter is like a placeholder that can take different values?

**Ravi:** Exactly. We call them variables or letter-numbers. They let us express general relationships in one compact formula instead of listing endless specific cases. When Ketaki buys coconuts at Rs 35 each and jaggery at Rs 60 per kg, the total cost for c coconuts and j kg of jaggery is 35c + 60j.

**Kiran:** And to find the actual cost, I just plug in the values?

**Ravi:** Yes. For 7 coconuts and 4 kg jaggery: 35 times 7 plus 60 times 4 = 245 + 240 = Rs 485. The formula works for any quantities. This is the power of algebra -- one expression captures infinite situations.

## Simplification and Like Terms

**Kiran:** Papa, I have the expression l + b + l + b for the perimeter of a rectangle. Can I simplify it?

**Ravi:** Of course! Group the like terms. l + l = 2l, and b + b = 2b. So the perimeter is 2l + 2b. This is the simplified form.

**Kiran:** What exactly makes terms "like" or "unlike"?

**Ravi:** Like terms involve the same letter-numbers. So 5c, 3c, and 10c are all like terms -- they all involve c. You can combine them: 5c + 3c + 10c = 18c. But 5c and 3d are unlike terms and cannot be combined. It is like adding apples and oranges -- 5 apples plus 3 oranges stays as 5 apples and 3 oranges.

**Kiran:** So 4v + 3v = 7v, but 4v + 3w stays as 4v + 3w?

**Ravi:** Perfect. And the reason 4v + 3v = 7v works is actually the distributive property: (4 + 3) times v = 7v. This connects back to what we learned in the previous chapter about arithmetic.

## Removing Brackets with Sign Changes

**Kiran:** Papa, a shop charges 40x + 75y for renting x chairs and y tables, then refunds 6x + 10y on return. What is the net cost?

**Ravi:** The net cost is (40x + 75y) - (6x + 10y). When you remove the brackets after the minus sign, every sign inside flips. So it becomes 40x + 75y - 6x - 10y.

**Kiran:** Then I group like terms: 40x - 6x = 34x, and 75y - 10y = 65y. So the answer is 34x + 65y?

**Ravi:** Exactly right! This is one of the most important algebra rules -- brackets preceded by a minus sign flip all signs inside. Brackets preceded by a plus sign keep all signs unchanged. In exams, careless sign errors are the most common mistake.

**Kiran:** I will remember: minus outside means flip everything inside.

**Ravi:** Good. And always double-check your sign changes. One wrong sign can make the entire answer wrong.

## Expressions Are Not Always Equal

**Kiran:** Papa, are 5u and 5 + u the same thing? They both have 5 and u.

**Ravi:** Let us test with values. When u = 2, 5u = 10 but 5 + u = 7. When u = 5, 5u = 25 but 5 + u = 10. They give completely different results!

**Kiran:** So 5u means 5 times u, while 5 + u means 5 added to u. They look similar but behave differently.

**Ravi:** Right. Similarly, 10y - 3 and 10(y - 3) are different. The first means "3 less than 10 times y," while the second means "10 times the quantity 3 less than y." When y = 7, the first gives 67 and the second gives 40.

**Kiran:** How do I avoid confusing these?

**Ravi:** Always substitute a test value and check. If two expressions give different numbers for the same value of the variable, they are different expressions. This is a powerful verification technique for exams too.

## The Distributive Property with Variables

**Kiran:** Papa, Charu scored 7p - 3q, 8p - 4q, and 6p - 2q in three quiz rounds. How do I find her total?

**Ravi:** Add all three: (7p - 3q) + (8p - 4q) + (6p - 2q). Since the plus sign precedes each bracket, signs stay unchanged. Now group: 7p + 8p + 6p = 21p, and -3q - 4q - 2q = -9q. Total is 21p - 9q.

**Kiran:** If each correct answer scores 4 marks and each wrong answer loses 1 mark, that is 21 times 4 minus 9 times 1 = 84 - 9 = 75!

**Ravi:** Excellent. Now Krishita scored 23p - 7q. Who scored more?

**Kiran:** Let me find the difference: 23p - 7q - (21p - 9q) = 23p - 7q - 21p + 9q = 2p + 2q. Since both p and q are positive, this is always positive. So Krishita always scores more!

**Ravi:** Brilliant reasoning. You just proved mathematically that Krishita wins regardless of the specific values of p and q. This is what algebra does -- it lets you prove general statements, not just compute specific answers.
