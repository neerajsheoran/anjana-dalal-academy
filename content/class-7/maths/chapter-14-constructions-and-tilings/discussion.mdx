## The Perpendicular Bisector

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I find the exact midpoint of a line segment using just a compass and a ruler -- no measuring?

**Ravi:** Construct the perpendicular bisector. Open your compass to any radius greater than half the segment. Draw arcs from both endpoints -- above and below the line. Connect the two intersection points. This line crosses your segment at its exact midpoint and is perpendicular to it.

**Kiran:** Why does this work?

**Ravi:** Every point that is equidistant from both endpoints lies on the perpendicular bisector. Your two arc intersection points are equidistant from both ends (same compass radius), so the line through them must be the perpendicular bisector.

**Kiran:** This is geometry using logic, not measurement!

**Ravi:** Exactly. And this method is ancient. The Shulba Sutras -- Indian geometric texts from over 2500 years ago -- describe the same principle using ropes instead of a compass. The Katyayana Shulba Sutra explains finding a midpoint by fastening a rope's ends to two pegs and pulling the midpoint taut.

## Constructing Standard Angles

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I construct a 60-degree angle without a protractor?

**Ravi:** Draw a ray. With the compass at the starting point, draw an arc cutting the ray. Without changing the radius, place the compass at the intersection point and draw another arc cutting the first arc. Connect to the starting point. The angle is exactly 60 degrees.

**Kiran:** Why 60 degrees?

**Ravi:** Because the triangle formed (starting point, first arc point, second arc point) has all three sides equal to the compass radius. It is equilateral, and every angle of an equilateral triangle is 60 degrees!

**Kiran:** Can I build other angles from this?

**Ravi:** Yes! Bisect 60 degrees to get 30 degrees. Construct a perpendicular (90 degrees) and bisect it to get 45 degrees. Two 60-degree angles side by side give 120 degrees. And 180 minus 30 gives 150 degrees. With just bisection and the 60-degree construction, you can make many standard angles.

## Angle Bisection

**Kiran:** How does the angle bisector construction work?

**Ravi:** Given an angle, place the compass at the vertex and draw an arc cutting both arms. Then, from each of those intersection points, draw arcs with the same radius. Connect the vertex to where those arcs meet. This line bisects the angle.

**Kiran:** And the proof?

**Ravi:** The two triangles formed are congruent by SSS -- same three sides. Since corresponding angles are equal, the two halves of the original angle are equal. We are using the congruence conditions from the previous geometry chapter!

**Kiran:** Everything connects. Congruence proves the bisector works, and the bisector helps us construct new angles.

**Ravi:** That is the beauty of geometry. Each construction is backed by a rigorous proof, not just a procedure. Understanding the proof makes you confident that the construction is exact.

## Which Shapes Can Tile?

**Kiran:** Papa, I noticed that our kitchen floor uses square tiles, and the footpath near the park uses hexagonal tiles. Can any shape tile a flat surface?

**Ravi:** Only if the angles at each meeting point add up to exactly 360 degrees. For regular polygons: equilateral triangles have 60-degree angles (6 fit around a point), squares have 90-degree angles (4 fit), and regular hexagons have 120-degree angles (3 fit). These are the only three regular polygons that tile the plane alone.

**Kiran:** What about regular pentagons?

**Ravi:** Their interior angle is 108 degrees. 360 divided by 108 is 3.33... -- not a whole number. So pentagons leave gaps. Regular octagons (135 degrees) also cannot tile alone, but they can tile with squares filling the gaps!

**Kiran:** So mixed tilings are possible too?

**Ravi:** Yes, as long as the angles at every vertex total exactly 360 degrees. Hexagons plus triangles, squares plus triangles -- many beautiful combinations work.

## Tilings in Indian Art

**Kiran:** Are tilings just a maths concept or do they appear in real life?

**Ravi:** They are everywhere in Indian art and architecture! Kolam and rangoli patterns are geometric tilings drawn at doorsteps across South India. Jali screens in Mughal architecture like the Taj Mahal use intricate tessellations. Temple floors across India feature square and hexagonal tilings.

**Kiran:** So artists and architects have been using these mathematical principles for centuries?

**Ravi:** For millennia! The Shulba Sutras dealt with geometric constructions for Vedic fire altars, which required precise tiling of specific shapes. When you draw a kolam, you are doing geometry. When you construct a perpendicular bisector, you are using the same principles as those ancient builders.

**Kiran:** That makes me appreciate both maths and art more.

**Ravi:** And that is the whole point. Mathematics is not separate from the real world -- it is the language that describes the patterns in art, nature, and architecture. A regular hexagon tiles perfectly because bees build their honeycombs that way, and for the same mathematical reason: maximum area with minimum material.
