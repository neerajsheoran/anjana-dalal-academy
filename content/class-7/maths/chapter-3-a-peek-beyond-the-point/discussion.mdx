## Why We Need Decimals

**Kiran:** Papa, I was measuring a screw with a ruler. It is longer than 2 cm but shorter than 3 cm. How do I write its length?

**Ravi:** That is exactly why decimals exist! When whole numbers are not precise enough, we go "beyond the point." If the space between 2 and 3 is divided into 10 equal parts, each part is one-tenth of a centimetre. If the screw reaches the 7th mark, its length is 2.7 cm.

**Kiran:** So we divide the gap between two whole numbers into 10 smaller pieces?

**Ravi:** Yes, because our number system is based on 10. And if tenths are still not precise enough, we divide each tenth into 10 parts to get hundredths. A body temperature of 36.9 degrees Celsius, a race time of 10.85 seconds, a price of Rs 49.50 -- all use decimals.

**Kiran:** The word "decimal" itself must be related to the number 10?

**Ravi:** Exactly. It comes from the Latin "decem" meaning ten, which is related to the Sanskrit word "dasha." The connection runs through many Indian languages too.

## Adding and Subtracting Decimals

**Kiran:** Papa, Sonu's lower arm is 2.7 units and upper arm is 3.6 units. How do I add them?

**Ravi:** Just add the units and tenths separately. Units: 2 + 3 = 5. Tenths: 7 + 6 = 13 tenths, which is 1 unit and 3 tenths. So the total is 5 + 1.3 = 6.3 units.

**Kiran:** And what if I need to subtract, like 12.4 minus 6.7?

**Ravi:** You cannot subtract 7 tenths from 4 tenths directly, so you borrow. Think of it like borrowing in whole-number subtraction. Take 1 unit from 12, making it 11 units and 14 tenths. Now 14 minus 7 is 7 tenths, and 11 minus 6 is 5 units. The answer is 5.7.

**Kiran:** So decimal arithmetic works just like whole-number arithmetic, but with the decimal point to keep track?

**Ravi:** Precisely. Align the decimal points, work column by column, carry over when needed. The same rules apply. This is why understanding place value is so important -- it makes everything from hundredths to crores follow the same pattern.

## The Decimal Point Changes Everything

**Kiran:** Papa, are 705, 70.5, and 7.05 related? They use the same digits.

**Ravi:** They look similar but are very different numbers. 705 means 7 hundreds and 5 ones. 70.5 means 7 tens and 5 tenths. 7.05 means 7 units and 5 hundredths. The decimal point completely changes what each digit represents.

**Kiran:** It is like how a comma changes meaning in a sentence!

**Ravi:** Good analogy. Now, here is a tricky question. Is 3.5 the same as 3.50?

**Kiran:** I think so... adding a zero at the end should not change the value?

**Ravi:** Correct! 3.5 and 3.50 and 3.500 are all equivalent. Adding trailing zeros after the last decimal digit does not change the value. But 3.5 is NOT the same as 3.05. The zero between the decimal point and the 5 shifts the 5 from the tenths place to the hundredths place.

## Comparing Decimals

**Kiran:** Which is bigger, 5.43 or 5.8? I am confused because 43 seems bigger than 8.

**Ravi:** That is a common trap! Compare decimal numbers step by step. First, the whole number parts -- both are 5, so equal. Next, compare the tenths: 4 tenths versus 8 tenths. Since 4 is less than 8, we know 5.43 is less than 5.8 without even looking at hundredths.

**Kiran:** So I should never compare the digits after the decimal point as if they were a whole number?

**Ravi:** Never. Always compare place by place from left to right. Think of it like comparing cricket scores -- you look at hundreds first, then tens, then ones. Same logic with decimals: units, then tenths, then hundredths.

**Kiran:** That makes sense. So 3.7 is greater than 3.07 because 7 tenths beats 0 tenths?

**Ravi:** Perfect reasoning. This kind of systematic comparison is exactly what examiners test, so practise it well.

## Decimals in Everyday Life

**Kiran:** Where do I actually encounter decimals outside the textbook?

**Ravi:** Everywhere! When you check the temperature -- normal body temperature is about 36.9 degrees. When Amma checks the price of petrol -- it might say Rs 103.47 per litre. When you time a race in school sports day -- the winner might run in 12.35 seconds.

**Kiran:** And in cricket, batting averages are always in decimals!

**Ravi:** Right. Virat Kohli's Test average of 48.72 means that across all innings, he averaged about 48.72 runs per innings. That one number summarises hundreds of innings. Decimals give us precision that whole numbers cannot. Even our currency uses decimals -- Rs 49.50 means 49 rupees and 50 paise, where 50 paise is 0.50 of a rupee.

**Kiran:** So mastering decimals is really about being precise in the real world.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Whether you become an engineer, a doctor, or a shopkeeper, decimals will be part of your daily life.
