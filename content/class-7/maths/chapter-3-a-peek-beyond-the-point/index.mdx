When we measure objects carefully, whole numbers are often not enough. A screw may be slightly longer than 2 cm but shorter than 3 cm. To express such measurements, we need numbers that go "beyond the point" -- decimal numbers. In this chapter, we will explore tenths, hundredths, and the decimal place value system.

## The Need for Smaller Units

Imagine measuring two screws with a ruler. One screw is between 2 cm and 3 cm. To find the exact length, we need a scale where the space between 2 and 3 is divided into **10 equal parts**. Each small part is **one-tenth** of a centimetre.

![Ruler zoomed in between 2 cm and 3 cm divided into 10 equal parts showing tenths](ruler-tenths-scale.png)

Using this finer scale, the screw measures **2 and 7 tenths cm**, written as 2 7/10 cm.

> **Why divide into 10 parts?** Because our number system is based on 10 (the decimal system). This makes calculations easier and aligns perfectly with place value.

## A Tenth Part

When we divide one unit into **10 equal parts**, each part is called **one-tenth**, written as 1/10.

**Key relationships:**
- 10 one-tenths = 1 unit
- 1/10 + 1/10 + 1/10 + ... (10 times) = 10 x 1/10 = 1

So a pencil that is 3 and 4/10 units long can also be described as **34 one-tenths** units:
- 34 x 1/10 = 34/10 = 30/10 + 4/10 = 3 + 4/10 = 3 4/10

### Reading Numbers with Tenths

| Number | How to Read It |
|--------|---------------|
| 4 1/10 | "four and one-tenth" |
| 4/10 | "four-tenths" |
| 41/10 | "forty-one tenths" |
| 41 1/10 | "forty-one and one-tenth" |

### Adding with Tenths

To find the total length of Sonu's arm (lower arm = 2 7/10 units, upper arm = 3 6/10 units):

**Method 1:** Add units and tenths separately
- Units: 2 + 3 = 5
- Tenths: 7/10 + 6/10 = 13/10 = 1 unit and 3/10
- Total: 5 + 1 + 3/10 = **6 3/10 units**

**Method 2:** Convert everything to tenths
- 27/10 + 36/10 = 63/10 = 6 3/10 units

### Subtracting with Tenths

To find the difference: 12 4/10 - 6 7/10

We cannot subtract 7 tenths from 4 tenths directly. So we borrow 1 unit (= 10 tenths) from 12:
- 12 4/10 becomes 11 14/10
- 11 14/10 - 6 7/10 = **5 7/10**

This is just like borrowing in whole-number subtraction!

## A Hundredth Part

Sometimes even tenths are not precise enough. If we fold a sheet of paper that is 8 9/10 units long in half, the folded length falls between 4 4/10 and 4 5/10. To measure it precisely, we split each tenth into **10 even smaller parts**.

Each of these smaller parts is **one-hundredth** of a unit, written as 1/100.

**Key relationships:**
- 10 one-hundredths = 1 one-tenth
- 100 one-hundredths = 1 unit

So the folded paper length can be expressed as **4 units, 4 tenths, and 5 hundredths**, or equivalently **4 and 45 hundredths**.

### Writing Numbers with Hundredths

A single length can be expressed in three equivalent ways:

| Way | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Units, tenths, and hundredths | 1 unit, 1 tenth, 4 hundredths |
| Units and hundredths | 1 and 14 hundredths |
| All hundredths | 114 hundredths |

### Adding with Hundredths

To add 15 3/10 4/100 and 2 6/10 8/100:

**Method 1:** Add units, tenths, and hundredths separately
- Units: 15 + 2 = 17
- Tenths: 3/10 + 6/10 = 9/10
- Hundredths: 4/100 + 8/100 = 12/100 = 1/10 + 2/100
- Carry the extra tenth: 9/10 + 1/10 = 10/10 = 1 unit
- Total: 17 + 1 + 0/10 + 2/100 = **18 2/100**

**Method 2:** Convert everything to hundredths
- 1534/100 + 268/100 = 1802/100 = **18 2/100**

> **Notice the similarity:** Adding tenths and hundredths works exactly like adding whole numbers. When hundredths add up to 10 or more, they "carry over" to tenths, just as ones carry over to tens!

### Subtracting with Hundredths

To find 25 9/10 - 6 4/10 7/100:

First, convert 9/10 to 8/10 and 10/100 (borrow from tenths):
- 25 8/10 10/100 - 6 4/10 7/100 = **19 4/10 3/100**

## Decimal Place Value

### Why Always Split into 10?

In the Indian place value system, each place is **10 times** the one to its right:
- 10 ones = 1 ten
- 10 tens = 1 hundred
- 10 hundreds = 1 thousand

We extend this pattern to the **right** of the ones place:
- 1 one / 10 = 1 tenth
- 1 tenth / 10 = 1 hundredth
- 1 hundredth / 10 = 1 thousandth

```
... 1000  100  10  1  .  1/10  1/100  1/1000 ...
      |    |   |  |     |      |       |
     x10  x10 x10  .   /10   /10     /10
```

> **Did You Know?** The word "decimal" comes from the Latin word "decem" meaning ten. This is related to the Sanskrit word "dasha" meaning ten, with similar words in many Indian languages including Hindi, Marathi, Gujarati, Odia, and Assamese.

![Place value table extending from thousands to hundredths with the decimal point highlighted](decimal-place-value-table.png)

### The Decimal Point

To distinguish between whole numbers and fractional parts, we use a **decimal point** (a dot).

| Quantity | Decimal Notation |
|----------|-----------------|
| 7 hundreds and 5 ones | 705 |
| 7 tens and 5 tenths | 70.5 |
| 7 units and 5 hundredths | 7.05 |

The decimal point sits between the **units place** and the **tenths place**.

### Place Value Table

| Hundreds | Tens | Units | . | Tenths | Hundredths |
|----------|------|-------|---|--------|------------|
| 7 | 0 | 5 | | | |
| | 7 | 0 | . | 5 | |
| | | 7 | . | 0 | 5 |

Each of these (705, 70.5, and 7.05) is a very different number, even though they use the same digits!

### Reading Decimal Numbers

- **3.8** is read as "three point eight" or "three and eight-tenths"
- **15.47** is read as "fifteen point four seven" or "fifteen and forty-seven hundredths"
- **0.05** is read as "zero point zero five" or "five hundredths"

### Equivalent Decimals

Adding zeros to the right of the last decimal digit does not change the value:
- 3.5 = 3.50 = 3.500
- 0.7 = 0.70
- 12.1 = 12.10

But adding zeros between the decimal point and the digits **does** change the value:
- 3.5 is NOT the same as 3.05

## Operations with Decimals

### Addition of Decimals

To add decimal numbers, align the decimal points and add column by column, just as with whole numbers.

**Example:** 13.45 + 2.38

```
  13.45
+  2.38
-------
  15.83
```

- Hundredths: 5 + 8 = 13 (write 3, carry 1)
- Tenths: 4 + 3 + 1 = 8
- Units: 3 + 2 = 5
- Tens: 1

### Subtraction of Decimals

Align the decimal points and subtract, borrowing when needed.

**Example:** 15.34 - 2.68

```
  15.34
-  2.68
-------
  12.66
```

- Hundredths: 4 - 8 (borrow: 14 - 8 = 6)
- Tenths: 2 - 6 (borrow: 12 - 6 = 6)
- Units: 4 - 2 = 2
- Tens: 1

## Comparing Decimals

To compare decimal numbers:
1. First compare the whole number parts
2. If equal, compare the tenths
3. If still equal, compare the hundredths
4. Continue until you find a difference

**Examples:**
- 3.7 > 3.07 (because 7 tenths > 0 tenths)
- 5.43 &lt; 5.8 (because 4 tenths &lt; 8 tenths)
- 12.50 = 12.5 (trailing zeros do not change the value)

## Real-Life Uses of Decimals

Decimals appear everywhere in daily life:
- **Temperature:** A normal body temperature is about 36.9 degrees Celsius
- **Money:** A price tag might read Rs 49.50
- **Sports:** An athlete's time in a race might be 10.85 seconds
- **Measurement:** The length of a pencil might be 15.3 cm

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Decimal** | A number that uses a decimal point to show values less than one (from Latin "decem" meaning ten) |
| **Decimal point** | The dot (.) that separates the whole number part from the fractional part |
| **Tenth** | One part when a unit is divided into 10 equal parts (1/10 = 0.1) |
| **Hundredth** | One part when a unit is divided into 100 equal parts (1/100 = 0.01) |
| **Thousandth** | One part when a unit is divided into 1000 equal parts (1/1000 = 0.001) |
| **Place value** | The value of a digit based on its position in the number |
| **Equivalent decimals** | Decimals that represent the same value, like 0.5 and 0.50 |
