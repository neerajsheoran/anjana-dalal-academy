## The Triangle Inequality

**Kiran:** Papa, I tried to make a triangle with sides 3 cm, 4 cm, and 8 cm using my compass, but the arcs would not meet. What went wrong?

**Ravi:** Nothing went wrong with your technique -- those three lengths simply cannot form a triangle. The two shorter sides, 3 and 4, add up to only 7. But the longest side is 8. Since 7 is less than 8, the two arcs from the endpoints of the 8 cm base can never reach each other.

**Kiran:** So there is a rule about which lengths work?

**Ravi:** Yes, the triangle inequality. The longest side must be strictly less than the sum of the other two sides. Think of it as the shortest path principle -- the direct route between two points is always shorter than going through a third point.

**Kiran:** Let me check: 4, 5, and 8. The longest is 8. Is 8 less than 4 + 5 = 9? Yes! So this triangle exists.

**Ravi:** Correct. And for 10, 20, 35? The longest is 35, and 10 + 20 = 30. Since 35 is not less than 30, no triangle. This quick check saves time in exams.

## Constructing Triangles

**Kiran:** How do I construct a triangle when I know all three sides?

**Ravi:** Step by step. For sides 4, 5, and 6 cm: draw the base AB = 4 cm. Open your compass to 5 cm, place it at A, and draw an arc above the line. Then open it to 6 cm, place at B, and draw another arc. The intersection point C gives you the third vertex.

**Kiran:** Why does this work?

**Ravi:** Point C is exactly 5 cm from A (it lies on the first arc) and exactly 6 cm from B (it lies on the second arc). Combined with AB = 4 cm, all three sides are correct. The triangle inequality guarantees the arcs will intersect.

**Kiran:** And an equilateral triangle is even simpler -- all three arcs have the same radius?

**Ravi:** Exactly. For a 4 cm equilateral triangle, draw AB = 4 cm, then arcs of 4 cm from both A and B. The intersection gives the third vertex. Every measurement is the same, which is why equilateral triangles are the most symmetric.

## The Angle Sum Property

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher said the three angles of any triangle always add up to 180 degrees. Is that really true for every triangle?

**Ravi:** Always. You can verify it yourself. Cut out a paper triangle, tear off the three corners, and place them together. They form a straight line -- which is exactly 180 degrees.

**Kiran:** But that is just one triangle. How do we know it works for all triangles?

**Ravi:** There is a beautiful proof using parallel lines. Draw a line through one vertex parallel to the opposite side. The alternate angles with the two other sides give you the three angles of the triangle sitting together on a straight line. Since a straight angle is 180 degrees, the three angles must sum to 180 degrees.

**Kiran:** So it connects back to what we learned about parallel lines and alternate angles!

**Ravi:** Yes, that is the beauty of mathematics. Different topics are deeply connected. Now, can you figure out why a triangle cannot have two right angles?

**Kiran:** If two angles are 90 degrees each, that is already 180 degrees, leaving 0 for the third angle. That is impossible!

**Ravi:** Perfect reasoning. Same logic shows a triangle cannot have two obtuse angles.

## Types of Triangles

**Kiran:** How do we classify triangles?

**Ravi:** Two ways -- by sides and by angles. By sides: equilateral has all three equal, isosceles has exactly two equal, and scalene has all different. By angles: acute has all angles less than 90 degrees, right has one angle of exactly 90 degrees, and obtuse has one angle greater than 90 degrees.

**Kiran:** In an equilateral triangle, if all angles are equal and they sum to 180 degrees, each must be 60 degrees?

**Ravi:** Correct! And in an isosceles triangle with a 40-degree angle at the top, the two base angles are equal. Let each be x. Then 40 + 2x = 180, so x = 70 degrees.

**Kiran:** What about the exterior angle? My teacher said it equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

**Ravi:** Right. If an exterior angle is at vertex C, and the interior angles at A and B are 50 and 60 degrees, the interior angle at C is 70 degrees, and the exterior angle is 180 - 70 = 110 degrees. But 50 + 60 = 110 as well. This exterior angle property is a direct consequence of the angle sum property, and it appears frequently in exam problems.

## When Can a Triangle Exist?

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I quickly check if three given lengths form a triangle?

**Ravi:** Find the longest length and check if it is less than the sum of the other two. That is the only check you need. Let me give you some practice.

**Kiran:** Okay. How about 2, 2, 5?

**Ravi:** Longest is 5. Sum of others: 2 + 2 = 4. Is 5 less than 4? No. So no triangle.

**Kiran:** And 1, 100, 100?

**Ravi:** Longest is 100. Sum of others: 1 + 100 = 101. Is 100 less than 101? Yes. So a triangle exists. It would be a very thin, elongated isosceles triangle, but it exists!

**Kiran:** This is like a test I can apply instantly. I just compare the longest side with the sum of the shorter two.

**Ravi:** Exactly. In competitive exams, this saves a lot of time. And remember, if the sum equals the longest side exactly, the three points are collinear -- they lie on a straight line and do not form a triangle.
