## Multiplying Decimals

**Kiran:** Papa, Arshad bought 5 pens at Rs 9.5 each. How do I calculate the total?

**Ravi:** Multiply 5 times 9.5. Here is the quick rule: ignore the decimal, multiply as whole numbers (5 times 95 = 475), then count the total decimal places in the original numbers (9.5 has 1 decimal place), and place the decimal accordingly. So 475 becomes 47.5. The total is Rs 47.5.

**Kiran:** What about multiplying two decimals, like 12.5 times 7.5?

**Ravi:** Same approach. 125 times 75 = 9375. Total decimal places: 1 + 1 = 2. Place the decimal: 93.75 km. You can verify using fractions: 125/10 times 75/10 = 9375/100 = 93.75.

**Kiran:** So the rule is: multiply as whole numbers, then count total decimal places?

**Ravi:** Exactly. For 5.96 times 24.8: 596 times 248 = 147,808. Total decimal places: 2 + 1 = 3. Result: 147.808. The fraction method gives the same answer but is slower. This shortcut is essential for exam speed.

## When Multiplying Makes Things Smaller

**Kiran:** Papa, I thought multiplication always makes numbers bigger. But 0.75 times 0.4 = 0.3, which is smaller than both!

**Ravi:** That is because multiplying by a number less than 1 shrinks the result. Think of it as taking a part of something. 0.4 of 0.75 is less than 0.75, just like 2/5 of 3/4 is less than 3/4.

**Kiran:** So when both numbers are between 0 and 1, the product is smaller than both?

**Ravi:** Correct. When both are greater than 1, the product is larger than both. When one is greater than 1 and the other less than 1, the product falls between them. Knowing this helps you verify your answers.

**Kiran:** Like a sanity check. If I multiply 0.3 by 0.5 and get 15, I know something is wrong because 15 is much bigger than both numbers.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The correct answer is 0.15. These checks prevent careless decimal-point errors, which are among the most common mistakes in exams.

## Dividing Decimals

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I divide 117.5 by 5?

**Ravi:** Convert to a fraction: 1175/10 divided by 5 = 1175/50. Using long division, 1175 divided by 50 = 23.5. Each person gets Rs 23.5.

**Kiran:** What about dividing by a decimal, like 6 divided by 0.75?

**Ravi:** The trick is to make the divisor a whole number. Multiply both numbers by 100: 600 divided by 75 = 8. So you can cut 8 pieces of 0.75 m from 6 m.

**Kiran:** And for decimal divided by decimal?

**Ravi:** Same idea. For 9.36 divided by 1.2, multiply both by 10: 93.6 divided by 12 = 7.8. The key is always to eliminate the decimal from the divisor first, then divide normally.

**Kiran:** That is much simpler than I expected!

**Ravi:** It works because multiplying both dividend and divisor by the same number does not change the quotient. This is the same principle as equivalent fractions -- 6/3 equals 12/6 equals 24/12.

## Using Known Products

**Kiran:** Papa, if I know that 18 times 12 = 216, can I figure out 1.8 times 1.2 without calculating from scratch?

**Ravi:** Absolutely! 1.8 has 1 decimal place and 1.2 has 1 decimal place, so the product has 2 decimal places. 216 with 2 decimal places is 2.16. So 1.8 times 1.2 = 2.16.

**Kiran:** And 0.18 times 0.12?

**Ravi:** 2 + 2 = 4 decimal places. 216 with 4 decimal places is 0.0216. Once you know one product, you can derive an entire family of related products.

**Kiran:** This saves so much time! I can build a whole table from one multiplication.

**Ravi:** This is a powerful exam strategy. When you see related decimal multiplications, compute the whole number version once and adjust the decimal point for each variant.

## Practical Applications

**Kiran:** Where do decimal calculations come up in real life?

**Ravi:** Everywhere. A rupee coin is 1.45 mm thick. A stack of 36 coins is 36 times 1.45 = 52.2 mm or 5.22 cm. Your weekly walking distance to school at 0.827 km each way for 6 days is 0.827 times 2 times 6 = 9.924 km.

**Kiran:** What about shopping?

**Ravi:** Meenu buys 4 notebooks at Rs 15.50 each and 3 erasers at Rs 2.75 each. Total = 4 times 15.50 + 3 times 2.75 = 62.00 + 8.25 = Rs 70.25. And if 2.25 kg of oranges costs Rs 56.50 per kg, the total is 2.25 times 56.50 = Rs 127.125.

**Kiran:** Decimal operations are basically how all money calculations work!

**Ravi:** Exactly. From billing to banking to budgeting, decimals are the language of commerce. Mastering these operations is not just about passing exams -- it is about being financially literate.
