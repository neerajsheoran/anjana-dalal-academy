## Why Does Negative Times Negative Give Positive?

**Kiran:** Papa, I understand that positive times negative gives negative. Like 4 times -2 is -8. But why is -4 times -2 equal to positive 8? That seems strange.

**Ravi:** Let me show you the pattern. Start with 4 times -3 = -12. Now decrease the first number by 1 each time: 3 times -3 = -9, 2 times -3 = -6, 1 times -3 = -3, 0 times -3 = 0. Notice the products increase by 3 each step.

**Kiran:** So continuing the pattern: -1 times -3 = 3, -2 times -3 = 6. The pattern forces negative times negative to be positive!

**Ravi:** Exactly. Mathematics must be consistent. The patterns that work for positive numbers must extend naturally to negative numbers. If we made negative times negative equal to negative, the pattern would break, and properties like the distributive property would fail.

**Kiran:** So it is not just a rule to memorise -- the pattern demands it?

**Ravi:** Yes. The sign rules are simple: same signs give positive, different signs give negative. And the magnitude is always the product of the magnitudes. If you know 123 times 456 = 56,088, then -123 times -456 is also 56,088, while -123 times 456 = -56,088.

## Division of Integers

**Kiran:** If multiplication has sign rules, does division follow the same rules?

**Ravi:** Yes, because division is the inverse of multiplication. If -4 times 3 = -12, then -12 divided by -4 = 3, and -12 divided by 3 = -4. Same signs give positive quotients, different signs give negative quotients.

**Kiran:** So -20 divided by -5 = positive 4, and 18 divided by -6 = -3?

**Ravi:** Correct. But there is one important difference from multiplication. Division is NOT commutative. 12 divided by 4 = 3, but 4 divided by 12 is not 3. And division by zero is always undefined -- there is no number that, multiplied by zero, gives a non-zero result.

**Kiran:** Why is division by zero undefined and not just zero?

**Ravi:** If 5 divided by 0 equalled some number x, then x times 0 would need to equal 5. But anything times 0 is 0, never 5. So no such x exists. This is why calculators show an error when you try to divide by zero.

## Properties and Patterns

**Kiran:** Papa, does multiplication of integers have the same properties as whole number multiplication?

**Ravi:** All three properties carry over. Commutative: a times b = b times a. For example, 3 times -5 = -15 and -5 times 3 = -15. Associative: (a times b) times c = a times (b times c). And distributive: a times (b + c) = a times b + a times c.

**Kiran:** What about multiplying by 0 and 1?

**Ravi:** Any integer times 0 is 0. Any integer times 1 stays the same. And here is a useful one -- any integer times -1 gives its additive inverse. So -1 times 7 = -7, and -1 times -7 = 7. Multiplying by -1 flips the sign.

**Kiran:** That is like the sign-change rule for brackets! When we multiply -(a + b), we get -a - b, which is -1 times (a + b) = -1 times a + -1 times b.

**Ravi:** Excellent connection! The distributive property with -1 is exactly what explains the bracket sign-flip rule from the algebra chapter. Everything is connected.

## Brahmagupta's Legacy

**Kiran:** Papa, who first figured out these rules for negative numbers?

**Ravi:** The great Indian mathematician Brahmagupta, who lived around 598 to 668 CE. In his work Brahmasphutasiddhanta from 628 CE, he systematically described rules for arithmetic with negative numbers and zero -- nearly 1400 years ago!

**Kiran:** He worked with negative numbers that long ago? I thought negative numbers were confusing even for adults!

**Ravi:** Brahmagupta called positive numbers "dhana" (fortune) and negative numbers "rina" (debt). He stated that a debt subtracted from zero gives a fortune -- meaning 0 minus a negative is positive. His rules are exactly what we use today.

**Kiran:** India really contributed a lot to mathematics!

**Ravi:** Absolutely. The concept of zero, the decimal place value system, and the rules for negative number arithmetic all trace back to Indian mathematicians. When you use these rules, you are using ideas perfected in India over a thousand years ago.

## Real-World Applications

**Kiran:** Where do negative numbers show up in real life?

**Ravi:** Temperature is a great example. If the temperature drops by 3 degrees every hour, after 5 hours the total change is 5 times -3 = -15 degrees. Debt is another. If you owe Rs 50 to each of 4 friends, your total debt is 4 times -50 = -Rs 200.

**Kiran:** What about a submarine?

**Ravi:** If a submarine descends 8 metres per minute, after 6 minutes its position is 6 times -8 = -48 metres -- meaning 48 metres below the surface. The negative sign naturally represents "below."

**Kiran:** So integers with their sign rules model real-world quantities that have direction -- like up and down, profit and loss, above and below?

**Ravi:** Precisely. That is why integers are so powerful. They extend our number system to handle situations where direction or opposition matters. Positive and negative are not just abstract concepts -- they represent real-world opposing quantities.
