## From Expressions to Equations

**Kiran:** Papa, what is the difference between an expression and an equation?

**Ravi:** An expression like 3x + 5 is just a mathematical phrase -- it has no equals sign. An equation like 3x + 5 = 20 says two things are equal. The equation makes a claim that can be true or false depending on the value of x.

**Kiran:** So an expression takes different values, but an equation has a specific solution?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The solution is the value of x that makes both sides equal. For 3x + 5 = 20, we need to find which x makes the left side equal to 20. Think of it like a weighing scale -- both sides must balance.

**Kiran:** Like finding the weight of a bag of rice by adding known weights to the other side until it balances?

**Ravi:** Perfect analogy. That is the fundamental idea of equations -- two sides in perfect balance.

---

## The Balance Principle

**Kiran:** How do I solve equations systematically instead of guessing?

**Ravi:** Use the balance principle. Whatever you do to one side, do the same to the other. If x - 3 = 10, add 3 to both sides: x = 13. If y + 12 = 20, subtract 12 from both sides: y = 8.

**Kiran:** And for multiplication and division?

**Ravi:** If 5x = 30, divide both sides by 5: x = 6. If n/4 = 7, multiply both sides by 4: n = 28. The key is to undo whatever has been done to the unknown. Subtraction undoes addition. Division undoes multiplication.

**Kiran:** It is like peeling layers off an onion to get to the unknown at the centre!

**Ravi:** Great analogy! Each step removes one operation until the unknown stands alone. And always check your answer by substituting back. If 5 times 6 = 30, we know x = 6 is correct.

---

## Two-Step Equations

**Kiran:** What about equations like 5x - 4 = 16?

**Ravi:** Two steps. First, undo the subtraction: add 4 to both sides, giving 5x = 20. Second, undo the multiplication: divide by 5, giving x = 4. Check: 5 times 4 minus 4 = 16. Correct!

**Kiran:** Let me try 3m + 7 = 22. Subtract 7: 3m = 15. Divide by 3: m = 5. Check: 3 times 5 + 7 = 22. Yes!

**Ravi:** Perfect. The general strategy is: first deal with addition or subtraction, then deal with multiplication or division. This two-step approach handles most of the equations you will encounter.

**Kiran:** It is like a recipe -- always follow the same steps in the same order.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Mathematicians call this an algorithm -- a systematic procedure that always produces the right answer when followed correctly.

## Variables on Both Sides

**Kiran:** Papa, what if the unknown appears on both sides, like 3x + 5 = 2x + 12?

**Ravi:** Collect all x terms on one side. Subtract 2x from both sides: x + 5 = 12. Then subtract 5: x = 7. Check: left side = 3 times 7 + 5 = 26. Right side = 2 times 7 + 12 = 26. Both equal!

**Kiran:** And for 5n - 3 = 3n + 7?

**Ravi:** Subtract 3n from both sides: 2n - 3 = 7. Add 3: 2n = 10. Divide by 2: n = 5. The strategy is always the same -- move unknowns to one side and constants to the other.

**Kiran:** This is like sorting clothes -- put all the similar items together.

**Ravi:** Good analogy. Variables go on one side, numbers on the other, and then you simplify. This technique works for increasingly complex equations as you progress through school.

## Word Problems as Equations

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I turn a word problem into an equation?

**Ravi:** Let us practice. Aadhira's age is 4 more than twice Bala's age. If Aadhira is 18, how old is Bala?

**Kiran:** Let Bala's age be b. "Twice Bala's age" is 2b. "4 more than" means add 4. So Aadhira's age = 2b + 4 = 18.

**Ravi:** Now solve it.

**Kiran:** 2b + 4 = 18. Subtract 4: 2b = 14. Divide by 2: b = 7. Bala is 7 years old!

**Ravi:** Excellent. Here is another. The sum of three consecutive numbers is 42.

**Kiran:** Let the smallest be x. Then the numbers are x, x + 1, x + 2. So x + x + 1 + x + 2 = 42. That is 3x + 3 = 42. So 3x = 39, x = 13. The numbers are 13, 14, 15!

**Ravi:** The word "algebra" comes from the Arabic "al-jabr" in a book by al-Khwarizmi. And Indian mathematicians Brahmagupta and Aryabhata developed methods for solving equations centuries earlier. When you solve equations, you are using techniques perfected over more than a thousand years.
