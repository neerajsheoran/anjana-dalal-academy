Numbers are everywhere around us -- from the population of cities to the distance between the Earth and the Sun. In this chapter, we will explore large numbers, learn how to read and write them in both the Indian and International systems, and develop a sense of just how big (or small!) these numbers really are.

## A Lakh Varieties!

Did you know that India once had about **one lakh** varieties of rice? Farmers across the country used to preserve and grow different seed varieties. Today, seed banks are working to collect and protect these indigenous varieties.

But how big is one lakh? Let us build up to it step by step:

| Number | In Words |
|--------|----------|
| 999 | Largest 3-digit number |
| 1,000 | Smallest 4-digit number (999 + 1) |
| 9,999 | Largest 4-digit number |
| 10,000 | Smallest 5-digit number (9,999 + 1) |
| 99,999 | Largest 5-digit number |
| **1,00,000** | **Smallest 6-digit number -- One Lakh** |

If you ate one new variety of rice every single day, it would take you about **274 years** to try all one lakh varieties -- far longer than any human lifetime!

### Is One Lakh Big or Small?

Whether a lakh is "big" or "small" depends on the context:

**A lakh feels large when...**
- If 1 lakh people stood shoulder to shoulder, the line would stretch about **38 kilometres**.
- Living 1 lakh days would mean living for **274 years**.

**A lakh feels small when...**
- The Narendra Modi Stadium in Ahmedabad seats more than 1 lakh people in one place.
- Most humans have between 80,000 and 1,20,000 hairs on their head -- about a lakh hairs in a tiny space!
- Some species of fish lay nearly one lakh eggs at once.

> **Think About It:** Whether a number is "large" or "small" always depends on what you are measuring. Context matters!

## Reading and Writing Numbers

In the **Indian place value system**, we use commas to group digits in a **3-2-2-2...** pattern from right to left. This helps us read large numbers easily.

For example:
- **12,78,830** is read as *twelve lakh seventy-eight thousand eight hundred thirty*
- **15,75,000** is read as *fifteen lakh seventy-five thousand*

### The Land of Tens

Every large number can be built from smaller units. Imagine special calculators with only one button each:

| Calculator | Button | Presses to make 1,00,000 |
|------------|--------|--------------------------|
| Thoughtful Thousands | +1,000 | 100 presses |
| Handy Hundreds | +100 | 1,000 presses |
| Tedious Tens | +10 | 10,000 presses |

This shows us that:
- **100 thousands** make a lakh
- **1,000 hundreds** make a lakh
- **10,000 tens** make a lakh

### Place Value Notation

When we write a number using the fewest possible button clicks, we naturally arrive at **place value notation**. For example:

**5,072** = (5 x 1,000) + (0 x 100) + (7 x 10) + (2 x 1)

The minimum number of button clicks for any number equals the **sum of its digits**.

## Of Crores and Crores!

What happens when we press +10,00,000 ten times? We get **1,00,00,000** -- that is **one crore** (1 followed by 7 zeroes).

### Indian System vs International (American) System

| Indian System | International System |
|---------------|---------------------|
| 1,000 -- One thousand | 1,000 -- One thousand |
| 10,000 -- Ten thousand | 10,000 -- Ten thousand |
| 1,00,000 -- One lakh | 100,000 -- Hundred thousand |
| 10,00,000 -- Ten lakhs | 1,000,000 -- One million |
| 1,00,00,000 -- One crore | 10,000,000 -- Ten million |
| 10,00,00,000 -- Ten crores | 100,000,000 -- Hundred million |
| 1,00,00,00,000 -- One arab | 1,000,000,000 -- One billion |

**Key differences:**
- In the **Indian system**, commas are placed in a **3-2-2-2** pattern from right to left (thousands, lakhs, crores...).
- In the **International system**, commas are placed in a **3-3-3** pattern from right to left (thousands, millions, billions...).

**Important relationships:**
- 1 lakh = 1 followed by 5 zeroes
- 1 crore = 1 followed by 7 zeroes
- 1 million = 10 lakhs
- 1 billion = 100 crores = 1 arab

> **Did You Know?** The words *lakh* and *crore* come from the Sanskrit words *laksha* and *koti*. The Indian system of naming numbers is also used in Nepal, Sri Lanka, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Bhutan, Maldives, Afghanistan, and Myanmar.

### Reading a Large Number in Both Systems

The number **9,87,65,01,234** can be read as:
- **Indian System:** 987 crore 65 lakh 1 thousand 234
- **International System:** 9 billion 876 million 501 thousand 234

## Exact and Approximate Values

In everyday life, we do not always need exact numbers. An **approximation** (or rounded value) is often good enough.

For example, if a town has a population of 76,068, we can simply say *"about 75,000"* to give a general idea.

### Rounding Up and Rounding Down

| Type | Meaning | When to Use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Rounding up** | The approximate number is more than the actual | When you want to be safe (e.g., ordering sweets for a school of 732 people -- order 750) |
| **Rounding down** | The approximate number is less than the actual | When you want a conservative estimate (e.g., a shopkeeper says an item costing Rs 470 costs "about Rs 450") |

### Nearest Neighbours

For large numbers, it helps to know the nearest thousand, lakh, or crore. For example, for the number **6,72,85,183**:

| Nearest... | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Thousand | 6,72,85,000 |
| Ten thousand | 6,72,90,000 |
| Lakh | 6,73,00,000 |
| Ten lakh | 6,70,00,000 |
| Crore | 7,00,00,000 |

### Estimation in Action

When adding or subtracting large numbers, estimation helps us check if our answer is reasonable.

For example, to estimate **4,63,128 + 4,19,682**:
- Both numbers are close to 4,50,000
- So the sum is close to 9,00,000
- The exact answer is **8,82,810**

## Patterns in Products

### A Multiplication Shortcut

Multiplying by 5 is the same as dividing by 2 and multiplying by 10:

**116 x 5** = (116 / 2) x 10 = 58 x 10 = **580**

Similarly, multiplying by 25 is the same as dividing by 4 and multiplying by 100:

**824 x 25** = (824 / 4) x 100 = 206 x 100 = **20,600**

And multiplying by 125 is the same as dividing by 8 and multiplying by 1,000:

**72 x 125** = (72 / 8) x 1,000 = 9 x 1,000 = **9,000**

### How Long is the Product?

There is a neat relationship between the number of digits in two numbers and the number of digits in their product:

| Multiplication | Product Digits |
|---------------|----------------|
| 1-digit x 1-digit | 1 or 2 digits |
| 2-digit x 2-digit | 3 or 4 digits |
| 3-digit x 3-digit | 5 or 6 digits |
| m-digit x n-digit | (m + n - 1) or (m + n) digits |

> **Rule of Thumb:** The product of an m-digit number and an n-digit number has either **(m + n - 1)** or **(m + n)** digits.

## Fascinating Facts About Large Numbers

Large numbers appear in many fascinating real-world contexts:

- **Purandaradasa**, the 15th-century composer who systematised Carnatic music teaching, is said to have composed about **4,75,000** kirtanas (1,250 x 380).
- The distance between the **Earth and the Sun** is approximately **14,70,00,000 km** (2,100 x 70,000).
- The **Amazon River** discharges about **40,00,00,000 litres** of water into the Atlantic Ocean every second (6,400 x 62,500).
- The longest single train journey in the world runs **9,300 km** from Moscow to Vladivostok in Russia, taking about 7 days. India's longest train route runs from Dibrugarh (Assam) to Kanyakumari (Tamil Nadu), covering 4,219 km.
- An adult **blue whale** can weigh more than **1,50,000 kg** -- its heart alone weighs nearly 700 kg!

## Did You Ever Wonder...?

Large number thinking lets us answer unusual and fun questions:

**"Could the entire population of Mumbai fit into 1 lakh buses?"**
- If each bus holds 50 people, then 1 lakh buses hold 50 lakh people.
- Mumbai's population is over 1 crore 24 lakhs.
- So **no**, 1 lakh buses would not be enough!

**"If I travel 100 km every day, can I reach the Moon in 10 years?"**
- In 10 years: 100 x 365 x 10 = 3,65,000 km.
- Distance to the Moon: 3,84,400 km.
- Almost, but not quite -- you would fall about 19,400 km short!

**"Can you reach the Sun in a lifetime travelling 1,000 km per day?"**
- Distance to the Sun: about 14,70,00,000 km.
- At 1,000 km per day, it would take about **403 years** -- far longer than any lifetime.
