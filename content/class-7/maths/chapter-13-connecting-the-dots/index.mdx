Every day, we encounter claims based on numbers -- "The average rainfall was 37 mm" or "Students spend about 7 hours in school." How do we collect data, organise it, and make sense of it? This chapter introduces the basics of **statistics** -- the science of understanding data.

## Statistical Questions and Statements

A **statistical question** is one that can be answered by collecting data and expects **variability** in the answers. For example:
- "How tall are Class 7 students in our school?" (statistical -- heights will vary)
- "How old are the dogs on this street?" (statistical -- ages will vary)

A non-statistical question has a single, fixed answer:
- "What is 5 + 3?" (not statistical -- the answer is always 8)

A **statistical statement** is a claim or summary about some phenomenon, expressed using numbers, proportions, or predictions. Examples:
- "I take about 15 minutes to cycle from school to home."
- "The population of our village has reduced by 100 in the last decade."
- "On average, a smartphone user checks their phone 58 times a day."

> **Statistics** is the study of collecting, organising, analysing, interpreting, and presenting data.

## Representative Values: The Mean

When comparing groups of numbers, a single **representative value** helps us summarise the data. The most common representative value is the **arithmetic mean** (or simply, the **average**).

### Calculating the Mean

> **Mean = Sum of all values / Number of values**

**Example:** Shubman scored 23, 7, 10, 52, and 18 runs in 5 matches.

Mean = (23 + 7 + 10 + 52 + 18) / 5 = 110 / 5 = **22 runs per match**

Yashasvi scored 26, 53, 2, and 15 runs in 4 matches.

Mean = (26 + 53 + 2 + 15) / 4 = 96 / 4 = **24 runs per match**

Even though Shubman scored more runs in total (110 vs 96), Yashasvi has a **higher average** because he played fewer matches. The mean gives a fair comparison when group sizes differ.

### Mean as Fair Share

The mean can be understood as the value each person gets if everything is **shared equally**.

If 5 friends collect 3, 8, 10, 5, and 4 guavas respectively, the mean is (3 + 8 + 10 + 5 + 4) / 5 = 30 / 5 = **6 guavas each** if shared equally.

> **Indian Heritage:** Ancient Indian mathematicians had terms for the arithmetic mean. **Brahmagupta** (628 CE) used the term *samarajju* (mean measure of a line segment). **Mahaviracharya** (850 CE) used *samkarana* (levelling, equalising). The terminology shows they perceived the mean as the "equalising" value that represents a collection.

## Visualising Data: Dot Plots

A **dot plot** shows each data value as a dot on a number line. It helps us see:
- Where data is **clustered** (many dots together)
- How data is **spread out**
- The **highest** and **lowest** values

**Example:** Monthly onion prices (Rs/kg) in two towns:

Yahapur: 25, 24, 26, 28, 30, 35, 39, 43, 49, 56, 59, 44
Wahapur: 19, 17, 23, 30, 38, 35, 42, 39, 53, 60, 52, 42

On a dot plot, we can see that Wahapur's prices are more spread out (from 17 to 60) compared to Yahapur (from 24 to 59).

> **Note:** Dot plots lose the original sequence of the data (we cannot tell which month had which price), but they make it easy to see patterns, clusters, and spread.

## Ways to Compare Data

When comparing two data sets, we can look at:

| Measure | What It Tells Us |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Sum total** | Overall quantity (but unfair if group sizes differ) |
| **Mean (average)** | Typical value per unit |
| **Maximum** | Highest value |
| **Minimum** | Lowest value |
| **Range** (max - min) | How spread out the data is |

## Outliers and the Median

### When the Mean Misleads

The mean does not always give a good summary. Consider family heights:

Poovizhi's family: 170 cm, 173 cm, 165 cm, 118 cm, 175 cm

Mean = (170 + 173 + 165 + 118 + 175) / 5 = 801 / 5 = **160.2 cm**

But 4 out of 5 family members are taller than 160.2 cm! The very short height of 118 cm (a young child) pulls the average down. This value of 118 is called an **outlier** -- a value that is significantly different from the rest.

### The Median

An alternative to the mean is the **median** -- the middle value when data is arranged in order.

**Finding the median:**
1. Sort the data from smallest to largest
2. If the number of values is **odd**, the median is the middle value
3. If the number of values is **even**, the median is the average of the two middle values

**Example (odd number of values):**
Poovizhi's family heights sorted: 118, 165, 170, 173, 175
Median = **170 cm** (the 3rd value out of 5)

This is much more representative than the mean of 160.2 cm!

**Example (even number of values):**
Yaangba's family heights sorted: 155, 160, 164, 165, 169, 173
Median = (164 + 165) / 2 = **164.5 cm**

### Mean vs Median

| Feature | Mean | Median |
|---------|------|--------|
| Affected by outliers? | **Yes** -- outliers pull it towards them | **No** -- outliers barely affect it |
| When to prefer? | When data has no extreme outliers | When outliers are present |
| When are they close? | When data has no outliers | Same situation |

## Bar Graphs

A **bar graph** uses rectangular bars to represent data. The height (or length) of each bar corresponds to the value it represents.

### Drawing a Bar Graph

1. Draw two perpendicular axes (horizontal and vertical)
2. Label the horizontal axis with categories
3. Mark a suitable **scale** on the vertical axis
4. Draw bars of appropriate height for each category
5. Keep bars of equal width with uniform gaps between them

**Example:** Number of students participating in different activities:

| Activity | Students |
|----------|---------|
| Music | 25 |
| Dance | 30 |
| Art | 15 |
| Sports | 40 |

A bar graph makes it easy to compare these values visually -- the tallest bar (Sports) shows the most popular activity.

### Double Bar Graphs

A **double bar graph** shows two related data sets side by side for easy comparison. For example, comparing boys and girls participating in each activity, or comparing rainfall in two different years.

## Choosing the Right Scale

When making bar graphs, the scale on the vertical axis should be chosen carefully:
- A scale of 1 unit = 5 works well for values up to 50
- A scale of 1 unit = 100 works for values up to 1000
- The scale should start from **0** to avoid misleading comparisons

> **Warning:** A bar graph that does not start from 0 can make small differences look very large. Always check the scale before drawing conclusions!

## Data Interpretation

Looking at data carefully can spark curiosity:
- Do seasons affect the price of onions?
- What factors determine commodity prices?
- How do price changes impact farmers and consumers?

Statistics helps us go beyond individual numbers to see **patterns**, **trends**, and **relationships** in data.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Statistics** | The study of collecting, organising, analysing, interpreting, and presenting data |
| **Statistical question** | A question that can be answered by collecting data, expecting variability in responses |
| **Mean (Arithmetic Mean)** | The sum of all values divided by the number of values; the "average" |
| **Median** | The middle value when data is arranged in order |
| **Outlier** | A value that is significantly different from the rest of the data |
| **Range** | The difference between the maximum and minimum values in a data set |
| **Dot plot** | A graph showing data values as dots on a number line |
| **Bar graph** | A graph using rectangular bars to represent and compare data |
| **Double bar graph** | A bar graph showing two related data sets side by side |
| **Scale** | The unit size chosen to represent values on the axis of a graph |
