## What Makes a Question Statistical?

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher asked us to identify statistical questions. What makes a question "statistical"?

**Ravi:** A statistical question expects variability in the answers -- different people or situations give different results. "How tall are Class 7 students?" is statistical because heights vary. But "What is 5 + 3?" is not, because the answer is always 8.

**Kiran:** So "How many hours do students in my class sleep?" is statistical?

**Ravi:** Yes, because sleep hours will vary from student to student. And a statistical statement summarises data -- like "On average, a smartphone user checks their phone 58 times a day." That statement comes from collecting data from many users and finding a representative number.

**Kiran:** So statistics is about understanding groups of numbers, not individual ones?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Statistics gives us tools to make sense of data that varies, to find patterns, and to compare groups meaningfully.

## The Mean as Fair Share

**Kiran:** Papa, Shubman scored 23, 7, 10, 52, and 18 runs in 5 matches. His mean is 22. But he never actually scored 22 in any match!

**Ravi:** That is the beauty of the mean -- it tells you the "fair share." If Shubman could redistribute his 110 total runs equally across 5 matches, each match would have 22 runs. It is not about what actually happened, but what the average performance looks like.

**Kiran:** And if Yashasvi scored 26, 53, 2, and 15 in 4 matches, his mean is 24. Even though he scored fewer total runs, his average is higher?

**Ravi:** Right. Because he played fewer matches. The mean adjusts for group size, making it a fair comparison tool. Ancient Indian mathematicians like Brahmagupta used the concept of mean, calling it "samarajju" -- the equalising measure.

**Kiran:** So the mean is like dividing a pot of money equally among friends?

**Ravi:** Perfect analogy. If 5 friends collect 3, 8, 10, 5, and 4 guavas, the mean is 6 each. It represents what everyone gets if everything is shared equally.

## When the Mean Misleads

**Kiran:** Papa, Poovizhi's family has heights 170, 173, 165, 118, and 175 cm. The mean is 160.2 cm. But four family members are taller than that!

**Ravi:** You have spotted the weakness of the mean. The 118 cm member (probably a young child) pulls the average way down. That extreme value is called an outlier.

**Kiran:** So what do we use instead?

**Ravi:** The median -- the middle value when data is sorted. Sorted heights: 118, 165, 170, 173, 175. The middle value is 170, which is much more representative of the family's typical height.

**Kiran:** How does the median work for an even number of values?

**Ravi:** You take the average of the two middle values. For Yaangba's family heights sorted as 155, 160, 164, 165, 169, 173, the median is (164 + 165) / 2 = 164.5 cm.

**Kiran:** So the median is not affected by outliers?

**Ravi:** Barely. Whether the smallest height is 118 or 50, the median stays at 170. This makes it a more robust measure when data has extreme values.

## Bar Graphs and Visual Comparison

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we draw bar graphs when we already have the numbers?

**Ravi:** Because visual comparison is much faster. If I tell you Music has 25 students, Dance has 30, Art has 15, and Sports has 40, you need to compare four numbers mentally. But in a bar graph, you instantly see that the Sports bar is tallest and Art is shortest.

**Kiran:** What about double bar graphs?

**Ravi:** Those compare two related data sets side by side. For example, boys and girls in each activity, or onion prices in two towns. The visual juxtaposition reveals patterns immediately -- like which town has higher prices or which activity is more popular among girls.

**Kiran:** My teacher warned us about misleading graphs. What should I watch for?

**Ravi:** Always check if the scale starts from zero. A bar graph that starts from 95 instead of 0 can make a difference of 3 look enormous. Also check the scale intervals -- are they equal? These tricks are sometimes used in advertisements and news to exaggerate differences.

## Dot Plots and Spread

**Kiran:** What is a dot plot and how is it different from a bar graph?

**Ravi:** A dot plot shows each individual data value as a dot on a number line. It is great for seeing how data is spread out and where it clusters. For example, monthly onion prices plotted on a number line immediately show whether prices are tightly grouped or widely scattered.

**Kiran:** So it shows the range and clustering at a glance?

**Ravi:** Yes. If one town's onion prices range from 17 to 60 and another's range from 24 to 59, the dot plot shows that the first town has more volatile prices. The range (max minus min) quantifies this spread.

**Kiran:** Statistics helps us see patterns that raw numbers hide!

**Ravi:** That is the whole point. Whether you are analysing cricket scores, exam results, or weather data, statistics gives you the tools to go beyond individual numbers and understand the bigger picture. Data without analysis is just noise -- statistics turns it into insight.
