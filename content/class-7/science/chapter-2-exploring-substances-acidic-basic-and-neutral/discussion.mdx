## The Haldi Stain Mystery

**Kiran:** Papa, something strange happened. I got a haldi stain on my white school uniform, and when Amma rubbed soap on it, the stain turned red! Why?

**Ravi:** That is actually chemistry in action! Haldi, or turmeric, is a natural indicator. It stays yellow in acidic and neutral solutions but turns red in basic solutions. Soap is a basic substance, so when it touched the turmeric stain, the colour changed to red.

**Kiran:** So turmeric can tell us whether something is acidic or basic?

**Ravi:** Yes, but only partially. Turmeric turns red in basic solutions but stays yellow in both acidic and neutral solutions. So it can confirm something is basic, but it cannot distinguish between acidic and neutral. That is why we need other indicators too.

**Kiran:** Like litmus paper?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Blue litmus turns red in acids. Red litmus turns blue in bases. Neither changes in neutral solutions. Between litmus and turmeric, you can classify most common substances.

## Acids in Our Kitchen

**Kiran:** Papa, are acids dangerous? The word sounds scary.

**Ravi:** Some strong acids are dangerous, but many mild acids are in your kitchen right now! Lemon juice contains citric acid. Vinegar contains acetic acid. Curd contains lactic acid. Tamarind has tartaric acid. Even your amla pickle has ascorbic acid, which is vitamin C.

**Kiran:** All sour things are acidic?

**Ravi:** Generally, yes. The sour taste is a sign of acidity. But here is an important safety rule -- never taste unknown substances to check if they are acidic. In a lab, always use indicators. Some acids are extremely corrosive and can cause severe burns.

**Kiran:** What about basic substances? How do they feel?

**Ravi:** Basic substances generally feel soapy or slippery and may taste bitter. Soap solution, baking soda solution, lime water, and washing powder solution are all basic. And substances like tap water, salt solution, and sugar solution are neutral -- neither acidic nor basic.

## Natural Indicators

**Kiran:** Can I make my own indicator at home?

**Ravi:** Absolutely! Crush some red rose petals, add hot water, filter the liquid, and you have a rose extract indicator. It turns red in acidic solutions and green in basic solutions. You can also try beetroot, purple cabbage, or jamun juice.

**Kiran:** That is so cool! Science experiments with kitchen ingredients!

**Ravi:** The best science happens with everyday materials. You can even make turmeric paper -- dip filter paper strips in turmeric paste and dry them. Dip a strip in soap solution and it turns red. Dip one in lemon juice and it stays yellow.

**Kiran:** Are there indicators that use smell instead of colour?

**Ravi:** Yes, those are called olfactory indicators. Onion-soaked cloth strips change their smell in the presence of acids or bases. This is useful for people who have difficulty distinguishing colours.

## Neutralisation

**Kiran:** What happens when you mix an acid and a base?

**Ravi:** They cancel each other out! This is called a neutralisation reaction. Acid plus base gives salt plus water plus heat. The resulting solution is neutral -- neither acidic nor basic.

**Kiran:** Where does this happen in real life?

**Ravi:** Everywhere! When a red ant bites you, it injects formic acid. Applying moist baking soda (a base) neutralises the acid and relieves the sting. Farmers add lime (basic) to acidic soil to make it suitable for crops. Factories neutralise acidic waste before releasing it into rivers.

**Kiran:** So neutralisation protects both people and the environment?

**Ravi:** Yes. It is one of the most practical applications of chemistry. Understanding acids, bases, and neutralisation helps farmers grow better crops, doctors treat digestive problems, and engineers manage industrial waste safely.

## The Science Fair Trick

**Kiran:** Papa, at the science fair, a blank paper suddenly showed a hidden message when sprayed with a liquid. How did that work?

**Ravi:** I can guess the method. Someone wrote the message using soap solution, which is colourless when dry. The spray probably contained turmeric solution. Since soap is basic, wherever it was written, the turmeric turned red, revealing the hidden message.

**Kiran:** So it is just the turmeric indicator reacting with the soap base!

**Ravi:** Yes. What looks like magic is simply chemistry. You could create your own secret messages this way -- write with soap solution, let it dry, and spray with turmeric water. The message appears as if by magic.

**Kiran:** I am going to try this at home with my friends!

**Ravi:** Perfect. That is exactly the kind of hands-on exploration that makes science memorable. Just make sure to clean up after -- turmeric stains are persistent!
