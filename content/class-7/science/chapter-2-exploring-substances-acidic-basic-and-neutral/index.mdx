## Introduction

Imagine you are at a science fair. At the entry gate, you are given a plain white sheet of paper. A volunteer sprays a liquid on it, and suddenly the words "Welcome to the Wonderful World of Science" appear! How did that happen?

The answer lies in the fascinating world of **acids**, **bases**, and **indicators**. In this chapter, you will learn how to classify substances as acidic, basic, or neutral, and discover the colourful science behind everyday materials.

> **Key idea:** Substances around us can be classified as **acidic**, **basic**, or **neutral** based on their chemical nature. We can use special substances called **indicators** to find out their nature.

---

## Nature -- Our Science Laboratory

### What is Litmus?

**Litmus** is a natural substance obtained from **lichens** (organisms formed by the association of a fungus and an alga). Litmus is available as a solution or as paper strips. Litmus paper comes in two colours -- **blue** and **red**.

Litmus is called an **acid-base indicator** because it shows different colours in acidic and basic solutions.

**How litmus works:**
- **Acidic substances** turn **blue litmus paper to red**
- **Basic substances** turn **red litmus paper to blue**
- **Neutral substances** do not change the colour of either litmus paper

> **Key idea:** An indicator is a substance that changes colour depending on whether the solution is acidic or basic. Litmus is the most common acid-base indicator.

- **Group C (Neutral):** Tap water, sugar solution, salt solution

## Acidic Substances

Substances that taste **sour** tend to contain acids and are **acidic** in nature.

**Caution:** Never taste any unknown substance! Only taste substances that you know are safe.

Here are some common edible substances and the acids they contain:

- **Lemon** -- Citric acid
- **Orange** -- Citric acid and Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C)
- **Amla** -- Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) and Citric acid
- **Tamarind** -- Tartaric acid
- **Vinegar** -- Acetic acid
- **Curd** -- Lactic acid
- **Tomato** -- Citric acid and Oxalic acid

> **Key idea:** Acidic substances taste sour and turn blue litmus paper to red.

---

## Basic Substances

**Basic** substances generally feel **soapy or slippery** to the touch. They usually taste **bitter**, but not everything that tastes bitter is basic. For example, bitter gourd (karela) tastes bitter but is not basic in nature.

Common basic substances include:
- Soap solution
- Baking soda solution
- Lime water (chuna dissolved in water)
- Washing powder solution

> **Key idea:** Basic substances feel soapy or slippery, often taste bitter, and turn red litmus paper to blue.

---

## Neutral Substances

Substances that are neither acidic nor basic are called **neutral** substances. They do not change the colour of either blue or red litmus paper.

Examples: Tap water, sugar solution, salt solution.

## Natural Indicators

If litmus paper is not available, you can use natural substances as acid-base indicators. Many flowers, fruits, and vegetables can serve this purpose!

### Red Rose as an Indicator

You can make your own indicator using red rose petals:

1. Collect some fallen petals of red roses (do not pluck flowers from plants).
2. Wash the petals with water and crush them using a mortar and pestle.
3. Place them in a glass tumbler and pour hot water over them.
4. Cover the tumbler and wait for 5-10 minutes until the water becomes coloured.
5. Filter the liquid. This filtrate is your **red rose extract indicator**.

**Results when testing with red rose extract:**
- In **acidic solutions**, the extract turns **red**
- In **basic solutions**, the extract turns **green**
- In **neutral solutions**, the colour remains **unchanged**

### Turmeric (Haldi) as an Indicator

Turmeric is a common spice found in every Indian kitchen. You can make **turmeric paper** at home:

1. Take a spoonful of turmeric (haldi) and add a little water to make a paste.
2. Dip a piece of filter paper in the turmeric paste until it turns yellow.
3. Take it out and allow it to dry.
4. Cut the yellow paper into thin strips -- these are your **turmeric paper strips**.

**Results when testing with turmeric paper:**
- In **basic solutions**, the yellow turmeric paper turns **red**
- In **acidic solutions**, the colour remains **unchanged** (stays yellow)
- In **neutral solutions**, the colour remains **unchanged** (stays yellow)

> **Key idea:** Turmeric paper can be used to test for basic substances. However, it cannot tell the difference between acidic and neutral substances, since it stays yellow in both.

This is why a haldi (turmeric) stain on your white school uniform turns red when you apply soap (which is basic) to wash it!

### Other Natural Indicators

You can also try making indicators from:
- **Beetroot**
- **Purple cabbage**
- **Indian blackberry (jamun)**
- **Red hibiscus (gudhal) flower**

### Olfactory Indicators

Some substances change their **smell** (not colour) in the presence of acids or bases. These are called **olfactory indicators**.

For example, if you soak cotton cloth strips in chopped onions overnight, the strips absorb the onion smell. When you put drops of an acidic substance on the strip, the smell may change. This can be useful for identifying acids and bases by smell.

## Neutralisation: When Acids Meet Bases

### What Happens When You Mix an Acid and a Base?

When an acidic substance is mixed with a basic substance in the right amount, they **cancel out** each other's effect. The resulting solution is neither acidic nor basic -- it becomes **neutral**.

This reaction is called a **neutralisation reaction**.

**The neutralisation reaction:**

**Acid + Base → Salt + Water + Heat**

For example, if you add drops of blue litmus solution to lemon juice (acidic), the solution turns red. Now, if you slowly add lime water (basic) to this solution, at some point the colour changes from red back to blue. This shows that the lime water has neutralised the acid.

> **Key idea:** In a neutralisation reaction, an acid and a base react to form **salt** and **water**, along with the release of **heat**. The resulting solution is neutral.

---

## Neutralisation in Daily Life

### Ant Bites

When a red ant bites you, it injects **formic acid** into your skin, causing a stinging pain. Applying **moist baking soda** (which is basic) to the affected area neutralises the acid and provides relief.

### Treating Acidic Soil

When farmers use too many chemical fertilisers, the soil can become **acidic**, and plants do not grow well. The soil can be treated by adding **lime** (which is basic) to neutralise the acidity.

If the soil is **too basic**, farmers add **organic matter** like manure and composted leaves. The organic matter releases acids that neutralise the basic nature of the soil.

### Industrial Waste

Factory waste released into lakes and rivers can be acidic, harming fish and other aquatic life. Before releasing the waste, it can be **neutralised** by adding basic substances. This helps protect the aquatic ecosystem.

> **Key idea:** Neutralisation is used in everyday life to treat ant bites, fix the pH of soil for farming, and manage industrial waste to protect the environment.

## The Science Fair Mystery Solved

Now you can explain the science fair trick from the beginning of the chapter!

One possible explanation:
- The message "Welcome to the Wonderful World of Science" was written on the white paper using a **soap solution** (basic substance) -- which is colourless when dry.
- The spray bottle contained a **turmeric solution** (indicator).
- When the turmeric solution was sprayed on the paper, it turned **red** wherever the soap solution was written, making the hidden message appear!

## Summary

- Substances around us can be classified as **acidic**, **basic**, or **neutral**.
- **Indicators** like litmus, red rose extract, and turmeric are substances that change colour in acidic or basic solutions.
- **Acids** turn blue litmus to red. **Bases** turn red litmus to blue.
- Red rose extract turns **red** in acidic solutions and **green** in basic solutions.
- Turmeric paper turns **red** in basic solutions but stays yellow in acidic and neutral solutions.
- When an acid and a base are mixed in the right amount, they undergo a **neutralisation reaction**, producing **salt**, **water**, and **heat**.
- Neutralisation has many practical uses: treating ant bites, fixing soil for farming, and managing industrial waste.
