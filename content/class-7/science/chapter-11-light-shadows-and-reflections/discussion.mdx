## Light Travels in Straight Lines

**Kiran:** Papa, why can I not see around corners?

**Ravi:** Because light travels in straight lines -- a property called rectilinear propagation. If you align three matchboxes with holes in a line, a torch beam passes through all three. But if you shift one slightly, the beam is blocked. Light cannot bend around obstacles.

**Kiran:** That is also why shadows have sharp edges?

**Ravi:** Exactly. When an opaque object blocks straight-line light rays, a dark shadow forms behind it. No source, no shadow. No opaque object, no shadow. No screen to see it on, no visible shadow. All three are needed.

**Kiran:** Can a shadow change size?

**Ravi:** Yes! Move the object closer to the light source and the shadow gets bigger. Move it closer to the screen and the shadow gets smaller. But the colour of the shadow is always dark regardless of the object's colour -- shadows have no colour because they are regions where light does not reach.

## Transparent, Translucent, and Opaque

**Kiran:** Papa, I can see clearly through glass but only blurrily through frosted glass, and not at all through a book. Why the difference?

**Ravi:** These represent three categories of materials. Transparent materials like clear glass and water let light pass through completely -- you see clearly. Translucent materials like frosted glass and tracing paper let some light through but scatter it -- objects appear blurred. Opaque materials like cardboard and metal block light completely.

**Kiran:** So only opaque objects cast dark shadows?

**Ravi:** Opaque objects cast the darkest shadows. Translucent objects create lighter, fainter shadows. Transparent objects may cast very faint shadows or none at all.

**Kiran:** Is this related to why we use curtains? Thick curtains (opaque) block light, thin curtains (translucent) let some light in but give privacy?

**Ravi:** Perfect practical application! And clear glass windows (transparent) let both light and the view through. Architects choose materials based on exactly these properties.

## Reflection and Mirrors

**Kiran:** Papa, how does a mirror work?

**Ravi:** When light hits a shiny, smooth surface, it bounces back in a specific direction. This is called reflection. A plane mirror (flat mirror) reflects light so that the image appears to be behind the mirror, at the same distance as the object is in front.

**Kiran:** Why does my image in a mirror look like everything is flipped left to right?

**Ravi:** That is called lateral inversion. When you raise your left hand, your mirror image appears to raise its right hand. This is why "AMBULANCE" is written in reverse on the front of ambulances -- so the word reads correctly in a car's rear-view mirror.

**Kiran:** That is clever! What other properties does a mirror image have?

**Ravi:** The image in a plane mirror is the same size as the object, upright (not inverted), and cannot be captured on a screen. You can only see it by looking into the mirror.

## The Pinhole Camera

**Kiran:** Papa, I made a pinhole camera from a cardboard box. The image on the screen is upside down! Why?

**Ravi:** Because light travels in straight lines. Light from the top of the object passes through the pinhole and goes downward to the bottom of the screen. Light from the bottom goes upward to the top of the screen. The criss-crossing of light rays at the pinhole inverts the image.

**Kiran:** So a pinhole camera image is inverted, while a mirror image is upright?

**Ravi:** Correct. And there is another difference. A mirror image is laterally inverted (left-right swapped) but a pinhole camera image is not. Also, the pinhole camera image appears on a screen, while the mirror image cannot be captured on a screen.

**Kiran:** The pinhole camera is like a simplified eye!

**Ravi:** Exactly! Our eyes work on the same principle -- light enters through the pupil (a small opening), and an inverted image forms on the retina. The brain then flips the image so we perceive things right-side up.

## Periscopes and Kaleidoscopes

**Kiran:** Papa, how does a submarine crew see above the water surface while staying underwater?

**Ravi:** Using a periscope -- a device with two mirrors arranged in a Z-shape. Light from above the water hits the top mirror, gets reflected downward, hits the bottom mirror, and gets reflected horizontally into the observer's eyes. The two reflections redirect light around corners.

**Kiran:** So periscopes let you see things that are not in your direct line of sight?

**Ravi:** Yes. They are used in submarines, military tanks, and even by spectators trying to see over a crowd. And kaleidoscopes use three mirrors in a triangular arrangement to create beautiful, symmetrical patterns through multiple reflections.

**Kiran:** India has a rich tradition of shadow art too, right?

**Ravi:** Absolutely! Shadow puppetry traditions like Tholu Bommalata in Andhra Pradesh, Togalu Gombeyaata in Karnataka, and Tholpavakoothu in Kerala are centuries old. Flat puppet figures between a light source and a screen create storytelling through shadows. It is science and art combined beautifully.
