## Introduction

Light is all around us. The Sun lights up our days. Electric lamps brighten our nights. Fireflies flash their tiny lights in the dark. Light helps us see the world, and understanding how light works opens up a fascinating world of science.

In this chapter, we will explore how light travels, how shadows are formed, how mirrors reflect light, and how simple devices like pinhole cameras and periscopes work.

---

## Sources of Light

### Luminous and Non-luminous Objects

Objects that **emit their own light** are called **luminous objects**.

Examples of luminous objects:
- **Natural sources:** The Sun, stars, lightning, fire, fireflies
- **Artificial sources:** Electric bulbs, tube lights, LED lamps, candles, torches

Objects that **do not emit their own light** are called **non-luminous objects**. Examples include the Moon, planets, books, tables, and mirrors.

The **Moon** does not produce its own light. It shines because it **reflects** the light of the Sun that falls on it.

> **Key idea:** **Luminous objects** emit their own light (like the Sun, stars, and electric bulbs). **Non-luminous objects** do not emit their own light but may be visible because they reflect light from luminous sources.

### LED Lamps

**LED (Light Emitting Diode) lamps** are modern light sources that use much less electricity, are brighter, and last longer than traditional bulbs. The Indian government has promoted the use of LED lamps across the country to save electricity and protect the environment.

## Light Travels in a Straight Line

One of the most important properties of light is that it **travels in a straight line**. This property is called **rectilinear propagation of light**.

### Activity 1: Three Matchboxes

1. Make a hole at the same position in three matchbox trays.
2. Arrange them in a straight line so all holes are aligned.
3. Shine a torch on one side -- you can see a spot of light on the other side through all three holes.
4. Now move one matchbox slightly to the side -- the light spot disappears!

**Conclusion:** Light travels in a straight line. When the holes are not aligned, light cannot bend around to reach the screen.

### Activity 2: Straight and Bent Pipe

1. Look at a candle flame through a straight hollow pipe -- you can see it clearly.
2. Now bend the pipe and try to see the flame -- you cannot see it!

**Conclusion:** Light cannot travel through a bent path. It only travels in straight lines.

> **Key idea:** Light travels in **straight lines**. This is why we cannot see around corners, and why shadows have sharp edges.

## Transparent, Translucent, and Opaque Materials

When light falls on different materials, three things can happen:

| Type of Material | What Happens to Light | Examples |
|-----------------|----------------------|----------|
| **Transparent** | Light passes almost completely through | Clear glass, water, air |
| **Translucent** | Light passes partially through (objects appear blurred) | Tracing paper, frosted glass, thin cloth |
| **Opaque** | Light does not pass through at all | Cardboard, wood, metal, thick cloth, stone |

> **Key idea:** **Transparent** materials let light pass through completely. **Translucent** materials let some light through. **Opaque** materials block light completely.

## Shadow Formation

### How Are Shadows Formed?

Since light travels in a straight line, when an **opaque object** is placed in its path, light is blocked. The dark area where light does not reach is called a **shadow**.

![Shadow formation showing a light source, an opaque object, and the shadow cast on a screen](shadow-formation-diagram.png)

To form a shadow, you need three things:
1. A **source of light** (like a torch or the Sun)
2. An **opaque object** (that blocks the light)
3. A **screen** (a wall, floor, or any surface where the shadow appears)

### Properties of Shadows

From experiments with shadows, we learn several interesting things:

- **No source, no shadow:** If the torch is switched off, no shadow forms.
- **No object, no shadow:** If the opaque object is removed, no shadow forms.
- **No screen, no shadow:** If there is no surface to fall on, you cannot see the shadow.
- **Size changes:** Moving the object closer to the torch makes the shadow **larger**. Moving it closer to the screen makes it **smaller**.
- **Shape changes:** Tilting the object changes the shape of its shadow.
- **Colour does not matter:** The shadow is always **dark**, regardless of the colour of the object. Changing the colour of the object does not change the colour of the shadow.

**What about translucent and transparent objects?**
- Opaque objects form the **darkest** shadows
- Translucent objects make **lighter, fainter** shadows
- Some transparent objects can create very **faint** shadows

> **Key idea:** A shadow is formed when an opaque object blocks light. The shape and size of the shadow depend on the position of the object relative to the light source and the screen. The colour of the object does not affect the colour of its shadow.

### Shadow Puppetry

Shadow play or shadow puppetry has been a part of Indian culture for centuries. Flat cut-out figures (shadow puppets) are placed between a light source and a screen. By moving the puppets, performers create life-like movements. Famous Indian shadow puppet traditions include:
- **Charma Bahuli Natya** (Maharashtra)
- **Tholu Bommalata** (Andhra Pradesh)
- **Togalu Gombeyaata** (Karnataka)
- **Ravana Chhaya** (Odisha)
- **Tholpavakoothu** (Kerala)
- **Bommalattam** (Tamil Nadu)

## Reflection of Light

### What is Reflection?

When light falls on a **shiny surface** (like a mirror or polished metal), it **bounces back** in a different direction. This change in the direction of light by a shiny surface is called **reflection of light**.

### Activity: Redirecting Sunlight

1. Take a plane mirror (a flat mirror) outside in sunlight.
2. Let sunlight fall on the mirror surface.
3. Tilt the mirror to redirect the light onto a wall or nearby surface.
4. You can see a bright spot of light on the wall!
5. Tilting the mirror in different ways changes the position of the light spot.

**Conclusion:** A mirror changes the direction of light that falls on it. This is reflection.

### Seeing a Light Beam Reflect

1. Close all openings of a comb except one to make a thin slit.
2. Shine a torch through the slit to create a thin beam of light on a white sheet of paper.
3. Place a mirror in the path of the beam.
4. You can clearly see that the light beam changes direction after hitting the mirror!

> **Key idea:** **Reflection** is the bouncing back of light from a shiny surface. Mirrors reflect light and change its direction.

## Images Formed by a Plane Mirror

When you look into a plane mirror (flat mirror), you see a **reflection** of yourself and other objects. This reflection is called an **image**.

![Plane mirror showing an object, its image, and lateral inversion with left-right reversal](plane-mirror-image-lateral-inversion.png)

### Properties of Images in a Plane Mirror

| Property | Description |
|----------|------------|
| **Size** | The image is the **same size** as the object |
| **Orientation** | The image is **erect** (upright, not upside down) |
| **Distance** | The image appears to be at the **same distance** behind the mirror as the object is in front |
| **Screen** | The image **cannot be obtained on a screen** |
| **Lateral inversion** | Left and right are **reversed** in the image |

### Lateral Inversion

When you look in a mirror:
- If you raise your **left** hand, your image appears to raise its **right** hand
- If you touch your **right** ear, your image appears to touch its **left** ear

This apparent left-right reversal is called **lateral inversion**.

This is why the word **AMBULANCE** is written in reverse on the front of ambulances. When a driver looks at the ambulance in their rear-view mirror, the reversed word reads correctly as "AMBULANCE", helping them understand they need to give way.

> **Key idea:** A plane mirror forms an image that is the same size as the object, erect, and laterally inverted (left and right are swapped). The image cannot be obtained on a screen.

## Pinhole Camera

A **pinhole camera** is a simple device that forms an image of an object using a tiny hole (pinhole).

![Pinhole camera showing light rays crossing through the pinhole to form an inverted image on the screen](pinhole-camera-working.png)

### How It Works

1. Light from an object passes through a small hole in a cardboard or box.
2. Since light travels in straight lines, rays from the top of the object travel downward through the hole, and rays from the bottom travel upward.
3. This creates an **inverted** (upside down) image on the screen placed on the other side.

### Make Your Own Pinhole Camera

1. Take two cardboard boxes, one slightly smaller than the other, so the smaller one can slide inside the larger one.
2. Make a small pinhole on one face of the larger box.
3. Cut out a square on the opposite face of the smaller box and cover it with tracing paper (this is the screen).
4. Slide the smaller box inside the larger one with the tracing paper facing inward.
5. Point the pinhole towards a bright object (like a tree in sunlight).
6. Look through the open side of the smaller box -- you will see an **inverted, coloured** image on the tracing paper!

### Pinhole Camera vs Plane Mirror

| Feature | Pinhole Camera | Plane Mirror |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Image orientation | Inverted (upside down) | Erect (upright) |
| Lateral inversion | No | Yes |
| Image on screen | Yes | No |

> **Key idea:** A **pinhole camera** creates an **inverted** (upside down) image of an object on a screen. It works because light travels in straight lines.

## Useful Devices Based on Light

### Periscope

A **periscope** is a device that uses two plane mirrors placed in a Z-shaped box to allow you to see objects that are not directly visible.

![Periscope diagram showing two mirrors in a Z-shaped arrangement reflecting light from an object to the observer](periscope-diagram.png)

How it works:
1. Light from the object hits the **first mirror** at the top and gets reflected downward.
2. The reflected light hits the **second mirror** at the bottom and gets reflected towards your eyes.

**Uses of periscopes:**
- In **submarines** -- to see above the water surface while staying underwater
- In **tanks** and **bunkers** -- for soldiers to see outside without exposing themselves
- To see over a crowd when you are standing behind taller people

### Kaleidoscope

A **kaleidoscope** is made by joining three rectangular mirror strips together in a triangular shape inside a tube. Coloured beads or broken bangle pieces are placed at one end. When you look through the other end and rotate the kaleidoscope, you see **beautiful, ever-changing patterns** formed by multiple reflections.

Designers and artists often use kaleidoscopes to get ideas for new patterns.

## Summary

- **Luminous objects** emit their own light (Sun, stars, bulbs). **Non-luminous objects** do not (Moon, books).
- Light travels in **straight lines** (rectilinear propagation).
- **Transparent** materials let light pass completely. **Translucent** materials let some light through. **Opaque** materials block light completely.
- A **shadow** forms when an opaque object blocks light. Three things are needed: a light source, an opaque object, and a screen.
- The size and shape of a shadow depend on the position of the object relative to the light source and screen.
- **Reflection** is the bouncing back of light from a shiny surface like a mirror.
- A **plane mirror** forms an image that is the same size as the object, erect, laterally inverted, and cannot be obtained on a screen.
- **Lateral inversion** means left appears as right and right appears as left in the mirror image.
- A **pinhole camera** forms an inverted image on a screen because light travels in straight lines.
- **Periscopes** use two mirrors to see objects not directly visible. **Kaleidoscopes** use three mirrors to create beautiful patterns.
- Light from the Sun takes about **8 minutes and 20 seconds** to reach the Earth.
