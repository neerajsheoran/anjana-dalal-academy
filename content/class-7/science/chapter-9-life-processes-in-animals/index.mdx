## Introduction

All living beings carry out certain processes that are essential for their survival. These include **nutrition**, **respiration**, **circulation**, **excretion**, and **reproduction**. Together, these are called **life processes**.

Animals, including humans, get energy from the food they eat. But the food we eat contains complex components like carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. These must be broken down into simpler forms before our body can use them. In this chapter, we will learn how digestion and respiration work in animals, with a special focus on humans.

> **Key idea:** Animals obtain energy from food. The complex components of food must be broken down into simpler forms through **digestion** before the body can use them.

---

## Nutrition in Animals: The Human Digestive System

The breaking down of complex food components into simpler forms happens in a long tube called the **alimentary canal**. This process starts at the **mouth** and ends at the **anus**.

![Labelled diagram of the human digestive system showing mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, liver, and pancreas](human-digestive-system.png)

The main parts of the human digestive system are:
1. Mouth
2. Oesophagus (food pipe)
3. Stomach
4. Small intestine
5. Large intestine
6. Rectum and Anus

Associated organs that help in digestion include the **liver** and the **pancreas**.

---

### The Mouth

The journey of food begins in the mouth.

**Teeth** break down food into smaller pieces by crushing and chewing. This initial breakdown of food into fine pieces is called **mechanical digestion**.

**Saliva** is produced in the mouth and plays an important role:
- It **moistens** the food, making it soft and easy to swallow
- It contains a digestive juice that **breaks down starch into sugar**

**Try this:** Take a small piece of chapati or boiled rice and chew it for 30-60 seconds. At first, it has its usual taste, but as you keep chewing, it begins to taste **sweet**! This is because the saliva breaks down the starch in chapati/rice into sugar.

The **tongue** helps in mixing the chewed food with saliva and pushing the softened food towards the food pipe.

> **Key idea:** Digestion begins in the mouth. Teeth break food into smaller pieces (mechanical digestion), while saliva starts breaking down starch into sugar (chemical digestion).

**Oral hygiene tip:** Brush your teeth and clean your tongue twice a day. Rinse your mouth with water after each meal to prevent tooth decay.

---

### The Food Pipe (Oesophagus)

The food pipe is a long, flexible tube that connects the mouth to the stomach. The walls of the food pipe **contract and relax** in a wave-like motion to push the food downwards into the stomach. This wave-like movement continues throughout the alimentary canal.

---

### The Stomach

In the stomach, the food is further broken down:

- The **walls of the stomach contract and relax** to churn the food thoroughly.
- The inner lining of the stomach secretes three important substances:
  - **Digestive juice** -- breaks down proteins into simpler forms
  - **Acid** -- helps break down proteins and also kills harmful bacteria in the food
  - **Mucus** -- protects the stomach lining from being damaged by the acid

In the stomach, food is partially digested and transformed into a **semi-liquid mass**, preparing it for the next stage.

---

### The Small Intestine

After the stomach, partially digested food moves into the **small intestine**. Despite its name, the small intestine is the **longest part** of the alimentary canal -- about **6 metres long** (almost twice the height of your classroom)!

The small intestine receives digestive secretions from three sources:

1. **Its own inner lining** -- secretes digestive juice that further breaks down fats, proteins, and carbohydrates
2. **Liver** -- secretes **bile**, which is mildly basic in nature. Bile neutralises the acid from the stomach and breaks down **fats into tiny droplets**, making them easier to digest.
3. **Pancreas** -- secretes **pancreatic juice**, which is also basic and breaks down carbohydrates, proteins, and fats

**Absorption of nutrients:** The inner lining of the small intestine has **thousands of tiny finger-like projections**. These projections increase the surface area for efficient **absorption** of nutrients. The digested nutrients pass into the **blood vessels** in the walls of the small intestine and are carried to all parts of the body.

> **Key idea:** The small intestine is where most of the digestion is completed and where nutrients are absorbed into the blood. The finger-like projections on its inner wall increase the surface area for better absorption.

---

### The Large Intestine

After most nutrients are absorbed in the small intestine, the remaining **undigested food** moves into the **large intestine**. It is about 1.5 metres long -- shorter than the small intestine, but **wider**.

The large intestine:
- **Absorbs water** and some salts from the undigested food
- Makes the waste **semi-solid** (called **stool**)
- Stores the stool in the **rectum** until it is ready to be expelled

The stool is finally expelled through the **anus**. This process is called **egestion**.

**Tip:** Eating **fibre-rich foods** like fruits, vegetables, and whole grains helps the large intestine function properly by making the stool easier to pass.

The large intestine also contains helpful **bacteria** that aid digestion. Fermented foods like **curd, buttermilk, pickles**, and **kanji** are good for a healthy digestive system.

---

### The Complete Journey of Food

Here is the complete path food takes through the alimentary canal:

**Mouth** --> **Oesophagus (Food Pipe)** --> **Stomach** --> **Small Intestine** --> **Large Intestine** --> **Rectum** --> **Anus**

| Part | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **Mouth** | Teeth crush food; saliva breaks down starch into sugar |
| **Food pipe** | Pushes food to the stomach by wave-like motion |
| **Stomach** | Churns food; digestive juice breaks down proteins; acid kills bacteria |
| **Small intestine** | Completes digestion; absorbs nutrients into blood |
| **Large intestine** | Absorbs water and salts; forms semi-solid stool |
| **Rectum and Anus** | Stores and expels waste (egestion) |

## Digestion in Other Animals

Not all animals digest food the same way as humans.

### Ruminants (Cows, Buffaloes, Goats)

Grass-eating animals like cows and buffaloes are called **ruminants**. Their digestion process is unique:

1. They partially chew the grass and swallow it into a special part of the stomach called the **rumen**.
2. Partial digestion takes place in the rumen.
3. The partially digested food is **brought back to the mouth** for further chewing. This process is called **rumination** (this is why cows seem to keep chewing even when they are not eating!).
4. A cow spends about **8 hours a day** just chewing food!
5. The thoroughly chewed food is then swallowed again for complete digestion.

### Birds

Birds do not have teeth. Instead, they have a special chamber called the **gizzard**. The gizzard's walls contract and relax to break down food, often with the help of **grit** (small stones) that birds swallow.

> **Key idea:** Different animals have different digestive systems adapted to the types of food they eat and their habitats.

## Respiration in Animals

After food is digested and nutrients are absorbed, the body needs to convert these nutrients into **energy**. This process requires **oxygen** and is called **respiration**.

### Breathing vs Respiration

These two terms are often confused, but they are different:

| Feature | Breathing | Respiration |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **What is it?** | The physical process of inhaling and exhaling air | The chemical process of breaking down food (glucose) to release energy |
| **Type of process** | Physical | Chemical |
| **Where does it happen?** | Lungs (nose, windpipe, lungs) | Inside every cell of the body |
| **What is exchanged?** | Oxygen is taken in; carbon dioxide is released | Glucose reacts with oxygen to produce energy, carbon dioxide, and water |

**The respiration equation:**

**Glucose + Oxygen --> Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy**

> **Key idea:** **Breathing** is the physical process of taking in air and releasing it. **Respiration** is the chemical process where oxygen breaks down glucose (from food) to release energy. Both are essential for survival.

---

## The Human Respiratory System

![Labelled diagram of the human respiratory system showing nostrils, nasal passages, windpipe, lungs, and alveoli](human-respiratory-system.png)

### Parts of the Respiratory System

1. **Nostrils** -- Two openings through which we inhale and exhale air
2. **Nasal passages** -- Small passages with tiny hair and mucus that trap dust and dirt from the air (this is why we should breathe through the nose, not the mouth)
3. **Windpipe (Trachea)** -- Carries air from the nasal passages to the lungs
4. **Lungs** -- A pair of organs protected by the **rib cage**. Inside the lungs, the windpipe branches into smaller and finer tubes that end in tiny balloon-like sacs called **alveoli**.

### How Do We Breathe?

![Diagram showing inhalation and exhalation: rib movement, diaphragm position, and air flow direction](breathing-mechanism-inhalation-exhalation.png)

**Inhalation (Breathing in):**
1. The **ribs** move up and outwards
2. The **diaphragm** (a dome-shaped muscle below the lungs) moves **downwards**
3. This increases the space inside the chest
4. Air enters the lungs

**Exhalation (Breathing out):**
1. The ribs move down and inwards
2. The diaphragm moves **upwards**
3. This decreases the space inside the chest
4. Air is pushed out of the lungs

### Gas Exchange in the Lungs

The **alveoli** have very thin walls surrounded by fine blood vessels. This is where the exchange of gases happens:

- **Oxygen** from the inhaled air passes through the thin walls of the alveoli into the **blood**
- **Carbon dioxide** from the blood passes into the alveoli and is **exhaled** out

### What is in the Air We Breathe?

| Component | Inhaled Air | Exhaled Air |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| **Oxygen** | About 21% | About 16-17% |
| **Carbon dioxide** | About 0.04% | About 4-5% |

Not all the oxygen is used up during breathing. The exhaled air still contains some oxygen, but it has much more carbon dioxide than inhaled air.

**Activity:** If you blow exhaled air through lime water using a straw, the lime water turns milky. This confirms that exhaled air contains carbon dioxide. If you pass normal air (using a syringe) through lime water, it does not turn milky -- because the air we inhale has very little carbon dioxide.

> **Key idea:** Gas exchange happens in the **alveoli** of the lungs. Oxygen passes from the air into the blood, while carbon dioxide passes from the blood into the air. The blood then carries oxygen to all parts of the body.

## The Circulatory System

The body has a transport system called the **circulatory system** to carry nutrients, oxygen, and other substances to all parts of the body.

It includes:
- **Heart** -- pumps blood through the body
- **Blood** -- carries nutrients, oxygen, and waste products
- **Blood vessels** -- tubes through which blood flows

The heart pumps blood through blood vessels, delivering oxygen and nutrients to every cell while removing waste products.

---

## Breathing in Other Animals

Different animals breathe in different ways, adapted to their habitats:

| Animal | Breathing Organ | Habitat |
|--------|----------------|---------|
| Humans, birds, elephants, lions, cows, lizards, snakes | **Lungs** | Land |
| Fish | **Gills** (exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide dissolved in water) | Water |
| Tadpoles (young frogs) | **Gills** | Water |
| Adult frogs | **Lungs** (on land) and **skin** (in water) | Both land and water |
| Earthworms | **Moist skin** | Soil |

> **Key idea:** Different animals have different breathing mechanisms adapted to their habitats. Land animals use lungs, fish use gills, and earthworms breathe through their moist skin.

## Health and Safety

### Oral Hygiene
- Brush teeth and clean tongue twice daily
- Rinse mouth with water after every meal

### Digestive Health
- Eat fibre-rich foods (fruits, vegetables, whole grains)
- Include fermented foods like curd and buttermilk
- Eat at regular times and practise mindful eating
- Avoid overeating

### Respiratory Health
- Always breathe through the nose (not the mouth) to filter dust
- Practise **pranayama** and deep breathing exercises for better lung health
- Avoid smoking -- it damages the lungs and causes serious diseases
- Passive smoking (inhaling smoke from others) is also dangerous, especially for children

## Summary

- **Life processes** (nutrition, respiration, circulation, excretion, reproduction) are essential for survival.
- The **human digestive system** consists of the alimentary canal (mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, anus) and associated organs (liver, pancreas).
- Digestion begins in the mouth where saliva breaks down starch. Proteins are broken down in the stomach. The small intestine completes digestion and absorbs nutrients.
- The large intestine absorbs water and salts from undigested food.
- **Ruminants** like cows chew their food multiple times through the process of rumination.
- **Breathing** is the physical process of inhaling and exhaling air. **Respiration** is the chemical process of using oxygen to break down glucose for energy.
- Gas exchange occurs in the **alveoli** of the lungs.
- The **circulatory system** (heart, blood, blood vessels) transports nutrients and oxygen throughout the body.
- Different animals breathe using different organs -- lungs, gills, or skin -- depending on their habitat.
