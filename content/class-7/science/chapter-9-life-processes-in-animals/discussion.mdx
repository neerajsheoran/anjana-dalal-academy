## The Journey of Food

**Kiran:** Papa, what happens to the chapati I eat? How does it give me energy to play cricket?

**Ravi:** The journey is fascinating. Your teeth crush the chapati into smaller pieces -- that is mechanical digestion. Saliva in your mouth starts breaking down the starch in the chapati into sugar. Try chewing a piece of plain chapati for about a minute -- it starts tasting sweet!

**Kiran:** So digestion begins in the mouth itself?

**Ravi:** Yes. From the mouth, food travels down the oesophagus through wave-like muscle contractions. In the stomach, digestive juices break down proteins, acid kills bacteria, and mucus protects the stomach walls. Then the food enters the small intestine -- the longest part at about 6 metres -- where bile from the liver and juice from the pancreas complete the digestion.

**Kiran:** How do the nutrients reach the rest of my body?

**Ravi:** The small intestine has thousands of tiny finger-like projections that increase the surface area. Digested nutrients pass through these projections into blood vessels, which carry them to every cell. The leftover undigested material goes to the large intestine, where water is absorbed, and the waste is expelled.

## Digestion in Other Animals

**Kiran:** Papa, do all animals digest food the same way?

**Ravi:** Not at all! Cows and buffaloes are ruminants. They partially chew grass, swallow it into a special stomach chamber called the rumen, then bring it back to the mouth for further chewing. This process -- rumination -- is why you see cows always chewing even when not eating. A cow spends about 8 hours a day just chewing!

**Kiran:** And birds?

**Ravi:** Birds have no teeth. They swallow food whole, and it goes to a chamber called the gizzard, which has strong muscular walls that crush the food. Birds often swallow small stones (grit) that help the gizzard grind food.

**Kiran:** So each animal's digestive system is adapted to the food it eats?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Herbivores need long digestive tracts to break down tough plant fibres. Carnivores have shorter digestive systems because meat is easier to digest. The diversity of digestive systems is a beautiful example of how evolution shapes organisms to suit their diet and habitat.

## Breathing vs Respiration

**Kiran:** Papa, are breathing and respiration the same thing?

**Ravi:** People often confuse them, but they are different. Breathing is the physical process of inhaling and exhaling air -- it happens in your lungs. Respiration is the chemical process where oxygen breaks down glucose inside every cell to release energy.

**Kiran:** So breathing gets the oxygen in, and respiration uses it?

**Ravi:** Precisely. The equation for respiration is: glucose + oxygen produces carbon dioxide + water + energy. Breathing provides the oxygen and removes the carbon dioxide. Gas exchange happens in tiny balloon-like sacs called alveoli in the lungs.

**Kiran:** Why do we breathe out more carbon dioxide than we breathe in?

**Ravi:** Inhaled air contains about 21% oxygen and 0.04% carbon dioxide. Exhaled air has about 16-17% oxygen and 4-5% carbon dioxide. Not all oxygen is used -- but a significant portion is exchanged for carbon dioxide in the alveoli. You can prove this by blowing exhaled air through lime water -- it turns milky due to the carbon dioxide.

## How Different Animals Breathe

**Kiran:** Do all animals breathe using lungs?

**Ravi:** No! Breathing organs are adapted to the habitat. Land animals -- humans, birds, snakes, elephants -- use lungs. Fish breathe using gills that extract oxygen dissolved in water. Earthworms breathe through their moist skin. And frogs are amazing -- tadpoles use gills in water, but adult frogs use both lungs on land and skin in water!

**Kiran:** So a frog changes its breathing system as it grows up?

**Ravi:** Yes. This transformation from tadpole to adult frog is called metamorphosis. The breathing system adapts to the changing habitat -- from fully aquatic to amphibious. It is a remarkable example of biological adaptation.

**Kiran:** Why do earthworms come to the surface when it rains?

**Ravi:** Because their skin needs to be moist for gas exchange. When the soil gets waterlogged in rain, they cannot get enough oxygen. They come to the surface to breathe. If the soil dries out too much, they cannot breathe either. They need just the right amount of moisture -- a delicate balance.

## Keeping Our Systems Healthy

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I keep my digestive and respiratory systems healthy?

**Ravi:** For digestion: eat fibre-rich foods like fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Include fermented foods like curd and buttermilk -- they contain helpful bacteria. Eat at regular times and practise mindful eating. Brush your teeth twice daily and rinse your mouth after meals.

**Kiran:** And for respiration?

**Ravi:** Always breathe through your nose, not your mouth -- your nasal passages filter dust and dirt. Practise pranayama and deep breathing exercises, which strengthen your lungs. And most importantly, avoid smoking. Tobacco smoke damages the delicate alveoli and causes serious diseases.

**Kiran:** Even passive smoking?

**Ravi:** Yes! Inhaling smoke from others' cigarettes is also harmful, especially for children. Your body is a remarkable machine with interconnected systems -- digestion breaks down food, respiration converts it to energy, and circulation delivers that energy everywhere. Taking care of all three systems is essential for a healthy life.
