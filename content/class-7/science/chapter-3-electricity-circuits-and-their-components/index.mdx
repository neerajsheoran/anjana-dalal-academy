## Introduction

Think about how much we depend on electricity every day! From the lights in our homes to the fans in our classrooms, from mobile phones to trains -- electricity powers almost everything around us.

Electricity is generated in many ways -- by windmills, solar panels, falling water (hydroelectric power), and by burning coal or natural gas. The electric supply from these sources reaches our homes and factories through wires.

Here are some common uses of electricity grouped by category:

- **Cooking:** Electric kettle, mixer grinder, toaster, oven, microwave
- **Lighting:** Homes, offices, streets, markets, factories
- **Transportation:** Trains, buses, electric cars, lifts, escalators
- **Heating and Cooling:** Fan, room heater, geyser, refrigerator, air conditioner
- **Entertainment:** Television, radio, speakers
- **Communication:** Mobile phone, internet

> **Key idea:** Electricity is one of the most important forms of energy in our daily life. It is generated from various sources and reaches our homes through wires.

**Caution:** Never experiment with the electricity from wall sockets or power supply at home or school. It can be extremely dangerous and even fatal. Only use batteries or cells (like those in torchlights, wall clocks, or remotes) for experiments.

## A Torchlight

A torchlight (also called a torch or flashlight) is a simple device that helps us understand how electricity works. When you open a torchlight, you find:

- An **electric lamp** (the part that glows)
- A **switch** (to turn it on and off)
- Two or more **electric cells** (the source of electrical energy)

When you slide the switch to one position, the torch lamp glows. Slide it back, and the lamp goes off. But why does the lamp glow in one position of the switch and not the other? To answer this, we need to understand electrical circuits.

## Components of a Simple Electrical Circuit

### Electric Cell

An **electric cell** is a portable source of electrical energy. If you look at an electric cell carefully, you will notice:

- A small **protruding metal cap** on one side -- this is the **positive terminal (+)**
- A **flat metal disc** on the other side -- this is the **negative terminal (-)**

Every electric cell has these two terminals clearly marked with **+** and **-** signs.

### Battery

When two or more cells are connected together, the combination is called a **battery**. In a battery:

- The **positive terminal** of one cell is connected to the **negative terminal** of the next cell
- This arrangement is called connecting cells **in series**

Connecting more cells together provides energy for a longer time and/or more energy to the circuit.

> **Key idea:** A single cell provides electrical energy. When two or more cells are connected in series (positive terminal of one to negative terminal of the next), they form a **battery**.

### Incandescent Lamp

An **incandescent lamp** (also called a light bulb) has these parts:

- A **glass bulb** that protects the inner parts
- A thin wire inside called the **filament** -- this is the part that glows
- Two **thick wires** that support the filament
- A **metal case** at the base (one terminal)
- A **metal tip** at the centre of the base (the other terminal)

When electric current passes through the filament, it gets very hot and **glows**, producing light. The two terminals of the lamp must not touch each other.

> **Key idea:** In an incandescent lamp, the thin wire called a **filament** gets hot and glows when electric current passes through it.

### LED Lamp

Many modern torches use a **Light Emitting Diode (LED)** lamp instead of an incandescent lamp.

Key differences between LED and incandescent lamps:

| Feature | Incandescent Lamp | LED Lamp |
|---------|------------------|----------|
| Has filament? | Yes | No |
| Terminals | Two (either way works) | Two (must connect correctly) |
| Current direction | Either direction | One direction only |
| Energy efficiency | Less efficient | More efficient |

An LED has two wires:
- The **longer wire** is the **positive terminal (+)**
- The **shorter wire** is the **negative terminal (-)**

> **Key idea:** An LED glows only when its positive terminal (longer wire) is connected to the positive terminal of the battery, and its negative terminal (shorter wire) is connected to the negative terminal. Current can flow through an LED in only one direction.

## Making a Lamp Glow

To make a lamp glow, you need to create a complete path for electric current to flow. Here is what you need:

- An electric cell (or battery)
- An electric lamp (incandescent or LED)
- Connecting wires

**Steps to set up the circuit:**

1. Remove about 1 cm of plastic covering from both ends of each wire to expose the metal.
2. Attach wires to the cell holder (or tape wires to the cell).
3. Attach wires to the lamp holder (or tape wires to the lamp).
4. Connect one terminal of the lamp to one terminal of the cell, and the other terminal of the lamp to the other terminal of the cell.

The lamp glows only when:
- One terminal of the lamp is connected to one terminal of the cell
- The other terminal of the lamp is connected to the other terminal of the cell
- There is a **complete path** for current to flow

This setup is called an **electrical circuit**.

> **Key idea:** An electrical circuit provides a complete path for electric current to flow. The lamp glows only when the circuit is complete and current passes through it.

### Direction of Electric Current

The direction of electric current in an electrical circuit is taken to be from the **positive terminal** to the **negative terminal** of the electric cell.

## Electric Switch

A **switch** is a simple device that either **completes** or **breaks** a circuit.

### How a Switch Works

- **ON position (closed circuit):** The switch completes the circuit, allowing current to flow. The lamp glows.
- **OFF position (open circuit):** The switch breaks the circuit, creating a gap that stops current flow. The lamp does not glow.

### Make Your Own Switch

You can build a simple switch using everyday materials:

1. Take two drawing pins, a safety pin (or paper clip), and a small piece of cardboard.
2. Insert a drawing pin through the ring of the safety pin and fix it to the cardboard.
3. Fix the second drawing pin so the free end of the safety pin can touch it.
4. Connect a wire to each drawing pin.

When the safety pin touches both drawing pins, the circuit is closed (ON). When the safety pin is away from the second drawing pin, the circuit is open (OFF).

A switch can be placed **anywhere** in a circuit. The switches used at home work on the same principle, though they are designed differently.

> **Key idea:** A switch is a device that completes (closes) or breaks (opens) an electrical circuit. In the ON position, current flows and the lamp glows. In the OFF position, current stops and the lamp goes off.

## Circuit Diagrams

Drawing actual pictures of cells, lamps, and wires every time is difficult. Instead, we use **symbols** to represent electrical components. A drawing of a circuit using these symbols is called a **circuit diagram**.

### Common Symbols

| Component | How It Looks in a Diagram |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Electric cell** | Two parallel lines (long line = positive, short line = negative) |
| **Battery** | Multiple cells drawn together |
| **Incandescent lamp** | A circle with a cross inside |
| **LED** | A triangle with a line, plus two small arrows for light |
| **Switch (ON)** | A line connecting two dots |
| **Switch (OFF)** | A line with a gap between two dots |
| **Wire** | A straight line |

In the symbol for a cell:
- The **long line** represents the **positive terminal**
- The **short line** represents the **negative terminal**

In the symbol for an LED:
- The **triangle** points in the direction current can flow
- The **two arrows** indicate that light is emitted

> **Key idea:** A **circuit diagram** is a simplified drawing of an electrical circuit using standard symbols. These symbols are used worldwide, so scientists and engineers from different countries can understand each other's circuits.

## Electrical Conductors and Insulators

Have you ever wondered why electric wires are made of metal? And why are they covered with plastic or rubber?

### Testing Materials

You can build a simple **conduction tester** to find out which materials allow electricity to pass through them:

1. Connect an electric cell and a lamp, leaving two wire ends free.
2. Touch the two free ends together -- if the lamp glows, your tester is ready.
3. Now, place different objects between the two free ends and observe whether the lamp glows.

### Results

| Material | Lamp Glows? | Type |
|----------|------------|------|
| Metal spoon | Yes | Conductor |
| Coin | Yes | Conductor |
| Key | Yes | Conductor |
| Aluminium foil | Yes | Conductor |
| Sewing needle | Yes | Conductor |
| Pencil lead (graphite) | Yes | Conductor |
| Wooden stick | No | Insulator |
| Plastic scale | No | Insulator |
| Rubber eraser | No | Insulator |
| Cork | No | Insulator |
| Glass bangle | No | Insulator |
| Paper | No | Insulator |
| Candle (wax) | No | Insulator |

### Conductors

Materials through which electric current can flow easily are called **conductors** of electricity. Most **metals** are good conductors. This is why electric wires are made of metals like copper.

Silver, copper, and gold are the best electrical conductors. However, **copper** is mainly used for making wires because it is relatively cheaper and available in large quantities.

### Insulators

Materials through which electric current cannot pass are called **insulators** (or poor conductors). Examples include plastic, rubber, wood, glass, and ceramics.

Insulators are used to **cover wires, plug tops, and switches** to protect people from electric shocks.

> **Key idea:** **Conductors** allow electric current to flow through them (e.g., metals like copper, iron, aluminium). **Insulators** do not allow current to flow (e.g., plastic, rubber, wood, glass). Both are equally important -- conductors carry electricity, while insulators keep us safe.

## Safety with Electricity

Our body is a **conductor** of electricity. Electric current passing through our body can cause severe injury or even death. Follow these safety rules:

- Never touch switches or plugs with **wet hands**
- Never use electrical devices in **wet areas**
- Never handle equipment with **damaged insulation** or **broken plugs**
- Never experiment with electricity from **wall sockets** or **generators**
- Only use **batteries or cells** for science experiments

---

## Types of Electric Current

There are two types of electric current:

- **Direct Current (DC):** This is the type of current produced by batteries and cells. It flows in only one direction. It is used in small devices like torchlights, mobile phones, and remote controls.
- **Alternating Current (AC):** This is the type of current that comes from power plants to our wall sockets. It can run larger appliances like refrigerators, washing machines, and air conditioners.

## Summary

- An **electric cell** is a portable source of electrical energy with a positive (+) and a negative (-) terminal.
- Two or more cells connected in series form a **battery**.
- In an **incandescent lamp**, a thin wire called the **filament** glows when current passes through it.
- An **LED** has no filament and allows current in only one direction. Its longer wire is positive and shorter wire is negative.
- An **electrical circuit** provides a complete path for current to flow.
- A **switch** completes or breaks a circuit.
- The direction of electric current is taken from the positive to the negative terminal of the cell.
- A **circuit diagram** uses standard symbols to represent electrical circuits.
- **Conductors** (like metals) allow current to flow; **insulators** (like plastic, rubber) do not.
- Always handle electricity safely and never experiment with household power supply.
