## Introduction

Have you ever noticed that coconut tree shadows are long in the morning but shorter in the afternoon? Or that the Sun appears to move across the sky from East to West? Does the Sun actually move, or is it the Earth that is moving?

In this chapter, we will explore the rotation and revolution of the Earth, understand why we have day and night, why seasons change, and what causes the fascinating events called eclipses.

> **Key idea:** The Earth both **rotates** on its own axis (causing day and night) and **revolves** around the Sun (causing seasons and changes in the night sky).

---

## Rotation of the Earth

### The Merry-Go-Round Analogy

Have you ever sat on a merry-go-round? When the merry-go-round spins in one direction, objects around you appear to move in the **opposite direction**. A tree appears to come into your view from one side and disappear from the other side.

This is exactly what happens with the Sun! The Sun is not actually moving across the sky. The **Earth is spinning**, and we see the Sun appear to move because we are on the spinning Earth.

### How the Earth Rotates

Just like a spinning top spins on its spindle, or a ball spins on its axis, the Earth **rotates** (spins) on its own axis in space.

Key facts about Earth's rotation:
- The Earth's **axis of rotation** passes through the **North Pole** and the **South Pole**
- The Earth completes one full rotation in about **24 hours**
- When viewed from above the North Pole, the Earth rotates in the **anti-clockwise direction** -- from **West to East**

> **Key idea:** The Earth rotates on its axis from **West to East**, completing one full rotation in about **24 hours**. This rotation causes the Sun to appear to move from East to West across the sky.

---

## Day and Night

### Why Do We Have Day and Night?

![Earth rotating on its axis with one half lit by the Sun (day) and the other half in darkness (night)](earth-rotation-day-night.png)

Since the Earth is a sphere, sunlight can only fall on **one half** of the Earth at any given time:
- The half facing the Sun experiences **daytime** (it is lit up)
- The other half, facing away from the Sun, experiences **nighttime** (it is in darkness)

As the Earth rotates from West to East:
- Your location moves into the sunlit side -- **sunrise** happens and day begins
- Your location moves into the dark side -- **sunset** happens and night begins

This is why **sunrise first occurs in eastern India** (like Arunachal Pradesh) and later in western India (like Gujarat).

### The Apparent Motion of the Sun

If you stand on the Equator and face North:
1. **Morning:** The Sun appears to rise from your **right side** (East)
2. **Noon:** The Sun appears overhead
3. **Evening:** The Sun disappears to your **left side** (West)

This is not because the Sun is moving. It is because the Earth is rotating and carrying you along with it!

> **Key idea:** Day and night are caused by the **rotation of the Earth**. The side facing the Sun has day, and the opposite side has night. As the Earth rotates, every place goes through a cycle of day and night.

## Stars and the Night Sky

### Stars Also Appear to Move

Since the Earth is rotating, **stars** also appear to move across the night sky, just like the Sun appears to move during the day.

However, there is one star that appears to stay almost still -- the **Pole Star (Dhruva Tara)**. This is because the Earth's axis of rotation points very close to the Pole Star. All other stars appear to move around it in circles.

If you observe a constellation like the **Big Dipper (Saptarishi)** at different times during the same night, you will notice it changes position, appearing to rotate around the Pole Star.

### Aryabhata's Observation

The great Indian mathematician and astronomer **Aryabhata** (5th century CE) understood the apparent motion of stars. In his famous work **Aryabhatiya**, he compared it to a person in a moving boat who sees stationary objects on the shore appear to move backward. Similarly, the stars appear to move because we are on the rotating Earth.

Aryabhata calculated that the Earth takes approximately **23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4.1 seconds** to complete one rotation -- impressively close to the modern accepted value!

## Revolution of the Earth

### What is Revolution?

While rotating on its own axis, the Earth also **revolves** (moves in a path) around the Sun. This movement is different from rotation.

- **Rotation** = the Earth spinning on its own axis (like a top spinning)
- **Revolution** = the Earth moving around the Sun (like going around a track)

Key facts about Earth's revolution:
- The Earth's orbit (path) around the Sun is **nearly circular**
- The Earth completes one revolution in about **365 days and 6 hours** (approximately 1 year)

### Changing Night Sky Throughout the Year

Because the Earth revolves around the Sun, the stars visible in the night sky gradually change over the course of a year. Different constellations are visible in different months. This is why you were told to look for certain constellations at specific times of the year in earlier grades.

> **Key idea:** The Earth revolves around the Sun in about **365 days and 6 hours**. This revolution causes the visible night sky to change gradually throughout the year.

## Seasons on Earth

### Why Do Seasons Change?

You may have noticed that days are longer in summer and shorter in winter. And that summers are hotter while winters are cold. But why?

The answer lies in two facts:
1. The Earth's **axis of rotation is tilted** (it is not upright with respect to its orbit)
2. The Earth maintains this tilt as it orbits around the Sun

![Earth's orbit around the Sun showing the tilted axis and positions for summer and winter solstices and equinoxes](earth-seasons-tilt-orbit.png)

### How Seasons Work

**In June (Summer in Northern Hemisphere):**
- The **Northern Hemisphere** is tilted **towards** the Sun
- Sunrays fall more directly on the Northern Hemisphere, spreading over a **smaller area** and heating it more
- The Northern Hemisphere receives sunlight for **more than 12 hours** each day
- Result: **Summer** in the Northern Hemisphere (and **winter** in the Southern Hemisphere)

**In December (Winter in Northern Hemisphere):**
- The **Northern Hemisphere** is tilted **away** from the Sun
- Sunrays fall at a greater angle, spreading over a **larger area** and providing less heat
- The Northern Hemisphere receives sunlight for **less than 12 hours** each day
- Result: **Winter** in the Northern Hemisphere (and **summer** in the Southern Hemisphere)

This is why when it is summer in India (Northern Hemisphere), it is winter in Australia (Southern Hemisphere), and vice versa!

> **Key idea:** Seasons occur because of the **tilt of the Earth's axis** and the **spherical shape** of the Earth. The hemisphere tilted towards the Sun experiences summer, while the hemisphere tilted away experiences winter.

### Important Dates

| Event | Date (approx.) | What Happens |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **Summer Solstice** | 21 June | Longest day in Northern Hemisphere |
| **Winter Solstice** | 22 December | Shortest day in Northern Hemisphere |
| **Spring Equinox** | 21 March | Day and night are equal (12 hours each) |
| **Autumn Equinox** | 23 September | Day and night are equal (12 hours each) |

### Special Cases

- At the **North Pole**, the Sun rises on 21 March and remains in the sky continuously for **6 months**, setting on 22 September. This is followed by 6 months of darkness. The South Pole experiences the opposite.
- On the **Equator**, there are always about 12 hours of sunlight and 12 hours of darkness throughout the year. This is why southern states of India (close to the equator) do not experience dramatic seasonal changes.

### Common Misconceptions

Two incorrect explanations often given for seasons:
- "The Northern Hemisphere is closer to the Sun when tilted towards it" -- The distance difference is too small to cause seasons.
- "The Earth's orbit is oval-shaped, so it is at different distances from the Sun" -- In fact, the Earth is **closest** to the Sun in **January** (during winter in the Northern Hemisphere)!

## Eclipses

### Solar Eclipse

A **solar eclipse** occurs when the **Moon comes between the Sun and the Earth**, blocking sunlight from reaching part of the Earth.

![Solar eclipse diagram showing the Moon between the Sun and the Earth casting a shadow on Earth](solar-eclipse-diagram.png)

**How is this possible?** The Moon is much smaller than the Sun. But the Moon is also much **closer** to the Earth than the Sun is. Because of this, the **apparent size** (how big it looks from Earth) of the Moon and the Sun are nearly the same. This allows the Moon to appear to cover the entire Sun!

You can understand this by holding your thumb at arm's length -- your small thumb can cover a friend's entire head far away because your thumb is much closer to your eye.

**Types of solar eclipse:**

| Type | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **Total solar eclipse** | The Moon completely covers the Sun. The sky becomes dark for a few minutes. Visible only from a small area on Earth. |
| **Partial solar eclipse** | The Moon partially covers the Sun. Some sunlight still reaches the Earth. Visible from a larger area. |

During a total solar eclipse, it turns dark during the day for a few minutes. As the Moon moves away, a beautiful **"diamond ring"** effect can be seen.

**Safety Warning:** Never look directly at the Sun during a solar eclipse -- not even through sunglasses, binoculars, or telescopes. The Sun is intense enough to cause **permanent eye damage and blindness**. The safest way to observe a solar eclipse is to attend viewing events organised by planetaria and astronomy clubs, where specialised eye protection is provided.

> **Key idea:** A **solar eclipse** occurs when the Moon comes between the Sun and the Earth, blocking sunlight. Never look directly at the Sun during an eclipse.

### Lunar Eclipse

![Lunar eclipse diagram showing the Earth between the Sun and the Moon with the Moon in Earth's shadow](lunar-eclipse-diagram.png)

A **lunar eclipse** occurs when the **Earth comes between the Sun and the Moon**, blocking sunlight from reaching the Moon.

**Types of lunar eclipse:**

| Type | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **Total lunar eclipse** | The Moon is completely in the Earth's shadow. It appears dark red in colour. |
| **Partial lunar eclipse** | Part of the Moon is in the Earth's shadow, while the rest remains visible. |

Unlike solar eclipses, lunar eclipses can be safely watched with the naked eye. A total lunar eclipse can also be seen from a much larger area of the Earth compared to a total solar eclipse.

> **Key idea:** A **lunar eclipse** occurs when the Earth comes between the Sun and the Moon, casting a shadow on the Moon. During a total lunar eclipse, the Moon appears dark red.

### Eclipses in Indian History

Eclipses (called **grahan** in Sanskrit) have been observed and recorded since ancient times in India. Texts like the **Surya Siddhanta** provide calculations to predict eclipses. Today, scientists travel around the world to observe eclipses, which provide opportunities to study phenomena not observable at other times.

## Summary

- The Earth **rotates** on its axis from West to East, completing one rotation in about **24 hours**. This causes **day and night**.
- The rotation of the Earth causes the apparent motion of the Sun, Moon, and stars across the sky.
- The **Pole Star** appears nearly stationary because the Earth's axis points close to it.
- The Earth **revolves** around the Sun in about **365 days and 6 hours** (1 year). This causes the night sky to change throughout the year.
- **Seasons** occur because of the **tilt of the Earth's axis** and its spherical shape. The hemisphere tilted towards the Sun experiences summer; the one tilted away experiences winter.
- The longest day in the Northern Hemisphere is around **21 June** (summer solstice), and the shortest day is around **22 December** (winter solstice).
- A **solar eclipse** occurs when the Moon blocks sunlight from reaching the Earth. Never view a solar eclipse directly.
- A **lunar eclipse** occurs when the Earth blocks sunlight from reaching the Moon. It can be viewed safely with the naked eye.
