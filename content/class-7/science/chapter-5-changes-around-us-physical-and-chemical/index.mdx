## Introduction

Look around you. Changes are happening everywhere, all the time! Ice melts in your glass of water. A banana gets brown spots and develops a strong smell. A bud on a rose plant becomes a flower. Wet clothes dry on the clothesline.

But are all these changes the same? In this chapter, we will learn to classify changes into two main types -- **physical changes** and **chemical changes** -- and understand what makes them different.

> **Key idea:** Changes happen all around us constantly. Some changes only alter the appearance of a substance, while others create entirely new substances.

---

## Physical Changes

### What is a Physical Change?

A **physical change** is one in which a substance or object changes its physical properties (like shape, size, or state) but **no new substance is formed**. The material itself remains the same.

**Examples of physical changes:**

- **Folding paper** into different shapes -- When you unfold them, you get the same paper back.
- **Inflating a balloon** -- When you let the air out, the balloon returns to its original form.
- **Crushing chalk** -- The chalk becomes powder, but the powder is still chalk.
- **Melting ice** -- Ice becomes water, but it is still the same substance (H2O). You can freeze it back into ice.
- **Boiling water** -- Water becomes steam, but it is still water. You can condense it back to liquid.
- **Drying wet clothes** -- The water evaporates, but both the clothes and the water remain unchanged.
- **Dissolving sugar in water** -- The sugar seems to disappear, but it is still there (you can taste it!).

> **Key idea:** In a **physical change**, only the physical properties (shape, size, state) of a substance change. No new substance is formed. Many physical changes can be reversed.

---

## Chemical Changes

### What is a Chemical Change?

A **chemical change** is one in which one or more **new substances** are formed. The original substance transforms into something entirely different. New substances are formed through a process called a **chemical reaction**.

### Activity: Blowing Air into Lime Water

Try this simple experiment to see a chemical change:

1. Take two glass tumblers. Fill one-fourth of tumbler A with **tap water** and one-fourth of tumbler B with **lime water**.
2. Blow air (exhale) into each tumbler using separate straws.
3. **Tumbler A:** Only bubbles form. The water remains unchanged.
4. **Tumbler B:** The lime water turns **milky** (cloudy)! A white substance settles at the bottom.

What happened? The **carbon dioxide** in your breath reacted with the lime water (calcium hydroxide) to form a new white substance called **calcium carbonate**, which is insoluble in water. This made the lime water appear milky.

**Chemical equation:**
Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide --> Calcium carbonate + Water

Since a new substance (calcium carbonate) was formed, this is a **chemical change**.

> **Key idea:** The turning of lime water milky is a standard test for **carbon dioxide** gas. Whenever lime water turns milky, it confirms the presence of carbon dioxide.

### Activity: Vinegar and Baking Soda

Another exciting chemical change you can try at home:

1. Take a teaspoonful of **vinegar** (or lemon juice) in a test tube or small bottle.
2. Add a pinch of **baking soda** (sodium hydrogen carbonate).
3. You will hear a **fizzing sound** and see **gas bubbles** forming.
4. If you pass this gas through lime water, it turns milky -- confirming the gas is **carbon dioxide**.

**Chemical equation:**
Vinegar + Baking soda --> Carbon dioxide + Other substances

Since a new substance (carbon dioxide gas) was formed, this is a **chemical change**.

> **Key idea:** In a **chemical change**, one or more new substances are formed through a chemical reaction. Chemical changes can often be identified by signs such as colour change, gas formation, heat production, or change in smell.

## Important Examples of Chemical Changes

### Rusting

When iron objects are left in moist air, they develop a **brown coating** called rust. Rust is a new substance -- **iron oxide**. Since a new substance is formed, rusting is a **chemical change**.

### Combustion (Burning)

**Combustion** is a chemical reaction in which a substance reacts with **oxygen** and produces **heat and/or light**.

**Example -- Burning a magnesium ribbon:**
- Magnesium + Oxygen --> Magnesium oxide + Heat + Light
- A new substance (magnesium oxide, a white powder) is formed, so this is a chemical change.

Substances that can undergo combustion are called **combustible substances**. Examples include wood, paper, cotton, kerosene, coal, and cooking gas (LPG).

### Three Requirements for Combustion (Fire Triangle)

For combustion to occur, three things are needed:

1. **A combustible substance** (fuel) -- such as wood, paper, or kerosene
2. **Oxygen** (from air) -- without oxygen, the fuel cannot burn
3. **Heat** -- to raise the temperature of the fuel to its **ignition temperature**

The **ignition temperature** is the minimum temperature at which a substance catches fire.

**Demonstration:** If you cover a burning candle with a glass tumbler, the flame goes out after a short time because the oxygen supply is cut off. The uncovered candle continues to burn because it has a continuous supply of air.

> **Key idea:** Combustion requires three things: a combustible substance (fuel), oxygen, and heat to reach the ignition temperature. Remove any one of these, and the fire goes out. This is called the **fire triangle**.

### Putting Out Fire

If a person's clothes catch fire, wrapping a blanket or thick cloth around them cuts off the air supply, and the fire is extinguished. **Never use synthetic cloth** for this purpose, as it can melt and stick to the skin.

## Can Both Physical and Chemical Changes Happen Together?

Yes! A great example is a **burning candle**:

- The **wax melts** due to heat -- this is a **physical change** (solid wax becomes liquid wax)
- The melted wax **flows down** and **solidifies** again in a new shape -- **physical change**
- The liquid wax is drawn up the wick and **evaporates** -- **physical change**
- The wax vapour **burns** in the flame, producing carbon dioxide and water -- **chemical change**

So, a burning candle involves **both physical and chemical changes** happening at the same time!

> **Key idea:** Some processes involve both physical and chemical changes simultaneously. The burning of a candle is a classic example.

---

## Can Changes Be Reversed?

Some changes can be **reversed** (you can get the original substance back), while others **cannot be reversed**.

| Change | Can it be reversed? | Type |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| Melting ice cubes | Yes (freeze them again) | Physical |
| Boiling water | Yes (condense the steam) | Physical |
| Rolling up a mat | Yes (unroll it) | Physical |
| Dissolving sugar in water | Yes (evaporate the water) | Physical |
| Chopping vegetables | No | Physical |
| Making popcorn from corn | No | Chemical |
| Burning wood | No | Chemical |
| Rusting of iron | No | Chemical |
| Curdling of milk | No | Chemical |
| Ripening of fruits | No | Chemical |

Most **chemical changes cannot be reversed** because new substances with different properties are formed. Some **physical changes** can be reversed, but not all (for example, chopping vegetables changes the shape permanently).

> **Key idea:** Changes that can be reversed are called **reversible changes**. Changes that cannot be reversed are called **irreversible changes**. Most chemical changes are irreversible.

## Are All Changes Desirable?

### Desirable Changes

Many changes are useful and welcome:
- Cooking food
- Changing milk into curd (dahi)
- Ripening of fruits
- Cutting vegetables for cooking

### Undesirable Changes

Some changes are harmful or unwanted:
- Rusting of iron (damages structures and tools)
- Decay of stored food (food waste)
- Burning of forests (destruction of nature)

### Changes Can Be Both!

A change that is undesirable in one situation may be desirable in another. For example:
- **Decomposition of food** is undesirable when it spoils food in your refrigerator, but it is **desirable** when food waste is converted into **compost** for gardening.

### Environmental Impact

Some changes caused by human activity have a long-term impact on the environment:
- Burning fuels in cars, trains, and aeroplanes increases **carbon dioxide** in the atmosphere, contributing to **climate change**.
- Drying paint on walls releases harmful substances through evaporation, causing **air pollution**.

## Slow Natural Changes

### Weathering of Rocks

Have you seen heaps of sand, soil, and stones at the base of mountains? These are called **sediments**, and they are formed by the slow breaking down of large rocks over thousands of years.

**What causes weathering?**

- **Temperature changes** due to weather and climate
- **Growing roots** of trees that push into cracks in rocks
- **Freezing of water** within cracks (water expands when it freezes, splitting the rock)
- **Chemical reactions** between water and the minerals in rocks

For example, basalt (a black rock containing iron) can develop a red-coloured layer when its surface is exposed to water and air for a long time. The red colour comes from **iron oxide** formed by a chemical reaction -- similar to rusting.

These physical and chemical changes in rocks are collectively called **weathering**, and they eventually lead to the formation of **soil**.

> **Key idea:** **Weathering** is the process by which rocks are broken down by physical and chemical changes over long periods. It is one of the main processes that form soil.

### Erosion

When rock pebbles, soil, and sediments are broken down and **moved from one place to another** by natural forces like wind and flowing water, the process is called **erosion**.

- River rocks appear smooth because of constant erosion by flowing water.
- When the speed of water or wind decreases, the material settles down (this is called **deposition**).
- Over time, deposited sediments harden and become new rocks.
- Most of these changes take thousands of years and cannot be reversed.

## Summary

- A **physical change** is one where only the physical properties (shape, size, state) of a substance change and no new substance is formed.
- A **chemical change** is one where one or more new substances are formed through a chemical reaction.
- **Combustion** is a chemical reaction in which a substance reacts with oxygen, producing heat and/or light.
- For combustion to occur, three things are needed: a combustible substance (fuel), oxygen, and heat to reach the **ignition temperature** (the fire triangle).
- Some processes (like a burning candle) involve both physical and chemical changes.
- Some changes can be **reversed** (like melting ice) and some **cannot** (like burning wood).
- Some changes are **desirable** (like cooking food) while others are **undesirable** (like rusting of iron).
- **Weathering** (physical and chemical breakdown of rocks) leads to the formation of soil.
- **Erosion** is the movement of broken rock material by wind and water.
