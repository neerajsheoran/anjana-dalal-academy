## Introduction

Imagine watching a 100 metre race at the Olympics. Two sprinters seem to cross the finish line at almost the same moment, yet the timing system can identify the winner with incredible precision. Modern technology can measure time down to one-thousandth of a second!

But how did people measure time in ancient days when there were no clocks and watches? And how do we decide if something is moving fast or slow? In this chapter, we will explore the measurement of time and understand the concept of speed and motion.

> **Key idea:** Measuring time accurately has been one of humanity's oldest challenges. From sundials to atomic clocks, we have come a long way in measuring time.

---

## Measurement of Time in Ancient Days

Long ago, people noticed that many events in nature repeat themselves after fixed intervals. The rising and setting of the Sun, the phases of the Moon, and the changing seasons all follow regular cycles. People used these cycles to keep track of time and create calendars.

To measure smaller intervals of time within a day, ancient people invented several clever devices:

### Sundial

A **sundial** measures time using the changing position of the shadow of an object cast by sunlight. As the Sun moves across the sky, the shadow moves and points to different markings that indicate the time.

The world's largest stone sundial, the **Samrat Yantra**, was built about 300 years ago at the **Jantar Mantar in Jaipur**, Rajasthan. It stands 27 metres tall, and its shadow moves at about 1 millimetre per second. It can measure time intervals as short as 2 seconds!

### Water Clock

Water clocks used the flow of water to measure time. There were two types:
- **Water flowing out type:** Water flowed out from a vessel that had markings for time on its side.
- **Floating bowl type (Ghatika-yantra):** A bowl with a tiny hole at the bottom was floated on water. It gradually filled up and sank in a fixed time (about 24 minutes). Then it was lifted and floated again.

In ancient India, the **Ghatika-yantra** was widely used at Buddhist monasteries, royal palaces, and town squares. Each time the bowl sank, it was announced by drums, conch shells, or striking a gong.

### Hourglass

An hourglass measured time based on the flow of sand from one glass bulb to another through a narrow passage.

### Candle Clock

Candle clocks were candles with markings on them. As the candle burned, the markings indicated how much time had passed.

> **Key idea:** Ancient time-measuring devices like sundials, water clocks, hourglasses, and candle clocks all worked on the principle of a repeating process happening at regular intervals.

## The Simple Pendulum

The invention of the **pendulum clock** in the 17th century was a major breakthrough in timekeeping. It was based on an important discovery by Galileo Galilei.

### Galileo's Discovery

It is said that once while sitting in a church, Galileo noticed a lamp suspended from the ceiling swinging back and forth. Using his pulse to measure time, he found that the lamp took the **same time for each swing**, regardless of how wide the swing was.

### What is a Simple Pendulum?

A **simple pendulum** consists of:
- A small metallic ball (called the **bob**) suspended from a rigid support by a long thread

When the bob is pulled slightly to one side and released, it swings back and forth. This is called **oscillatory motion** -- it repeats its path after a fixed interval of time.

### One Oscillation

The pendulum completes **one oscillation** when its bob:
1. Starts from its resting position (mean position O)
2. Moves to one extreme position (A)
3. Changes direction and moves to the other extreme position (B)
4. Changes direction and comes back to O

The time taken to complete one oscillation is called the **time period** of the pendulum.

### Activity: Measure the Time Period

1. Tie a heavy metal ball or stone (bob) to one end of a string about 100 cm long.
2. Fix the other end to a rigid support.
3. Pull the bob slightly to one side and release it gently (do not push it).
4. Using a stopwatch, measure the time for 10 oscillations.
5. Divide by 10 to get the time period of one oscillation.
6. Repeat 3-4 times. You will find the time period is nearly the same each time.

### What Affects the Time Period?

| Factor | Effect on Time Period |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Length of the pendulum** | Longer pendulum = longer time period |
| **Mass of the bob** | Does NOT affect the time period |
| **Location** | The time period at a given location is constant |

> **Key idea:** The time period of a simple pendulum depends only on its **length**, not on the mass of the bob. All pendulums of the same length have the same time period at a given location. This property is used in pendulum clocks.

## Modern Clocks

All clocks -- old or modern -- are based on the same basic principle: a **process that repeats continuously** at regular intervals.

- **Pendulum clocks** use the swinging of a pendulum
- **Quartz clocks** use tiny, very rapid vibrations of a quartz crystal
- **Atomic clocks** use vibrations of specific atoms

While early pendulum clocks could gain or lose 10 seconds each day, modern atomic clocks are so precise that they lose only **one second in millions of years**!

## The SI Unit of Time

The **SI unit of time** is the **second** (symbol: **s**).

Larger units of time:
- 60 seconds = 1 minute (min)
- 60 minutes = 1 hour (h)

**Important rules for writing units:**
- Units start with a lowercase letter (second, minute, hour), except at the beginning of a sentence
- Symbols are written in lowercase and singular form: **s**, **min**, **h**
- No full stop after the symbol (unless at the end of a sentence)
- Always leave a space between the number and the unit (e.g., 10 s, not 10s)
- Writing "sec" for second or "hrs" for hour is incorrect

## Slow or Fast: Understanding Speed

When you watch a race, how do you decide who is running faster? The runner who covers **more distance in the same time** is faster. We say that the faster runner has a higher **speed**.

### What is Speed?

**Speed** is the distance covered by an object in unit time. It tells us how fast or slow something is moving.

**Formula:**

**Speed = Total distance covered / Total time taken**

### Units of Speed

Since speed = distance / time:
- If distance is in **metres** and time is in **seconds**, speed is in **m/s** (metres per second)
- If distance is in **kilometres** and time is in **hours**, speed is in **km/h** (kilometres per hour)

### Example Problems

**Example 1:** Swati's school is 3.6 km from her house. She reaches school in 15 minutes by bicycle. What is the speed of her bicycle?

- Distance = 3.6 km = 3600 m
- Time = 15 min = 900 s
- Speed = 3600 / 900 = **4 m/s**

**Example 2:** A bus moves at a speed of 50 km/h for 2 hours. How far does it travel?

- Distance = Speed x Time = 50 x 2 = **100 km**

**Example 3:** A train travels at 90 km/h. How long will it take to cover 360 km?

- Time = Distance / Speed = 360 / 90 = **4 hours**

> **Key idea:** Speed = Distance / Time. This formula can be rearranged to find distance (Distance = Speed x Time) or time (Time = Distance / Speed).

### Speedometer and Odometer

Vehicles like cars, buses, and bikes have two important instruments:
- **Speedometer:** Measures and displays the vehicle's current speed in km/h
- **Odometer:** Measures the total distance travelled by the vehicle in kilometres

## Uniform and Non-uniform Linear Motion

When an object moves along a straight line, its motion is called **linear motion**.

### Uniform Linear Motion

An object moving along a straight line with a **constant speed** (speed that does not change) is said to be in **uniform linear motion**.

In uniform linear motion, the object covers **equal distances in equal intervals of time**.

**Example:** A train moves along a straight track covering 20 km every 10 minutes. In 60 minutes, it covers:
- 0-10 min: 20 km
- 10-20 min: 20 km
- 20-30 min: 20 km
- 30-40 min: 20 km
- 40-50 min: 20 km
- 50-60 min: 20 km

Each 10-minute interval has the same distance (20 km), so this is uniform motion.

### Non-uniform Linear Motion

If the speed of an object moving along a straight line **keeps changing**, it is said to be in **non-uniform linear motion**.

In non-uniform motion, the object covers **unequal distances in equal intervals of time**.

**Example:** A car in city traffic speeds up, slows down, and stops at traffic signals. In 10-minute intervals, it might cover 15 km, then 35 km, then 50 km -- different distances in the same time. This is non-uniform motion.

### Real Life

A train starting from one station and reaching the next illustrates both types:
1. It starts **slowly** from station A (non-uniform)
2. It reaches a **constant speed** between stations B and C (uniform)
3. It **slows down** and stops at station D (non-uniform)

> **Key idea:** **Uniform motion** means constant speed (equal distances in equal time intervals). **Non-uniform motion** means changing speed (unequal distances in equal time intervals). In everyday life, non-uniform motion is much more common.

## Average Speed

In real life, objects rarely move at a constant speed throughout their journey. A car in city traffic speeds up, slows down, and stops at signals. In such cases, we calculate the **average speed**:

**Average Speed = Total distance covered / Total time taken**

**Example:** A car covers 60 km in the first hour, 70 km in the second hour, and 50 km in the third hour.
- Total distance = 60 + 70 + 50 = 180 km
- Total time = 3 hours
- Average speed = 180 / 3 = **60 km/h**

Even though the car did not maintain a constant speed of 60 km/h throughout, its average speed gives us a useful measure of how fast it was moving overall.

## Summary

- Ancient time-measuring devices include **sundials**, **water clocks** (Ghatika-yantra), **hourglasses**, and **candle clocks**.
- A **simple pendulum** completes one oscillation when its bob moves from the mean position to one extreme, then to the other extreme, and back to the mean position.
- The **time period** of a pendulum is the time for one complete oscillation. It depends on the **length** of the pendulum but **not** on the mass of the bob.
- The **SI unit of time** is the **second (s)**. 60 s = 1 min, 60 min = 1 h.
- **Speed** = Total distance covered / Total time taken. Its SI unit is **m/s**.
- An object in **uniform linear motion** moves along a straight line with constant speed, covering equal distances in equal time intervals.
- An object in **non-uniform linear motion** moves along a straight line with changing speed, covering unequal distances in equal time intervals.
- In everyday life, **non-uniform motion** is much more common than uniform motion.
