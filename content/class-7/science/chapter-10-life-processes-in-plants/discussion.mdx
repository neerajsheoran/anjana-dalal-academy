## Plants Make Their Own Food

**Kiran:** Papa, animals eat food, but I have never seen a plant eat. How do they get their energy?

**Ravi:** Plants are extraordinary -- they make their own food through photosynthesis! They use sunlight, water from the soil, and carbon dioxide from the air to produce glucose, releasing oxygen as a by-product. This happens mainly in the leaves, which is why leaves are called the food factories of plants.

**Kiran:** Is that why leaves are green?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Leaves contain a green pigment called chlorophyll that captures sunlight. Without chlorophyll, photosynthesis cannot happen. If you test a leaf that has both green and white patches, only the green patches test positive for starch (using the iodine test). The white patches -- without chlorophyll -- produce no starch.

**Kiran:** And without sunlight?

**Ravi:** A plant kept in the dark produces no starch either, even in its green parts. Both chlorophyll and sunlight are essential. This is why plants near a window grow towards the light -- they are literally reaching for their energy source.

## Proving Photosynthesis

**Kiran:** How do scientists know that carbon dioxide is needed for photosynthesis?

**Ravi:** A clever experiment proves it. Cover half a leaf with a bottle containing caustic soda (which absorbs carbon dioxide). The other half stays exposed to normal air. After a few hours in sunlight, test both halves with iodine. The exposed half turns blue-black (starch present), but the covered half does not.

**Kiran:** And oxygen is released during photosynthesis?

**Ravi:** Yes! Place a water plant like hydrilla in a beaker of water in sunlight. You will see bubbles rising from the plant. Collect these bubbles and test with a lit matchstick -- it produces an intense flame, confirming the gas is oxygen.

**Kiran:** So the complete equation is: carbon dioxide + water, in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll, produces glucose and oxygen?

**Ravi:** Perfect. The glucose serves as immediate energy and is also converted to starch for storage. Plants store starch in different parts -- seeds (rice, wheat), roots (carrot, sweet potato), stems (potato, ginger), and fruits (mango, apple).

## Transport in Plants

**Kiran:** Papa, how does water from the roots reach leaves that may be 30 metres above the ground in a tall tree?

**Ravi:** Through a network of tube-like structures called xylem. Try this experiment at home -- place a plant with white flowers in water mixed with red ink. Within a day, the stem, leaves, and even the flowers turn red. The ink travels upward through the xylem, just as water and dissolved minerals do.

**Kiran:** And how does food from the leaves reach the roots?

**Ravi:** Through another set of tubes called phloem. Xylem carries water and minerals upward from roots to leaves. Phloem carries food downward from leaves to all other parts. Together, they form the plant's complete transport system.

**Kiran:** It is like a two-way highway -- water going up and food going down!

**Ravi:** Great analogy. And the stomata -- tiny pores on leaf surfaces -- are like toll plazas where carbon dioxide enters and oxygen exits. This transport system is remarkably efficient and powers everything from tiny herbs to giant banyan trees.

## Plants Respire Too

**Kiran:** Papa, do plants also breathe?

**Ravi:** Yes! All living cells of a plant respire -- whether green or non-green, day and night. Respiration breaks down glucose using oxygen to release energy, producing carbon dioxide and water.

**Kiran:** But during the day, do not plants absorb carbon dioxide for photosynthesis?

**Ravi:** They do both simultaneously! During the day, photosynthesis produces far more oxygen than respiration consumes, so the net effect is oxygen release. At night, with no photosynthesis, only respiration occurs, so plants release carbon dioxide.

**Kiran:** That is why people sometimes say you should not sleep under a tree at night?

**Ravi:** That is the reasoning, though the amount of carbon dioxide is usually too small to be harmful outdoors. The important point is that photosynthesis and respiration are complementary processes. Photosynthesis stores energy from sunlight in glucose. Respiration releases that energy when the plant needs it. Together, they maintain the oxygen-carbon dioxide balance in nature.

## Why Photosynthesis Matters for All Life

**Kiran:** If plants stopped doing photosynthesis, what would happen?

**Ravi:** It would be catastrophic. Plants produce the oxygen that all animals breathe. They are also the base of every food chain -- herbivores eat plants, carnivores eat herbivores. Without photosynthesis, there would be no food and no oxygen for any animal on Earth.

**Kiran:** So plants are the foundation of all life?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Every bite of rice, every fruit, every vegetable exists because of photosynthesis. Even the chicken or fish you eat depends on plants somewhere in the food chain. And the oxygen you are breathing right now was produced by a plant or an algae.

**Kiran:** That makes me appreciate trees and plants so much more.

**Ravi:** As you should. Protecting forests and green cover is not just an environmental cause -- it is essential for human survival. When we plant trees, we are literally investing in our planet's ability to sustain life. That is why India's afforestation drives are so important.
