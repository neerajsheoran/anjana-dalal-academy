## Introduction

Animals eat food to grow, but have you ever seen plants eating food like animals do? Plants do not eat food the way animals do. Instead, they **make their own food** using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide from the air. This makes plants very special in the living world.

In this chapter, we will explore how plants prepare food, transport water and nutrients, and respire to release energy.

> **Key idea:** Unlike animals, plants prepare their own food through a process called **photosynthesis**. This makes them the foundation of almost all food chains on Earth.

---

## How Do Plants Grow?

As a plant grows, you can observe several changes:
- New leaves and branches appear
- Its height increases
- Its stem becomes thicker

But what causes these changes? Plants need certain things to grow:

### The Role of Sunlight and Water

An experiment with three pots of plants helps us understand what plants need:

| Pot | Conditions | Result after 2 weeks |
|-----|-----------|---------------------|
| **Pot A** | Sunlight + Water | Plant grows well, green leaves |
| **Pot B** | Sunlight + No water | Plant dies or wilts badly |
| **Pot C** | No sunlight + Water | Plant grows poorly, leaves turn yellow |

**Conclusion:** Plants require **both sunlight and water** for proper growth. Without either one, the plant cannot survive or grow well.

> **Key idea:** Plants need **sunlight**, **water**, and **minerals from the soil** to grow. Without these, they cannot prepare food or survive.

---

## Photosynthesis: How Plants Make Food

### Leaves -- The Food Factories of Plants

The **leaves** are where food is produced in plants. They are often broad and flat to capture maximum sunlight. Most leaves are **green** because they contain a green pigment called **chlorophyll**.

**Chlorophyll** plays a very important role -- it **captures sunlight** and uses it to prepare food.

Plants store food mainly in the form of **starch**, a type of carbohydrate. You can test for the presence of starch in a leaf using the **iodine test** -- if a decolourised leaf turns **blue-black** when iodine solution is added, it means starch is present.

### What is Needed for Photosynthesis?

Through several experiments, scientists have found that **four things** are needed for photosynthesis:

1. **Sunlight** -- the source of energy
2. **Water** -- absorbed by the roots from the soil
3. **Carbon dioxide** -- taken from the air
4. **Chlorophyll** -- the green pigment in leaves that captures sunlight

### Evidence from Experiments

**Experiment 1: Role of sunlight and chlorophyll**

Take a leaf that has both green and non-green (white or pale) patches. After testing with iodine:
- The **green patches** (with chlorophyll) that received sunlight turn **blue-black** (starch present)
- The **non-green patches** do NOT turn blue-black (no starch)
- A leaf from a plant kept in the dark shows **no starch** even in green patches

**Conclusion:** Both **chlorophyll** and **sunlight** are essential for producing starch.

**Experiment 2: Role of carbon dioxide**

Half a leaf is placed inside a bottle containing caustic soda (which absorbs carbon dioxide from the air). The other half remains outside in normal air.
- The half **outside** the bottle turns blue-black with iodine (starch present)
- The half **inside** the bottle does NOT turn blue-black (no starch)

**Conclusion:** **Carbon dioxide** from the air is essential for photosynthesis.

**Experiment 3: Oxygen is released**

When a water plant (like hydrilla) is placed in water under sunlight, **bubbles of gas** emerge and collect in an inverted test tube. When a lit matchstick is inserted into this tube, it produces an **intense flame**, confirming the gas is **oxygen**.

**Conclusion:** **Oxygen** is released as a by-product during photosynthesis.

> **Key idea:** During photosynthesis, plants use **carbon dioxide** and **water** in the presence of **sunlight** and **chlorophyll** to produce **glucose** (food) and release **oxygen**.

---

## The Photosynthesis Equation

The word equation for photosynthesis is:

**Carbon dioxide + Water ---(Sunlight, Chlorophyll)---> Glucose + Oxygen**

- **Raw materials:** Carbon dioxide (from air) and water (from soil)
- **Energy source:** Sunlight
- **Catalyst:** Chlorophyll (green pigment)
- **Products:** Glucose (food) and Oxygen (released into the air)

The glucose produced serves as an instant source of energy and is later converted into **starch** for storage. This starch can be stored in various parts of the plant -- leaves, stems, roots, seeds, and fruits.

---

## How Do Leaves Exchange Gases?

If you carefully peel a thin layer from the lower surface of a leaf and observe it under a microscope, you will notice tiny pores. These pores are called **stomata**.

**Stomata** are found on the surface of leaves and have two important functions:
- **Taking in carbon dioxide** from the air for photosynthesis
- **Releasing oxygen** produced during photosynthesis
- They also help in the release of excess water (transpiration)

> **Key idea:** **Stomata** are tiny pores on the surface of leaves that allow the exchange of gases -- carbon dioxide enters and oxygen exits during photosynthesis.

## Transport in Plants

### Transport of Water and Minerals

Water and minerals are absorbed from the soil by the **roots** of the plant. But how do they reach the leaves and other parts that may be many metres above the ground?

**Activity:** Place a plant with white flowers in a glass of water mixed with red ink. After a day, you will see that the stem, leaves, and even the flowers have turned red! If you cut the stem, you can see red-coloured tube-like structures.

These tube-like structures are called **xylem**. The xylem transports water and dissolved minerals **upward** from the roots to all parts of the plant -- stems, branches, leaves, and flowers.

### Transport of Food

Food is prepared in the leaves through photosynthesis. But all parts of the plant need food -- roots, stems, flowers, and fruits. How does the food reach them?

The food prepared in the leaves is transported to all other parts of the plant through another set of tube-like structures called **phloem**. The phloem carries food **from the leaves to all parts** of the plant.

The food may also be stored in various parts:
- **Seeds** (like wheat, rice, pulses)
- **Roots** (like carrot, radish, sweet potato)
- **Stems** (like potato, ginger)
- **Fruits** (like mango, apple)

> **Key idea:** Plants have two transport systems: **Xylem** carries water and minerals upward from roots to leaves. **Phloem** carries food from leaves to all parts of the plant.

## Do Plants Respire?

Yes! Just like animals, **all parts of a plant respire** -- whether green or non-green.

### Proving that Plants Respire

If you place soaked, germinating moong seeds in a sealed flask and leave them in the dark for 24 hours, and then pass the air from the flask through lime water, the lime water turns **milky**. This proves that the seeds produced **carbon dioxide** during respiration.

### The Respiration Equation

**Glucose + Oxygen --> Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy**

During respiration:
- **Glucose** (food) is broken down using **oxygen**
- **Carbon dioxide** and **water** are released
- **Energy** is produced, which the plant uses for growth and development

### Photosynthesis vs Respiration

| Feature | Photosynthesis | Respiration |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **What happens?** | Food (glucose) is produced | Food (glucose) is broken down |
| **Raw materials** | Carbon dioxide + Water | Glucose + Oxygen |
| **Products** | Glucose + Oxygen | Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy |
| **Energy** | Energy from sunlight is absorbed | Energy is released |
| **When does it occur?** | Only in the presence of sunlight | All the time (day and night) |
| **Where does it occur?** | Only in green parts (leaves mainly) | In all parts of the plant |

> **Key idea:** **Photosynthesis** produces food and oxygen using sunlight. **Respiration** breaks down food using oxygen to release energy. Both processes are essential for the plant's survival and together they help maintain the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in nature.

---

## Why Photosynthesis Matters for All Life

Photosynthesis is not just important for plants. It is essential for **all life on Earth**:

- Plants produce **oxygen** during photosynthesis, which animals (including humans) need to breathe.
- Plants are the **primary producers of food** -- animals get their food directly or indirectly from plants.
- If all photosynthesising organisms disappeared, there would be no food and no oxygen for other living beings.

---

## Summary

- Plants need **sunlight**, **water**, and **minerals** for growth.
- **Photosynthesis** is the process by which plants make food (glucose) using carbon dioxide, water, sunlight, and chlorophyll. Oxygen is released as a by-product.
- **Leaves** are the primary site of photosynthesis and are called the "food factories" of plants.
- **Chlorophyll**, the green pigment in leaves, captures sunlight for photosynthesis.
- **Stomata** are tiny pores on leaf surfaces that allow the exchange of carbon dioxide and oxygen.
- **Xylem** transports water and minerals from roots upward to all parts of the plant.
- **Phloem** transports food from leaves to all other parts of the plant.
- Plants **respire** by breaking down glucose with oxygen to release energy, carbon dioxide, and water. All parts of the plant respire, day and night.
- Photosynthesis and respiration together maintain the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in nature.
