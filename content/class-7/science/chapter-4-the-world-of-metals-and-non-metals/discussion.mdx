## Properties That Set Metals Apart

**Kiran:** Papa, why is our cooking kadhai made of iron but its handle is made of wood?

**Ravi:** Because iron is a good conductor of heat -- it transfers heat from the flame to the food efficiently. But if the handle were also iron, you would burn your hand! Wood is a poor conductor, so it stays cool enough to hold.

**Kiran:** So metals conduct heat and non-metals do not?

**Ravi:** That is one key difference. Metals are also lustrous (shiny), malleable (can be beaten into sheets), ductile (can be drawn into wires), and sonorous (produce a ringing sound when struck). Non-metals are generally dull, brittle, not ductile, and produce dull sounds.

**Kiran:** That is why temple bells and ghungroos are made of metal -- for that beautiful ringing sound!

**Ravi:** Exactly. The property is called sonority. And gold is so malleable that one gram can be beaten into a sheet covering several square metres. It is so ductile that one gram can be drawn into a 2-kilometre wire! That is why gold is used both for jewellery (sheets) and fine electronic wiring.

## Rusting and Corrosion

**Kiran:** Papa, the iron gate at our building has turned brown and flaky in places. What happened?

**Ravi:** That is rust -- iron oxide formed when iron reacts with both oxygen and moisture in the air. An experiment with three bottles proves this. An iron nail in dry air alone does not rust. A nail in water with no air does not rust. But a nail in moist air -- both air and water -- rusts quickly.

**Kiran:** So both air and water are needed?

**Ravi:** Yes. That is why iron objects in dry desert areas rust slowly, while those near the sea rust much faster due to the moisture. Copper develops a green coating and silver develops a black coating -- this general damage is called corrosion.

**Kiran:** How do we prevent rusting?

**Ravi:** Painting, oiling, greasing, or galvanisation (coating with zinc). The famous Iron Pillar near Qutub Minar in Delhi was made over 1600 years ago and has barely any rust! Ancient Indian metalworkers had remarkable skills in creating rust-resistant iron alloys.

## Metals React with Air

**Kiran:** What happens when metals burn in air?

**Ravi:** They form metal oxides, which are basic in nature. Magnesium ribbon burns with a dazzling white flame and forms white magnesium oxide powder. When you dissolve this in water and test with litmus, it turns red litmus blue -- confirming it is basic.

**Kiran:** And what about non-metals burning in air?

**Ravi:** Non-metals form non-metal oxides, which are acidic. When sulfur burns, it produces sulfur dioxide gas. Dissolved in water, this forms sulfurous acid, which turns blue litmus red.

**Kiran:** So metals give basic oxides and non-metals give acidic oxides. That is a clean distinction!

**Ravi:** Yes, and it connects to the acids and bases chapter. The chemistry of metals and non-metals is interlinked with indicators and neutralisation. This is why sodium is stored in kerosene -- it reacts violently with both oxygen and water. And phosphorus is stored in water because it catches fire in air!

## Exceptions to the Rules

**Kiran:** Are there metals that do not behave like typical metals?

**Ravi:** Yes! Sodium and potassium are so soft you can cut them with a knife -- unlike hard metals like iron. Mercury is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature -- you may have seen it in old thermometers.

**Kiran:** And are all non-metals brittle?

**Ravi:** Most are, but graphite (a form of carbon) conducts electricity even though it is a non-metal. Diamond (also carbon) is the hardest known natural substance. So even within non-metals, there is variety.

**Kiran:** Science always has exceptions!

**Ravi:** That is what makes it interesting. The general patterns help us understand and predict, but the exceptions challenge us to dig deeper.

## Why Non-Metals Matter

**Kiran:** Metals seem more useful since they are used for construction, tools, and jewellery. Are non-metals important too?

**Ravi:** Absolutely essential! We breathe oxygen, a non-metal. All living things are built from carbon, a non-metal. Nitrogen is used in fertilisers that feed India's crops. Chlorine purifies our drinking water. Iodine is used as an antiseptic on wounds.

**Kiran:** So metals build our infrastructure, but non-metals sustain our lives?

**Ravi:** Well put. And remember, metals and non-metals are types of elements -- pure substances. Materials like plastic, glass, and wood are not elements. There are 118 known elements, and understanding their properties helps us choose the right material for every purpose.

**Kiran:** From the iron in our buildings to the oxygen in our lungs, chemistry is everywhere.

**Ravi:** And that connection between materials and their properties is exactly what this chapter teaches you to appreciate.
