## Introduction

Look around you. The utensils in your kitchen, the coins in your pocket, the wires in your walls, and the tools used by farmers -- so many things are made of metals! But have you ever wondered what makes metals different from other materials like coal, wood, or sulfur?

In this chapter, we will explore the properties of **metals** and **non-metals**, understand how they behave with air and water, and learn why they are so important in our everyday lives.

> **Key idea:** Materials around us can be broadly classified as **metals** and **non-metals** based on their physical and chemical properties.

---

## Properties of Metals

### Lustre

Metals have a shiny appearance. This shine is called **metallic lustre**. Think of a new copper coin, a gold ring, or a polished steel plate -- they all have a characteristic shine.

Non-metals like coal, sulfur, and wood are **non-lustrous** (they do not shine like metals).

### Malleability

If you place an iron nail on a hard surface and beat it with a hammer, it becomes flat. It does not break. This property of metals by which they can be **beaten into thin sheets** is called **malleability**.

- **Gold** and **silver** are the most malleable metals.
- Thin silver foil (chandi ka warq) on sweets and aluminium foil used for wrapping food are examples of malleability.

Non-metals like coal and sulfur are **brittle** -- they break into pieces when you try to beat them with a hammer.

> **Key idea:** **Malleability** is the property that allows metals to be beaten into thin sheets without breaking. Most metals are malleable, while non-metals are brittle.

### Ductility

Have you seen copper or aluminium wires used in electrical fittings? Or the metal strings on a sitar, veena, or guitar? These wires are possible because metals can be drawn into thin wires.

This property of metals by which they can be **drawn into wires** is called **ductility**.

- **Gold** is so ductile that one gram of it can be drawn into a wire 2 kilometres long!
- Copper, aluminium, and iron are also highly ductile.

Non-metals like coal and sulfur are **not ductile** -- you cannot draw them into wires.

### Sonority

Drop a metal spoon on the floor. Now drop a piece of wood. Do you notice the difference in sound?

The metal spoon produces a **ringing sound**, while wood produces a dull sound. This property of metals that enables them to produce a ringing sound when struck is called **sonority**. Metals are said to be **sonorous**.

This is why:
- School bells are made of metal
- Musical instruments like cymbals, ghungroos, and manjiras are made of metal
- Temple bells produce a beautiful ringing sound

### Conduction of Heat

Why are cooking vessels made of metals like iron, aluminium, or copper? Why are their handles made of wood or plastic?

**Activity:** Place a metal spoon and a wooden spoon in a glass of hot water. After a few minutes, touch the upper end of each spoon. The metal spoon feels much hotter than the wooden spoon.

This shows that metals are **good conductors of heat** -- they transfer heat easily from one end to the other. Wood, on the other hand, is a **poor conductor of heat**.

This is why:
- **Metal vessels** are used for cooking (they conduct heat well)
- **Wooden or plastic handles** are used on cooking vessels (they do not conduct heat, protecting your hands)

### Conduction of Electricity

Using the tester circuit you learned about in the chapter on electricity, you can test which materials conduct electricity.

| Material | Lamp Glows? | Conductor/Insulator |
|----------|------------|-------------------|
| Aluminium foil | Yes | Good conductor |
| Iron nail | Yes | Good conductor |
| Copper wire | Yes | Good conductor |
| Sulfur | No | Poor conductor |
| Coal | No | Poor conductor |
| Wood | No | Poor conductor |
| Rubber eraser | No | Poor conductor |
| Nylon rope | No | Poor conductor |

Metals are **good conductors of electricity**, while non-metals are **poor conductors** (insulators).

This is why electricians' screwdrivers have **plastic handles** and electricians wear **rubber gloves** and **rubber shoes** -- to protect themselves from electric shock.

> **Key idea:** Metals are generally hard, lustrous, malleable, ductile, sonorous, and good conductors of heat and electricity. Non-metals usually lack these properties.

---

## Some Exceptions

Not all metals behave the same way:

- **Sodium** and **potassium** are so soft that they can be cut with a knife.
- **Mercury** is the only metal that is found in **liquid state** at room temperature (you may have seen it in thermometers).
- **Wood** is interesting because it neither flattens into a sheet nor breaks into pieces when hammered -- it is neither malleable nor brittle.

## Effect of Air and Water on Metals

### Rusting of Iron

Have you noticed that iron objects left in the open develop a **brown coating** after a few days? This brown deposit is called **rust**, and the process is called **rusting**.

**What causes rusting?**

An experiment with three glass bottles helps us understand:

| Bottle | Conditions | Result |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| A | Iron nail in dry air only (with silica gel) | No rust |
| B | Iron nail in water only (covered with oil to keep air out) | No rust |
| C | Iron nail in contact with both air and water | Rust forms |

**Conclusion:** Both **air** and **water** are needed for rusting to occur. Dry air alone or water alone does not cause rust. It is **moist air** that causes iron to rust.

> **Key idea:** Rusting of iron requires the presence of both **air (oxygen)** and **water (moisture)**. Iron objects in dry air or in water without air do not rust.

### Corrosion of Other Metals

Rusting is not limited to iron. Other metals also get damaged when exposed to air and water:

- **Copper** objects develop a **green coating**
- **Silver** objects develop a **black coating**

The gradual damage to metal surfaces caused by air, water, or other substances is called **corrosion**.

### Preventing Rust

Rusting causes enormous damage to iron structures in India every year. It can be prevented by:

- **Painting** the iron surface
- **Oiling** or **greasing** the surface
- **Galvanisation** -- applying a protective layer of zinc metal on iron

### The Iron Pillar of Delhi

The famous Iron Pillar near Qutub Minar in Delhi was made more than **1600 years ago** during the time of Chandragupta II. It is about 8 metres tall and weighs over 6000 kilograms. Despite facing winds, rainfall, and harsh weather for centuries, it has barely any rust! This tells us about the remarkable skills of ancient Indian metalworkers.

## Reaction of Metals with Air (Oxygen)

When metals react with oxygen in the air, they form **metal oxides**. These metal oxides are **basic** in nature.

**Example -- Burning Magnesium Ribbon:**

1. A magnesium ribbon is cleaned with sandpaper and held with tongs.
2. It is ignited using a spirit lamp or candle.
3. It burns with a **dazzling white flame** and forms a white powder called **magnesium oxide**.
4. When water is added to magnesium oxide and tested with litmus paper, it turns **red litmus to blue**.
5. This means magnesium oxide is **basic** in nature.

> **Key idea:** When metals burn in air (oxygen), they form **metal oxides** that are **basic** in nature. This is a key difference between metals and non-metals.

**Special case -- Sodium:** Sodium is stored in kerosene because it reacts vigorously with both oxygen and water, producing a lot of heat.

---

## Reaction of Non-metals with Air and Water

### Burning Sulfur in Air

When sulfur (a non-metal) is burned in air:
1. It burns and produces **sulfur dioxide gas**.
2. When sulfur dioxide dissolves in water, it forms **sulfurous acid**.
3. Testing with litmus paper shows that the solution turns **blue litmus to red** -- it is **acidic**.

> **Key idea:** When non-metals burn in air (oxygen), they form **non-metal oxides** that are **acidic** in nature.

### Non-metals in Water

When sulfur powder is placed in water, there is **no reaction**. Generally, non-metals do not react with water.

**Special case -- Phosphorus:** Phosphorus is stored in water because it catches fire when exposed to air.

## Comparing Metals and Non-metals

| Property | Metals | Non-metals |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Lustre | Lustrous (shiny) | Non-lustrous (dull) |
| Malleability | Malleable (can be beaten into sheets) | Brittle (break into pieces) |
| Ductility | Ductile (can be drawn into wires) | Not ductile |
| Sonority | Sonorous (produce ringing sound) | Produce dull sound |
| Heat conduction | Good conductors | Poor conductors |
| Electrical conduction | Good conductors | Poor conductors |
| Hardness | Generally hard | Generally soft |
| State | Mostly solid (except mercury) | Solid, liquid, or gas |
| Oxides | Basic in nature | Acidic in nature |
| Reaction with water | Some metals react | Generally do not react |

## Are Non-metals Essential?

Non-metals may not be as shiny or strong as metals, but they are absolutely essential for life:

- **Oxygen** -- We breathe in oxygen to survive. It is also needed for burning fuels.
- **Carbon** -- It is the building block of all living things. Proteins, fats, and carbohydrates all contain carbon.
- **Nitrogen** -- Used in manufacturing fertilisers that help plants grow.
- **Chlorine** -- Used in water purification to make drinking water safe.
- **Iodine** -- Applied on wounds as an antiseptic to prevent infection.

> **Key idea:** While metals are important for tools, construction, and technology, non-metals like oxygen, carbon, and nitrogen are essential for life itself.

---

## Metals and Non-metals vs. Other Materials

It is important to understand that **metals** and **non-metals** are types of **elements** -- pure substances that cannot be broken down into simpler substances.

Materials like **plastic, glass, wood, rubber, and paper** are not classified as metals or non-metals because they are not elements. They are made from combinations of different elements.

Currently, **118 elements** are known. Some occur naturally, while others are made artificially in laboratories.

---

## Summary

- **Metals** are generally lustrous, malleable, ductile, sonorous, and good conductors of heat and electricity.
- **Non-metals** are generally non-lustrous, brittle, not ductile, not sonorous, and poor conductors of heat and electricity.
- Metals react with oxygen to form **metal oxides** that are **basic** in nature.
- Non-metals react with oxygen to form **non-metal oxides** that are **acidic** in nature.
- **Rusting** of iron occurs when iron is exposed to both air and water (moist air). It can be prevented by painting, oiling, greasing, or galvanisation.
- The gradual damage to metal surfaces by air and water is called **corrosion**.
- Both metals and non-metals have important uses in everyday life and are essential for human survival.
