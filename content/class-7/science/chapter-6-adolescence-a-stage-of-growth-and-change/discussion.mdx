## Understanding Adolescence

**Kiran:** Papa, my friends and I are all changing so quickly -- some are growing taller, some are getting pimples. Why is this happening?

**Ravi:** You are entering adolescence -- the stage between childhood and adulthood, roughly from age 10 to 19. During this time, your body is preparing itself for adulthood. These changes are completely natural and happen to everyone, though the timing and pace differ from person to person.

**Kiran:** Why do some of my classmates look much older while others still look the same as last year?

**Ravi:** Because everyone's body has its own timeline. Some people start puberty earlier, others later. Height, weight, voice changes, and other developments happen at different rates for different individuals. There is no "normal" timeline -- the variation itself is normal.

**Kiran:** That is reassuring. I was worried because my friend grew 10 cm this year and I only grew 5.

**Ravi:** Your growth spurt may come later. The important thing is that these changes are driven by hormones -- chemicals your body produces that signal different parts to grow and develop.

## Physical Changes and Puberty

**Kiran:** What exactly are the physical changes during adolescence?

**Ravi:** Both boys and girls experience increased height and weight. Boys may notice broadening shoulders, deeper voice due to growth of the voice box (sometimes visible as the Adam's apple), and facial hair. Girls may notice development of breasts and widening of hips. Both experience growth of hair in new areas and may develop pimples or acne.

**Kiran:** Why do pimples happen?

**Ravi:** During adolescence, the skin produces more oil. This extra oil can clog pores and cause infections, leading to pimples. Keeping your face clean, drinking plenty of water, and eating a balanced diet helps manage acne.

**Kiran:** These changes that help distinguish males from females -- what are they called?

**Ravi:** Secondary sexual characteristics. They are signs that the body is maturing but are not directly involved in reproduction. The stage when these changes begin is called puberty. All of this is a natural part of growing up.

## Emotional Changes

**Kiran:** Papa, sometimes I feel really happy and then suddenly irritated for no reason. Is that normal?

**Ravi:** Completely normal. Hormonal changes during adolescence affect your emotions as well as your body. Mood swings, stronger feelings, increased sensitivity to what others say or think -- these are all common.

**Kiran:** How do I handle these feelings?

**Ravi:** Several ways. Physical activity -- sports, yoga, cycling -- helps regulate mood. Creative outlets like music, drawing, or writing give you a way to express emotions. Talking to someone you trust -- a parent, teacher, or friend -- also helps. The key is to understand that these feelings are temporary and manageable.

**Kiran:** Is it normal to feel more curious and excited about everything?

**Ravi:** Very normal and very healthy! Channel that curiosity into learning, hobbies, and positive activities. Many of the world's great inventors, artists, and scientists discovered their passions during adolescence. This curiosity is a gift -- use it wisely.

## Nutrition and Hygiene

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher emphasised that nutrition is extra important during adolescence. Why?

**Ravi:** Because your body is growing rapidly and needs fuel. Calcium from milk and millets for strong bones. Proteins from dal, eggs, and nuts for building and repairing tissues. Iron from spinach and rajma for healthy blood. Vitamins from fruits and vegetables for immunity.

**Kiran:** What about personal hygiene?

**Ravi:** More important than ever. Regular bathing, clean clothes, and keeping underarms and other areas clean prevents infections. For girls, menstrual hygiene is essential -- using sanitary pads or other products, disposing of them properly, and understanding that menstruation is a completely natural and healthy process.

**Kiran:** Some of my classmates feel embarrassed talking about menstruation.

**Ravi:** There should be no embarrassment or shame. Menstruation is a natural biological process that occurs approximately every 28 to 30 days in girls. Myths and taboos around it have no scientific basis. The government has initiatives like the Menstrual Hygiene Scheme to provide sanitary pads to girls in rural areas. Open discussion helps everyone understand and support each other.

## Saying No to Harmful Substances

**Kiran:** Papa, some older students at school smoke behind the building. They say it is cool. Is it?

**Ravi:** Not at all. Tobacco, gutka, cigarettes, and alcohol are addictive and harmful. They damage lungs, affect memory, and cause serious health problems. What starts as "just once" often becomes a lifelong addiction.

**Kiran:** How do I say no if someone pressures me?

**Ravi:** Be direct and confident. "No, I do not want to." You do not need to give a reason. Surround yourself with friends who share your values. If you ever feel pressured or unsafe, talk to your parents or teachers immediately. The National De-addiction Helpline number is 14446.

**Kiran:** So the best strategy is to never start in the first place.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Someone who is addicted today started with "just one time." A balanced diet, regular exercise, good hygiene, and a supportive social circle -- these are the pillars of a healthy adolescence. Take care of your body now, and it will serve you well for decades to come.
