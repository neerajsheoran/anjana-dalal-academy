## Exploring the World Around Us

Every day, we use many things -- books, pencils, bags, toys, fans and more. Have you ever wondered **how these things work**? Do they always work the same way? In this chapter, we will explore how different objects spin, float and sink.

---

## Spinning Objects

When you spin a coin on a table, it twirls around and around. As it slows down, it starts to wobble and shake, and finally it stops and falls flat.

**Can all objects spin?** No! Some objects spin well, while others do not. It depends on their **shape** and how balanced they are.

| Object | Does It Spin? | What Happens? |
|---|---|---|
| **Coin** | Yes | Spins, wobbles as it slows down, then falls |
| **Bangle** | Yes | Spins and makes a sound as it slows |
| **Wooden top (lattu)** | Yes | Spins smoothly for a long time |
| **Eraser** | Not well | Too flat and uneven to spin properly |
| **Piece of stone** | Not well | Irregular shape makes it hard to spin |

---

## Making Your Own Spinner

You can make a simple spinner using a **piece of cardboard** and a **toothpick**:

1. Cut a circle from cardboard.
2. Poke a toothpick through the **centre** of the circle.
3. Spin it by twisting the toothpick!

**Important discoveries about spinners:**

- A spinner works best when the hole is at the **centre** of the cardboard.
- If the hole is away from the centre, the spinner **wobbles** and does not spin properly.
- When you spin a **square** piece of cardboard, it looks **circular** while spinning!
- If you colour the spinner with different colours and spin it, the colours **blend** together and look different.

> **Did you know?** The **charkha** (spinning wheel) is used to twist cotton fibre into thread. It has a large wheel that spins when turned by hand. **Mahatma Gandhi** used the charkha to promote handmade clothes and self-reliance.

![A spinning top (lattu) and a charkha (spinning wheel) as examples of spinning objects](spinning-top-and-charkha.png)

> **Did you know?** Spinning tops called **lattu** have been used in India for thousands of years. In earlier times, they were made of clay. Later, wooden tops became popular.

Many things in our daily life spin:

## Floating and Sinking

Have you ever placed a paper boat in water? Some objects **float** on water (stay on top), while others **sink** (go to the bottom). Let us find out why.

### What Floats and What Sinks?

| Object | Float or Sink? | Why? |
|---|---|---|
| **Leaf** | Floats | Light and flat |
| **Iron nail** | Sinks | Heavy metal |
| **Empty steel bowl** | Floats | Has air trapped inside, spread out shape |
| **Piece of stone** | Sinks | Heavy and dense |
| **Empty plastic bottle (closed)** | Floats | Light, has air inside |
| **Bottle full of water (closed)** | Sinks | Heavy, no air inside |

---

## Is It Only About Weight?

You might think that **light things float** and **heavy things sink**. But that is not always true!

Think about it:
- A **heavy steel ship** floats on water.
- A **small iron nail** sinks.

So, floating and sinking depends not just on weight, but also on the **shape** of the object.

---

## The Role of Shape

The **shape** of an object affects whether it floats or sinks.

**Try this experiment with aluminium foil:**

| Shape of Foil | What Happens? |
|---|---|
| **Spread out flat** | Floats |
| **Cup-like shape** | Floats |
| **Pressed tightly into a ball** | Sinks |

When the foil is spread out or shaped like a cup, it traps **air** and spreads over a larger area of water. This helps it float. When pressed into a ball, it becomes small and dense, so it sinks.

> This is the same reason why **boats and ships** are built with a wide, hollow shape -- the trapped air inside helps them float even though they are very heavy.

## Making Boats from Different Materials

You can build fun boats using everyday materials:

| Material | Type of Boat |
|---|---|
| **Paper** | Classic paper boat |
| **Ice-cream sticks** | Stick raft boat |
| **Coconut shell** | Shell boat |
| **Walnut shells** | Tiny shell boat |
| **Clay** | Shaped clay boat |
| **Cardboard** | Cardboard boat (needs waterproofing) |

When making a boat, think about:
- **Balance** -- Will it tip over easily?
- **Strength** -- Will it hold together in water?
- **Shape** -- Is the bottom wide enough to float?

## Key Points to Remember

- Objects spin best when they are **balanced** and the spinning point is at the **centre**.
- A **square spinner** looks circular when it spins fast.
- The **charkha** and **lattu** are traditional Indian spinning objects.
- Whether an object floats or sinks depends on its **weight** and **shape**.
- By changing the **shape** of an object (like aluminium foil), you can make it float or sink.
- Boats are designed with a **wide, hollow shape** to trap air and float on water.
- Experimenting with everyday objects helps us understand **how things work**.
