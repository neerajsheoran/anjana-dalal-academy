## Seeing Objects from Different Views

Have you ever looked at a tree from the top of a building? It looks very different from how it looks when you stand in front of it! The same object can look different depending on **where you are looking from**.

There are three main views of any object:

| View | What it means | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Front view** | What you see when you look at the object from the front | Looking at a cupboard from the front |
| **Side view** | What you see from the left or right side | Looking at a cupboard from its side |
| **Top view** | What you see when you look down from above | Looking at a cupboard from above |

### A Brick from Three Views

Imagine three friends drawing the same brick:
- One draws it from the **top** — it looks like a rectangle
- One draws it from the **front** — it looks like a smaller rectangle
- One draws it from the **side** — it looks like an even smaller rectangle

All three drawings look different, but they are all showing the **same brick**!

> **Try this:** Place a water bottle on your desk. Draw what it looks like from the front, from the side, and from the top. You will get three different drawings of the same bottle!

## Views of Common Objects

Here are some everyday objects and how their views might look:

| Object | Front View | Top View |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Glass (tumbler)** | A rectangle | A circle |
| **Book** | A thin rectangle | A big rectangle |
| **Ball** | A circle | A circle |
| **Birthday cap (cone)** | A triangle | A circle |
| **Pencil** | A long thin rectangle | A small circle |

> **Key idea:** A **ball** looks the same from every direction — always a circle! But most objects look different from different views.

## Building Models and Drawing Views

You can make buildings using empty matchboxes or small boxes, and then draw their views:

- **Front view** — stand in front of the model and draw what you see
- **Back view** — go behind the model and draw what you see
- **Top view** — look down at the model from above and draw what you see

The front view and back view of a building may look quite different because the back might have different windows or no entrance.

## Describing Positions

When we want to tell someone exactly where something is, we use words like:

- **Row** — a line going from left to right (horizontal)
- **Column** — a line going from top to bottom (vertical)
- **Top**, **bottom**, **left**, **right**, **middle**, **corner**

### Example: In a Classroom

Imagine a classroom with 3 rows and 3 columns of desks:
- "Jagat is sitting on the **third desk** in the **first row**"
- "The blue bag is on the **first desk** in the **second row**"
- "The notebook is on the **middle desk** in the **third row**"

> **Tip:** When describing a position, always mention both the **row** and the **column** (or use words like corner, middle, top, bottom).

## Using a Grid

A **grid** is like a checkerboard with rows and columns. We use grids to place and find objects.

```
         Column 1    Column 2    Column 3
Row 3   [pencil]    [          ] [eraser ]
Row 2   [          ] [apple    ] [       ]
Row 1   [ball     ] [bottle   ] [       ]
```

In this grid:
- The **ball** is at Row 1, Column 1 (bottom left corner)
- The **eraser** is at Row 3, Column 3 (top right corner)
- The **apple** is at Row 2, Column 2 (the middle)

---

## Moving on a Grid — Steps and Paths

On a grid, you can move:
- **Left** or **Right** (along the row)
- **Up** or **Down** (along the column)

You count the number of **steps** to go from one place to another.

### Example

To go from the bottom-left corner to an object 4 steps to the right and 2 steps up, you would say:
- "Move 4 steps to the right, then 2 steps up."

There can be **many different paths** to reach the same place! Some paths are longer and some are shorter. The **shortest path** uses the fewest steps.

> **Important:** In grid games, you usually cannot move **diagonally** — only up, down, left, or right.

## Reading a Map

A **map** (or **sight map**) shows a place as seen from the top. It helps you find where things are and how to get from one place to another.

### Using a School Map

Imagine a map of your school showing:
- Classrooms, library, playground, office, parking, stage, medical room

To read a map:
1. **Find where you are** on the map
2. **Find where you want to go**
3. **Trace the path** along the corridors and roads
4. **Give directions** using left, right, straight, and the names of landmarks

### Giving Directions

When telling someone the way, use clear instructions like:
- "Go straight from the entrance"
- "Turn left at the playground"
- "Walk past the library"
- "The medical room is on your right"

> **Try this:** Draw a simple map of your home showing the rooms. Can you describe the path from the main door to your bedroom?

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Front view** | The appearance of an object when seen from the front |
| **Side view** | The appearance of an object when seen from the left or right |
| **Top view** | The appearance of an object when seen from above |
| **Row** | A horizontal line of objects (left to right) |
| **Column** | A vertical line of objects (top to bottom) |
| **Grid** | A pattern of rows and columns used to place and locate objects |
| **Position** | The exact place where something is located (described using row and column) |
| **Path** | The route taken to go from one place to another on a grid or map |
| **Steps** | The number of moves needed to go from one place to another |
| **Map** | A drawing of a place as seen from above, showing locations and routes |
| **Directions** | Instructions like left, right, straight, up, down used to describe a route |
