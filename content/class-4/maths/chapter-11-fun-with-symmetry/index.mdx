## What is Symmetry?

A shape or design is **symmetrical** when you can divide it into two parts that are **exact mirror images** of each other. The line that divides the shape into two equal halves is called the **line of symmetry**.

Think of a butterfly — if you draw a line down the middle, the left wing looks exactly like the right wing. That is symmetry!

### How to Check for Symmetry

1. **Fold the shape** along a line
2. If both halves **match exactly** when folded, the shape is symmetrical
3. The fold line is the **line of symmetry**

> **Try this:** Take a sheet of paper, fold it in half, and put drops of paint near the fold. Press the paper together and open it. You will get a beautiful **symmetrical** ink design!

## Line of Symmetry

The **line of symmetry** divides a shape into two halves that are mirror images of each other.

### Shapes and Their Lines of Symmetry

| Shape | Number of Lines of Symmetry |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Circle | Infinite (countless) |
| Square | 4 |
| Rectangle | 2 |
| Equilateral Triangle | 3 |
| Isosceles Triangle | 1 |
| Regular Pentagon | 5 |
| Regular Hexagon | 6 |

Some shapes have **no line of symmetry** — they are called **asymmetrical** shapes.

> **Key idea:** A shape can have more than one line of symmetry. A square has 4 lines of symmetry — two through the middle (horizontal and vertical) and two along the diagonals.

## Symmetry in Everyday Life

Symmetry is all around us:

- **Nature:** Butterflies, leaves, flowers, and many insects are symmetrical
- **Buildings:** Many monuments like the Taj Mahal are symmetrical
- **Art:** Rangolis and mehndi designs use symmetry
- **Letters:** Some English letters are symmetrical: A, H, M, O, T, U, V, W, X, Y
- **Paper airplanes:** A symmetrical paper airplane flies better than an asymmetrical one!

> **Fun experiment:** Make two paper airplanes — one symmetrical and one asymmetrical (with one wing bigger than the other). Which one flies straighter and longer?

## Mirror Images (Reflections)

When you place a mirror on the line of symmetry, you see the **mirror image** (or **reflection**) of the shape.

### Mirror Images of Numbers

Some numbers look the **same** in a mirror:

| Number | Mirror Image | Same? |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| 181 | 181 | Yes |
| 686 | 686 | Yes |
| 808 | 808 | Yes |
| 123 | Not the same | No |

The digits that look the same in a mirror are: **0, 1, 8**

Numbers made only from these digits (like 1001, 1881, 8008) will look the same in a mirror!

### Mirror Images of Words

The word **AMBULANCE** is written in mirror-image on the front of ambulances. This is so that drivers can read it correctly in their **rear-view mirror**!

Some words that read the same in a mirror (when the mirror is placed horizontally): **MOM, WOW, TOOT**

> **Fun activity:** Write your name backwards and hold it up to a mirror. Can your friends read it?

## Completing Symmetrical Shapes

If you are given **half** of a symmetrical shape and the line of symmetry, you can draw the other half by making it an **exact mirror image**.

Rules for completing a symmetrical shape:
1. Every point on one side must have a matching point on the other side
2. Both points must be at the **same distance** from the line of symmetry
3. The overall shape must look identical on both sides

> **Tip:** Use a dot grid to help draw the mirror half accurately. Count the dots from the line of symmetry to each point on the given half, then mark the same number of dots on the other side.

## Holes and Cuts — Folding Paper

When you fold paper and make cuts, the result has symmetry:

### One Fold
- Fold a paper **once** and cut a shape at the fold
- When you open it, the shape appears on **both sides** of the fold line (1 line of symmetry)

### Two Folds
- Fold a paper **twice** and cut a shape
- When you open it, the shape appears in **all four sections** (2 lines of symmetry)

> **Challenge:** Fold a paper twice. Where would you cut to make a **square hole** in the centre? Think about where the centre falls when the paper is folded!

## Tiling — Patterns Without Gaps

**Tiling** means covering a surface with shapes so that there are **no gaps** and **no overlaps**. Floor tiles, wall patterns, and mosaics are all examples of tiling.

### How Tiling Works

1. Choose a **tile shape** (like a square, rectangle, triangle, or hexagon)
2. Place the tiles next to each other
3. Make sure they fit perfectly — no empty spaces and no tiles on top of each other

### Making Creative Tiles

You can make interesting tile shapes by modifying a simple shape:

1. Start with a **square** piece of paper
2. **Cut** a shape from one side
3. **Slide** the cut piece to the opposite side
4. **Tape** it in place
5. This new shape will still tile perfectly!

> **Example:** Cut a curve from the top of a square and slide it to the bottom. The new shape looks like a cat's head — and it tiles just like a square, with no gaps or overlaps!

### Shapes That Tile

| Shape | Can it tile? |
|-------|-------------|
| Square | Yes |
| Rectangle | Yes |
| Triangle | Yes |
| Regular Hexagon | Yes |
| Circle | No (leaves gaps) |
| Regular Pentagon | No (leaves gaps) |

## Symmetry in Nature

Go on a nature walk and observe:

- **Leaves:** Many leaves are symmetrical — the left half mirrors the right half along the central vein
- **Flowers:** Most flowers have symmetrical petals
- **Butterflies:** Wings are mirror images of each other
- **Insects:** Bodies are usually symmetrical

Not everything in nature is perfectly symmetrical. Some leaves and shells are **asymmetrical** — and that is interesting too!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Symmetry** | When a shape can be divided into two identical mirror-image halves |
| **Line of symmetry** | The line that divides a shape into two equal mirror-image halves |
| **Symmetrical** | A shape that has at least one line of symmetry |
| **Asymmetrical** | A shape that has no line of symmetry |
| **Mirror image** | The reflection of a shape as seen in a mirror |
| **Reflection** | The mirror image of a shape across a line |
| **Tiling** | Covering a surface with shapes leaving no gaps and no overlaps |
| **Tile** | A single shape used repeatedly to cover a surface |
| **Fold and cut** | A method of creating symmetrical designs by folding paper and cutting |
