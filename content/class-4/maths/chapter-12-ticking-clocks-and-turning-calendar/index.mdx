## The Calendar

A calendar helps us keep track of days, months, and years. Let us learn more about how calendars work.

### Months and Days

There are **12 months** in a year:

| Month | Days |
|-------|------|
| January | 31 |
| February | 28 (or 29 in a leap year) |
| March | 31 |
| April | 30 |
| May | 31 |
| June | 30 |
| July | 31 |
| August | 31 |
| September | 30 |
| October | 31 |
| November | 30 |
| December | 31 |

A regular year has **365 days**. A leap year has **366 days** (because February gets an extra day).

### Days of the Week

There are **7 days** in a week:
Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, Saturday

---

## Leap Years

A **leap year** is a special year that has **366 days** instead of 365. In a leap year, February has **29 days** instead of 28.

Leap years happen **every 4 years**: 2016, 2020, 2024, 2028, 2032...

> **Parv's special birthday:** Parv was born on 29 February 2016. Since 29 February only comes in leap years, his birthday date appears on the calendar only once every 4 years!

### How to Check if a Year is a Leap Year

If the year can be divided by 4 with no remainder, it is usually a leap year.
- 2024 / 4 = 606 (no remainder) -- leap year!
- 2025 / 4 = 506 remainder 1 -- not a leap year

## Reading and Writing Dates

Dates can be written in the format: **DD/MM/YYYY**

| Part | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| DD | Day of the month | 04 |
| MM | Month number | 07 (July) |
| YYYY | Year | 2024 |

So **04/07/2024** means the **4th of July, 2024**.

> **Tip:** When comparing dates to find who is older, first compare the **year**. If the years are the same, compare the **month**. If the months are also the same, compare the **day**.

---

## A Pattern in Calendar Days

Here is an interesting pattern: if you know what day a date falls on this month, you can predict the day for the same date next month!

For example, if July 15 is a Tuesday:
- August 15 will be a **Friday** (because July has 31 days, and 31 = 4 weeks + 3 days, so the day shifts by 3)
- September 15 will shift by however many days August has

The number of days in a month determines how many days the weekday shifts.

## How Many Hours in a Day?

A full day has **24 hours** — from midnight to midnight.

We can split the day into two halves:
- **12 hours** from midnight to noon (12:00 AM to 12:00 PM)
- **12 hours** from noon to midnight (12:00 PM to 12:00 AM)

---

## AM and PM

Since a clock only shows numbers from 1 to 12, we use **AM** and **PM** to tell whether it is morning/night or afternoon/evening.

| Time Period | Label | Hours |
|------------|-------|-------|
| Midnight to Noon | **AM** | 12:00 AM, 1:00 AM, 2:00 AM ... 11:00 AM |
| Noon to Midnight | **PM** | 12:00 PM, 1:00 PM, 2:00 PM ... 11:00 PM |

### Examples

| What you are doing | Time | AM or PM? |
|-------------------|------|-----------|
| Waking up | 6:00 | AM |
| Going to school | 8:00 | AM |
| Eating lunch | 12:30 | PM |
| Playing in the evening | 5:00 | PM |
| Going to bed | 9:00 | PM |
| Sleeping at night | 11:00 | PM |

> **Key idea:** 8:00 AM and 8:00 PM are **different times**! 8:00 AM is in the morning and 8:00 PM is at night.

---

## The 24-Hour Clock (Digital Clock)

Digital clocks often use a **24-hour format** where the hours go from 00:00 to 23:59.

| 12-Hour (AM/PM) | 24-Hour Clock |
|-----------------|---------------|
| 12:00 AM (midnight) | 00:00 |
| 1:00 AM | 01:00 |
| 7:15 AM | 07:15 |
| 12:00 PM (noon) | 12:00 |
| 1:00 PM | 13:00 |
| 3:00 PM | 15:00 |
| 5:20 PM | 17:20 |
| 8:00 PM | 20:00 |
| 11:45 PM | 23:45 |

> **Tip:** For PM times, add 12 to the hour. 3:00 PM = 3 + 12 = 15:00. For AM times before noon, the number stays the same (just add a leading zero if needed).

## Hours and Minutes

There are **60 minutes** in **1 hour**.

| Part of an Hour | Minutes |
|----------------|---------|
| Full hour (1 hour) | 60 minutes |
| Half hour (1/2 hour) | 30 minutes |
| Quarter hour (1/4 hour) | 15 minutes |
| Three-quarters hour (3/4 hour) | 45 minutes |

### Calculating Time Duration

To find how much time has passed:

**Example:** Raghav leaves home at 8:20 AM and returns at 8:35 AM.
- From 8:20 to 8:35 = **15 minutes**

**Example:** Akira's morning at school:
- 8:00 AM: Starts for school
- 8:15 AM: Reaches school (15 minutes travel)
- 8:30 AM: Assembly finishes (15 minutes for assembly)
- 8:50 AM: Dance practice ends (20 minutes)
- 9:00 AM: Break ends (10 minutes)

From 8:00 AM to 9:00 AM = **1 hour = 60 minutes**

## Reading the Clock Correctly

When reading an analogue clock:
- The **short hand** (hour hand) points to the hour
- The **long hand** (minute hand) points to the minutes

The minute hand moves through **small marks** on the clock face. Each small mark = 1 minute. Each number on the clock = 5 minutes.

| Long hand points to | Minutes |
|--------------------|---------|
| 12 | 0 (or 60) |
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 15 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 5 | 25 |
| 6 | 30 |
| 7 | 35 |
| 8 | 40 |
| 9 | 45 |
| 10 | 50 |
| 11 | 55 |

> **Common mistake:** When the hour hand is between two numbers (say between 3 and 4), the hour is still **3** (not 4). The hour only changes when the minute hand reaches 12.

## Expiry Dates

Many food items have a **manufacturing date** and an **expiry date** printed on them. The time between these two dates tells you how long the food is safe to eat.

**Example:** If a biscuit packet was made on 01/03/2025 and expires on 01/09/2025, it is safe for **6 months**.

> **Always check** the expiry date before eating packaged food!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Calendar** | A chart showing days, weeks, and months of a year |
| **Leap year** | A year with 366 days (February has 29 days); occurs every 4 years |
| **AM** | Ante Meridiem — the time from midnight to noon (morning hours) |
| **PM** | Post Meridiem — the time from noon to midnight (afternoon/evening/night hours) |
| **24-hour clock** | A system where hours go from 00:00 to 23:59 instead of using AM/PM |
| **Hour** | A unit of time equal to 60 minutes |
| **Minute** | A unit of time; 60 minutes = 1 hour |
| **Duration** | The amount of time between two events |
| **Manufacturing date** | The date on which a product was made |
| **Expiry date** | The date after which a product is no longer safe to use |
| **DD/MM/YYYY** | A format for writing dates (Day/Month/Year) |
