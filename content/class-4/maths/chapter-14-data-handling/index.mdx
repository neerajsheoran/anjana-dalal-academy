## What is Data?

**Data** is information that we collect about something. When we ask a question and record people's answers, we are collecting data.

For example:
- "What is your favourite subject?" — the answers you collect from all your classmates is data
- "How many goals did each team score?" — the numbers you write down is data
- "What colour gola do children eat the most?" — recording each child's choice is data

> **Key idea:** Data is just a collection of facts, numbers, or answers. We collect data to find out things, compare, and make decisions.

---

## Asking the Right Question

Before collecting data, we need to ask a **clear and specific** question.

### Good vs Not-So-Good Questions

| Not-so-good question | Good question |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "Which subjects do you like?" (too broad — allows many answers) | "Which subject do you like **the most**?" (specific — one clear answer) |

When we ask "Which subject do you like the most?", each person gives **one answer**, making it easy to count and compare.

---

## Recording Data

Once we collect answers, we need to **record** (write down) the data. Here are different ways:

### 1. List of Responses

Write each answer one after another:

```
M, L, T, A, P.E., M, L, M, T, A, A, P.E., M, L...
```

Where M = Mathematics, L = Languages, T = The World Around Us, A = Arts, P.E. = Physical Education

This works but is **hard to read** when there is a lot of data.

### 2. Tally Marks

A faster and cleaner way is to use **tally marks**. For every response, draw a line. After every 5th line, draw a line across the previous 4.

```
| = 1    || = 2    ||| = 3    |||| = 4    ||||  = 5
                                            /
```

![Tally marks grouped in fives showing how to count data quickly](tally-marks-counting.png)

### 3. A Table

Organise the data into a **table** with clear columns:

| Subject | Number of Children |
|---------|--------------------|
| Mathematics | 10 |
| Languages | 8 |
| The World Around Us | 7 |
| Physical Education | 11 |
| Arts | 9 |

> **Tip:** A table is much easier to read and understand than a long list of letters! Always organise your data in a table when possible.

---

## Reading Data from a Table

Once data is in a table, we can answer questions like:

- **Which is the most common?** — Look for the largest number
- **Which is the least common?** — Look for the smallest number
- **How many in total?** — Add all the numbers
- **How many more?** — Subtract the smaller from the larger

### Example

Using the table above:
- The most common favourite subject: **Physical Education** (11 children)
- The least common favourite subject: **The World Around Us** (7 children)
- Total children: 10 + 8 + 7 + 11 + 9 = **45 children**
- How many more children like P.E. than Languages? 11 - 8 = **3 more**

## Pictographs

A **pictograph** uses pictures or symbols to show data. Each picture stands for a certain number.

### Example: Food Sales at a Bal Mela (Fair)

Suppose each picture of a plate stands for **5 plates sold**:

**Fruit Chaat Sales:**

| Day | Pictures | Total Sold |
|-----|---------|------------|
| Day 1 | 4 pictures | 20 |
| Day 2 | 6 pictures | 30 |
| Day 3 | 3 pictures | 15 |

**Sandwich Sales:**

| Day | Pictures | Total Sold |
|-----|---------|------------|
| Day 1 | 5 pictures | 25 |
| Day 2 | 4 pictures | 20 |
| Day 3 | 7 pictures | 35 |

From the pictograph we can see:
- **Day 3** had the most sandwiches sold (35)
- **Day 2** had the most fruit chaats sold (30)
- Total sales on Day 1: 20 + 25 = 45
- Total sales on Day 2: 30 + 20 = 50 (highest sales day!)
- Total sales on Day 3: 15 + 35 = 50

> **Key idea:** In a pictograph, always check what **each picture represents** (it could be 1, 2, 5, 10, or any other number).

## Collecting Your Own Data

You can collect data about many things in your class or school. Here are the steps:

### Step 1: Frame a Clear Question
- "Which game do you play: chess, cricket, both, or neither?"

### Step 2: Collect Responses
- Ask every student and write down their answer

### Step 3: Organise in a Table

| Category | Number of Girls | Number of Boys |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| Chess only | | |
| Cricket only | | |
| Both | | |
| Neither | | |

### Step 4: Analyse and Answer Questions
- Who plays chess the most — boys or girls?
- How many children play both games?
- How many children do not play either game?

## Different Ways to Display Data

| Method | What it looks like | Best for |
|--------|-------------------|----------|
| **List** | A, B, A, C, A, B... | Small amounts of data |
| **Table** | Rows and columns with numbers | Any data — easy to read |
| **Tally marks** | Lines grouped in fives | Counting responses quickly |
| **Pictograph** | Pictures representing numbers | Making data visual and fun |

> **Important:** A table is almost always the best way to organise data because it is clear, easy to read, and easy to compare.

## Data in Real Life

Data is used everywhere in India:

- **Schools** collect data about student attendance and marks
- **Shops** track what items sell the most
- **Hospitals** record patient information
- **Weather stations** collect temperature and rainfall data
- **Census** counts the population of every village and city in India
- **Cricket** uses data for scores, batting averages, and bowling figures

> **Fun fact:** When Anjali and Rohan recorded their classmates' favourite subjects, they were doing what real scientists and researchers do every day — collecting, organising, and analysing data!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Data** | A collection of facts, numbers, or information gathered for a purpose |
| **Survey** | Asking people questions to collect data |
| **Response** | An answer given by a person when asked a question |
| **Tally marks** | Lines used to count data; every 5th line crosses the previous 4 |
| **Table** | Data organised in rows and columns for easy reading |
| **Pictograph** | A chart that uses pictures or symbols to represent data |
| **Most common** | The item with the highest count in the data |
| **Least common** | The item with the lowest count in the data |
| **Total** | The sum of all values in the data |
| **Compare** | Looking at two or more values to see which is more, less, or equal |
| **Organise** | Arranging data in a clear and useful way |
