## Shapes in Buildings

Look around you — shapes are everywhere! When Diksha visited Delhi with her father, she saw many famous monuments like **India Gate**, **Qutub Minar**, **Safdarjung Tomb**, **Akshardham**, and **Sansad Bhawan**. Each building is made up of different shapes — rectangular pillars, triangular roofs, curved domes, and square bases.

Even the **Qutub Minar** — a World Heritage site and the tallest building made of bricks — uses shapes! It has **5 storeys** and **379 stairs**. If you look at it carefully, its shape is like a tall cylinder that gets narrower as it goes up.

> **Did you know?** Earlier, people made buildings with clay bricks, stone blocks, or wood. Today we also use concrete blocks, hollow blocks, and many other materials. What is common to all bricks? They are all **3D shapes** — mostly rectangular blocks!

## 3D Shapes — Prisms and Pyramids

Three-dimensional (3D) shapes have **length**, **width**, and **height**. You can hold them in your hands — like a box, a ball, or a can.

### Prisms

A **prism** has two identical flat faces (called bases) connected by rectangular faces. Think of it like a long shape with the same cross-section all the way through.

| Prism | Base Shape | Number of Faces |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Triangular Prism** | Triangle | 5 |
| **Square Prism (Cube)** | Square | 6 |
| **Rectangular Prism (Cuboid)** | Rectangle | 6 |
| **Hexagonal Prism** | Hexagon | 8 |

> **Tip:** All prisms have **rectangular faces** on their sides. The shape of the base tells us the name of the prism.

### Pyramids

A **pyramid** has one base, and all other faces are **triangles** that meet at a single point at the top.

| Pyramid | Base Shape | Number of Faces |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Triangular Pyramid** | Triangle | 4 |
| **Square Pyramid** | Square | 5 |
| **Pentagonal Pyramid** | Pentagon | 6 |

> **Tip:** All pyramids have **triangular faces** that meet at one point at the top. A cube is also a special kind of prism!

## Faces, Edges, and Corners

Every 3D shape has three important features:

- **Face (F)** — a flat or curved surface of the shape
- **Edge (E)** — the line where two faces meet
- **Corner / Vertex (V)** — the point where edges meet

| Shape | Faces (F) | Corners (V) | Edges (E) |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Cube** | 6 | 8 | 12 |
| **Cuboid** | 6 | 8 | 12 |
| **Triangular Pyramid** | 4 | 4 | 6 |
| **Square Pyramid** | 5 | 5 | 8 |
| **Triangular Prism** | 5 | 6 | 9 |

### Euler's Formula

There is a special relationship between faces, corners, and edges:

**F + V - E = 2**

This is called **Euler's formula**. Try it with any shape above — it always works!

For a cube: 6 + 8 - 12 = **2**
For a square pyramid: 5 + 5 - 8 = **2**

## Nets of 3D Shapes

A **net** is what you get when you unfold a 3D shape and lay it flat. If you fold the net along the dotted lines, you get the 3D shape back.

For example, if you flatten an open box, you will see its net — a cross-like pattern of rectangles. Different 3D shapes have different nets:

- A **cube** net has 6 connected squares
- A **triangular prism** net has 2 triangles and 3 rectangles
- A **square pyramid** net has 1 square and 4 triangles

> **Try this:** Take an empty toothpaste box or cereal box. Carefully cut along the edges and flatten it. You have just made a net!

## Sorting 3D Shapes

We can sort 3D shapes in different ways:

**By faces:**
- Shapes where all faces look the same (e.g., cube)
- Shapes where faces are different (e.g., triangular prism)

**By edges:**
- Shapes with curved edges (e.g., cone, cylinder)
- Shapes with straight edges (e.g., cube, pyramid)

**By face types:**
- Shapes with rectangular faces only
- Shapes with triangular faces only
- Shapes with both rectangular and triangular faces

## When Lines Meet — Angles

When two lines meet at a point, they create an **angle**. You can see angles everywhere — in the corner of a book, in an open door, in yoga poses, and even in the letter "V".

### Types of Angles

There are three main types of angles:

**1. Right Angle**

A **right angle** is like the corner of a book or a window. It is exactly 90 degrees. You can make a right angle by folding a piece of paper — first fold it in half, then fold it in half again. The corner you get is a right angle.

**2. Acute Angle**

An **acute angle** is **smaller than a right angle**. Think of a partly closed book or the tip of a pizza slice.

**3. Obtuse Angle**

An **obtuse angle** is **bigger than a right angle**. Think of a fan that is opened wide or a reclining chair leaning back.

> **Tip:** To check if an angle is a right angle, hold the corner of a folded paper against it. If it matches exactly, it is a right angle. If the opening is smaller, it is acute. If larger, it is obtuse.

## Shapes with Straws — Rigidity

Try making shapes using straws and clay balls at the corners:

- Make a **triangle** — now gently push one side. Does the shape change? **No!** The triangle is the most **rigid** shape. It keeps its form.
- Make a **rectangle** — now gently push one side. Does the shape change? **Yes!** The rectangle can be pushed into a slanted shape (parallelogram), and the right angles become acute and obtuse angles.

> **This is why triangles are used in bridges and buildings** — they do not change shape when pushed!

## Rectangles and Squares

A **rectangle** has:
- 4 sides (opposite sides are equal)
- 4 right angles
- 2 pairs of equal sides

A **square** has:
- 4 sides (all sides are equal)
- 4 right angles

> **Key idea:** A square is a **special rectangle** where all four sides are equal!

## Circles — Centre, Radius, and Diameter

A **circle** is a shape where every point on the border is at the **same distance** from the centre.

### Parts of a Circle

- **Centre** — the point in the middle of the circle
- **Radius** — the distance from the centre to the border of the circle
- **Diameter** — the distance across the circle through the centre

### How Radius and Diameter are Related

The **diameter** is always **double** the radius.

**Diameter = 2 x Radius**

Or, the **radius** is always **half** the diameter.

If the radius is 5 cm, the diameter is 10 cm.

> **Try this:** Take a circular piece of paper. Fold it in half — the crease is a diameter. Fold it in half again. Now the crease from the centre to the edge is the radius. All diameters pass through the centre!

You can draw a circle using a **compass**. The distance you set on the compass is the **radius** of the circle.

![A circle with centre, radius, and diameter clearly labeled](circle-parts-labeled.png)

### Circles in Real Life

All **wheels** are circles! A truck wheel has a longer radius than a bicycle wheel. That is why it is bigger.

## Sides and Angles — A Special Relationship

Shapes have an interesting property:

| Shape | Number of Sides | Number of Angles |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Triangle | 3 | 3 |
| Rectangle | 4 | 4 |
| Pentagon | 5 | 5 |
| Hexagon | 6 | 6 |

The **number of sides** is always **equal to** the **number of angles** in any flat (2D) shape!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **3D shape** | A shape that has length, width, and height (e.g., cube, cone) |
| **Face** | A flat or curved surface of a 3D shape |
| **Edge** | The line where two faces of a 3D shape meet |
| **Corner (Vertex)** | The point where two or more edges meet |
| **Prism** | A 3D shape with two identical bases connected by rectangular faces |
| **Pyramid** | A 3D shape with one base and triangular faces meeting at a point |
| **Net** | A flat pattern that can be folded to make a 3D shape |
| **Euler's formula** | F + V - E = 2 (faces + corners - edges = 2) |
| **Angle** | The space formed when two lines meet at a point |
| **Right angle** | An angle of exactly 90 degrees, like a book corner |
| **Acute angle** | An angle smaller than a right angle |
| **Obtuse angle** | An angle bigger than a right angle |
| **Rigid shape** | A shape that does not change form when pushed (e.g., triangle) |
| **Centre** | The middle point of a circle |
| **Radius** | The distance from the centre to the edge of a circle |
| **Diameter** | The distance across a circle through the centre (= 2 x radius) |
