## Why Standard Units Matter

**Kiran:** Papa, our teacher said that long ago people measured length using their hand span or foot length. Why did they stop doing that?

**Ravi:** Try this experiment. Measure the length of this table using your hand span. I will do the same with mine.

**Kiran:** I get about 8 hand spans. You get about 6. But the table has not changed!

**Ravi:** Exactly. My hand is larger than yours, so I get a different number. If a carpenter in Chennai measures a door in his hand spans and sends the measurement to a carpenter in Delhi, the Delhi carpenter's hands will be different — and the door will not fit.

**Kiran:** So we need a measurement that is the same for everyone — a standard unit.

**Ravi:** That is why the International System of Units — SI units — was created. The standard unit of length is the metre. Whether you are in Mumbai, Tokyo, or London, 1 metre is exactly the same. No confusion.

**Kiran:** And the smaller units are millimetres and centimetres, and the bigger one is kilometres — all connected by powers of 10.

**Ravi:** Right. 1 km is 1,000 m. 1 m is 100 cm. 1 cm is 10 mm. The metric system is beautifully consistent. Your height is about 135 cm. The distance from our home to school is about 3 km. The thickness of your notebook cover is about 2 mm. Each unit fits a different scale.

## Measuring Correctly — Avoiding Errors

**Kiran:** Papa, when I measure with a ruler, my friend and I sometimes get slightly different readings. Are we doing something wrong?

**Ravi:** Probably one of you is making a parallax error. If you look at the ruler from an angle instead of straight on — eye level — the reading appears shifted. Always position your eye directly above the mark you are reading.

**Kiran:** Also, sometimes I start measuring from the edge of the ruler instead of the zero mark.

**Ravi:** That is another common mistake. Some rulers have a small gap between the edge and the zero line. Always align your object with the zero mark, not the end of the ruler. And for curved objects — like measuring the circumference of a cricket ball — a ruler will not work. You need to wrap a thread around the ball, mark where it meets, then measure the thread against a ruler.

**Kiran:** That is clever! We did that in class to measure the circumference of a bangle.

**Ravi:** These practical measurement skills are essential. In science, accurate measurement is everything. An error of even 1 mm can matter when building machines or conducting experiments.

## Types of Motion

**Kiran:** Papa, everything that moves seems different. A swing goes back and forth, a car goes straight, a fan blade goes in circles. Are there different types of motion?

**Ravi:** Scientists classify motion into types based on the path of the object. When something moves in a straight line — like a ball rolling down a ramp — that is linear motion. When something moves in a circle — like the blades of a ceiling fan — that is circular motion. When something moves back and forth around a fixed point — like a swing or a pendulum — that is oscillatory motion.

**Kiran:** And some motions repeat at regular intervals, right?

**Ravi:** Those are called periodic motions. A pendulum swings back and forth every 2 seconds — periodic. The Earth orbits the Sun once every 365 days — periodic. Your heartbeat is periodic. Oscillatory motions are always periodic, but not all periodic motions are oscillatory.

**Kiran:** What about a car in city traffic? It speeds up, slows down, stops, turns...

**Ravi:** That is a combination of different motions and speeds. In traffic, the car covers unequal distances in equal times — that is non-uniform motion. On a straight highway at constant speed, it covers equal distances in equal time — uniform motion.

## Speed — How Fast?

**Kiran:** Papa, I ran 100 metres in 18 seconds during sports day. What was my speed?

**Ravi:** Speed equals distance divided by time. So your speed was 100 divided by 18, which is about 5.6 metres per second. Usain Bolt's world record 100-metre sprint was in 9.58 seconds — his speed was about 10.4 metres per second.

**Kiran:** He is nearly twice as fast as me! But how do we convert metres per second to kilometres per hour?

**Ravi:** Multiply by 3.6. So your speed was about 20 km per hour. A typical car in a city travels at about 40 km per hour. A train goes at about 100 to 150 km per hour. The Vande Bharat Express reaches up to 180 km per hour.

**Kiran:** And an aeroplane?

**Ravi:** About 800 to 900 km per hour. And light travels at 3 lakh km per second — so fast it could go around the Earth 7.5 times in one second!

**Kiran:** These comparisons help me understand what speed actually means.

**Ravi:** That is the point of measurement — to make abstract ideas concrete. When someone says "that car is going fast," you can now ask "how fast?" and understand the answer.

## Distance-Time Graphs — Pictures of Motion

**Kiran:** Papa, our teacher drew a graph with time on the X-axis and distance on the Y-axis. A straight sloping line meant uniform motion. But what does a curved line mean?

**Ravi:** A curved line means the speed is changing — the object is speeding up or slowing down. If the curve bends upward, the object is accelerating. If it flattens out, the object is slowing down.

**Kiran:** And a flat horizontal line?

**Ravi:** That means the object is not moving — it is at rest. The distance stays the same as time passes. Think of a parked car — no matter how much time passes, the distance does not change.

**Kiran:** Can I tell the speed from the graph?

**Ravi:** Yes! For a straight line, the steepness — the slope — gives the speed. A steeper line means faster speed. A gentle slope means slower speed. In higher classes, you will learn to calculate the exact speed from the slope, but for now, just remember: steeper means faster.

**Kiran:** So a graph turns motion into a picture I can read. That is really useful!

**Ravi:** Data visualization — turning numbers into pictures — is one of the most powerful tools in science. Whether it is motion, temperature, population, or anything else, graphs help us see patterns that raw numbers hide.
