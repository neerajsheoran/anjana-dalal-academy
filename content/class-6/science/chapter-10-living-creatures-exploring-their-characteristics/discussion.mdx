## What Makes Something Alive?

**Kiran:** Papa, a dog moves, eats, and grows. A tree does not move around but it also grows. A car moves but it does not grow. How do we decide what is actually alive?

**Ravi:** Scientists have identified seven characteristics that all living things share: movement, nutrition, respiration, growth, excretion, response to stimuli, and reproduction. An organism must show all of these to be considered alive.

**Kiran:** A car moves but does not grow or reproduce. A crystal in a science experiment can grow but does not eat or respond. So neither is alive.

**Ravi:** Exactly. All seven characteristics must be present. A dog clearly moves, eats, breathes, grows, excretes, responds when you call its name, and can have puppies. A neem tree in our garden also does all of these — just differently.

**Kiran:** But the tree does not move!

**Ravi:** It does not walk, but it moves. Its roots grow downward, its shoots grow upward. The sunflower turns its face toward the sun. The touch-me-not plant — chhuimui — folds its leaves when you touch it. Plants move, just not the way animals do.

## Autotrophs and Heterotrophs — Who Makes the Food?

**Kiran:** Papa, plants make their own food through photosynthesis. But animals have to eat. Why can't animals just make food from sunlight too?

**Ravi:** That is one of the deepest divisions in the living world. Plants have chlorophyll — the green pigment in their leaves — that captures sunlight energy and converts carbon dioxide and water into glucose. This is photosynthesis. Animals lack chlorophyll, so they cannot do this.

**Kiran:** So plants are the producers and animals are consumers?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Plants are autotrophs — self-feeders. Animals are heterotrophs — they feed on others. Among animals, herbivores like cows and deer eat plants directly. Carnivores like tigers eat other animals. Omnivores like us humans eat both. And decomposers like mushrooms and bacteria break down dead organisms.

**Kiran:** In our school garden, the grass is an autotroph. The caterpillar eating the grass is a herbivore. The mynah bird eating the caterpillar is a carnivore. It is a food chain!

**Ravi:** And at the bottom of every food chain is a plant — an autotroph capturing sunlight. Without photosynthesis, there would be no food chain and no animal life on Earth. That is why forests are so critical — they are the foundation of life.

## Respiration — Not Just Breathing

**Kiran:** Papa, I thought respiration means breathing. But our textbook says they are different.

**Ravi:** Breathing is the mechanical process — inhaling oxygen and exhaling carbon dioxide. Respiration is the chemical process inside your cells where glucose is broken down using oxygen to release energy. Breathing supplies the oxygen, but the actual energy release happens at the cellular level.

**Kiran:** So the formula is glucose plus oxygen gives carbon dioxide, water, and energy?

**Ravi:** Right. And this happens in every cell of your body — your muscle cells, brain cells, liver cells — constantly. When you exercise hard, your muscles need more energy, so respiration speeds up, and you breathe faster to supply more oxygen and remove more carbon dioxide.

**Kiran:** What about organisms that respire without oxygen?

**Ravi:** Some organisms can do anaerobic respiration. Yeast, for example, breaks down sugar without oxygen to produce alcohol and carbon dioxide. That is how we make idli and dosa batter rise — the yeast produces carbon dioxide bubbles. It is also how bread becomes fluffy and how fermented drinks are made.

**Kiran:** And when I exercise too hard and my muscles cramp — that is also anaerobic respiration?

**Ravi:** Yes! When your muscles cannot get enough oxygen fast enough, they respire anaerobically, producing lactic acid instead of carbon dioxide. That lactic acid buildup causes the burning, cramping feeling. Once you rest and catch your breath, oxygen clears the lactic acid and the cramp goes away.

## J.C. Bose and Plants That Feel

**Kiran:** Papa, our textbook says Jagadish Chandra Bose proved that plants can feel. That sounds unbelievable!

**Ravi:** Bose was one of the greatest scientists India has ever produced. In the early 1900s, most scientists believed plants were completely passive — no feelings, no responses. Bose invented an incredibly sensitive instrument called the crescograph that could magnify tiny plant movements thousands of times.

**Kiran:** What did he discover?

**Ravi:** He showed that when a plant is touched, heated, or injured, it responds — much like an animal. The cells at the injury site react, and electrical signals travel through the plant. The touch-me-not plant is the most dramatic example — touch it and the leaves fold in seconds — but Bose showed that even plants that seem perfectly still are responding to their environment.

**Kiran:** So the boundary between plants and animals is not as sharp as we think?

**Ravi:** That was Bose's remarkable conclusion. Both plants and animals are living things that sense and respond to their world — just in different ways. Bose was also a pioneer in radio waves — he demonstrated wireless transmission of electromagnetic signals even before Marconi's famous radio experiment. A true genius in multiple fields.

## Cells — The Building Blocks of Life

**Kiran:** Papa, what is the smallest unit of something that is still alive?

**Ravi:** The cell. Every living organism is made of cells. Some organisms — like amoeba and bacteria — are just one cell. They are called unicellular. A single cell does everything — eating, breathing, growing, reproducing.

**Kiran:** And we are made of many cells — multicellular?

**Ravi:** The human body has about 37 lakh crore cells — 37 trillion! And different cells are specialised for different jobs. Nerve cells carry electrical signals. Muscle cells contract to create movement. Red blood cells carry oxygen. Skin cells form a protective barrier. Each type is designed for its specific function.

**Kiran:** How do cells organise into a whole body?

**Ravi:** There is a beautiful hierarchy. Similar cells group together to form tissues — like muscle tissue. Different tissues combine to form organs — like the heart. Organs work together as organ systems — like the circulatory system. And all organ systems together make the organism — you!

**Kiran:** Cell to tissue to organ to organ system to organism. It is like how bricks make walls, walls make rooms, rooms make a floor, and floors make a building!

**Ravi:** That is an excellent analogy. And just as a building is only as strong as its individual bricks, your body's health depends on the health of your individual cells. That is why nutrition, exercise, and rest matter — they keep your cells functioning well.
