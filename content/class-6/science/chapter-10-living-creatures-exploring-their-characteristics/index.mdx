## Are You Alive? — A Simple Question with a Deeper Answer

Look at the world around you. A dog barks, runs, and eats. A tree stands still but grows taller every year. A stone just sits there, never growing, never moving on its own.

How do we decide if something is **alive**? Scientists have thought deeply about this and found that all living things share certain common features — called **characteristics of life**. If something shows all these features, it is a living organism.

> **Key idea:** A living organism is something that grows, takes in food or energy, responds to changes around it, and can reproduce.

---

## Characteristics of Living Organisms

![Seven characteristics of living things shown as icons: movement, nutrition, respiration, growth, excretion, response to stimuli, and reproduction](characteristics-of-living-things.png)

All living things — from a tiny bacterium to a giant banyan tree to a blue whale — share these key characteristics:

1. Movement
2. Nutrition (food/energy intake)
3. Respiration
4. Growth
5. Excretion
6. Response to stimuli
7. Reproduction

Let us explore each one.

---

## 1 — Movement

**All living things move.** But movement looks very different in different organisms.

### Movement in Animals

Animals move their whole body from place to place. This is called **locomotion**. A fish swims, a bird flies, a horse runs, a snake slithers.

*Why do animals move?*
- To find food
- To escape from predators
- To find a partner
- To find shelter

### Movement in Plants

Plants cannot walk — but they are NOT completely still. Plants show **growth movements** and **responses** to their environment.

*Examples:*
- **Sunflower** — the flower head turns to face the sun throughout the day. This is called **phototropism**.
- **Touch-me-not plant (Mimosa pudica / Lajwanti / छुईमुई)** — when you touch its leaves, they fold inward immediately. This is a **response to touch**.
- Roots always grow downward (toward gravity) and shoots grow upward (toward light).
- Tendrils of a creeper curl and wind around a support.

> *Activity:* Gently touch the leaves of a Mimosa pudica (touch-me-not) plant. Watch the leaves fold. Leave it for a minute — they unfold again. This is a movement response!

**Key distinction:**
| | Animals | Plants |
| -- | ------- | ------ |
| Type of movement | Locomotion (whole body moves) | Growth movements and stimulus responses (parts move, not whole body) |

## 2 — Nutrition

All living organisms need **energy** to carry out life processes. They get this energy from food. The way an organism gets its food is called **nutrition**.

### Two Main Types

**a) Autotrophs ("self-feeders") — make their own food**

![Diagram of photosynthesis showing a plant absorbing sunlight, water from roots, and carbon dioxide from air to produce glucose and oxygen](photosynthesis-diagram.png)

Plants, algae, and some bacteria can make their own food using:
- **Sunlight** (energy source)
- **Water** (from soil)
- **Carbon dioxide** (from air)

This process is called **photosynthesis**. The food made is **glucose** (a type of sugar). The green pigment **chlorophyll** in leaves is what makes photosynthesis possible.

> Plants are the **producers** of food in nature. They make the food that almost all other organisms ultimately depend on.

**b) Heterotrophs ("other-feeders") — consume other organisms**

Animals, fungi, and most bacteria cannot make their own food. They eat plants or other animals.

*Types of heterotrophs:*
- **Herbivores** — eat only plants (cow, goat, rabbit, deer)
- **Carnivores** — eat only animals (lion, tiger, eagle, snake)
- **Omnivores** — eat both plants and animals (humans, crow, bear, cockroach)
- **Decomposers** — break down dead organisms (mushrooms, bacteria)

*Indian examples:*
- A cow eating grass in a field — herbivore.
- A tiger hunting a deer — carnivore.
- You eating dal-chawal with some chicken — omnivore!

## 3 — Respiration

After taking in food, living organisms need to **release the energy stored in food** so the body can use it. This process is called **respiration**.

> **Important:** Respiration is NOT the same as breathing. Breathing is just the process of taking air in and out. Respiration is the chemical process inside cells where food (glucose) is broken down to release energy.

### Aerobic Respiration (with oxygen)

Most organisms use oxygen to break down glucose:

**Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy**

This is what happens inside the cells of our body, all the time.

### Anaerobic Respiration (without oxygen)

Some organisms can release energy even without oxygen. Example:
- Yeast releases energy from sugar without oxygen — this produces **alcohol and carbon dioxide** (used in making bread, idli, dosa, and fermented drinks).
- Bacteria in our muscles sometimes respire anaerobically when we exercise very hard → produces **lactic acid** (which causes muscle cramps!).

### How Different Organisms Breathe

| Organism | How they take in oxygen |
| -------- | ---------------------- |
| Humans and mammals | Lungs |
| Fish | Gills |
| Frogs | Skin (when young), lungs (when adult) |
| Insects | Tiny holes called **spiracles** |
| Plants | Tiny pores on leaves called **stomata** |
| Earthworms | Moist skin |

> *Think:* When you exercise hard, you breathe faster. Why? Because your muscles need more energy, so respiration speeds up — and you need more oxygen and need to remove more carbon dioxide.

## 4 — Growth

**All living organisms grow.** Non-living things do not grow on their own.

A seed grows into a plant. A baby grows into an adult. A puppy grows into a dog.

*Examples from India:*
- A mango seed planted in soil slowly grows into a mango tree that can live for hundreds of years.
- A baby elephant weighs about 100 kg at birth and grows to over 4,000 kg!

### Growth in Plants vs Animals

| | Plants | Animals |
| -- | ------ | ------- |
| Does growth stop? | Plants grow throughout their life (at tips of roots and shoots) | Most animals stop growing after reaching adulthood |
| Where does growth happen? | At special growing tips (meristems) | All over the body (in childhood), then mostly stops |

### Lifespan

Every organism has a **lifespan** — the time from birth to natural death.

| Organism | Approximate Lifespan |
| -------- | -------------------- |
| Housefly | 20–30 days |
| Mosquito | 2 weeks to 2 months |
| Hen | 5–10 years |
| Dog | 10–15 years |
| Elephant | 60–70 years |
| Tortoise | 150–200 years |
| Banyan tree | Hundreds of years |
| Bristlecone Pine | Over 5,000 years! |

> The **banyan tree** at Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Botanical Garden in Kolkata is believed to be over 250 years old and is one of the largest trees in the world!

## 5 — Excretion

During life processes, the body produces waste substances that can be harmful if they stay inside. Living organisms must remove these wastes. This is called **excretion**.

*In humans:*
- **Kidneys** filter waste from blood and produce **urine** (containing urea, salts, water).
- **Lungs** excrete **carbon dioxide** (produced during respiration).
- **Skin** excretes some salts and water as **sweat**.
- **Liver** processes and removes many toxins.

*In plants:*
- Plants release **oxygen** through stomata (a by-product of photosynthesis).
- Some plants store waste products in their leaves, which then fall off (leaf-fall).
- Some plants store waste in **resins, gums, and latex**.

*In other animals:*
- Fish excrete waste through gills and kidneys.
- Birds produce uric acid (white paste in droppings) — this saves water.

> **Did you know?** The white part of bird droppings is **uric acid** — their nitrogenous waste. Unlike urea (which is dissolved in urine and needs lots of water), uric acid is nearly solid, which helps birds save water — very useful when flying!

## 6 — Response to Stimuli

Living organisms sense changes in their surroundings and **respond** to them. A change in the environment that causes a response is called a **stimulus** (plural: stimuli).

*Examples of stimuli and responses:*
- **Light → Sunflower** turns toward the sun.
- **Touch → Touch-me-not** folds its leaves.
- **Danger → A deer** runs away when it senses a predator.
- **Cold → Humans** shiver to generate heat.
- **Smell of food → Dog** salivates.
- **Bright light → Human pupil** becomes smaller.

### J.C. Bose and Plants Feeling Pain

For a long time, people thought only animals could sense and respond to things. A great Indian scientist changed this understanding.

**Jagadish Chandra Bose (1858–1937)** was a Bengali scientist, one of the first great scientists of modern India. He invented a very sensitive instrument called the **crescograph** that could measure tiny, tiny movements in plants — movements too small for the human eye to see.

His experiments showed:
- Plants **respond to touch, heat, cold, and chemicals** — just as animals do.
- When a plant is injured (cut or burned), it shows a **response** similar to an animal in pain — the cells at the injury site react.
- Plants have a kind of **electrical signal** that travels through them when stimulated — similar to nerve signals in animals.

> **Bose's remarkable finding:** He showed that the boundary between plant and animal life is not as sharp as once thought. Plants are sensitive, living things that respond to the world around them.

J.C. Bose was also a pioneer in **radio waves** — he invented and demonstrated wireless transmission of electromagnetic waves even before Marconi. He was a true polymath (a person brilliant in many fields).

## 7 — Reproduction

Every living organism eventually dies. For a species to survive, organisms must produce **offspring** (children). This is called **reproduction**.

### Two Types of Reproduction

**a) Asexual Reproduction** — only one parent, offspring is identical to parent

*Examples:*
- **Amoeba** splits into two identical amoeba (binary fission).
- **Yeast** forms buds that grow into new yeast cells.
- **Hydra** forms buds on its body.
- **Plants** can grow new plants from: cuttings (rose, hibiscus), leaves (money plant), or runners (grass, strawberry).

**b) Sexual Reproduction** — two parents (male + female), offspring has features of both

*Examples:*
- Humans, dogs, cats, birds, fish — most animals reproduce sexually.
- Flowering plants — pollen from one flower fertilizes the egg of another → seeds form → new plant grows.

*Indian examples:*
- A mango seed contains the embryo of a new mango plant — sexual reproduction.
- A piece of sugarcane stalk planted in soil grows into a new plant — asexual reproduction.
- Potatoes have **eyes** (buds) from which new potato plants can grow — asexual reproduction.

## Are Viruses Alive?

This is a fascinating question scientists still debate!

**Viruses** (like the virus that causes COVID-19, or common cold, or influenza):
- Can **reproduce** — but only inside a living host cell. They cannot reproduce on their own.
- Do NOT carry out respiration, nutrition, or excretion on their own.
- Are not made of cells.

So viruses are on the **borderline** between living and non-living. Most scientists do not classify them as fully living organisms, but they are not completely non-living either.

## Cells — The Basic Unit of Life

If we cut any living organism into smaller and smaller pieces, we eventually reach the smallest unit that is still alive — the **cell**.

> **All living organisms are made of cells.** The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.

Some organisms, like **bacteria** and **amoeba**, are made of just **one cell** — they are called **unicellular organisms**.

Most plants, animals, and fungi are made of **millions of cells** — they are called **multicellular organisms**.

| Unicellular | Multicellular |
| ----------- | ------------- |
| Made of 1 cell | Made of many cells |
| Amoeba, bacterium, yeast | Humans, trees, fish, mushrooms |
| The single cell does all life functions | Different cells are specialised for different jobs |

### Levels of Organisation in the Body

In multicellular organisms, cells are organised into more complex structures:

![Levels of organisation shown as a staircase: cell, tissue, organ, organ system, organism with examples at each level](levels-of-organisation.png)

**Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism**

*Example in humans:*
- **Muscle cells** group together → **muscle tissue**
- Muscle tissue + bone tissue + nerve tissue → **heart** (an organ)
- Heart + arteries + veins → **circulatory system** (organ system)
- All organ systems together → **human body** (organism)

*Example in plants:*
- **Leaf cells** → **leaf tissue** → **leaf** (organ) → **shoot system** → **plant**

## What Makes Living Things Special — Summary

| Characteristic | What it means |
| -------------- | ------------- |
| **Movement** | All living things move — animals locomote, plants show growth movements and stimulus responses |
| **Nutrition** | All living things need food/energy — autotrophs make food (plants), heterotrophs eat food (animals) |
| **Respiration** | All living things release energy from food using oxygen (mostly) |
| **Growth** | All living things grow and develop |
| **Excretion** | All living things remove waste products |
| **Response to stimuli** | All living things sense and react to changes around them |
| **Reproduction** | All living things produce offspring — asexual (one parent) or sexual (two parents) |

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
| ---- | ------- |
| **Stimulus** | A change in the environment that causes a living thing to respond |
| **Photosynthesis** | The process by which plants make food using sunlight, water, and CO₂ |
| **Autotroph** | An organism that makes its own food (plants, algae) |
| **Heterotroph** | An organism that eats other organisms for food (animals, fungi) |
| **Respiration** | The breakdown of glucose to release energy inside cells |
| **Excretion** | The removal of waste products from the body |
| **Locomotion** | Movement of the whole body from one place to another |
| **Reproduction** | Producing offspring |
| **Unicellular** | Made of a single cell (e.g., amoeba, bacteria) |
| **Multicellular** | Made of many cells (e.g., humans, trees) |
| **Cell** | The basic unit of structure and function of all living things |
| **Crescograph** | Instrument invented by J.C. Bose to measure tiny plant movements |
| **Lifespan** | The total length of time an organism lives |
