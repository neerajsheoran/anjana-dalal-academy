## Water's Three Faces

**Kiran:** Papa, ice, water, and steam look completely different. How can they all be the same substance — H2O?

**Ravi:** Think of it like this. The molecules are the same — two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom. What changes is how those molecules behave. In ice, the molecules are locked in a rigid structure — they vibrate but stay in fixed positions. That is why ice has a definite shape.

**Kiran:** In liquid water, the molecules can slide past each other?

**Ravi:** Yes. They are close together but free to flow. That is why water takes the shape of whatever container you pour it into. And in steam — water vapour — the molecules are far apart and moving very fast in all directions. That is why steam fills up the entire space available.

**Kiran:** So when we heat ice, we are giving the molecules energy to break free from their rigid positions?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Adding heat makes molecules move faster. First they break free from the solid structure — ice melts into water at 0 degrees Celsius. Keep adding heat and eventually the molecules move fast enough to escape the liquid entirely — water boils into steam at 100 degrees Celsius.

**Kiran:** And cooling does the reverse — taking away energy so molecules slow down and lock together again.

**Ravi:** Right. Water is the only substance we encounter in all three states in our daily lives — ice in the fridge, water from the tap, and steam from the kettle. That makes it perfect for understanding how states of matter work.

## Evaporation — The Slow Disappearing Act

**Kiran:** Papa, I hung my wet school uniform on the clothesline this morning. When I came back from school, it was dry. Where did the water go?

**Ravi:** The water molecules at the surface of the wet cloth gradually escaped into the air as water vapour. This process — evaporation — happens at any temperature, not just at 100 degrees.

**Kiran:** But boiling happens at 100 degrees. What is the difference between boiling and evaporation?

**Ravi:** Boiling is rapid — it happens throughout the liquid when you heat it to its boiling point. Bubbles form everywhere and the liquid churns. Evaporation is slow and quiet — it happens only at the surface, at any temperature. Your clothes dry at 30 degrees, not because they are boiling, but because surface molecules gradually escape.

**Kiran:** Our teacher said evaporation is faster on a hot sunny day, on a windy day, and when the cloth is spread out.

**Ravi:** That is because higher temperature gives molecules more energy to escape. Wind carries away the escaped vapour, creating room for more to escape. And a larger surface area means more molecules are at the surface where they can escape. That is why you spread clothes out wide to dry them — not fold them into a ball.

## Condensation — Water Appearing from Thin Air

**Kiran:** Papa, why do water droplets appear on the outside of a cold glass of nimbu paani in summer? The water is inside the glass, not outside!

**Ravi:** Those droplets come from the air. Air always contains some invisible water vapour. When this vapour touches the cold glass surface, it loses heat and converts back into liquid water. That is condensation — the reverse of evaporation.

**Kiran:** So the air around us always has water in it?

**Ravi:** Yes! The amount of water vapour in air is called humidity. On a humid day — like during the monsoon — the air is heavy with moisture. Sweat does not evaporate easily, so you feel hot and sticky. On a dry winter day, there is less moisture, and sweat evaporates quickly, keeping you comfortable.

**Kiran:** Is dew also condensation?

**Ravi:** Exactly. On clear, cold nights, surfaces like grass and leaves cool down a lot. Water vapour from the air condenses on these cold surfaces as tiny droplets — that is dew. Morning fog is the same thing — water vapour condensing on tiny dust particles floating in cold air, creating a cloud at ground level.

## The Water Cycle — Nature's Recycling System

**Kiran:** Papa, where does the water go after it evaporates from our clothes or from a lake?

**Ravi:** It joins the air as invisible water vapour. The vapour rises because warm air rises. As it goes higher, the air gets cooler. Eventually the vapour cools enough to condense around tiny dust particles, forming tiny water droplets. Millions of these droplets together form a cloud.

**Kiran:** And when the droplets in a cloud grow heavy enough, they fall as rain?

**Ravi:** Right — that is precipitation. The rainwater flows into rivers, lakes, and oceans, or soaks into the ground as groundwater. Then the Sun heats the surface water again, causing evaporation, and the cycle continues. This has been happening for billions of years — the water you drank today might have been part of the ocean a thousand years ago.

**Kiran:** The same water just keeps recycling? We never get new water?

**Ravi:** Almost never. The total amount of water on Earth has remained roughly the same for billions of years. It just moves between the ocean, the atmosphere, and the land in an endless cycle. That is why conserving and protecting our water is so important — there is no "new" supply coming from anywhere.

## The Cooling Power of Evaporation

**Kiran:** Papa, why does a matka — a clay pot — keep water cool without any electricity?

**Ravi:** Because of evaporation! The clay pot is slightly porous — tiny holes allow water to seep to the outer surface. When this water evaporates, it takes heat from the pot and the water inside. This heat removal cools the water.

**Kiran:** So evaporation removes heat from the surroundings?

**Ravi:** Always. When you step out of a swimming pool, you feel cold. That is because water on your skin evaporates and takes body heat with it. When you sit under a fan, you feel cooler — not because the fan makes air colder, but because the wind speeds up the evaporation of sweat from your skin, removing more heat.

**Kiran:** Is that why desert coolers work? They blow air through wet pads.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The water on the pads evaporates, cooling the air that passes through. It is the same principle as the matka, the same principle as sweating, just applied with a fan and a pump.

**Kiran:** Indian people figured out cooling by evaporation centuries before air conditioners existed — using matkas, khas-khas screens, and sprinkling water on floors.

**Ravi:** Traditional Indian knowledge often had deep scientific principles behind it. The matka is not just a pot — it is an evaporative cooler. Science helps us understand why these things work, and that understanding lets us appreciate our heritage even more.
