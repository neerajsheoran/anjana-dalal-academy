## Air — The Invisible Resource

**Kiran:** Papa, we talk about saving water and saving forests. But nobody talks about saving air. Is air unlimited?

**Ravi:** Air itself is abundant, but clean air is not. The atmosphere is a thin blanket around the Earth — if you imagine the Earth as a basketball, the atmosphere would be thinner than a sheet of paper wrapped around it. And we are pumping pollutants into this thin layer at an alarming rate.

**Kiran:** Like vehicle exhaust and factory smoke?

**Ravi:** Yes. Vehicle engines burn petrol and diesel, releasing carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, and tiny particles. Factories release sulphur dioxide and toxic chemicals. In north India every winter, the burning of crop stubble in Punjab and Haryana fills the air with smoke, creating dangerous smog in Delhi and surrounding areas.

**Kiran:** I remember last winter — we could not go out for our morning walk because the air quality was so bad.

**Ravi:** That is air pollution directly affecting your daily life. But clean air is essential — oxygen for respiration, carbon dioxide for plant photosynthesis, nitrogen for soil fertility. Moving air — wind — carries rain clouds and even generates electricity through wind turbines. India is one of the top five countries in the world for wind energy production.

**Kiran:** So air is not just for breathing — it is an energy source too?

**Ravi:** Exactly. And protecting air quality is as important as protecting water and forests.

## Water — Precious and Finite

**Kiran:** Papa, if 71 percent of Earth is covered with water, why do we have water shortages?

**Ravi:** Because 97 percent of that water is salty ocean water — you cannot drink it or use it for farming. Only 3 percent is freshwater, and most of that is locked in glaciers and ice caps. The tiny fraction that is available as rivers, lakes, and groundwater has to serve 800 crore people, all agriculture, and all industry.

**Kiran:** That is a very small amount for so many needs!

**Ravi:** And it is getting scarcer. Groundwater levels are falling across India because we pump more water out than rain can replenish. Rivers like the Ganga and Yamuna carry heavy pollution loads from factories and sewage. Climate change is making rainfall patterns unpredictable.

**Kiran:** Our textbook mentions traditional Indian water conservation — stepwells, johads, and bamboo drip irrigation.

**Ravi:** Indians were water management experts for centuries. The stepwells — bawdis — of Gujarat and Rajasthan are architectural marvels that stored rainwater underground where it would not evaporate. Johads in Rajasthan are earthen dams that capture rainwater and recharge groundwater. The bamboo drip irrigation system in Meghalaya channels water precisely to crops through bamboo pipes — a centuries-old drip irrigation system!

**Kiran:** And today we have rainwater harvesting?

**Ravi:** Yes — collecting rain from rooftops and storing it or directing it into the ground. Many Indian cities now require rainwater harvesting in new buildings. Every drop saved is a drop that does not need to be pumped from a depleting aquifer.

## Forests — More Than Just Trees

**Kiran:** Papa, why do people say "save the forests" instead of just "plant more trees"?

**Ravi:** Because a forest is not just a collection of trees. It is an entire ecosystem — trees, shrubs, herbs, mosses, fungi, bacteria, insects, birds, mammals, reptiles, all living together in complex relationships. A planted row of trees is not a forest. A forest has layers — the canopy, the understorey, the shrub layer, and the forest floor — each with its own community of organisms.

**Kiran:** What happens on the forest floor?

**Ravi:** Fallen leaves, dead wood, and animal droppings decompose — broken down by fungi, bacteria, and earthworms into rich humus. This humus feeds the soil, which feeds the trees, which drop more leaves. It is nature's recycling system — nothing is wasted.

**Kiran:** And deforestation breaks this cycle?

**Ravi:** Completely. Without tree roots, soil erodes in heavy rain — leading to floods and landslides. Without the canopy, the forest floor dries out. Animals lose their habitat. Carbon stored in trees is released into the atmosphere, worsening climate change. India's Chipko Movement in 1973 — where women in Uttarakhand hugged trees to prevent them from being cut — showed how communities can protect their forests.

**Kiran:** Gaura Devi and the other women physically protected the trees. That takes real courage.

**Ravi:** It does. And their action changed India's forest policy. The Chipko Movement inspired environmental movements worldwide and showed that ordinary people can make a difference.

## Fossil Fuels and Renewable Energy

**Kiran:** Papa, petrol, diesel, and cooking gas all come from fossil fuels. What exactly are fossil fuels?

**Ravi:** They are the remains of ancient living things — plants and marine organisms that lived hundreds of millions of years ago. Over geological time, heat and pressure deep inside the Earth transformed these remains into coal, petroleum, and natural gas.

**Kiran:** So when we burn petrol, we are using energy that the Sun gave to plants millions of years ago?

**Ravi:** Exactly! It is stored ancient solar energy. But the problem is twofold. First, fossil fuels took millions of years to form but we are using them up in just a few centuries. They will run out. Second, burning them releases carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, causing climate change, and other pollutants that damage health.

**Kiran:** That is why we need renewable energy — solar, wind, and hydro?

**Ravi:** Yes. Solar panels convert sunlight directly into electricity. Wind turbines capture wind energy. Hydroelectric dams use flowing water. These sources do not run out and produce little to no pollution. India has massive potential — the Bhadla Solar Park in Rajasthan is one of the world's largest solar installations. The government aims for 500 gigawatts of renewable energy by 2030.

**Kiran:** Can India really replace fossil fuels with renewable energy?

**Ravi:** It is a huge challenge, but the transition is underway. Electric vehicles are becoming more common. Solar panels are appearing on rooftops across the country. CNG — compressed natural gas — is already used in buses and autorickshaws in many cities as a cleaner alternative to diesel. Every step toward renewable energy is a step toward a cleaner future.

## What Can We Do?

**Kiran:** Papa, all these problems — air pollution, water shortage, deforestation, fossil fuel depletion — seem overwhelming. What can one person do?

**Ravi:** A lot, actually. Turn off lights and fans when you leave a room — that saves electricity and reduces the need to burn coal. Fix leaking taps — a dripping tap wastes thousands of litres a year. Plant trees — even one tree absorbs carbon dioxide and provides shade. Carry a cloth bag instead of using plastic. Recycle paper, metal, and glass.

**Kiran:** And eat local seasonal food to reduce food miles?

**Ravi:** Yes! Composting kitchen waste enriches soil instead of filling landfills. Walking or cycling instead of using a car reduces air pollution. Supporting organic farming reduces pesticide pollution in rivers.

**Kiran:** None of these seems very hard individually.

**Ravi:** That is the point. If 140 crore Indians each make a few small changes, the collective impact is enormous. Nature's treasures — air, water, soil, forests, minerals — belong to all of us, including the generations yet to come. Protecting them is not someone else's job. It is everyone's responsibility, starting with awareness.

**Kiran:** Starting with understanding the science behind it.

**Ravi:** Exactly. And that is what this chapter is really about.
