## What Are Natural Resources?

Everything we use in daily life ultimately comes from nature. The water you drink, the air you breathe, the fuel in vehicles, the wood in your furniture, the cotton in your clothes — all of it comes from the Earth.

**Natural resources** are the materials and substances found in nature that living organisms use to survive and thrive.

> **Key idea:** Natural resources belong to everyone. How we use them today decides whether they will be available for future generations.

---

## Air — The Invisible Treasure

Air surrounds us everywhere, yet we cannot see it. Without air, no living thing could survive for more than a few minutes.

### What Is Air Made Of?

Air is a **mixture of gases**:

| Gas | Approximate percentage in air |
| --- | ----------------------------- |
| Nitrogen (N₂) | ~78% |
| Oxygen (O₂) | ~21% |
| Argon and other gases | ~0.9% |
| Carbon dioxide (CO₂) | ~0.04% |
| Water vapour | Variable |

### Why Is Air Important?

- **Oxygen** is needed by almost all living things for respiration (to release energy from food).
- **Carbon dioxide** is needed by plants for photosynthesis (to make food).
- **Nitrogen** is needed by plants to make proteins (taken up through roots via special bacteria in soil).
- The **atmosphere** (blanket of air around Earth) protects us from harmful ultraviolet (UV) rays from the sun.
- Moving air (wind) carries rain clouds, helps in pollination, and dries wet things.

### Harnessing Wind Energy

Moving air carries **kinetic energy** (energy of motion). Humans have learned to capture this energy:

- **Windmills** — traditional windmills in Rajasthan and Gujarat use wind to pump groundwater for irrigation.
- **Wind turbines** — modern tall towers with large blades that spin in wind to generate electricity. India has large wind farms along the coasts of Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, and Rajasthan.

> **Did you know?** India is one of the top five countries in the world for wind energy production!

### Air Pollution — Harming the Treasure

Air gets polluted when harmful gases and particles mix with it:
- Vehicle exhaust releases carbon monoxide (CO), sulphur dioxide (SO₂), and nitrogen oxides.
- Factory smoke releases toxic particles.
- Burning of crop stubble in Punjab and Haryana creates smog in northern India every winter.

*Effects:* Breathing problems, asthma, eye irritation, acid rain, damage to the ozone layer.

**Electric vehicles (EVs)** produce no exhaust — they are a cleaner alternative. India's government is actively promoting EVs.

## Water — The Liquid of Life

About **71%** of the Earth's surface is covered with water. Yet **97%** of this water is **salty ocean water** — not suitable for drinking. Only **3%** is freshwater, and most of that is locked in glaciers and ice caps. Only a tiny fraction is easily available as rivers, lakes, and groundwater.

This makes **freshwater one of the most precious natural resources on Earth.**

### Sources of Freshwater

- **Rivers** — Ganga, Yamuna, Godavari, Krishna, Brahmaputra
- **Lakes** — Dal Lake (Kashmir), Chilika Lake (Odisha)
- **Groundwater** — water stored underground, accessed through wells and borewells
- **Glaciers** — Himalayan glaciers feed India's major rivers
- **Rainwater** — the original source of all freshwater on land (through the water cycle)

### The Water Cycle

![The water cycle showing sun heating the ocean, water evaporating, clouds forming, rain falling on mountains, and rivers flowing back to the sea](water-cycle-nature.png)

Water moves continuously between land, oceans, and the atmosphere:

1. The sun heats ocean and lake water → water **evaporates** (turns to vapour).
2. Water vapour rises and cools → **condenses** into tiny droplets → forms clouds.
3. Water droplets in clouds combine and fall as **rain or snow** (precipitation).
4. Water flows back to rivers, lakes, and oceans, or seeps into the ground.
5. The cycle repeats.

> This cycle has been running for billions of years — the water you drink today may have once been in a dinosaur's body!

### Traditional Water Conservation in India

Indians have been conserving rainwater for centuries through ingenious systems:

- **Bawdi / Stepwell (Vav)** — beautifully built stepwells where people could walk down to collect water even in dry seasons. The famous **Rani ki Vav** in Patan, Gujarat (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) is an outstanding example.
- **Kund** — underground cisterns to collect and store rainwater in Rajasthan.
- **Johad** — traditional ponds in Rajasthan that capture rainwater and recharge groundwater.
- **Bamboo drip irrigation** — traditional system in Meghalaya where bamboo channels carry water precisely to crops.

### Rainwater Harvesting

**Rainwater harvesting** is the modern practice of collecting rain from rooftops and directing it into tanks or into the ground.

*How it works:*
1. Rain falls on a rooftop.
2. Water is collected through gutters and pipes.
3. It is filtered and stored in a tank for use.
4. Or it is directed into the ground to recharge the water table.

Many cities in India now make rooftop rainwater harvesting **mandatory** for new buildings.

### Water Pollution

Water becomes polluted when harmful substances are added to it:
- Industrial waste and chemicals from factories.
- Sewage (human waste) flowing into rivers.
- Pesticides and fertilisers from farms washing into rivers.
- Dumping of garbage and plastic into rivers and lakes.

*Effect:* The Ganga and Yamuna rivers carry a heavy pollution load. Polluted water causes diseases like cholera, diarrhoea, typhoid, and hepatitis.

**Ganga Action Plan** and **Namami Gange** are government initiatives to clean up the Ganga.

## Energy from the Sun

The Sun is the **primary source of energy** for almost all life on Earth.

- Plants use sunlight for **photosynthesis** → make food → animals eat plants → energy flows through the food chain.
- **Wind energy** is ultimately caused by the Sun (uneven heating of Earth's surface creates wind).
- **Rain** happens because sunlight evaporates water → water cycle.
- Even **fossil fuels** (coal, petroleum) are stored ancient solar energy — they formed from dead plants and animals that lived millions of years ago.

### Solar Energy Technology

![Three panels showing solar panels on a rooftop, a solar cooker box, and a wind turbine as examples of renewable energy technology](renewable-energy-technology.png)

Humans now directly capture solar energy using:

**a) Solar panels (photovoltaic cells)**
- Convert sunlight directly into electricity.
- Used on rooftops of homes, schools, and factories.
- India's ambitious **Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission** and projects like the **Bhadla Solar Park** (Rajasthan) — one of the world's largest — generate massive amounts of electricity.

**b) Solar cooker**
- A box with a mirror/reflective panel that concentrates sunlight to cook food.
- No fuel needed — completely free and clean energy.
- Used in many villages and remote areas of India.
- Can cook rice, dal, vegetables, and even boil water.

**c) Solar water heater**
- Panels on rooftops absorb sunlight to heat water for bathing and washing.
- Very common on rooftops across India — saves electricity.

> **India's target:** India aims to produce 500 GW of energy from renewable sources (solar + wind + hydro) by 2030. Solar energy is the biggest part of this plan.

## Forests — The Green Treasure

A **forest** is not just a collection of trees — it is a **complex ecosystem** where thousands of species of plants, animals, fungi, and microorganisms live and depend on each other.

### Why Are Forests Important?

| Benefit | How |
| ------- | --- |
| **Clean air** | Trees absorb CO₂ and release O₂ through photosynthesis |
| **Clean water** | Forest soil filters rainwater that seeps into rivers and groundwater |
| **Prevent soil erosion** | Tree roots hold the soil; without trees, rain washes topsoil away |
| **Climate regulation** | Forests influence local and global rainfall patterns |
| **Biodiversity** | Home to millions of species of plants, animals, and microorganisms |
| **Livelihoods** | Tribal and rural communities depend on forests for food, medicine, and materials |

### Forest Products

*From the forest, we get:*
- **Timber** (wood for furniture, construction)
- **Firewood** (fuel for cooking in villages)
- **Bamboo** (construction, furniture, baskets, bamboo shoots for food)
- **Gum and resins** (for adhesives, medicines, varnish)
- **Honey** (from wild bees)
- **Medicinal plants** — neem, tulsi, amla, ashwagandha
- **Fruits and nuts** — many tribal communities collect wild fruits
- **Paper** (made from wood pulp)

### Forest Floor — Nature's Recycler

The forest floor is covered with fallen leaves, dead wood, and animal droppings. **Decomposers** (bacteria, fungi, earthworms) break all of this down into rich **humus** — dark, fertile material that feeds the soil. This is nature's way of recycling nutrients.

> **Earthworms** are called **"farmers' friends"** because they constantly dig and aerate the soil, making it more fertile.

### Deforestation — Losing the Green Treasure

Forests are being cleared at an alarming rate for:
- Agriculture (new farmland)
- Construction of roads, dams, and cities
- Timber and paper industries
- Mining

*Effects of deforestation:*
- Loss of habitat → animals become endangered or extinct.
- Soil erosion → floods worsen.
- Reduced rainfall → droughts.
- Increased carbon dioxide in atmosphere → climate change.

### Saving Forests — Indian Movements

**Chipko Movement (1973)**
One of India's most famous environmental movements. In 1973, in Chamoli district of Uttarakhand, local women (led by Gaura Devi) **hugged the trees** to prevent them from being cut by contractors. "Chipko" means "to stick to" or "to hug". This simple act became a powerful movement that spread across India and influenced forest protection laws.

**Van Mahotsav**
An annual festival of tree planting in India, started in 1950 by K.M. Munshi. During Van Mahotsav week (first week of July), millions of trees are planted across India. Schools, colleges, and communities participate.

## Soil, Rocks, and Minerals

### Soil — Where Life Begins

**Soil** is the thin layer of material on the Earth's surface where plants grow. It is formed over hundreds and thousands of years by the **weathering** (breaking down) of rocks by wind, water, and temperature changes, combined with the decomposition of dead plants and animals.

![Cross-section of soil layers from top to bottom: dark topsoil with plants and roots, lighter subsoil, rock fragments, and solid bedrock](soil-layers-cross-section.png)

*Layers of soil (from top to bottom):*
1. **Topsoil** — dark, rich in humus and minerals; where plant roots grow and life is most abundant.
2. **Subsoil** — less humus, more minerals and clay; deeper roots reach here.
3. **Rock fragments** — partially broken rock.
4. **Bedrock** — solid rock.

*Types of soil:*
- **Sandy soil** — large particles, drains water quickly, not very fertile; good for growing watermelons and some vegetables.
- **Clayey soil** — tiny particles, holds water too well, can become waterlogged; good for growing rice.
- **Loamy soil** — mixture of sand, clay, and silt; holds water and is well-drained; most fertile; best for most crops.

### Rocks

The Earth's crust is made of **rocks**. Rocks are made of one or more **minerals**.

| Rock | Type | Found in India | Use |
| ---- | ---- | -------------- | --- |
| Granite | Igneous | Rajasthan, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka | Flooring, kitchen counters, buildings |
| Sandstone | Sedimentary | Rajasthan (Red Fort, Qutub Minar built from it!) | Construction, monuments |
| Limestone | Sedimentary | Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan | Cement, glass |
| Marble | Metamorphic | Rajasthan (Taj Mahal is made of Makrana marble!) | Flooring, sculpture |
| Slate | Metamorphic | Himachal Pradesh | Roofing, writing slates |
| Coal | Sedimentary (fossil fuel) | Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh | Fuel, electricity |
| Laterite | Sedimentary | Kerala, Goa | Building blocks for houses |

### Minerals

**Minerals** are natural substances found in rocks and soil. They are non-living, have a definite chemical composition, and are found in nature.

*Important minerals and where they are found in India:*

| Mineral | Use | Found in India |
| ------- | --- | -------------- |
| Iron ore | Making steel — used in buildings, vehicles, machines | Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh |
| Aluminium (Bauxite ore) | Lightweight metal — aircraft, utensils, wires | Odisha, Jharkhand |
| Copper | Electrical wires, plumbing | Rajasthan, Jharkhand |
| Gold | Jewellery, electronics | Karnataka (Kolar Gold Fields) |
| Coal | Fuel for electricity | Jharkhand, West Bengal |
| Petroleum (crude oil) | Petrol, diesel, kerosene, plastics | Gujarat, Mumbai offshore, Assam |
| Natural gas | Cooking gas (LPG), CNG | Gujarat, Rajasthan |

> **Did you know?** Your mobile phone contains over 60 different minerals — including gold, silver, copper, lithium, cobalt, tin, and rare earth elements! This is why old mobile phones should be properly recycled — they contain valuable minerals.

## Fossil Fuels — Ancient Stored Energy

![Diagram showing formation of fossil fuels: ancient forests being buried under layers of sediment over millions of years turning into coal](fossil-fuel-formation.png)

**Fossil fuels** (coal, petroleum, and natural gas) are the remains of plants and animals that lived hundreds of millions of years ago. Over millions of years, heat and pressure inside the Earth transformed these remains into energy-rich fuels.

### Coal

- Formed from ancient forests that died and were buried under sediment.
- A **black rock** that burns to release heat and energy.
- Used to generate **electricity** in thermal power plants (by heating water to make steam to spin turbines).
- Also used in steel-making and as industrial fuel.
- India is one of the world's largest producers and consumers of coal (mainly from Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, West Bengal).

### Petroleum (Crude Oil)

- Formed from ancient marine organisms (plankton, algae) that died and were buried under the sea floor.
- A thick, dark liquid found deep underground (also under the ocean floor), extracted by **drilling**.
- After extraction, crude oil is sent to **refineries** where it is separated (refined) into:
  - **Petrol** (for cars, motorcycles)
  - **Diesel** (for trucks, buses, trains)
  - **Kerosene** (for lamps, cooking in villages)
  - **LPG** (Liquefied Petroleum Gas — the gas cylinder in your kitchen)
  - **Bitumen** (for surfacing roads)
  - Raw material for making **plastics**

*India's oil fields:* Mumbai High (offshore), Bombay High, Gujarat, Assam.

### Natural Gas

- Found alongside petroleum or in separate underground reservoirs.
- Mainly methane (CH₄).
- Piped directly to homes and used as **PNG (Piped Natural Gas)** for cooking.
- Compressed into cylinders as **CNG (Compressed Natural Gas)** for vehicles — much cleaner than petrol/diesel.
- Used to generate electricity and in chemical industries.

### Problems with Fossil Fuels

| Problem | Detail |
| ------- | ------ |
| **They will run out** | Fossil fuels took millions of years to form. At current rates of use, we could exhaust them within decades to a few centuries. |
| **Air pollution** | Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂, SO₂, carbon monoxide, and particles — major cause of air pollution and smog. |
| **Climate change** | CO₂ and other greenhouse gases trap heat in the atmosphere, causing global warming and climate change. |
| **Oil spills** | Accidents during shipping or drilling pollute oceans and harm marine life. |

## Renewable and Non-Renewable Resources

Natural resources can be classified based on whether they can be replenished (renewed):

### Non-Renewable Resources

Resources that take **millions of years** to form and **cannot be replaced** once used up.

*Examples:* Coal, petroleum, natural gas, and most minerals (iron, copper, gold).

Once we use them up, they are gone forever.

### Renewable Resources

Resources that are **continuously replenished** by natural processes and will not run out (if used wisely).

*Examples:*

| Resource | How it renews |
| -------- | ------------- |
| **Sunlight** | Continuously produced by the Sun |
| **Wind** | Continuously generated by uneven heating of Earth |
| **Water** | Renewed by the water cycle (rain) |
| **Forests** | Can regrow if trees are replanted and protected |
| **Soil** | Slowly renews through decomposition (but takes centuries) |

> **Important note:** Even renewable resources can be destroyed if misused. Forests take decades to regrow. Clean water can be permanently polluted. Soil can be eroded. We must use even renewable resources carefully.

## How Can We Protect Nature's Treasures?

| Resource | What YOU can do |
| -------- | --------------- |
| **Air** | Use public transport or walk/cycle; support electric vehicles; plant trees |
| **Water** | Fix leaking taps; use water wisely while bathing and washing; collect rainwater; don't pollute rivers |
| **Forests** | Don't waste paper; buy wood from sustainable sources; support tree planting |
| **Soil** | Compost kitchen waste; avoid littering; support organic farming |
| **Fossil fuels** | Switch off lights and fans; use energy-efficient appliances; use solar energy where possible |
| **Minerals** | Recycle old electronics and metals |

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
| ---- | ------- |
| **Natural resource** | A material found in nature that is used by living organisms |

| **Fossil fuel** | Fuel formed from remains of ancient organisms — coal, petroleum, natural gas |
| **Photovoltaic (solar) cell** | A device that converts sunlight directly into electricity |

| **Humus** | Dark, fertile material in soil formed from decomposed plant and animal matter |
| **Weathering** | The breaking down of rocks by wind, water, and temperature changes |
| **Rainwater harvesting** | Collecting and storing rainwater for use |
| **Chipko Movement** | A famous Indian environmental movement where people hugged trees to prevent cutting |
