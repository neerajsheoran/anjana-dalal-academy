## Why Different Materials for Different Jobs

**Kiran:** Papa, why is a cooking pan made of steel but its handle is made of plastic or wood?

**Ravi:** Because each part of the pan needs a different property. The pan body must conduct heat well — steel does that. It must be hard so it does not dent easily. But the handle must NOT conduct heat, otherwise you would burn your hand. Plastic and wood are insulators — they do not let heat pass through.

**Kiran:** So the choice of material depends on what job it needs to do?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Window panes are glass because glass is transparent — light passes through. But you would never make a door from glass because it is not strong enough and provides no privacy. Electrical wires use copper because it is an excellent conductor of electricity. But the plastic coating around the wire is an insulator — it prevents the current from reaching your hand.

**Kiran:** Every material has its own set of properties — hardness, transparency, conductivity, flexibility — and we choose the material that matches the need.

**Ravi:** That is materials science in a nutshell. Engineers and designers think about this every single day. Why are cricket bats made of willow wood? Because willow is light, strong, and has a springy quality that helps the ball travel further.

## Properties of Materials

**Kiran:** Papa, how do I remember all the different properties — hard, soft, rough, smooth, transparent, opaque, soluble, insoluble...

**Ravi:** Instead of memorizing, think of examples. Touch the steel railing — hard. Squeeze the rubber ball — soft. Run your hand on the glass table — smooth. Feel the brick wall — rough. Look through the glass window — transparent. Look at the wooden door — opaque.

**Kiran:** And solubility? Salt dissolves in water but sand does not.

**Ravi:** Right. Try this at home — add a spoonful each of salt, sugar, chalk powder, and sand to separate glasses of water. Stir each. Salt and sugar dissolve — they are soluble. Chalk and sand settle at the bottom — they are insoluble.

**Kiran:** What about lustre? Our textbook says metals are lustrous — shiny.

**Ravi:** When your mother polishes her brass pooja thali, it becomes shiny and golden. That is lustre. Metals like gold, silver, copper, and brass are naturally lustrous. Non-metals like wood, chalk, and rubber are dull — non-lustrous. But metals can lose their lustre if they corrode or rust. An old iron gate looks dull because of the rust layer.

**Kiran:** So rust is actually the iron losing its lustrous surface?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The iron reacts with oxygen and moisture in the air to form iron oxide — rust. That is chemistry in action, right in our everyday environment.

## States of Matter

**Kiran:** Papa, I know solids have a fixed shape, liquids take the shape of the container, and gases fill up all the space. But can something change from one state to another?

**Ravi:** Of course! You see it every day. When Mummy puts water in the freezer, it becomes ice — liquid to solid. When she boils water for chai, steam rises — liquid to gas. When you take a cold bottle out of the fridge on a humid day, water droplets appear on the outside — gas to liquid.

**Kiran:** So heating makes things go from solid to liquid to gas, and cooling reverses it?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Solid to liquid is melting. Liquid to gas is evaporation or boiling. Gas to liquid is condensation. Liquid to solid is freezing. The same water molecules — H2O — exist in all three states. Only their arrangement and movement change.

**Kiran:** In solids, the molecules are tightly packed and vibrate. In liquids, they are a bit looser and flow. In gases, they are far apart and move freely.

**Ravi:** That is a clear picture. Temperature determines which state the molecules settle into. Add heat and they move more, becoming less organized. Remove heat and they slow down, becoming more structured.

## Mixtures and Separation

**Kiran:** Papa, is everything around us a pure substance or a mixture?

**Ravi:** Most things are mixtures! Air is a mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, and other gases. Soil is a mixture of sand, clay, minerals, and organic matter. Even the milk you drink is a mixture of water, fats, proteins, and sugars.

**Kiran:** But salt is a pure substance?

**Ravi:** Pure common salt — sodium chloride — is. But the "salt" in your kitchen also has added iodine, so even that is technically a mixture. Very few things we encounter in daily life are truly pure.

**Kiran:** And we separate mixtures using different methods depending on the properties — sieving for different sizes, filtration for insoluble solids in liquids, evaporation for dissolved solids, and magnetic separation for iron in a mixture.

**Ravi:** Right. Each method exploits a specific property difference. Sieving uses size. Filtration uses solubility. Evaporation uses boiling point. Magnetic separation uses, well, magnetism. Choosing the right method is like choosing the right tool from a toolbox.

## Recyclable Materials and Sustainability

**Kiran:** Papa, our school started a recycling drive. We sort paper, plastic, glass, and metal into different bins. Why does recycling matter?

**Ravi:** Think about where materials come from. Metal comes from ore dug out of mines. Paper comes from trees that take years to grow. Plastic comes from petroleum — a non-renewable resource. Making new materials from scratch uses huge amounts of energy and produces pollution.

**Kiran:** But recycling reuses the material, so less digging, less cutting, less pollution?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Recycling aluminium uses only about 5 percent of the energy needed to make aluminium from bauxite ore. Recycling paper saves trees. Recycling glass reduces the need for sand mining.

**Kiran:** But some plastics cannot be recycled, right?

**Ravi:** Multi-layer packaging — like the inside of chip packets — is very hard to recycle because it combines plastic, aluminium, and other materials bonded together. That is why reducing and reusing is even better than recycling. Carry a cloth bag instead of getting a new plastic bag each time. Use a steel water bottle instead of buying plastic bottles.

**Kiran:** So the best approach is reduce first, reuse second, recycle third?

**Ravi:** That is the hierarchy. And it starts with understanding materials — knowing what things are made of, where they come from, and what happens to them after we are done. That knowledge turns a passive consumer into a responsible citizen.
