## Looking Up at the Sky

On a clear night, far from city lights, look up at the sky. Thousands of tiny points of light sparkle above you. Ancient humans too looked at the same sky — and they wondered. Over thousands of years, that wonder turned into science.

Today we know that those tiny dots of light are **stars** — massive balls of hot, glowing gas — many of them far larger than our own Sun, and some so far away that the light you see left them thousands of years ago.

> **Key idea:** The universe is unimaginably vast. Understanding it — from the stars above to the planet we live on — is one of humanity's greatest achievements.

## Stars and the Night Sky

### What Is a Star?

A **star** is a huge ball of hot, glowing gas (mainly hydrogen and helium) that produces its own light and heat through nuclear reactions in its core.

Our **Sun** is a star — but because it is so close to us (about 150 million km away), it appears large and bright. Other stars are much further away, so they appear as tiny points of light in the night sky.

### How Far Are the Stars?

Stars are so far away that we measure their distance in **light years**.

> **One light year** = the distance light travels in one year = about 9.46 lakh crore kilometres (9.46 × 10¹² km).

The nearest star to the Sun is **Proxima Centauri** — about 4.2 light years away. Even at the speed of light (3 lakh km per second!), it would take 4.2 years to reach it.

### Constellations — Pictures in the Sky

Ancient people noticed that some groups of stars seemed to form patterns in the sky. They named these patterns after animals, people, and objects from their myths and stories. These patterns are called **constellations**.

The **International Astronomical Union (IAU)** recognises **88 official constellations** that together cover the entire sky.

![Night sky showing the constellations Orion the Hunter with belt of three stars, and Ursa Major Saptarishi in the shape of a ladle](constellations-orion-saptarishi.png)

Some well-known constellations:

**Orion (The Hunter)**
- One of the most recognisable constellations in the world.
- Visible in winter nights in India.
- Features a distinctive row of **three bright stars** that form "Orion's Belt".
- Contains the bright star **Betelgeuse** (reddish, top-left shoulder of Orion) and **Rigel** (bluish-white, bottom-right foot).
- In Indian tradition, this region of the sky contains the **Mrigashira** nakshatra.

**Canis Major (The Great Dog)**
- Found just below Orion — as if following the hunter.
- Contains **Sirius** — the **brightest star in the night sky** (excluding the Sun).
- Sirius is so bright that it is easily visible even from cities.

**Taurus (The Bull)**
- Found near Orion.
- Contains the **Pleiades** — a beautiful cluster of stars sometimes called the **Seven Sisters** (Krittika nakshatra in Indian astronomy).
- Also contains the bright star **Aldebaran**.

**Ursa Major (The Great Bear) — Saptarishi**
- One of the most famous constellations.
- Contains the **Big Dipper** (or Plough) — seven bright stars in the shape of a large ladle or dipper.
- In India, these seven stars are called the **Saptarishi** (Seven Sages) — named after seven great Hindu sages (Marichi, Atri, Angiras, Pulaha, Kratu, Pulastya, Vashistha).
- These stars are visible throughout the year in northern India.

**Ursa Minor (The Little Bear)**
- Contains the **Little Dipper** — seven stars forming a smaller ladle.
- The most important star in Ursa Minor is **Polaris** — the **Pole Star (Dhruva Tara)**.

### The Pole Star (Dhruva Tara / Polaris)

The **Pole Star** (Polaris) is a special star:
- It appears almost exactly above the **North Pole** of the Earth.
- While all other stars appear to move across the sky during the night (because the Earth is rotating), the Pole Star appears almost **stationary**.
- Sailors, travellers, and explorers for thousands of years used the Pole Star to find **north** — it is nature's compass!

*How to find the Pole Star:*
1. Find the Big Dipper (Saptarishi) — seven bright stars.
2. Look at the two stars at the "front" of the dipper's bowl.
3. Draw an imaginary line through these two stars and extend it about five times the distance between them.
4. The bright star you reach is Polaris — the Pole Star.

> In Indian tradition, the Pole Star is called **Dhruva Tara**, named after the young devotee Dhruva from Hindu mythology who stood perfectly still in meditation and was placed in the sky as the unmoving star.

### Indian Astronomy — Nakshatras

Indian astronomers have been studying the night sky for thousands of years. The Indian system divides the sky into **27 nakshatras** (lunar mansions) — groups of stars near the path of the Moon. The Moon passes through one nakshatra each day of its 27-day orbit.

Famous ancient Indian observatories include the **Jantar Mantar** in Jaipur (and Delhi), built by Maharaja Jai Singh II in the early 18th century, with massive stone instruments to measure time, track stars, and predict eclipses with great accuracy.

## The Solar System

Our star — the **Sun** — has a family of objects orbiting it. This entire family is called the **Solar System**.

### The Sun

- The Sun is a **star** at the centre of our Solar System.
- It is about **150 million km** from Earth (this distance is called **1 Astronomical Unit, AU**).
- The diameter of the Sun is about **139 lakh km** — roughly **109 times** the diameter of Earth.
- The Sun's surface temperature is about **5,500°C**; the core temperature is about **15 million°C**.
- The Sun provides **light** and **heat** to all planets, making life on Earth possible.
- Light from the Sun takes about **8 minutes 20 seconds** to reach Earth.

### The Eight Planets

![The eight planets of the solar system in order from the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune with relative sizes shown](solar-system-planets.png)

Eight planets orbit the Sun in fixed paths called **orbits**. In order from the Sun:

| Planet | Memory Trick | Key Feature |
| ------ | ------------ | ----------- |
| **Mercury** | **M**y | Smallest planet; closest to Sun; no atmosphere; extreme temperatures |
| **Venus** | **V**ery | Hottest planet (due to thick CO₂ atmosphere); brightest object in the sky after the Moon; called the **Morning Star** or **Evening Star** |
| **Earth** | **E**xcited | Only known planet with life; called the **Blue Planet** (70% water); has one natural satellite — the Moon |
| **Mars** | **M**artin | Called the **Red Planet** (iron oxide/rust on surface); has the tallest volcano in the solar system (Olympus Mons); India's **Mangalyaan** (Mars Orbiter Mission) reached Mars in 2014 |
| **Jupiter** | **J**umped | **Largest planet** in the solar system; has the Great Red Spot (a storm larger than Earth that has lasted centuries); has 95 known moons |
| **Saturn** | **S**lowly | Famous for its spectacular **rings** (made of ice and rock); second largest planet; has 146 known moons, including Titan |
| **Uranus** | **U**p | **Rotates on its side** (its axis is nearly horizontal); has faint rings; appears blue-green due to methane gas |
| **Neptune** | **N**eptune | **Farthest** from the Sun; deep blue colour; has the strongest winds in the solar system |

**Memory trick:** **My Very Excited Martin Jumped Slowly Up Neptune**

### Revolution and Rotation

- **Rotation** — spinning of a planet on its own axis (causes day and night). Earth takes **24 hours** (1 day) to rotate once.
- **Revolution** — movement of a planet around the Sun in its orbit (causes a year). Earth takes **365.25 days** (1 year) to complete one revolution.

| Planet | Time to orbit the Sun (1 year) |
| ------ | ------------------------------ |
| Mercury | 88 Earth days |
| Venus | 225 Earth days |
| Earth | 365.25 days |
| Mars | 687 Earth days |
| Jupiter | 12 Earth years |
| Saturn | 29.5 Earth years |
| Neptune | 165 Earth years |

### Inner Planets and Outer Planets

The **asteroid belt** between Mars and Jupiter divides the solar system into:

- **Inner planets** (closer to Sun, smaller, rocky): Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars
- **Outer planets** (farther from Sun, larger, mostly gas): Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune (also called **Gas Giants** or **Ice Giants**)

### Venus — The Morning/Evening Star

Venus is often called the **Morning Star** or **Evening Star** — but it is not a star! It is a planet.

- Venus is so bright it can be seen with the naked eye just before sunrise (in the east) or just after sunset (in the west).
- It is bright because it is close to Earth and its thick clouds **reflect sunlight** very well.
- Venus is the **hottest planet** (even hotter than Mercury, which is closer to the Sun!) because its thick atmosphere of CO₂ traps heat — a runaway greenhouse effect.

### Pluto — The Dwarf Planet

Pluto was considered the 9th planet for many years. In **2006**, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) reclassified it as a **dwarf planet** because it has not cleared its orbital neighbourhood of other objects.

There are now five officially recognised dwarf planets, including Pluto, Eris, and Ceres.

## The Moon — Earth's Natural Satellite

A **natural satellite** is a natural object that orbits a planet. The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite.

### Facts About the Moon

- Distance from Earth: about **3,84,000 km** (3.84 lakh km)
- Time to orbit Earth: **27.3 days** (~1 month)
- The Moon rotates on its own axis in the same time as it orbits Earth → we always see the **same face** of the Moon from Earth.
- The Moon has **no atmosphere**, no water, and no weather.
- The Moon does not produce its own light — it **reflects sunlight**.
- The Moon's surface has many **craters** — formed billions of years ago when meteorites crashed into it.
- Gravity on the Moon is about **1/6th** of Earth's gravity → you would weigh 6 times less on the Moon!

### Phases of the Moon

![Diagram showing the eight phases of the Moon in a circle: new moon, crescent, quarter, gibbous, full moon, and back](phases-of-the-moon.png)

As the Moon orbits Earth, the angle between the Sun, Moon, and Earth changes → we see different amounts of the Moon's lit surface → this creates the **phases of the Moon**:

New Moon → Crescent → Quarter → Gibbous → Full Moon → Gibbous → Quarter → Crescent → New Moon

This cycle takes about **29.5 days** (called a **lunar month**).

### India's Moon Missions — Chandrayaan

India has sent three missions to the Moon:

- **Chandrayaan-1 (2008)** — India's first Moon mission. Discovered evidence of **water molecules on the Moon's surface** — a landmark discovery!
- **Chandrayaan-2 (2019)** — Orbiter (still functioning), lander and rover (crash-landed near the south pole).
- **Chandrayaan-3 (2023)** — **Lander Vikram** and **Rover Pragyan** successfully landed on the **lunar south pole** on **23 August 2023** — making India the **first country to land near the Moon's south pole** and the **fourth country overall** to achieve a soft lunar landing (after USA, USSR, and China).

> This was a historic moment for India and all of science. The Chandrayaan-3 landing site was named **Shiv Shakti Point** by Prime Minister Narendra Modi.

## Asteroids and Comets

### Asteroids

**Asteroids** are rocky objects that orbit the Sun — but are much smaller than planets (too small to be called planets).

- Most asteroids are found in the **Asteroid Belt** — a zone between Mars and Jupiter.
- They range in size from tiny pebbles to nearly 1,000 km across.
- The largest asteroid is **Ceres** (now classified as a dwarf planet).
- Scientists believe the asteroid belt contains material left over from the formation of the solar system, about 4.6 billion years ago.

### Comets

**Comets** are icy objects that travel through the solar system in long, elliptical (stretched oval) orbits.

- They are made of ice, dust, and rock — sometimes called **"dirty snowballs"**.
- When a comet comes close to the Sun, the ice heats up and turns directly to gas (sublimation) → this gas and dust stream away from the Sun as the **comet's tail** — which can be millions of kilometres long!
- A comet's tail always points **away from the Sun** (blown by solar wind).

**Halley's Comet** is the most famous comet:
- Named after British astronomer Edmond Halley who correctly predicted its return.
- Visible from Earth with the naked eye roughly every **76 years**.
- Last visible in 1986 — next expected around **2061**.
- Has been recorded in ancient Indian, Chinese, and Babylonian texts for thousands of years.

> The appearance of Halley's Comet in 1066 CE is famously depicted in the **Bayeux Tapestry** in Europe. Ancient Indians recorded it as a **ketu** (a tail-bearing celestial object) in their astronomical texts.

## Our Galaxy — The Milky Way

![The Milky Way galaxy shown from above as a spiral with a bright centre and our Sun's position marked on one of the spiral arms](milky-way-galaxy.png)

The Sun is just one of **hundreds of billions of stars** in a huge system called the **Milky Way Galaxy**.

- The Milky Way is a **spiral galaxy** — it has a bright central bulge and spiral arms spreading outward.
- It is so vast that light takes about **1 lakh years** to cross from one side to the other.
- Our Solar System is located about **26,000 light years** from the centre of the Milky Way.
- On a very dark, clear night (away from city lights), you can see the Milky Way as a **hazy white band** stretching across the sky — millions of stars so far away they merge into a milky glow.

In Indian tradition, the Milky Way is called **Akasha Ganga** (the river of the sky) — a beautiful name linking the celestial river to India's sacred Ganga.

## The Universe

The **universe** is everything that exists — all matter, energy, space, and time.

- The universe contains **hundreds of billions of galaxies**.
- Each galaxy contains **hundreds of billions of stars**.
- The universe is approximately **13.8 billion years old**.
- It began with the **Big Bang** — a rapid expansion from an extremely hot and dense state.
- The universe is still **expanding** today — galaxies are moving farther apart.

### Is There Life Beyond Earth?

One of the greatest questions in science: **Are we alone in the universe?**

- So far, Earth is the **only known planet with life**.
- Scientists are searching for life (or signs of past life) on **Mars**, on **Europa** (a moon of Jupiter with a liquid water ocean under its ice), and on **Enceladus** (a moon of Saturn).
- Astronomers have discovered thousands of **exoplanets** — planets orbiting other stars. Some are in the **"habitable zone"** (right distance from their star for liquid water to exist).
- The search for life beyond Earth is called **astrobiology**.
- India's space agency **ISRO** continues to develop missions — including the planned **Gaganyaan** mission that will send Indian astronauts (called **Vyomanauts**) to space.

> **The universe is so vast that it would be strange if Earth were the only place with life. The search continues — and it is one of the most exciting frontiers of modern science!**

## Light Pollution — Losing the Night Sky

In ancient times, everyone could see thousands of stars at night. Today, in cities, you may see only a few dozen.

**Light pollution** is the excess artificial light from street lamps, buildings, signs, and vehicles that brightens the night sky and makes faint stars invisible.

*Effects:*
- We lose our connection with the night sky.
- Nocturnal animals (owls, moths, sea turtles) are disturbed — bright lights confuse them.
- Astronomers must travel to remote areas to use telescopes.

*What can we do?* Use **shielded lights** that point downward, switch off unnecessary outdoor lights, and use warmer-toned LEDs. Visit a dark-sky area to see the Milky Way!
