## Stars — Not Just Points of Light

**Kiran:** Papa, on our trip to the mountains last winter, I saw thousands of stars. In the city I can barely see a dozen. Why?

**Ravi:** Light pollution. City streetlights, building lights, and traffic create a glow that washes out the faint stars. In the mountains, away from artificial lights, you can see the sky as our ancestors saw it — full of stars, with the Milky Way stretching across like a river of light.

**Kiran:** The Milky Way — our textbook calls it Akasha Ganga!

**Ravi:** A beautiful name. In the night sky, it appears as a hazy white band. But it is actually our galaxy — a spiral collection of hundreds of billions of stars. Our Sun is just one of those stars, located about 26,000 light years from the centre. When you look at the Milky Way, you are looking at the dense heart of our galaxy from within.

**Kiran:** And a light year is the distance light travels in one year — about 9.46 lakh crore kilometres. The nearest star after the Sun, Proxima Centauri, is 4.2 light years away. Even at the speed of light it would take over 4 years to get there!

**Ravi:** The universe is vast beyond imagination. And every star you see is a massive ball of hot gas — many far larger than our Sun — producing light and heat through nuclear reactions. The red star Betelgeuse in Orion is so large that if you placed it where the Sun is, it would swallow Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars.

## Constellations and Indian Astronomy

**Kiran:** Papa, our textbook talks about Saptarishi — the seven sages — as a constellation. Is that the same as the Big Dipper?

**Ravi:** Yes. The seven bright stars of Ursa Major — the Big Dipper or the Plough — are called Saptarishi in Indian astronomy, named after seven great rishis: Marichi, Atri, Angiras, Pulaha, Kratu, Pulastya, and Vashistha. Indian astronomers have been mapping the sky for thousands of years.

**Kiran:** And the Pole Star — Dhruva Tara — is the one that never moves?

**Ravi:** It appears almost stationary because it sits almost exactly above Earth's North Pole. As the Earth rotates, all other stars seem to move across the sky, but Polaris stays put. To find it, use the two front stars of the Big Dipper bowl, draw an imaginary line through them, and extend it about five times — the bright star you reach is Polaris.

**Kiran:** The story of Dhruva is that he meditated so steadily that he was placed as the unmoving star in the sky!

**Ravi:** A lovely connection between mythology and astronomy. Indian astronomy also divides the sky into 27 nakshatras — lunar mansions — based on the Moon's path. The Jantar Mantar observatories in Jaipur and Delhi, built by Maharaja Jai Singh II, used massive stone instruments to track stars and predict eclipses with remarkable accuracy — all without telescopes!

## The Solar System

**Kiran:** Papa, I know the order of planets — My Very Excited Martin Jumped Slowly Up Neptune. But why is Pluto no longer a planet?

**Ravi:** In 2006, the International Astronomical Union defined a planet as an object that orbits the Sun, has enough mass to be roughly round, and has cleared its orbital neighbourhood of other objects. Pluto meets the first two criteria but not the third — its orbit passes through a zone full of other icy objects. So it was reclassified as a dwarf planet.

**Kiran:** That seems a bit unfair to Pluto!

**Ravi:** Many people felt that way. But definitions in science have to be precise. There are now five recognised dwarf planets, and Pluto is the most famous. The important thing is that our understanding keeps improving.

**Kiran:** What about the planets themselves? Venus is the hottest even though Mercury is closer to the Sun — that seems wrong.

**Ravi:** It makes sense once you know why. Venus has an extremely thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide, which traps heat — a runaway greenhouse effect. Mercury has almost no atmosphere, so heat escapes easily into space. It is like comparing a room with closed windows to one with open windows — the closed room heats up more even if the heater is farther away.

## India's Moon Missions

**Kiran:** Papa, tell me about Chandrayaan-3! I saw the landing on TV and everyone was cheering!

**Ravi:** On 23 August 2023, India made history. The Chandrayaan-3 lander Vikram and rover Pragyan successfully landed near the Moon's south pole. India became the first country ever to land near the lunar south pole and the fourth country overall to achieve a soft landing on the Moon.

**Kiran:** Why is the south pole special?

**Ravi:** Scientists believe that permanently shadowed craters near the south pole may contain water ice. If water exists on the Moon, future astronauts could use it for drinking, growing food, and even splitting it into hydrogen and oxygen for rocket fuel. The south pole could be the key to establishing a long-term human presence on the Moon.

**Kiran:** And Chandrayaan-1 in 2008 actually discovered water on the Moon?

**Ravi:** Yes! Chandrayaan-1's Moon Impact Probe detected water molecules on the lunar surface — a discovery that changed our understanding of the Moon. Before that, many scientists believed the Moon was completely dry. India's contribution to lunar science has been truly significant.

**Kiran:** ISRO is also planning Gaganyaan — India's first crewed space mission?

**Ravi:** Yes. Indian astronauts — called Vyomanauts — will be sent to space orbit. India will join a very small club of nations that have sent humans to space. From Chandrayaan to Mangalyaan to Gaganyaan, India's space programme is achieving extraordinary things on a comparatively small budget.

## The Scale of the Universe

**Kiran:** Papa, how big is the universe really? I cannot imagine distances in light years.

**Ravi:** Let me try a scale model. If the Sun were a grape — about 1 centimetre across — then Earth would be a tiny grain of sand about 1 metre away. Jupiter would be a peppercorn about 5 metres away. Neptune would be about 30 metres away. The nearest star, Proxima Centauri, would be another grape — about 270 kilometres away!

**Kiran:** 270 kilometres away from a 1-centimetre grape? The emptiness between stars is enormous!

**Ravi:** And the Milky Way galaxy, in this scale, would be about 10 crore kilometres across. The observable universe contains hundreds of billions of galaxies like ours, each with hundreds of billions of stars. The universe is about 13.8 billion years old and still expanding.

**Kiran:** Are we alone? Is there life out there?

**Ravi:** That is one of the greatest unanswered questions in science. Scientists are searching for signs of life on Mars, on Jupiter's moon Europa which has a liquid ocean under its ice, and on Saturn's moon Enceladus. Thousands of exoplanets — planets orbiting other stars — have been discovered, some in the habitable zone where liquid water could exist.

**Kiran:** The universe is so vast that it would be strange if Earth were the only place with life.

**Ravi:** Many scientists agree. But so far, we have no confirmed evidence. The search continues — through telescopes, space probes, and the curiosity of young minds like yours. Who knows, beta — perhaps your generation will be the one to find the answer.
