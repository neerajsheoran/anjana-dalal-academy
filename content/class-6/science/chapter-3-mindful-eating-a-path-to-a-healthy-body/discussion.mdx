## Why Different Foods for Different Jobs

**Kiran:** Papa, my friend Priya says she only eats rice and dal because that is enough. Is she right?

**Ravi:** Rice gives mainly carbohydrates — energy to run and think. Dal gives protein — building blocks for muscles and growth. But what about strong bones? What about fighting infections? What about keeping her digestion healthy?

**Kiran:** She would need calcium for bones, vitamins to fight infections, and roughage for digestion. Those come from other foods!

**Ravi:** Exactly. Our body is like a factory that needs different raw materials. Carbohydrates are the fuel. Proteins are the bricks. Fats store extra energy and keep us warm. Vitamins and minerals are the maintenance workers — small in number but absolutely essential. Roughage keeps the machinery clean. And water is the transport system.

**Kiran:** So no single food can do everything?

**Ravi:** No. That is why a balanced diet — one that includes cereals, pulses, vegetables, fruits, and some fats — is so important. Think of a thali — roti, dal, sabzi, rice, curd, salad, and a small piece of jaggery. Each item contributes something different. The Indian thali is actually one of the most balanced meals in the world!

## Testing Food — Kitchen Science

**Kiran:** Papa, our teacher showed us how to test food for starch using iodine. We put drops on a boiled potato and it turned blue-black! But when we tested oil, nothing happened.

**Ravi:** Because potatoes are rich in starch — a type of carbohydrate — and the iodine reacts with starch to produce that blue-black colour. Oil has no starch, so no colour change. What about the fat test?

**Kiran:** We rubbed a groundnut on paper and held it up to light. There was a translucent oily patch. But when we rubbed an apple, there was no oily patch.

**Ravi:** So you discovered that groundnuts contain fat but apples do not. These are simple but powerful tests. The protein test uses copper sulphate and caustic soda — if the solution turns violet, protein is present. Your teacher would demonstrate that one because the chemicals need careful handling.

**Kiran:** So we can actually test food to find out what nutrients it contains!

**Ravi:** Yes. And here is a hypothesis for you to think about — if you tested paneer, would it show positive for protein, fat, or both?

**Kiran:** I think both! Paneer is made from milk, which has protein. And it feels oily, so it probably has fat too.

**Ravi:** Test it and find out. That is the scientific method in the kitchen!

## Millets — India's Forgotten Superfoods

**Kiran:** Papa, our school canteen started serving ragi dosa and bajra roti. My friends say millets are "poor people's food." Is that true?

**Ravi:** That is a misconception. Millets were the staple food of Indians for thousands of years — long before rice and wheat became dominant. Ragi has the highest calcium content of any cereal — it is given to babies in Karnataka as their first solid food and to elderly people to prevent weak bones.

**Kiran:** More calcium than milk?

**Ravi:** Per serving, ragi is comparable to milk in calcium content. Bajra is rich in protein and iron. Jowar has high iron and calcium. These grains also need very little water to grow — much less than rice — making them better for the environment.

**Kiran:** So millets are actually healthier than rice and wheat?

**Ravi:** In many ways, yes. They have more fibre, more minerals, and a lower glycemic index, which means they release energy slowly and do not cause blood sugar spikes. That is why the Indian government calls them "nutri-cereals" and the United Nations declared 2023 as the International Year of Millets — on India's proposal.

**Kiran:** I should tell my friends that eating millets is not old-fashioned — it is actually smarter!

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Traditional food wisdom often turns out to be nutritionally excellent. Our grandparents knew this intuitively. Now modern science is confirming it.

## Vitamins and Minerals — Small But Mighty

**Kiran:** Papa, if vitamins are needed in such tiny amounts, why are they so important?

**Ravi:** Think of a recipe. You need 2 kg of flour for rotis but only a pinch of salt. If you forget the flour, you have no rotis. But if you forget the salt — the rotis taste terrible and your body misses an essential mineral. Small quantities, big impact.

**Kiran:** Our textbook says vitamin C helps fight infections and heal wounds. And amla has more vitamin C than even an orange!

**Ravi:** Much more. One small amla has as much vitamin C as two or three oranges. That is why in Indian homes, amla murabba, amla juice, and amla pickle have been traditional health foods for centuries. Another example — vitamin D helps build strong bones, and we get it free from sunlight. Just 15 minutes of morning sun on your skin is enough.

**Kiran:** What happens if you do not get enough iron?

**Ravi:** Iron makes haemoglobin — the molecule in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Without enough iron, your blood cannot carry enough oxygen, you feel tired and weak, and you may become anaemic. This is a serious problem in India, especially for women and girls. Eating spinach, dates, jaggery, and green vegetables helps.

**Kiran:** So the sabzi and gur that Mummy makes us eat are actually fighting anaemia?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Every ingredient in a well-planned meal has a purpose. That is what "mindful eating" means — knowing what your food does for your body.

## Food Miles — Where Does Your Food Come From?

**Kiran:** Papa, the apple I ate today — where did it come from?

**Ravi:** Probably Himachal Pradesh or Kashmir. It travelled hundreds of kilometres to reach our market in a refrigerated truck. That distance is called "food miles."

**Kiran:** Does it matter how far food travels?

**Ravi:** It does. Food picked before it is ripe — so it survives the long journey — often tastes worse and has fewer nutrients. Long transport uses fuel and releases greenhouse gases. And the food is less fresh by the time it reaches you.

**Kiran:** So eating local food — like the bananas from the garden next door or the tomatoes from our local farmer — is better?

**Ravi:** In many ways, yes. It is fresher, more nutritious, cheaper, and supports local farmers. Traditional Indian eating was almost entirely local and seasonal. People ate mangoes only in summer, not in December. They ate leafy greens available in their region. This was not just convenient — it was healthy and sustainable.

**Kiran:** I never thought about the journey my food takes before it reaches my plate.

**Ravi:** That awareness is the first step toward eating mindfully. Know what you eat, know where it comes from, and know what it does for your body. That is what this chapter is really about.
