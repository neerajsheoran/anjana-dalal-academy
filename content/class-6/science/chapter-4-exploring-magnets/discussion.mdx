## The Mystery of Attraction

**Kiran:** Papa, I held a magnet near different things on my study table. It pulled the iron compass box toward it but completely ignored my rubber eraser and wooden pencil. Why does a magnet attract some things and not others?

**Ravi:** That is one of the fundamental questions about magnetism. Materials like iron, nickel, and cobalt have a special internal structure — their atoms have tiny magnetic properties that can align when a magnet is nearby. This alignment creates a pull. Materials like wood, plastic, rubber, and glass do not have this property — their atoms do not respond to magnetic fields.

**Kiran:** So it is about what the material is made of at the atomic level?

**Ravi:** Exactly. We call materials that respond to magnets "magnetic materials" and those that do not "non-magnetic materials." Here is a surprising fact — not all metals are magnetic. Copper, aluminium, and gold are metals but they are non-magnetic.

**Kiran:** That is unexpected! I thought all metals were attracted to magnets.

**Ravi:** That is a common misconception. Only specific metals — mainly iron, nickel, and cobalt — are magnetic. You can test this yourself. Try bringing a magnet near an aluminium foil, a copper wire, and an iron nail. Only the iron nail will be attracted.

## Poles and the Rule of Attraction

**Kiran:** Papa, every magnet has two poles — north and south. Why do like poles repel and unlike poles attract?

**Ravi:** That is actually a deep physics question that scientists are still studying at the fundamental level. But we can observe and state the rule clearly: north attracts south, south attracts north, but north repels north and south repels south.

**Kiran:** I felt it today! When I brought two bar magnets' north poles close to each other, I could feel them pushing apart — even before they touched! It was like an invisible cushion between them.

**Ravi:** That invisible force is the magnetic field. It exists in the space around every magnet. You can visualize it with iron filings — sprinkle them on a paper placed over a magnet, tap gently, and the filings arrange into beautiful curved lines from one pole to the other.

**Kiran:** The field is strongest at the poles, right?

**Ravi:** Yes. The iron filings are densest near the poles, showing that the magnetic force is strongest there. As you move away from the magnet, the field weakens. This is why a magnet can pick up a pin from a centimetre away but not from across the room.

## The Compass — Nature's Direction Finder

**Kiran:** Papa, how does a compass always point north? Is there a huge magnet at the North Pole?

**Ravi:** Almost! The Earth itself behaves like a giant magnet. Deep inside the Earth, flowing molten iron and nickel create a magnetic field. The geographic North Pole is near the Earth's magnetic south pole — so the north-seeking pole of a compass needle is attracted toward geographic north.

**Kiran:** Wait — the geographic North Pole is a magnetic south pole? That is confusing!

**Ravi:** It is! But think about it — the north pole of the compass needle is attracted toward geographic north. Since unlike poles attract, the geographic north must be near a magnetic south pole. It is a bit of a naming quirk from history.

**Kiran:** Sailors used compasses for thousands of years for navigation. Did Indian sailors use compasses too?

**Ravi:** Ancient Indian texts mention the use of magnetic rocks for finding direction. Indian, Chinese, and Arab sailors all used magnetic navigation. Before GPS and satellite technology, a compass was the most reliable way to find your direction on the open sea — just a small magnetic needle floating freely, pointing the way home.

## Making and Losing Magnetism

**Kiran:** Papa, can I make my own magnet?

**Ravi:** Yes! Take an iron nail and stroke it repeatedly in one direction with one pole of a bar magnet — say, from the head to the tip, lifting the magnet away each time and repeating. After about 50 strokes, test the nail — hold it near some iron filings or small pins.

**Kiran:** It will attract them?

**Ravi:** It should! The repeated stroking aligns the tiny internal magnetic regions — called domains — inside the nail. When enough domains point in the same direction, the nail becomes a temporary magnet.

**Kiran:** And can a magnet lose its magnetism?

**Ravi:** Yes — if you drop it repeatedly, hammer it, or heat it strongly, the aligned domains get disturbed and jumble up again. The magnet weakens or loses its magnetism entirely. This is called demagnetisation.

**Kiran:** What about electromagnets? They use electricity, right?

**Ravi:** When electric current flows through a coil of wire wrapped around an iron core, the iron becomes a magnet — but only while the current flows. Turn off the current and the magnetism disappears. Electromagnets are incredibly useful — cranes use them to lift heavy iron scrap, electric bells use them, and MRI machines in hospitals use powerful electromagnets to scan the human body.

## Magnets in Our Daily Life

**Kiran:** Papa, where else are magnets used that I might not notice?

**Ravi:** Open your refrigerator door. Notice how it seals shut? There is a magnetic strip in the rubber seal around the door. Your earphones and speakers use tiny magnets to convert electrical signals into sound. The hard disk in a computer stores data using magnetic fields. Maglev trains in Japan and China use powerful magnets to float above the track — they literally ride on magnetic force!

**Kiran:** Even my ATM card has a magnetic strip on the back!

**Ravi:** Right — though that is being replaced by chips now. But the principle remains. And here is something interesting — animals use magnetism too. Migratory birds like the bar-tailed godwit, which flies from Alaska to New Zealand, are thought to sense the Earth's magnetic field to navigate. Some species of bacteria have tiny magnetic crystals inside their cells that help them orient.

**Kiran:** Birds have built-in compasses? That is incredible!

**Ravi:** Nature figured out magnetism long before humans did. Our magnets are just clever imitations of what the Earth and its creatures have been using for millions of years.
