## Factors and Multiples — Two Sides of One Coin

**Kiran:** Papa, I keep getting confused between factors and multiples. They seem so similar.

**Ravi:** Think of it this way. You have 12 laddoos and you want to arrange them in equal rows. What are the possible arrangements?

**Kiran:** 1 row of 12, 2 rows of 6, 3 rows of 4, 4 rows of 3, 6 rows of 2, or 12 rows of 1.

**Ravi:** So the numbers you can divide 12 into equal groups of are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12. These are the factors of 12 — they divide 12 exactly with no remainder.

**Kiran:** And multiples?

**Ravi:** Multiples go the other direction. What do you get when you multiply 3 by different whole numbers? 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18... Those are all multiples of 3. So 12 is a multiple of 3, and 3 is a factor of 12. Two sides of the same relationship!

**Kiran:** Oh! If A is a factor of B, then B is a multiple of A. They are connected!

**Ravi:** Exactly. Factors divide into a number. Multiples are produced by multiplying a number. Factors are finite — 12 only has six factors. Multiples are infinite — you can keep multiplying forever.

## Prime Numbers — The Atoms of Mathematics

**Kiran:** Papa, why are prime numbers called the "building blocks" of all numbers?

**Ravi:** What are atoms?

**Kiran:** The smallest units that make up all matter. Everything is made of atoms.

**Ravi:** Prime numbers are the atoms of mathematics. Every whole number greater than 1 is either a prime itself, or can be built by multiplying primes together. Take 60 — it equals 2 times 2 times 3 times 5. All primes. Take 91 — it is 7 times 13. All primes.

**Kiran:** And this prime factorisation is unique? There is no other way to break 60 into primes?

**Ravi:** That is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic — one of the most important results in maths. No matter how you factor 60, you always end up with two 2s, one 3, and one 5. Just as water is always H2O — two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom — 60 is always 2 squared times 3 times 5.

**Kiran:** Is 1 a prime number? It only has one factor — itself.

**Ravi:** That is precisely why 1 is NOT prime. A prime must have exactly two factors — 1 and itself. The number 1 has only one factor. If we called 1 a prime, the Fundamental Theorem would break — factorisation would not be unique because you could multiply by 1 as many times as you want.

## The Sieve of Eratosthenes

**Kiran:** Papa, how do you find all the prime numbers up to 100? Do you check each one individually?

**Ravi:** There is a brilliant shortcut from about 2,300 years ago. The Greek mathematician Eratosthenes invented what we call the Sieve of Eratosthenes. Write all numbers from 2 to 100. Circle 2, then cross out all multiples of 2. Circle 3, cross out all multiples of 3. Circle 5, cross out multiples of 5. Circle 7, cross out multiples of 7.

**Kiran:** Why do we stop at 7? There are bigger primes.

**Ravi:** Because any composite number up to 100 must have a prime factor less than or equal to the square root of 100, which is 10. The primes up to 10 are 2, 3, 5, and 7. After sieving out their multiples, everything remaining must be prime. It is like using a series of filters — each sieve catches different composite numbers.

**Kiran:** After the sieve, I can see that 2 is the only even prime. All other even numbers got crossed out in the first step.

**Ravi:** Good observation. 2 is special — the only even prime and the smallest prime. Every other even number has 2 as an extra factor, making it composite. This also means that except for 2, all primes are odd — though not all odd numbers are prime, as 9 and 15 show.

## HCF and LCM — Solving Real Problems

**Kiran:** Papa, when would I actually use HCF and LCM in real life?

**Ravi:** Here is a Diwali problem. Your mother has 36 gulab jamuns and 48 rasgullas. She wants to make gift boxes where each box has the same number of gulab jamuns AND the same number of rasgullas, using up everything with nothing left over. What is the maximum number of boxes she can make?

**Kiran:** I need to find a number that divides both 36 and 48. The biggest such number — that is the HCF!

**Ravi:** Go ahead and find it.

**Kiran:** 36 is 2 squared times 3 squared. 48 is 2 to the fourth times 3. The common primes are 2 and 3. Take the lower power of each — 2 squared and 3. That gives 4 times 3, which is 12. So she can make 12 boxes!

**Ravi:** Each box gets 36 divided by 12 equals 3 gulab jamuns and 48 divided by 12 equals 4 rasgullas. Now for LCM — two temple bells ring at different intervals. One rings every 12 minutes, the other every 18 minutes. If both ring together at 6 AM, when do they ring together again?

**Kiran:** I need the smallest time that is a multiple of both 12 and 18. That is the LCM. 12 is 2 squared times 3. 18 is 2 times 3 squared. Take the higher power of each prime — 2 squared and 3 squared. That is 4 times 9, which is 36 minutes. They ring together at 6:36 AM!

**Ravi:** Perfect. HCF helps when you want to divide things into the largest equal groups. LCM helps when you want to find when repeating events coincide.

## Primes and Internet Security

**Kiran:** Papa, our textbook says prime numbers are used in internet security. How can maths protect passwords?

**Ravi:** When you shop online or use UPI to pay, your data is encrypted — scrambled so nobody can read it. The encryption relies on a simple mathematical fact: multiplying two large prime numbers is easy, but finding those prime factors from the product is extremely hard.

**Kiran:** How hard?

**Ravi:** Imagine I give you two prime numbers, each with 150 digits, and ask you to multiply them. A computer does it in a fraction of a second. Now imagine I give you only the product — a 300-digit number — and ask you to find the two primes. Even the world's fastest supercomputer would take billions of years.

**Kiran:** So the "lock" is the big number, and the "key" is knowing the two primes?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Your bank knows the primes. A hacker would have to factor the product, which is essentially impossible with current technology. This system, called RSA encryption, protects every online transaction. So when your mother uses her phone to pay for groceries through Google Pay, prime numbers are silently keeping her money safe.

**Kiran:** Mathematics literally protects our money. That is incredible!

**Ravi:** And that is why mathematicians say prime numbers are the most important numbers in all of mathematics. They are simple, ancient, and yet at the heart of modern technology.
