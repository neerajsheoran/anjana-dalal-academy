## What Fractions Really Mean

**Kiran:** Papa, I know that 3/4 means three out of four parts. But why do we even need fractions? Whole numbers seem easier.

**Ravi:** Try sharing 3 rotis equally among 4 people. Can you do it with whole numbers?

**Kiran:** No... each person gets less than one roti. Oh! Each person gets 3/4 of a roti!

**Ravi:** Exactly. Fractions exist because the world does not always divide into neat whole numbers. When the shopkeeper weighs 750 grams of dal, that is 3/4 of a kilogram. When you have played 45 minutes of a 60-minute cricket match, you have played 3/4 of the match. Fractions are everywhere.

**Kiran:** So the denominator tells me how many equal pieces the whole is cut into, and the numerator tells me how many pieces I have?

**Ravi:** Right. And this is important — the pieces must be equal. If you cut a roti into 4 unequal pieces and take 3, you cannot call that 3/4 because the pieces are different sizes. Fractions only make sense when the whole is divided into equal parts.

## Equivalent Fractions — Same Value, Different Look

**Kiran:** Papa, how can 1/2 and 2/4 and 3/6 all be the same thing? They look completely different!

**Ravi:** Take a roti and cut it in half. You have 1 out of 2 pieces — that is 1/2. Now take each half and cut it in half again. You now have 4 pieces and you are holding 2 of them — that is 2/4. But the amount of roti in your hand has not changed!

**Kiran:** So equivalent fractions are just different ways of describing the same amount?

**Ravi:** Exactly. To create an equivalent fraction, multiply both numerator and denominator by the same number. 1/2 becomes 2/4 (multiply by 2), or 5/10 (multiply by 5), or 50/100 (multiply by 50). All the same value.

**Kiran:** And to simplify, I divide both by their HCF. Like 36/48 — the HCF of 36 and 48 is 12 — so 36 divided by 12 is 3, and 48 divided by 12 is 4. So 36/48 equals 3/4.

**Ravi:** See how HCF from the prime numbers chapter connects here? Maths is not a collection of separate topics — everything links together. And here is a quick check: to verify two fractions are equivalent, cross-multiply. If 3 times 48 equals 4 times 36, which is 144 equals 144, they are equivalent.

## Comparing Fractions — Which Is Bigger?

**Kiran:** Papa, which is more — 3/5 of a pizza or 5/8 of the same pizza? I cannot tell by just looking.

**Ravi:** There are two approaches. The first is to find a common denominator using LCM. The LCM of 5 and 8 is 40. Convert: 3/5 becomes 24/40, and 5/8 becomes 25/40. Now compare the numerators — 25 is bigger than 24, so 5/8 is slightly more.

**Kiran:** What is the second approach?

**Ravi:** Cross-multiplication. Compare 3/5 and 5/8. Compute 3 times 8, which is 24, and 5 times 5, which is 25. Since 25 is greater than 24, the fraction on the right — 5/8 — is larger.

**Kiran:** They are so close though! 24/40 versus 25/40. The difference is just 1/40 of a pizza.

**Ravi:** Excellent observation. In many market situations, these small differences matter. When a jeweller measures gold, even a fraction of a gram changes the price. When a tailor cuts cloth, being off by a fraction of a centimetre can ruin the garment. Precision with fractions is a practical life skill.

## Adding and Subtracting Fractions

**Kiran:** Papa, adding fractions with the same denominator is easy — 3/8 plus 2/8 is 5/8. But why does it become complicated when denominators are different?

**Ravi:** Because you cannot add unlike things directly. Imagine I say "I have 2 five-rupee coins and 3 two-rupee coins. How many coins do I have?" You can say 5 coins. But if I ask "how much money do I have?" you cannot just add 2 and 3 — you need to convert to the same unit first. 10 rupees plus 6 rupees is 16 rupees.

**Kiran:** So with fractions, the denominator is like the "unit" — I need the same denominator before I can add the numerators?

**Ravi:** Precisely. To add 2/3 and 3/4, first find the LCM of 3 and 4, which is 12. Convert: 2/3 becomes 8/12, and 3/4 becomes 9/12. Now add: 8/12 plus 9/12 equals 17/12, which is 1 and 5/12.

**Kiran:** And for subtraction, same process but subtract?

**Ravi:** Right. 5/6 minus 3/8 — LCM of 6 and 8 is 24. Convert: 20/24 minus 9/24 equals 11/24. The method is always the same — make denominators equal, then work with numerators.

## Fractions and Indian Mathematical Heritage

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook mentions Brahmagupta and fractions. What did he do?

**Ravi:** Brahmagupta was a mathematician from Rajasthan who lived around 628 CE. He wrote down clear rules for adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions — the same rules we use today. He wrote them in his book Brahmasphutasiddhanta, which is over 1,400 years old.

**Kiran:** So the rules for fractions were invented in India?

**Ravi:** The notation and systematic rules we use today were developed by Indian mathematicians — Brahmagupta, Mahavira, and others. They wrote fractions with the numerator on top and denominator below, without the bar. Arab mathematicians later added the horizontal line, and European mathematicians adopted the system centuries after that.

**Kiran:** And the textbook also mentions Egyptian fractions — where they only used fractions with numerator 1?

**Ravi:** Yes! Ancient Egyptians wrote every fraction as a sum of unit fractions. So instead of 3/4, they would write 1/2 plus 1/4. Instead of 2/5, they would write 1/3 plus 1/15. It is a different but valid approach. Indian mathematicians were more flexible — they worked with any numerator — which made arithmetic simpler.

**Kiran:** I am glad we use the Indian system. Adding 1/3 plus 1/15 every time I want 2/5 would be exhausting!

**Ravi:** Ha! Yes, practical convenience drove mathematical progress. The Indian way of handling fractions spread across the world because it was simply more efficient.
