## What Is a Fraction?

When we cut a roti into 4 equal pieces and take 3 of them, we have taken **three-fourths** of the roti. This is written as **3/4** — a fraction.

![A circle divided into 4 equal parts with 3 parts shaded, showing the fraction three-fourths](fraction-parts-of-whole.png)

A **fraction** represents a part of a whole. It tells us:
- How many equal parts the whole is divided into (denominator)
- How many of those parts we are considering (numerator)

> **Fraction = Numerator / Denominator**
> - **Numerator** (top number) — how many parts are taken
> - **Denominator** (bottom number) — how many equal parts the whole is divided into

*Examples:*
- 1/2 — one out of 2 equal parts (half)
- 3/4 — three out of 4 equal parts (three-quarters)
- 2/7 — two out of 7 equal parts

---

## Fractions as Part of a Collection

Fractions can also represent part of a group.

*Example:* In a class of 30 students, 18 are girls. What fraction are girls?

> Fraction of girls = 18/30 = 3/5

*Example:* In a bag of 24 marbles, 8 are red. Fraction that are red = 8/24 = 1/3.

---

## Types of Fractions

### Proper Fractions

In a **proper fraction**, the numerator is **less than** the denominator.

> Proper fraction: numerator < denominator

*Examples:* 1/3, 2/5, 7/9, 11/12

A proper fraction is always between 0 and 1.

### Improper Fractions

In an **improper fraction**, the numerator is **greater than or equal to** the denominator.

> Improper fraction: numerator ≥ denominator

*Examples:* 5/3, 8/5, 7/7, 11/4

An improper fraction is always ≥ 1.

### Mixed Numbers

A **mixed number** has a whole number part and a fractional part combined.

*Examples:* 1½, 2¾, 3⅓, 5⅖

**Converting improper fraction to mixed number:**

*Example:* Convert 17/5 to a mixed number.

> Divide: 17 ÷ 5 = **3 remainder 2**
> Mixed number: **3 2/5**
> (3 whole parts and 2/5 left over)

**Converting mixed number to improper fraction:**

*Example:* Convert 4 3/7 to an improper fraction.

> Multiply whole number by denominator, add numerator:
> (4 × 7) + 3 = 28 + 3 = **31**
> Improper fraction: **31/7**

## Fractions on a Number Line

![Number line from 0 to 2 divided into thirds showing positions of one-third, two-thirds, one, four-thirds, five-thirds, and two](fractions-on-number-line.png)

Fractions have a place on the number line between whole numbers.

For thirds (denominator = 3), divide each unit interval into 3 equal parts:
```
0        1/3      2/3       1        4/3      5/3       2
|---------|--------|---------|---------|--------|---------|
0                            1                            2
```

*Important:* 3/3 = 1, 6/3 = 2, 9/3 = 3 (a fraction equals a whole number when numerator is a multiple of denominator).

## Equivalent Fractions

![Rectangles showing one-half, two-fourths, three-sixths, and four-eighths with equal shaded portions proving they are equivalent](equivalent-fractions-bars.png)

**Equivalent fractions** are different fractions that represent the same value.

*Example:* 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8 = 5/10 — all equal half!

**Rule:** Multiply (or divide) both numerator and denominator by the **same non-zero number**.

*Creating equivalent fractions of 2/3:*
- 2/3 = (2×2)/(3×2) = **4/6**
- 2/3 = (2×5)/(3×5) = **10/15**
- 2/3 = (2×10)/(3×10) = **20/30**

**Checking equivalence:** Cross multiply.
- Are 3/4 and 9/12 equivalent? → 3×12 = 36 and 4×9 = 36. **Equal → Yes, equivalent.**
- Are 2/5 and 3/8 equivalent? → 2×8 = 16 and 5×3 = 15. **Not equal → Not equivalent.**

## Simplest Form (Lowest Terms)

A fraction is in its **simplest form** (or **lowest terms**) when the HCF of numerator and denominator is 1 — i.e., they share no common factor other than 1.

**To simplify:** Divide both numerator and denominator by their HCF.

*Example:* Simplify 36/48.

> HCF(36, 48):
> 36 = 2² × 3², 48 = 2⁴ × 3. HCF = 2² × 3 = 12.
> 36/48 = (36÷12)/(48÷12) = **3/4** ✓

*Example:* Simplify 75/100.

> HCF(75, 100):
> 75 = 3 × 5², 100 = 2² × 5². HCF = 5² = 25.
> 75/100 = (75÷25)/(100÷25) = **3/4** ✓

## Comparing Fractions

### Same Denominator

When denominators are equal, the fraction with the **larger numerator** is greater.

> 5/9 **&gt;** 3/9 (because 5 > 3, denominator same)

### Same Numerator

When numerators are equal, the fraction with the **smaller denominator** is greater.
(Smaller denominator = larger equal parts)

> 3/5 **&gt;** 3/8 (because 5 < 8, so each fifth is larger than each eighth)

### Different Numerator and Denominator — Cross Multiplication

Compare a/b and c/d by computing a×d and b×c:
- If a×d > b×c → a/b > c/d
- If a×d = b×c → a/b = c/d
- If a×d < b×c → a/b < c/d

*Example:* Compare 5/7 and 7/10.

> 5 × 10 = 50 and 7 × 7 = 49.
> 50 > 49, so **5/7 > 7/10**.

### Using Common Denominator (LCM Method)

Convert both fractions to the same denominator (use LCM of denominators), then compare numerators.

*Example:* Compare 3/4 and 5/7.

> LCM(4, 7) = 28.
> 3/4 = 21/28 and 5/7 = 20/28.
> 21 > 20, so **3/4 > 5/7**.

## Addition of Fractions

### Same Denominator

> **a/c + b/c = (a+b)/c**

*Example:* 3/8 + 2/8 = **5/8**

### Different Denominators

1. Find the **LCM** of the denominators.
2. Convert each fraction to an equivalent fraction with the LCM as the new denominator.
3. Add the numerators.
4. Simplify if needed.

**Worked Example:** 2/3 + 3/4

> LCM(3, 4) = 12.
> 2/3 = 8/12 and 3/4 = 9/12.
> 8/12 + 9/12 = **17/12 = 1 5/12**

**Worked Example:** 5/6 + 2/9

> LCM(6, 9) = 18.
> 5/6 = 15/18 and 2/9 = 4/18.
> 15/18 + 4/18 = **19/18 = 1 1/18**

### Adding Mixed Numbers

Add whole parts separately, then add fractional parts.

*Example:* 2 1/3 + 1 3/4

> Whole parts: 2 + 1 = 3.
> Fractional parts: 1/3 + 3/4. LCM(3,4)=12. → 4/12 + 9/12 = 13/12 = 1 1/12.
> Total: 3 + 1 1/12 = **4 1/12**

---

## Subtraction of Fractions

### Same Denominator

> **a/c − b/c = (a−b)/c**

*Example:* 7/9 − 4/9 = **3/9 = 1/3**

### Different Denominators

Same process as addition — find LCM, convert, then subtract.

**Worked Example:** 5/6 − 3/8

> LCM(6, 8) = 24.
> 5/6 = 20/24 and 3/8 = 9/24.
> 20/24 − 9/24 = **11/24**

**Worked Example:** 3 1/2 − 1 2/3

> Convert to improper: 3 1/2 = 7/2 and 1 2/3 = 5/3.
> LCM(2, 3) = 6. → 21/6 − 10/6 = **11/6 = 1 5/6**

---

## Multiplication of Fractions

> **a/b × c/d = (a×c)/(b×d)**

*Example:* 3/5 × 2/7 = **(3×2)/(5×7) = 6/35**

*Example:* Find 2/3 of 24 (i.e., 2/3 × 24):

> 2/3 × 24 = (2×24)/3 = 48/3 = **16**

*Shortcut — simplify before multiplying:*

> 4/9 × 3/8 = (4×3)/(9×8) → cancel 4 with 8 (÷4) and 3 with 9 (÷3) → 1/3 × 1/2 = **1/6**

**Worked Example (real life):** A recipe needs 3/4 cup of sugar. If you make 1/2 the recipe, how much sugar do you need?

> 1/2 × 3/4 = 3/8 cup of sugar.

## Unit Fractions — An Ancient Story

A **unit fraction** is a fraction with numerator 1 (like 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5, …).

Ancient Egyptians only used unit fractions! They wrote every fraction as a sum of different unit fractions.

*Example:* Egyptians would write 3/4 as 1/2 + 1/4.

> 2/5 = 1/3 + 1/15 (check: 5/15 + 1/15 = 6/15 = 2/5 ✓)

**Indian connection:** Indian mathematicians like **Brahmagupta** (628 CE) worked extensively with fractions — including rules for adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions, which they recorded centuries before European mathematicians.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Fraction** | A part of a whole — written as numerator/denominator |
| **Numerator** | The top number — how many parts are taken |
| **Denominator** | The bottom number — total equal parts |
| **Proper fraction** | Numerator < denominator (value between 0 and 1) |
| **Improper fraction** | Numerator ≥ denominator (value ≥ 1) |
| **Mixed number** | A whole number + a proper fraction (e.g., 2¾) |
| **Equivalent fractions** | Different fractions with the same value |
| **Simplest form** | A fraction where HCF(numerator, denominator) = 1 |
| **Unit fraction** | A fraction with numerator 1 (1/2, 1/3, 1/5, …) |
| **LCM method** | Using the Lowest Common Multiple to add/subtract fractions with different denominators |
