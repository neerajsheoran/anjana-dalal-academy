## Perimeter — Walking Around the Boundary

**Kiran:** Papa, what exactly is the difference between perimeter and area? I keep mixing them up.

**Ravi:** Imagine our rectangular terrace. If you walk all the way around the edge — along every side — the total distance you walk is the perimeter. Now if you want to paint the entire floor, the amount of floor you cover is the area.

**Kiran:** So perimeter is the boundary length, and area is the space inside?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Perimeter is measured in metres or centimetres — it is a length. Area is measured in square metres or square centimetres — it is a surface. Different things, different units.

**Kiran:** Our terrace is 12 metres long and 8 metres wide. So the perimeter is 12 plus 8 plus 12 plus 8, which is 40 metres. Or using the formula, 2 times 12 plus 8, which is 2 times 20, which is 40 metres.

**Ravi:** Good. Now suppose we want to put a railing around the terrace. The railing costs 250 rupees per metre. What is the total cost?

**Kiran:** 40 times 250 is 10,000 rupees!

**Ravi:** See how perimeter connects directly to real costs? Fencing a field, stitching lace around a dupatta, running a border light around a stage during the school annual day — all are perimeter problems.

## Area — Covering the Surface

**Kiran:** Papa, for our terrace the area would be 12 times 8, which is 96 square metres. But what does "square metres" actually mean?

**Ravi:** Imagine covering the terrace floor with tiles, each exactly 1 metre by 1 metre. You would need 96 of those tiles to cover the entire floor. That is what 96 square metres means — 96 unit squares of side 1 metre.

**Kiran:** That makes sense! And if we use smaller tiles — say 50 cm by 50 cm — we would need more tiles but the total area stays the same?

**Ravi:** Right. With 50 cm tiles, each tile covers 0.25 square metres, so you would need 96 divided by 0.25, which is 384 tiles. Same area, different tiles.

**Kiran:** Our classroom floor is being re-tiled. The room is 8 metres long and 6 metres wide, and tiles cost 350 rupees per square metre. So the cost is 48 times 350, which is 16,800 rupees.

**Ravi:** You just solved a real-world problem that a contractor does every day. In Indian cities, flat prices are often quoted per square foot. A flat might cost 8,000 rupees per square foot. A 1,000-square-foot flat would cost 80 lakh rupees. Understanding area directly affects major financial decisions!

## Same Perimeter, Different Areas

**Kiran:** Papa, something surprising happened in class. Teacher asked us to draw different rectangles, all with a perimeter of 24 cm. We got different areas for each one!

**Ravi:** What did you find?

**Kiran:** With length 11 and breadth 1, the area is just 11. With length 8 and breadth 4, the area is 32. And with length 6 and breadth 6 — a square — the area is 36. The square had the biggest area!

**Ravi:** That is one of the most beautiful results in geometry. Among all rectangles with the same perimeter, the square always has the maximum area. Think about what this means for a farmer. If she has 100 metres of fencing to enclose a plot, a square plot of 25 metres per side gives her 625 square metres — more land than any rectangular shape with the same fencing.

**Kiran:** And the reverse is true too — for the same area, the square uses the least fencing?

**Ravi:** Exactly. That is why many parks and gardens are roughly square. It is the most efficient shape — maximum space for minimum boundary. Nature figured this out too. Honeycomb cells are hexagons because, among shapes that tile perfectly, the hexagon gives the most area for the least perimeter.

## Compound Shapes — Breaking and Building

**Kiran:** Papa, our school building has an L-shaped corridor. How do I find its area? It is not a simple rectangle.

**Ravi:** The trick is to break the L-shape into simpler shapes — two rectangles. Find each rectangle's area, then add them. Or you can imagine the full rectangle and subtract the missing corner.

**Kiran:** Let me try. The L-shape is like a big rectangle 10 metres by 8 metres, but with a 4 by 5 metre piece cut from one corner. So the full rectangle is 80 square metres, minus the missing piece of 20 square metres, giving 60 square metres.

**Ravi:** Perfect. This idea of decomposition — breaking complex shapes into simple ones — is used everywhere. When architects design a house plan, each room might be rectangular, but the overall floor plan could be T-shaped, L-shaped, or U-shaped. They calculate each room's area separately and add them up.

**Kiran:** What about really irregular shapes — like a lake on a map?

**Ravi:** You use the grid method. Place the shape on squared paper. Count all the fully covered squares. Count the half-covered squares and take half their number. Add them up. It is an estimate, but a good one. Geographers use this method to estimate the area of lakes, forest patches, and even states on a map.

## Units of Area — Why Hectares?

**Kiran:** Papa, news channels talk about "1,000 hectares of forest destroyed" or "the farm is 5 hectares." What is a hectare?

**Ravi:** A hectare is 10,000 square metres — that is a square with sides of 100 metres. Imagine a plot 100 metres long and 100 metres wide. That is 1 hectare.

**Kiran:** Why not just say 10,000 square metres?

**Ravi:** For the same reason we say kilometres instead of thousands of metres — convenience. Farm sizes in India are often a few hectares. Saying "2.5 hectares" is much simpler than saying "25,000 square metres." And 1 square kilometre is 100 hectares — so a forest that is 10 square kilometres is 1,000 hectares.

**Kiran:** The conversion between area units is interesting. 1 metre is 100 centimetres, but 1 square metre is not 100 square centimetres — it is 10,000!

**Ravi:** Brilliant catch. Because area involves two dimensions. 1 metre equals 100 cm, so 1 square metre equals 100 times 100, which is 10,000 square centimetres. You have to square the conversion factor. This is a common trap in exams — and now you know to watch for it.

**Kiran:** Maths has so many hidden connections. Perimeter is one-dimensional, area is two-dimensional — and that changes everything about units.

**Ravi:** You are thinking like a mathematician, beta. That dimensional reasoning will help you all the way through higher maths and science.
