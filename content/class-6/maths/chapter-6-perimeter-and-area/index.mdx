## Why Do We Measure Shapes?

A farmer needs to know how much fencing to buy for his field. A painter needs to know how much wall to paint. An architect needs to plan how much land a building will cover.

These are everyday situations where we need to measure shapes — not just their length, but how much space they enclose and how long their boundaries are.

Two fundamental measurements of any flat (2D) shape are:
- **Perimeter** — the total length of its boundary
- **Area** — the amount of surface it covers

---

## Perimeter

The **perimeter** of a shape is the **total length of its boundary** — the distance you would walk if you walked all the way around the outside of the shape.

> **Perimeter** = sum of all the sides

**Units of perimeter:** cm, m, km (since it is a length)

![Rectangle with length and breadth labeled on all four sides and a dotted line tracing the perimeter around it](perimeter-rectangle-labeled.png)

### Perimeter of a Rectangle

A rectangle has **4 sides**: two lengths (l) and two breadths (b).

> **Perimeter of rectangle = 2 × (length + breadth) = 2(l + b)**

**Worked Example 1:** Find the perimeter of a rectangle with length 8 cm and breadth 5 cm.

> P = 2(l + b) = 2(8 + 5) = 2 × 13 = **26 cm**

**Worked Example 2:** A rectangular garden is 15 m long and 9 m wide. How much fencing is needed to go around it?

> P = 2(15 + 9) = 2 × 24 = **48 m of fencing**

**Worked Example 3 (finding missing side):** The perimeter of a rectangle is 54 cm. Its length is 16 cm. Find its breadth.

> P = 2(l + b)
> 54 = 2(16 + b)
> 27 = 16 + b
> b = 27 − 16 = **11 cm**

---

### Perimeter of a Square

A square has **4 equal sides** of length s.

> **Perimeter of square = 4 × side = 4s**

**Worked Example:** A square plot has a side of 23 m. Find the cost of fencing it at ₹45 per metre.

> P = 4 × 23 = **92 m**
> Cost = 92 × 45 = **₹4,140**

---

### Perimeter of a Triangle

> **Perimeter of triangle = sum of all three sides = a + b + c**

**Worked Example:** Find the perimeter of a triangle with sides 7 cm, 9 cm, and 12 cm.

> P = 7 + 9 + 12 = **28 cm**

*Special case — Equilateral triangle* (all 3 sides equal, each = s):
> **P = 3s**

---

### Perimeter of Any Polygon

For any polygon, perimeter = sum of all its sides.

*Example:* A pentagonal (5-sided) field has sides 10 m, 13 m, 9 m, 11 m, and 14 m.
> P = 10 + 13 + 9 + 11 + 14 = **57 m**

---

## Perimeter — Important Indian Applications

- **Cloth border:** When stitching a border (lace or ribbon) around a tablecloth or dupatta, you need to know the perimeter.
- **Running track:** A rectangular track 120 m long and 60 m wide has perimeter = 2(120+60) = 360 m. Athletes running 4 rounds cover 4 × 360 = 1,440 m ≈ 1.44 km.
- **Vastu and plots:** When buying a plot of land, the boundary (perimeter) determines fencing costs.

## Area

The **area** of a shape is the **amount of flat surface** it covers.

![Rectangle on a square grid with unit squares shaded inside showing area as the count of squares](area-counting-unit-squares.png)

We measure area by counting how many **unit squares** (squares of side 1 unit) fit inside the shape.

> **Units of area:** cm² (square centimetres), m² (square metres), km² (square kilometres)

*Note:* A 1 cm × 1 cm square has area = **1 cm²** (one square centimetre).

### Area by Counting Squares

For shapes drawn on squared paper, count the number of unit squares inside the shape.

*Rules for counting:*
- Fully covered squares — count as 1 each.
- Half-covered squares — count as ½ each (roughly: 2 half-squares = 1 whole).
- Very small portions — usually ignored or rounded.

*Example:* A shape covers 6 full squares and 4 half-squares.
> Area ≈ 6 × 1 + 4 × ½ = 6 + 2 = **8 sq units**

---

### Area of a Rectangle

> **Area of rectangle = length × breadth = l × b**

**Worked Example 1:** Find the area of a rectangle 12 cm long and 7 cm wide.

> A = l × b = 12 × 7 = **84 cm²**

**Worked Example 2:** A room is 5.5 m long and 4 m wide. Find the cost of tiling the floor at ₹350 per m².

> A = 5.5 × 4 = **22 m²**
> Cost = 22 × 350 = **₹7,700**

**Worked Example 3 (finding a missing side):** The area of a rectangle is 143 cm². Its length is 13 cm. Find its breadth.

> A = l × b
> 143 = 13 × b
> b = 143 ÷ 13 = **11 cm**

---

### Area of a Square

> **Area of square = side × side = s²**

**Worked Example:** Find the area of a square with side 15 m.

> A = 15² = **225 m²**

**Worked Example (reverse):** The area of a square is 196 cm². Find its side length and perimeter.

> s² = 196 → s = √196 = **14 cm**
> Perimeter = 4 × 14 = **56 cm**

---

## Relationship Between Perimeter and Area

A very important insight: **shapes with the same perimeter can have different areas**, and **shapes with the same area can have different perimeters**.

### Same Perimeter, Different Areas

All rectangles below have perimeter = 24 cm:

| Length (cm) | Breadth (cm) | Perimeter (cm) | Area (cm²) |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|------------|
| 11 | 1 | 24 | 11 |
| 10 | 2 | 24 | 20 |
| 9 | 3 | 24 | 27 |
| 8 | 4 | 24 | 32 |
| 7 | 5 | 24 | 35 |
| 6 | 6 | 24 | **36** |

**Observation:** Among all rectangles with the same perimeter, the **square has the largest area**!

> **Why does this matter?** If you have a fixed length of fencing, you get the most area from a square shape. This is why many fields and parks are roughly square.

### Same Area, Different Perimeters

All rectangles below have area = 36 cm²:

| Length (cm) | Breadth (cm) | Area (cm²) | Perimeter (cm) |
|-------------|-------------|------------|----------------|
| 36 | 1 | 36 | 74 |
| 18 | 2 | 36 | 40 |
| 12 | 3 | 36 | 30 |
| 9 | 4 | 36 | 26 |
| 6 | 6 | 36 | **24** |

**Observation:** Among all rectangles with the same area, the **square has the smallest perimeter**!

## Area of Irregular Shapes

For shapes that don't fit a standard formula (like an irregular pond or a leaf), we can estimate area using squared paper:

1. Place the shape on squared paper (or trace it on squared paper).
2. Count all **fully covered** squares.
3. Count all **half-covered or more** squares (count these as 1).
4. Ignore squares less than half covered.
5. Total = estimated area.

*Alternative rule (easier for younger students):* Count all squares that are **more than half covered** as 1, and ignore all squares that are **half or less covered**. This gives a quick, reasonable estimate.

## Compound Shapes

![An L-shaped figure split into two rectangles with dotted lines showing how to break compound shapes into simpler parts](compound-shape-area.png)

Many real-world shapes are combinations of rectangles and squares. Break them into simpler shapes, find each area, and add (or subtract).

**Worked Example:** Find the area of an L-shaped room:

```
┌──────┐
│  A   │ 6 m
│      ├────┐
└──────┘    │ B  3 m
   8 m  ────┘
         4 m
```

*Method:* Split into two rectangles.
- Rectangle A: 8 m × (6 − 3) = 8 × 3 = 24 m²
- Rectangle B: 4 × 3 = 12 m²
- Total area = 24 + 12 = **36 m²**

*Alternative method:* Find the full rectangle and subtract the missing corner.
- Full rectangle: 8 × 6 = 48 m²
- Missing corner: (8−4) × 3 = 4 × 3 = 12 m²
- Area = 48 − 12 = **36 m²** ✓

## Units of Area — Conversions

| Relationship | Conversion |
|--------------|-----------|
| 1 cm = 10 mm | 1 cm² = 100 mm² |
| 1 m = 100 cm | 1 m² = 10,000 cm² |
| 1 km = 1,000 m | 1 km² = 10,00,000 m² |

**For land measurement in India:**

| Unit | Equivalent |
|------|-----------|
| 1 Hectare (ha) | 10,000 m² |
| 1 Are | 100 m² |
| 1 km² | 100 hectares |

*Example:* A farm is 2.5 hectares. How many m² is this?
> 2.5 hectares = 2.5 × 10,000 = **25,000 m²**

## Solved Problems — Mixed

**Problem 1:** A rectangular swimming pool is 25 m long and 10 m wide. Find:
(a) The perimeter of the pool.
(b) The area of the pool.
(c) The cost of tiling the bottom at ₹800 per m².

> (a) P = 2(25 + 10) = 2 × 35 = **70 m**
> (b) A = 25 × 10 = **250 m²**
> (c) Cost = 250 × 800 = **₹2,00,000**

**Problem 2:** A square field has perimeter 240 m. Find its area.

> P = 4s → 240 = 4s → s = 60 m
> A = s² = 60² = **3,600 m²**

**Problem 3:** A rectangular piece of cloth is 3.5 m long and 2.2 m wide. Find the cost of stitching lace all around it at ₹12 per metre.

> P = 2(3.5 + 2.2) = 2 × 5.7 = **11.4 m**
> Cost = 11.4 × 12 = **₹136.80**

**Problem 4:** Two squares have sides 6 cm and 8 cm. Can a single rectangle with the same total area be made? If yes, find all possible whole-number dimensions.

> Area of first square = 36 cm²
> Area of second square = 64 cm²
> Total area = 100 cm²
> Rectangle dimensions (l × b = 100):
> → 100 × 1, 50 × 2, 25 × 4, 20 × 5, 10 × 10 (all valid!)

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Perimeter** | Total length of the boundary of a shape |
| **Area** | Amount of surface (flat space) enclosed by a shape |
| **Unit square** | A square with side 1 unit, used as the standard unit of area |
| **cm²** | Square centimetre — unit of area |
| **m²** | Square metre — unit of area |
| **Hectare** | Unit of land area = 10,000 m² |
| **l × b** | Formula for area of rectangle (length × breadth) |
| **s²** | Formula for area of square (side × side) |
| **2(l + b)** | Formula for perimeter of rectangle |
| **4s** | Formula for perimeter of square |
| **Compound shape** | A shape made by combining two or more simpler shapes |
