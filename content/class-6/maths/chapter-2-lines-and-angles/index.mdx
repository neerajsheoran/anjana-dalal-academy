## Starting with Points

Everything in geometry begins with a **point** — a location in space with no length, width, or height. We draw a point as a tiny dot and label it with a capital letter.

*Examples:* Point A, Point B, Point P

---

## Lines, Line Segments, and Rays

### Line Segment

A **line segment** is a straight path between **two fixed endpoints**. It has a definite, measurable length.

*Examples in real life:*
- The edge of a ruler
- A side of a triangle
- The edge of your textbook

> Written as: **AB** (with a bar on top, meaning segment from A to B)
> Length is finite and can be measured.

---

### Ray

A **ray** starts at one fixed point (called the **starting point** or **initial point**) and goes on infinitely in one direction.

Think of a torch beam — it starts at the torch and goes on and on in one direction.

*Examples in real life:*
- A ray of sunlight
- A laser beam
- The hands of a clock — each hand is a ray starting from the centre

> Written as: **→AB** (arrow on top, from A going through B and beyond)
> It has a definite start but no end.

---

### Line

A **line** extends infinitely in **both directions** — it has no endpoints.

*How to think about it:* A line segment stretched infinitely in both directions becomes a line.

> Written as: **↔AB** (arrows on both sides)
> A line has no endpoints — it goes on forever in both directions.

### Comparing the Three

| | Endpoints | Length | Symbol |
|--|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Line segment** | 2 (both fixed) | Finite — can be measured | — (bar on top) |
| **Ray** | 1 (starting point) | Infinite in one direction | → (arrow one side) |
| **Line** | 0 (none) | Infinite in both directions | ↔ (arrows both sides) |

---

## Angles

When two rays start from the **same point**, they form an **angle**.

- The common starting point is called the **vertex** of the angle.
- The two rays are called the **arms** of the angle.

*Think of opening a door:* As the door swings open, the angle between the door and the wall increases. The hinge is the vertex; the door and the wall are the arms.

> **Angle** = the amount of **turning** from one ray to the other.

### Measuring Angles

Angles are measured in **degrees (°)**. We use a **protractor** to measure angles.

- A full turn (coming back to where you started) = **360°**
- A half turn = **180°**
- A quarter turn = **90°**

### Types of Angles

| Type | Measure | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Zero angle** | 0° | Both arms on top of each other |
| **Acute angle** | Between 0° and 90° | Corner of a slice of pizza, hands of a clock at 1 o'clock |
| **Right angle** | Exactly 90° | Corner of a book, corner of a room, L-shape |
| **Obtuse angle** | Between 90° and 180° | A door opened wide, hands of clock at 10 o'clock |
| **Straight angle** | Exactly 180° | A straight line — like a ruler placed flat |
| **Reflex angle** | Between 180° and 360° | The larger angle when a door is opened past 90° |
| **Complete angle** | Exactly 360° | One full rotation |

*How to remember:* **A**cute = **A** small angle (less than 90°). **O**btuse = sounds like "obtuse" — it is "blunt" and bigger than 90°.

---

## How to Measure an Angle with a Protractor

A **protractor** is a semi-circular measuring tool marked from 0° to 180°.

**Steps to measure angle ABC:**
1. Place the protractor so the **midpoint (small hole)** is exactly on the **vertex B**.
2. Align the **0° line** of the protractor along the arm **BA**.
3. Read the number where the other arm **BC** crosses the protractor scale.
4. That reading is the measure of angle ABC.

> **Tip:** A protractor has two scales (inner and outer) — both running from 0° to 180° but in opposite directions. Always use the scale that starts at 0° from the arm you aligned.

**Steps to draw an angle of 65°:**
1. Draw a ray. Mark its starting point as O.
2. Place the protractor's midpoint on O, 0° line along the ray.
3. Mark a point at 65° on the protractor.
4. Remove the protractor. Draw a ray from O through the marked point.
5. Label the angle — it is 65°.

## Pairs of Angles

### Complementary Angles

Two angles are **complementary** if they add up to **90°**.

*Example:* 30° and 60° are complementary because 30° + 60° = 90°.

*Memory trick:* **C**omplementary → **C**orner (right angle = 90°).

*Real life:* The two acute angles in a right-angled triangle are always complementary.

**Worked Example:** If one angle is 37°, find its complement.
> Complement = 90° − 37° = **53°**

---

### Supplementary Angles

Two angles are **supplementary** if they add up to **180°**.

*Example:* 110° and 70° are supplementary because 110° + 70° = 180°.

*Memory trick:* **S**upplementary → **S**traight line (180°).

*Real life:* When you open a book flat, the two pages form a straight angle (180°). Any angle and its "partner on the other side of a straight line" are supplementary.

**Worked Example:** If one angle is 123°, find its supplement.
> Supplement = 180° − 123° = **57°**

---

### Adjacent Angles

Two angles are **adjacent** if they:
1. Share the **same vertex**
2. Share a **common arm**
3. Do **not** overlap

*Think of a clock:* The angle from 12 to 3 and the angle from 3 to 6 are adjacent — they share the arm pointing at 3.

---

### Linear Pair

When two adjacent angles together form a **straight line (180°)**, they are called a **linear pair**.

*Key fact:* A linear pair is always supplementary (adds to 180°).

*Real life:* When you break a stick — the two pieces form a linear pair of angles at the break point.

**Worked Example:** Two angles form a linear pair. One angle is 72°. Find the other.
> Other angle = 180° − 72° = **108°**

---

### Vertically Opposite Angles

When two lines **intersect** (cross each other), they form 4 angles at the point of intersection.

The angles that are **across from each other** (not adjacent) are called **vertically opposite angles**. They are always **equal**.

```
      B
      |
 A ———+——— C
      |
      D
```
At the intersection: angle AOB = angle COD, and angle AOD = angle BOC.

*Why are they equal?* Because both pairs of vertically opposite angles are supplementary to the same angle — so they must be equal to each other.

**Worked Example:** Two lines intersect. One of the four angles is 65°. Find all four angles.
> - The vertically opposite angle = 65°
> - The adjacent angle = 180° − 65° = 115° (linear pair)
> - The remaining angle = 115° (vertically opposite to the 115° angle)
> - **Four angles: 65°, 115°, 65°, 115°**

## Parallel Lines and a Transversal

**Parallel lines** are lines that are always the same distance apart and never meet, no matter how far they are extended. Think of railway tracks.

A **transversal** is a line that crosses two or more parallel lines.

When a transversal cuts two parallel lines, it creates 8 angles (4 at each intersection). These angles have special relationships:

### Corresponding Angles (F-shape)
Angles in the same position at each intersection. They are **equal**.

*How to spot them:* Look for an **F-shape** (or its mirror/flip).

### Alternate Interior Angles (Z-shape)
Angles on **opposite sides** of the transversal, **between** the parallel lines. They are **equal**.

*How to spot them:* Look for a **Z-shape** (or its mirror **S-shape**).

### Co-interior Angles (C-shape)
Angles on the **same side** of the transversal, between the parallel lines. They add up to **180°** (they are supplementary).

*How to spot them:* Look for a **C-shape** (or U-shape).

| Angle Pair | Relationship | Shape to look for |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Corresponding | Equal | F-shape |
| Alternate interior | Equal | Z-shape |
| Co-interior | Add to 180° | C-shape |

**Worked Example:** A transversal cuts two parallel lines. One of the angles formed is 70°. Find the corresponding, alternate, and co-interior angles.
> - Corresponding angle = **70°** (equal)
> - Alternate interior angle = **70°** (equal)
> - Co-interior angle = 180° − 70° = **110°**

---

## Angle Sum in a Triangle

The **sum of all three angles** in any triangle is always **180°**.

**Worked Example 1:** A triangle has angles 60°, 80°, and x°. Find x.
> 60° + 80° + x° = 180°
> 140° + x° = 180°
> x° = 180° − 140° = **40°**

**Worked Example 2:** An equilateral triangle has all three sides equal. What are its angles?
> Since all sides are equal, all angles are equal.
> 3 × angle = 180°
> angle = 180° ÷ 3 = **60°**
> All three angles of an equilateral triangle are **60°** each.

## Using a Protractor and Ruler — Key Constructions

### Draw a Line Segment of Given Length
1. Mark a point A.
2. Place the ruler with 0 at A.
3. Mark another point B at the required length.
4. Join A and B with a straight line.

### Bisecting an Angle (Using a Protractor)
To bisect an angle means to divide it into **two equal halves**.

If angle = 80°:
1. Measure the angle with a protractor.
2. Find half: 80° ÷ 2 = 40°.
3. Draw a ray from the vertex at 40° from one arm.
4. This ray divides the original angle into two 40° angles.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Point** | A location in space, no size |
| **Line segment** | A straight path between two endpoints — has definite length |
| **Ray** | Starts at a point, goes on infinitely in one direction |
| **Line** | Goes on infinitely in both directions — no endpoints |
| **Angle** | Formed by two rays with the same starting point (vertex) |
| **Vertex** | The common point where the two arms of an angle meet |
| **Protractor** | Tool used to measure angles, marked 0° to 180° |
| **Acute angle** | Angle between 0° and 90° |
| **Right angle** | Angle of exactly 90° |
| **Obtuse angle** | Angle between 90° and 180° |
| **Straight angle** | Angle of exactly 180° |
| **Reflex angle** | Angle between 180° and 360° |
| **Complementary** | Two angles that add to 90° |
| **Supplementary** | Two angles that add to 180° |
| **Linear pair** | Two adjacent angles on a straight line (add to 180°) |
| **Vertically opposite** | Angles across from each other at an intersection — always equal |
| **Parallel lines** | Lines that never meet — always same distance apart |
| **Transversal** | A line crossing two or more parallel lines |
