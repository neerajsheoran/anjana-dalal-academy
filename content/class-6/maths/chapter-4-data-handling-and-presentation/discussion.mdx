## Why Data Matters

**Kiran:** Papa, we are learning about data in maths. But data sounds like a science or computer thing. Why is it in maths?

**Ravi:** Because maths gives us the tools to make sense of data. Think about it — your school collects marks for every student, every subject, every test. That is a lot of numbers. Without maths, those numbers are just a jumble. With maths, you can find averages, compare performances, and draw conclusions.

**Kiran:** Like when the teacher says our class average in maths was 72 out of 100?

**Ravi:** Exactly. That single number — the mean — summarises 40 students' marks into one value. The government uses data too. When India conducts a census, it counts over 140 crore people and records their age, language, occupation, education. All of that data shapes decisions about where to build new schools, hospitals, and railways.

**Kiran:** So data is not just numbers — it is numbers collected for a reason?

**Ravi:** Well said. Data without a purpose is just noise. Data with a purpose is information.

## Tally Marks and Frequency Tables

**Kiran:** Papa, tally marks seem so basic — just drawing lines. Why do we need to learn that?

**Ravi:** Have you ever tried counting moving things? Imagine you are standing at the school gate counting how many autorickshaws, cars, buses, and bikes pass in one hour. You cannot write full numbers fast enough. But quick strokes — one, two, three, four, then a diagonal slash to make a group of five — that is fast and accurate.

**Kiran:** I see! And then I just count the groups of five and add any extras.

**Ravi:** Right. After collecting, you organize the tally marks into a frequency table. Frequency just means "how many times." If 14 cars passed, the frequency of cars is 14. The table makes the raw data neat and readable.

**Kiran:** In our class survey, we found that mango is the most popular fruit. 11 out of 30 students chose it. Is that surprising?

**Ravi:** Not in India! But notice what you did — you collected primary data by asking your classmates directly. If instead you had read a newspaper article about fruit preferences, that would be secondary data — someone else collected it. Both are useful, but primary data answers your specific question.

## Bar Graphs — Pictures Worth Numbers

**Kiran:** Papa, why do we need bar graphs when we already have the frequency table? The table has all the numbers.

**Ravi:** Show me your frequency table of favourite sports from class.

**Kiran:** Cricket — 14, Football — 9, Badminton — 11, Kabaddi — 7, Chess — 5.

**Ravi:** Now imagine showing this to someone in two seconds. With the table, they have to read five numbers and compare them mentally. With a bar graph, they instantly see that cricket has the tallest bar. The picture tells the story at a glance.

**Kiran:** So the graph is for quick understanding and the table is for exact numbers?

**Ravi:** Exactly. When BCCI shows cricket match statistics on TV, they use bar graphs and pie charts because millions of viewers need to understand the data instantly. The X-axis shows categories — teams, players, grounds. The Y-axis shows values — runs, wickets, averages.

**Kiran:** I noticed that the scale matters a lot. If I choose each gridline as 1 student, my graph needs to be very tall. But if each gridline is 5 students, it is compact.

**Ravi:** Smart observation. Choosing the right scale is an important skill. The scale should make the graph easy to read without wasting space. If your highest value is 14, a scale of 0, 5, 10, 15 works perfectly — just three intervals.

## The Mean — One Number for the Whole Group

**Kiran:** Papa, my marks in five subjects are 82, 75, 91, 68, and 84. My friend Asha got 78, 80, 79, 81, and 82. She says she is better because she has more marks above 80. But my total is higher! Who is actually better?

**Ravi:** Let us calculate the mean for both. Your total is 82 plus 75 plus 91 plus 68 plus 84, which is 400. Divided by 5, your mean is 80. Asha's total is 78 plus 80 plus 79 plus 81 plus 82, which is 400. Her mean is also 80!

**Kiran:** We have the same average? But our marks look so different!

**Ravi:** And that is an important lesson. The mean tells you the typical value, but it hides the variation. You have very high marks in some subjects and lower in others — your scores are spread out. Asha's marks are all clustered near 80 — very consistent. The mean alone does not tell the full story.

**Kiran:** So the mean can be misleading?

**Ravi:** It can. Imagine five friends. Four earn 10,000 rupees per month and one earns 5,00,000 rupees. The mean income is about 1,06,000 — but that does not represent any of the five people accurately! That is why data handling requires careful interpretation, not just calculation.

## Drawing Conclusions from Data

**Kiran:** Papa, our teacher gave us a table showing books borrowed from our school library each month. Borrowing was highest in October and lowest in June. She asked us to explain why.

**Ravi:** What do you think? What happens in June?

**Kiran:** Summer holidays! Nobody is in school, so nobody borrows books. And October is just before the exams start in November, so everyone is studying.

**Ravi:** That is exactly the kind of reasoning data should lead to. You looked at the numbers, noticed the pattern — high before exams, low during holidays — and connected it to what you know about school life. That is drawing a conclusion from data.

**Kiran:** But could there be other explanations? Maybe the library was closed for repairs in June.

**Ravi:** Excellent point! Data tells you what happened but not always why. You might need to investigate further — check if the library was actually open, look at whether the pattern repeats every year. Good data analysis always asks "could there be another explanation?" That critical thinking is what separates someone who truly understands data from someone who just reads numbers.

**Kiran:** Data handling is more about thinking than calculating, isn't it?

**Ravi:** The calculation is easy. The thinking is the real skill.
