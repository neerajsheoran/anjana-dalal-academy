## Going Below Zero

**Kiran:** Papa, how can a number be less than zero? Zero means nothing. How can you have less than nothing?

**Ravi:** Think about money. You have zero rupees in your pocket. Now you borrow 50 rupees from your friend. How much do you really have?

**Kiran:** I have 50 rupees in my hand, but I owe 50. So in a way, my balance is minus 50.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Negative 50 represents a debt — you have less than nothing because you owe something. Now think about temperature. In Shimla during January, the temperature can drop to minus 5 degrees Celsius. That means 5 degrees below the freezing point of water.

**Kiran:** And in a building, the basement is below the ground floor. If the ground floor is zero, the first basement is minus 1!

**Ravi:** You are getting it. Negative numbers are not abstract — they describe real situations where values go below a reference point. Below sea level, below zero temperature, below zero balance, before a reference year in history.

## Brahmagupta — The Father of Negative Numbers

**Kiran:** Papa, our textbook says Brahmagupta was the first person to write rules for negative numbers. Was he really the first in the whole world?

**Ravi:** Yes. Brahmagupta was a mathematician and astronomer from Rajasthan, born in 598 CE. In his book Brahmasphutasiddhanta, written in 628 CE, he clearly described positive numbers as "fortunes" and negative numbers as "debts." And he gave precise rules — a debt plus a debt is a bigger debt, a fortune minus a fortune can be a debt, and crucially, a debt subtracted from zero is a fortune.

**Kiran:** "A debt subtracted from zero is a fortune" — that means 0 minus negative 5 equals positive 5!

**Ravi:** Exactly. These are the same rules we use today, written 1,400 years ago. European mathematicians did not fully accept negative numbers until the 17th century — over a thousand years after Brahmagupta. They considered negative numbers "absurd" or "fictitious."

**Kiran:** So India was way ahead in understanding negative numbers?

**Ravi:** Way ahead. Brahmagupta also gave the first clear rules for zero — including that zero times any number is zero, and zero plus any number is that number. The Indian mathematical tradition gave the world both zero and negative numbers. Without these contributions, modern mathematics, science, and computing would not exist.

## The Number Line — Ordering Integers

**Kiran:** Papa, I know that 5 is greater than 3. But is minus 5 greater than minus 3? It seems like it should be because 5 is bigger than 3.

**Ravi:** Picture the number line. Zero is in the middle. Positive numbers go to the right — 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Negative numbers go to the left — minus 1, minus 2, minus 3, minus 4, minus 5.

**Kiran:** So minus 5 is further to the left than minus 3.

**Ravi:** And on a number line, further left always means smaller. So minus 5 is less than minus 3. Think of it as temperature — minus 5 degrees is colder than minus 3 degrees. Or debt — owing 500 rupees is worse than owing 300 rupees.

**Kiran:** I see! With negative numbers, the "bigger" looking number is actually smaller because it is further from zero in the negative direction.

**Ravi:** Right. The absolute value — the distance from zero — of minus 5 is 5, and the absolute value of minus 3 is 3. But in terms of actual value, minus 5 is less than minus 3. This is one of the trickiest ideas for students, and you have grasped it well.

## Adding and Subtracting Integers

**Kiran:** Papa, I understand adding two positive numbers and adding two negative numbers. But adding a positive and a negative confuses me.

**Ravi:** Think of it as a tug-of-war. Positive pulls to the right, negative pulls to the left. The result depends on which side is stronger.

**Kiran:** So 7 plus minus 4 — the positive side pulls 7, the negative side pulls 4. Positive wins by 3, so the answer is positive 3?

**Ravi:** Perfect. Now try minus 8 plus 5.

**Kiran:** Negative pulls 8, positive pulls 5. Negative wins by 3, so the answer is minus 3.

**Ravi:** And what about 6 plus minus 6?

**Kiran:** Equal pull on both sides — it is a tie! The answer is zero. They cancel each other out.

**Ravi:** That is the idea of opposites. Every number has an opposite — 6 and minus 6, 100 and minus 100. When you add a number and its opposite, you always get zero.

## Subtraction and the Double Negative

**Kiran:** Papa, the rule says "subtracting a negative is the same as adding a positive." So 5 minus minus 3 equals 5 plus 3, which is 8. But why?

**Ravi:** Think about it in terms of debt. You owe me 3 rupees — that is minus 3 on your balance. Now I say "I am removing that debt" — subtracting the minus 3 from your life. Removing a debt of 3 is the same as gaining 3 rupees. Your balance goes up by 3.

**Kiran:** Oh! Taking away something negative is a positive change!

**Ravi:** Here is another way to see it through a pattern. Look: 5 minus 3 is 2. 5 minus 2 is 3. 5 minus 1 is 4. 5 minus 0 is 5. What is the pattern?

**Kiran:** Each time we subtract 1 less, the answer increases by 1. So 5 minus minus 1 should be 6. And 5 minus minus 2 is 7. And 5 minus minus 3 is 8!

**Ravi:** The pattern forces the rule. "Minus minus equals plus" is not an arbitrary rule — it follows logically from the pattern of subtraction. This is how Brahmagupta reasoned too — the patterns in arithmetic lead inevitably to the rules for negative numbers.

**Kiran:** So negative numbers are not weird at all — they follow the same patterns as positive numbers, just extended to the other side of zero.

**Ravi:** Exactly, beta. The "other side of zero" is not a strange new world. It is the same mathematical universe, just seen from a wider perspective.
