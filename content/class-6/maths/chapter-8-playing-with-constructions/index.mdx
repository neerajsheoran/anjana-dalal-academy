## The Art of Geometric Construction

For thousands of years, mathematicians have drawn perfect geometric shapes using only two tools:

- **A straight ruler (or straightedge)** — for drawing straight lines
- **A compass** — for drawing circles and arcs

These constructions are called **Euclidean constructions**, named after the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid (around 300 BCE). His book *Elements* described how to construct geometric figures precisely using only these two tools.

> **Why no measuring?** A ruler is used only for drawing straight lines — not for measuring. Length is transferred using the compass. This approach guarantees perfect precision regardless of how the ruler is scaled.

In India, geometric constructions appear in the ancient **Sulba Sutras** (approximately 800–200 BCE) — texts that describe how to precisely construct altars of specific shapes and sizes using ropes and pegs.

## Tools and Their Use

![A compass and a straightedge ruler side by side, the two basic tools of geometric construction](compass-and-ruler-tools.png)

### Compass
- A compass has two arms: one with a sharp point (anchor) and one with a pencil.
- It draws circles and arcs.
- It can transfer distances accurately.

### Ruler (Straightedge)
- Used to draw straight lines between two points.
- In pure construction problems, only the straight-line drawing function is used (not measurement).

### Protractor
- A semi-circular tool marked from 0° to 180°.
- Used to measure and draw angles.
- Not strictly a "Euclidean" tool — but used alongside compass and ruler in school geometry.

## Basic Constructions

### 1. Drawing a Circle

**Given:** Radius r = 4 cm. Draw a circle with this radius.

**Steps:**
1. Mark a point O (the centre).
2. Open the compass to 4 cm using the ruler.
3. Place the compass point on O.
4. Rotate the compass fully to draw the circle.

---

### 2. Drawing a Line Segment of Given Length

**Given:** Draw line segment AB = 6.5 cm.

**Steps:**
1. Draw a ray starting at point A.
2. Open compass to 6.5 cm on the ruler.
3. Place compass point on A, mark an arc on the ray at B.
4. AB = 6.5 cm. ✓

---

### 3. Copying a Line Segment

**Given:** Copy line segment PQ to a new location.

**Steps:**
1. Draw a new ray starting at point A'.
2. Open compass to exactly the length of PQ (place compass on P, pencil on Q).
3. Without changing compass opening, place on A' and mark B'.
4. A'B' = PQ. ✓

---

### 4. Perpendicular Bisector of a Line Segment

The **perpendicular bisector** of a line segment:
- Cuts it into two equal halves (bisects it), AND
- Is perpendicular (90°) to it.

![Step-by-step construction of a perpendicular bisector showing compass arcs from both endpoints crossing above and below the line segment](perpendicular-bisector-construction.png)

**Given:** Construct the perpendicular bisector of line segment AB.

**Steps:**
1. Open compass to more than half of AB.
2. Place compass on A, draw arcs above and below AB.
3. Without changing compass opening, place on B, draw arcs above and below (crossing the first arcs at two points — call them P and Q).
4. Join P and Q with a straight line.
5. Line PQ is the perpendicular bisector of AB.
6. The point where PQ crosses AB is the **midpoint M** of AB.

> **Applications:** Finding the midpoint of a segment without measuring. Used in constructions and in Origami.

---

### 5. Angle Bisector

The **angle bisector** divides an angle into two equal halves.

**Given:** Bisect angle ABC.

**Steps:**
1. Place compass point on vertex B. Draw an arc that cuts both arms BA and BC at points P and Q.
2. Place compass point on P, draw an arc inside the angle.
3. Without changing compass opening, place on Q, draw another arc (crossing the first arc at point R).
4. Join B to R. Ray BR is the angle bisector of angle ABC.
5. Angle ABR = Angle RBC = (angle ABC)/2. ✓

> **Check:** Measure both halves with a protractor — they should be equal.

---

### 6. Drawing a Perpendicular from a Point to a Line

**Given:** Point P not on line l. Draw a perpendicular from P to l.

**Steps:**
1. Place compass on P. Draw an arc that cuts line l at two points A and B.
2. Place compass on A, draw an arc on the other side of l.
3. Without changing opening, place on B, draw another arc (crossing at Q).
4. Join P and Q. Line PQ is perpendicular to l.
5. The foot of the perpendicular is where PQ meets l.

---

### 7. Drawing Parallel Lines

**Given:** Draw a line parallel to line l, at distance d.

**Method using perpendicular distances:**
1. Pick any two points A and B on line l.
2. At both A and B, draw perpendiculars to l.
3. On each perpendicular, mark a point at distance d from l (A' above A, B' above B).
4. Join A' and B'. This line is parallel to l at distance d.

## Constructing Standard Angles

### Constructing 60°

An **equilateral triangle** has all angles = 60°. We use this fact.

**Steps:**
1. Draw a ray OA.
2. With O as centre and any radius r, draw an arc cutting OA at P.
3. With P as centre and the same radius r, draw an arc cutting the first arc at Q.
4. Join O to Q. Angle AOQ = **60°**. ✓

*Why does it work?* OP = PQ = OQ = r (all equal). Triangle OPQ is equilateral, so all angles are 60°.

---

### Constructing 120°

Using the same construction as 60°, continue:
- After marking Q at 60°, use Q as centre with same radius r, mark another arc at R.
- Angle AOR = **120°**.

---

### Constructing 90°

**Method:** Draw the perpendicular bisector of a line segment — the right angle is formed at the midpoint.

*Alternatively:* Construct 60°, then bisect the remaining 120° angle.

---

### Constructing 30° and 45°

- **30°** = bisect the 60° angle.
- **45°** = bisect the 90° angle.

> Using bisection, we can get: 90° → 45° → 22.5° → …
> From 60°: bisect → 30° → 15° → …

## Constructing Triangles

### SSS (Side-Side-Side)

**Given:** Construct a triangle with sides 5 cm, 7 cm, and 8 cm.

**Steps:**
1. Draw base BC = 8 cm (longest side as base).
2. With B as centre, draw arc of radius 5 cm.
3. With C as centre, draw arc of radius 7 cm.
4. The two arcs meet at A.
5. Join AB and AC. Triangle ABC has sides 5, 7, 8 cm. ✓

> **Note:** If you try to construct a triangle where one side is longer than the sum of the other two, the arcs will not intersect — a triangle cannot be formed. This illustrates the **Triangle Inequality**: any side must be less than the sum of the other two sides.

### SAS (Side-Angle-Side)

**Given:** Construct triangle PQR with PQ = 6 cm, angle PQR = 45°, QR = 4 cm.

**Steps:**
1. Draw QR = 4 cm.
2. At Q, construct angle of 45°.
3. Along the angle ray, mark P at 6 cm from Q.
4. Join P and R. Triangle PQR is complete.

## Design and Patterns Using Constructions

Compass and ruler constructions are used to create beautiful geometric patterns:

- **Rangoli patterns** — symmetrical designs made by repeating geometric units.
- **Islamic geometric art** — complex patterns based on star polygons constructed using compass and ruler.
- **Mandalas** — circular symmetrical designs.
- **Tiling patterns** — equilateral triangles, squares, and hexagons tile the plane perfectly.

![A regular hexagon inscribed in a circle with six equally spaced compass arcs marked on the circumference](regular-hexagon-construction.png)

**Constructing a regular hexagon:**
1. Draw a circle of any radius r.
2. Without changing compass opening, step around the circle marking 6 equally spaced points (since the radius equals the side of an inscribed regular hexagon).
3. Connect consecutive points for the hexagon.

> **Why?** A regular hexagon's side = the radius of its circumscribed circle — a beautiful fact!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Compass** | A drawing tool used to make circles and arcs, and to transfer distances |
| **Construction** | Drawing a geometric figure using only compass and straightedge |
| **Perpendicular bisector** | A line that cuts a segment into two equal halves at 90° |
| **Angle bisector** | A ray that divides an angle into two equal angles |
| **Perpendicular** | A line meeting another at exactly 90° |
| **Parallel lines** | Lines that never meet, always the same distance apart |
| **Equilateral triangle** | A triangle with all three sides equal and all angles = 60° |
| **SSS construction** | Constructing a triangle when all three side lengths are known |
| **SAS construction** | Constructing a triangle when two sides and the included angle are known |
| **Triangle inequality** | Any side of a triangle must be less than the sum of the other two sides |
| **Sulba Sutras** | Ancient Indian texts (800–200 BCE) describing precise geometric constructions |
