## What Is Symmetry?

Look at a butterfly — both wings are mirror images of each other. Look at a rangoli — it looks the same from multiple directions. Look at a snowflake — it has six identical sections.

These objects have **symmetry** — a property where a shape or pattern looks the same after a transformation (reflection, rotation).

Symmetry is one of the most beautiful concepts in mathematics, and it appears everywhere in nature, art, and architecture.

---

## Line Symmetry (Reflection Symmetry)

A shape has **line symmetry** if a line can be drawn through it such that one half is the **mirror image** of the other half.

This line is called the **line of symmetry** (or **axis of symmetry** or **mirror line**).

*How to test:* If you fold the shape along the line of symmetry, the two halves match exactly.

![Common shapes with their lines of symmetry drawn: isosceles triangle with 1 line, equilateral triangle with 3 lines, square with 4 lines, circle with many lines](lines-of-symmetry-shapes.png)

### Lines of Symmetry in Common Shapes

| Shape | Number of lines of symmetry |
|-------|-----------------------------|
| Scalene triangle (all sides different) | 0 |
| Isosceles triangle (2 equal sides) | 1 |
| Equilateral triangle (all sides equal) | 3 |
| Rectangle (not square) | 2 |
| Square | 4 |
| Regular pentagon | 5 |
| Regular hexagon | 6 |
| Circle | Infinitely many |
| Letter A | 1 |
| Letter H | 2 |
| Letter X | 2 |
| Letter O | Infinitely many |

### Lines of Symmetry in Letters of the Alphabet

| Horizontal symmetry | Vertical symmetry | Both |
|---------------------|-------------------|------|
| B, C, D, E, K | A, M, T, U, V, W, Y | H, I, O, X |

*No symmetry:* F, G, J, L, N, P, Q, R, S, Z

## Identifying Lines of Symmetry

**For a square:** Draw one vertical, one horizontal, and two diagonal lines — all four are lines of symmetry.

**For an equilateral triangle:** Draw a line from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side — these 3 lines (medians/altitudes) are all lines of symmetry.

**For a rectangle (not square):** Only the two lines through midpoints of opposite sides work (not the diagonals — the diagonals of a rectangle are NOT lines of symmetry).

> **Tip:** A line is a line of symmetry only if every point on one side has a matching point exactly the same distance on the other side.

---

## Reflection in a Line

When a shape is reflected in a line (mirror line), every point of the shape maps to a point on the other side such that:
- The mirror line is the **perpendicular bisector** of the line joining each point and its image.
- The reflected image is the same size and shape (congruent) — just flipped.

*Real-life reflections:*
- Reflection of a tree in a still lake.
- A mirror on a wall.
- Rangoli designs where one half is folded to match the other.
- The Taj Mahal — its reflection in the pool in front.

## Rotational Symmetry

A shape has **rotational symmetry** if it looks the same after being rotated by some angle less than 360° around a central point (the **centre of rotation**).

### Key Terms

- **Centre of rotation** — the fixed point around which the shape is rotated.
- **Angle of rotation** — the smallest angle through which the shape can be rotated and still look the same.
- **Order of rotational symmetry** — the number of times the shape looks identical during one full rotation (360°).

> **Order = 360° ÷ angle of rotation**

### Examples

![An equilateral triangle shown in three positions after 120-degree rotations, looking identical each time](rotational-symmetry-triangle.png)

**Equilateral triangle:**
- Looks same after 120° rotation.
- Order = 360° ÷ 120° = **3**
- (It looks identical 3 times during a full rotation.)

**Square:**
- Looks same after 90° rotation.
- Order = 360° ÷ 90° = **4**
- (It looks identical at 90°, 180°, 270°, 360°.)

**Regular hexagon:**
- Looks same after 60° rotation.
- Order = 360° ÷ 60° = **6**

**Rectangle (not square):**
- Looks same after 180° rotation.
- Order = 360° ÷ 180° = **2**

**Circle:**
- Looks same after any rotation.
- Order = **infinite**

**Letter Z or S or N:**
- Rotational symmetry of order 2 (looks same after 180°) but NO line symmetry.

| Shape | Angle of rotation | Order |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| Equilateral triangle | 120° | 3 |
| Square | 90° | 4 |
| Rectangle | 180° | 2 |
| Regular pentagon | 72° | 5 |
| Regular hexagon | 60° | 6 |
| Circle | Any angle | Infinite |

> **Note:** Every shape has a trivial rotational symmetry of order 1 (a full 360° rotation). We usually say a shape has rotational symmetry only if the order is 2 or more.

## Shapes With Both Types of Symmetry

Many regular shapes have both line symmetry AND rotational symmetry:

| Shape | Lines of symmetry | Order of rotation |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Equilateral triangle | 3 | 3 |
| Square | 4 | 4 |
| Regular hexagon | 6 | 6 |
| Circle | ∞ | ∞ |

### Shapes With Only One Type

- **Rectangle (not square):** 2 lines of symmetry, rotational order 2 — has both.
- **Parallelogram (not rectangle):** 0 lines of symmetry, rotational order 2 — **only rotational**.
- **Isosceles triangle:** 1 line of symmetry, order 1 — **only line symmetry**.
- **Letter Z:** 0 lines of symmetry, order 2 — **only rotational**.

## Symmetry in Indian Art and Culture

India has one of the richest traditions of symmetric art in the world.

![A traditional Kolam pattern made of dots and curved lines showing multiple lines of symmetry and rotational symmetry](kolam-symmetry-pattern.png)

### Rangoli / Kolam
- Geometric designs made with coloured powders or rice flour.
- Common in Tamil Nadu (Kolam), Andhra Pradesh (Muggulu), Maharashtra (Rangoli), and across India during festivals like Diwali and Pongal.
- Kolam patterns are typically made of dots connected by lines — and have multiple lines of symmetry and rotational symmetry.
- Many kolam patterns can be traced in one continuous line — a mathematical curiosity!

### Mandalas
- Circular symmetric designs used in Hindu and Buddhist traditions.
- A mandala has many lines of symmetry (passing through the centre) and high-order rotational symmetry.
- Used in temple floor designs and in meditation.

### Islamic Geometric Art
- Mughal architecture (Red Fort, Taj Mahal, Humayun's Tomb) features intricate geometric patterns on walls and ceilings.
- These patterns — called *girih* — use stars, hexagons, and other polygons in perfectly symmetric arrangements.
- The Taj Mahal garden is famously symmetric — divided into 4 equal quadrants by watercourses.

### Warli Art (Maharashtra) and Madhubani Art (Bihar)
- Both use repeated, symmetric geometric patterns.
- Circles, triangles, and squares arranged symmetrically.

### Temple Architecture
- The plan of a Hindu temple follows strict **Vastu Shastra** rules — a square mandala (*mandapam*) with four-fold symmetry is the basic plan.
- The tower (shikhara) above a temple has vertical line symmetry.

## Tessellations — Filling the Plane

A **tessellation** is a pattern of shapes that covers a surface completely, with no gaps or overlaps.

![Three regular tessellations side by side: equilateral triangles, squares, and regular hexagons covering a surface with no gaps](regular-tessellations.png)

The shapes that tessellate on their own (regular tessellations) are:
1. **Equilateral triangle** — 6 meet at each point
2. **Square** — 4 meet at each point
3. **Regular hexagon** — 3 meet at each point

> **Why do hexagons work so well?** Hexagons tessellate with no gaps, and the hexagonal shape is the most efficient — it uses the least total boundary for the most area. This is why bees build hexagonal honeycombs!

*Semi-regular tessellations* use two or more regular polygon shapes together.

## Symmetry in Nature

- **Snowflakes** — 6-fold symmetry (order 6 rotation, 6 lines of symmetry).
- **Flowers** — many flowers have 3, 4, 5, or 6-fold symmetry.
- **Starfish** — 5-fold symmetry.
- **Leaves** — bilateral (line) symmetry.
- **Spider webs** — radial symmetry.
- **Human face** — approximate bilateral symmetry.
- **Crystals** — specific types have particular crystal symmetry groups.

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Symmetry** | A property of shapes that look the same after reflection or rotation |
| **Line of symmetry** | A line along which a shape can be folded so both halves match exactly |
| **Reflection** | A flip of a shape over a line — creating a mirror image |
| **Rotational symmetry** | A shape that looks the same after rotation by less than 360° |
| **Centre of rotation** | The fixed point around which rotation occurs |
| **Order of symmetry** | Number of times a shape looks the same during one full rotation (360°) |
| **Angle of rotation** | The smallest angle for which the rotated shape looks identical |
| **Tessellation** | A pattern of shapes filling a plane with no gaps or overlaps |
| **Rangoli / Kolam** | Traditional Indian floor designs with high symmetry |
| **Mandala** | Circular symmetric design used in Hindu and Buddhist art and worship |
