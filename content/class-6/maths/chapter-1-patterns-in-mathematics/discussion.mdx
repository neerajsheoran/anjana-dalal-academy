## Patterns Are Everywhere

**Kiran:** Papa, my teacher said mathematics is all about patterns. But I thought maths was about calculating — adding, multiplying, dividing.

**Ravi:** Calculation is a tool, beta. But the real heart of maths is noticing patterns and asking why they work. Look at the rangoli your mother drew for Diwali — do you see the repeating shapes?

**Kiran:** Yes! The same flower design repeats in all four corners. And the border has a triangle-circle-triangle-circle pattern.

**Ravi:** That is mathematics in action. A pattern is anything that follows a rule. Days of the week repeat in a cycle — that is a pattern. Even numbers go 2, 4, 6, 8 — that is a pattern. Once you know the rule, you can predict what comes next.

**Kiran:** So maths is like being a detective — finding the hidden rule!

**Ravi:** Exactly. And India has a long tradition of this. The Fields Medal winner Manjul Bhargava describes maths as a creative art, like music. Indian mathematicians like Virahanka were discovering patterns in poetry rhythms over a thousand years ago.

## Number Sequences and Shapes

**Kiran:** We learned about triangular numbers — 1, 3, 6, 10, 15. But why are they called "triangular"?

**Ravi:** Imagine arranging bindis in a triangle shape. One bindi on top, two in the next row, three in the next. The total number of bindis gives you a triangular number.

**Kiran:** Oh! So 6 means three rows — 1 plus 2 plus 3. And 10 means four rows — 1 plus 2 plus 3 plus 4. I see the rule now — each time you add the next counting number!

**Ravi:** Very good. Now think about this — what happens if you put two consecutive triangular numbers together?

**Kiran:** Let me try. 3 and 6 — that is 9. And 6 and 10 is 16. Wait, 9 is 3 squared and 16 is 4 squared!

**Ravi:** Brilliant observation! Two consecutive triangular numbers always add up to a square number. Ancient Indian and Greek mathematicians figured this out by literally arranging dots. Maths was very visual for them.

## The Virahanka Numbers

**Kiran:** Papa, our textbook says the Fibonacci sequence should actually be called Virahanka numbers. Why?

**Ravi:** Because the Indian mathematician and poet Virahanka described this sequence around 700 CE — centuries before the European mathematician Fibonacci. Virahanka was studying Sanskrit poetry. He wanted to know how many ways you can arrange short beats and long beats to fill a line of poetry.

**Kiran:** And the answer turned out to be 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13 — each number is the sum of the two before it?

**Ravi:** Yes! And here is the magical part. Count the spirals on a sunflower head. You will find 34 going one way and 55 going the other — both Virahanka numbers! Pine cones have spirals of 8 and 13. Pineapples too. Nature seems to follow this sequence everywhere.

**Kiran:** But why would a sunflower follow a maths pattern?

**Ravi:** Because this arrangement is the most efficient way to pack seeds. Each seed gets maximum sunlight and space. Nature has been doing maths long before humans invented it. We just discovered the rules nature was already using.

## The Power of Doubling

**Kiran:** Papa, the wheat and chessboard story in our textbook says the total grains would be more than all the wheat ever grown on Earth. Is that really true?

**Ravi:** Let us think about it step by step. On the first square, 1 grain. Second square, 2. Third square, 4. By the tenth square, you already have 512 grains. By the twentieth, over 5 lakh. By the thirtieth, over 100 crore!

**Kiran:** And there are 64 squares on a chessboard!

**Ravi:** Right. By the 64th square, you need about 1800 crore crore grains. India produces roughly 11 crore tonnes of wheat per year. The chessboard amount would take thousands of years of the entire world's wheat production.

**Kiran:** So doubling looks small at first but becomes enormous?

**Ravi:** That is the key insight — powers of 2 grow much faster than we expect. This is exactly how computer memory works. Your phone stores data in powers of 2 — kilobytes, megabytes, gigabytes. And it is also how a single cell divides — 1 becomes 2, then 4, then 8 — building an entire human body from one tiny cell.

## Finding the Hidden Rule

**Kiran:** Papa, on my homework there is a sequence — 2, 6, 18, 54. I need to find the next number. How do I figure it out?

**Ravi:** There are three strategies. First, look at the differences between consecutive terms. Second, look at the ratios. Third, connect each term to its position number.

**Kiran:** Let me try differences first. 6 minus 2 is 4. 18 minus 6 is 12. 54 minus 18 is 36. The differences are not the same, so it is not a constant-difference pattern.

**Ravi:** Good. Now try ratios.

**Kiran:** 6 divided by 2 is 3. 18 divided by 6 is 3. 54 divided by 18 is 3. Each term is multiplied by 3! So the next term is 54 times 3, which is 162.

**Ravi:** Perfect reasoning. You tried one strategy, it did not work, so you tried another. That is exactly how mathematicians think — systematically, not by guessing. In cricket, when Virat Kohli analyses his batting statistics, he looks for patterns too — which bowler gives him trouble, which shots score the most runs on which grounds. Pattern-finding is everywhere.

**Kiran:** I never thought cricket could be about maths!

**Ravi:** Everything is, beta. Once you learn to spot patterns, the whole world looks different.
