## Divisibility Rules — Shortcuts for Big Numbers

**Kiran:** Papa, my homework asks whether 4,572 is divisible by 3. Do I have to actually divide the whole thing?

**Ravi:** Not at all! That is where divisibility rules come in. For 3, just add up all the digits. If the digit sum is divisible by 3, the whole number is too.

**Kiran:** So 4 plus 5 plus 7 plus 2 is 18. And 18 divided by 3 is 6. So yes, 4,572 is divisible by 3!

**Ravi:** Exactly. These rules are like shortcuts — sabzi kaise check karo bina kaatke. A shopkeeper squeezes a mango to check ripeness without cutting it open. Similarly, divisibility rules let you check without actually dividing.

**Kiran:** What about divisibility by 11? That rule with odd and even position digits seems strange.

**Ravi:** Let me show you with an example. Take 2,728. The digits in odd positions counting from the right are 8 and 7 — their sum is 15. The digits in even positions are 2 and 2 — their sum is 4. The difference is 15 minus 4, which is 11. Since 11 is divisible by 11, the number is too.

**Kiran:** Why does that actually work?

**Ravi:** That is a great question — you are thinking like a mathematician now. It comes from how place values relate to powers of 10, and how 10 leaves a remainder of minus 1 when divided by 11. We will explore that proof in higher classes, but for now, the rule always works!

## Kaprekar's Constant — The Magic of 6174

**Kiran:** Papa, I read about D. R. Kaprekar in our textbook. He found that every 4-digit number leads to 6174. That sounds impossible!

**Ravi:** Try it yourself. Pick any 4-digit number where not all digits are the same.

**Kiran:** I will pick my birth year — 2014. Digits in descending order: 4210. Ascending order: 0124. Now 4210 minus 0124 is... 4086.

**Ravi:** Keep going with 4086.

**Kiran:** Descending: 8640. Ascending: 0468. Difference: 8172. Next — 8721 minus 1278 is 7443. Then 7443 minus 3447 is 3996. Then 9963 minus 3699 is 6264. Then 6642 minus 2466 is 4176. Then 7641 minus 1467 is 6174!

**Ravi:** And if you do one more step — 7641 minus 1467 is 6174 again. It loops! Kaprekar was a schoolteacher from Devlali, Maharashtra. He was not at a fancy university — just a person deeply curious about numbers. He discovered this in 1949.

**Kiran:** A schoolteacher discovered something that works for every 4-digit number? That is amazing!

**Ravi:** It shows that great maths does not need expensive equipment or big labs. Just curiosity, a pencil, and patience. India has a rich tradition of recreational mathematics — playing with numbers for the joy of it.

## The Collatz Conjecture — A Simple Unsolved Puzzle

**Kiran:** Papa, our book mentions the Collatz Conjecture — an unsolved problem. But the rule is so simple! If even, divide by 2. If odd, multiply by 3 and add 1. How can something so simple be unsolved?

**Ravi:** That is exactly what makes it fascinating. Let us try it with 7.

**Kiran:** 7 is odd, so 3 times 7 plus 1 is 22. Even, so divide — 11. Odd — 34. Even — 17. Odd — 52. Even — 26. Even — 13. Odd — 40. Even — 20. Even — 10. Even — 5. Odd — 16. Even — 8. Even — 4. Even — 2. Even — 1! It reached 1!

**Ravi:** Now computers have checked this for every number up to 10 to the power 20 — that is a 1 followed by 20 zeros. Every single number eventually reaches 1. But no one has been able to prove it must always happen.

**Kiran:** Why can't they prove it if it always works?

**Ravi:** Because "always works for every number we have checked" is different from "must work for every number that exists." There are infinitely many numbers. The famous mathematician Paul Erdos said this problem is so hard that "mathematics is not yet ready for such problems." Even simple rules can lead to incredibly deep questions.

**Kiran:** So there are still mysteries in maths? Things nobody knows?

**Ravi:** Thousands of them! And that is what makes mathematics a living, growing subject — not a dead collection of formulas.

## Magic Squares and Indian Mathematics

**Kiran:** Papa, the magic square in our textbook has rows, columns, and diagonals all adding to 15. Is there only one way to arrange the numbers?

**Ravi:** For a 3 by 3 magic square using numbers 1 to 9, there is essentially one arrangement — you can rotate it or flip it, but the pattern is the same. The number 5 must always go in the centre.

**Kiran:** Why 5?

**Ravi:** The magic sum is 15. The centre number appears in one row, one column, and both diagonals — that is four lines. The total of all numbers 1 to 9 is 45. Each row sum is 15. If you work through the algebra, only 5 works in the centre.

**Kiran:** Our textbook also mentions a magic square in a Jain temple in Khajuraho, Madhya Pradesh. Is that really from 900 CE?

**Ravi:** Yes! The Parshvanath temple has a 4 by 4 magic square with a magic sum of 34, carved in stone over a thousand years ago. Indian mathematicians were exploring magic squares in texts like the Garga Samhita even earlier. These were not just puzzles — they were considered to have mystical significance.

**Kiran:** Maths and history are more connected than I thought.

**Ravi:** Always. When you study maths, you are walking in the footsteps of Virahanka, Brahmagupta, Kaprekar, and thousands of thinkers who came before us.

## Estimation — The Art of Smart Guessing

**Kiran:** Papa, why would I ever estimate when I can calculate the exact answer?

**Ravi:** Imagine you are at a stationery shop with a 500-rupee note. You want to buy a notebook for 85 rupees, a pen for 42 rupees, a geometry box for 195 rupees, and an eraser for 12 rupees. Do you need the exact total to know if you have enough money?

**Kiran:** I can round them — 85 is about 90, 42 is about 40, 195 is about 200, 12 is about 10. So roughly 90 plus 40 plus 200 plus 10 is 340. Yes, 500 rupees is enough!

**Ravi:** You just did estimation. It took you five seconds instead of carefully adding four numbers. A cricket commentator says "that six cleared the boundary by about 10 metres" — an estimate, not an exact measurement. A cook estimates how much dal to make for 20 guests. A civil engineer estimates bricks needed for a wall before doing precise calculations.

**Kiran:** So estimation gives a quick, "good enough" answer?

**Ravi:** Right. And knowing when you need an exact answer versus when an estimate will do — that is a very important skill in both maths and real life.
