## Numbers Are Full of Surprises

You have been using numbers since you were very young. But numbers hide many secrets — tricks, puzzles, and patterns that have fascinated mathematicians for thousands of years. Some of these puzzles are still unsolved today!

In this chapter, we will **play** with numbers — and discover how surprising and beautiful they can be.

---

## Numbers in Our World

Before we play with numbers, notice how numbers are all around us:
- The number on your house
- The PIN of your bank account
- A cricket score
- The population of India (about 140 crore!)
- The distance from Delhi to Mumbai (about 1,400 km)
- The size of a bacterium (about 0.000001 m)

We use different types of numbers for different purposes. Some numbers name things (**nominal** — like bus number 36, or jersey number 10). Some numbers **count** (how many students in a class). Some numbers **measure** (how many kg, km, or °C).

## Interesting Number Properties

### Supercells

Look at a row of numbers. A number is a **supercell** if it is **greater than all its neighbours** (the numbers directly next to it).

*Example:* In the row: 3, **7**, 2, **9**, 1, **8**, 4

- 7 is greater than 3 and 2 → **supercell** ✓
- 9 is greater than 2 and 1 → **supercell** ✓
- 8 is greater than 1 and 4 → **supercell** ✓

Numbers at the ends only have one neighbour.

> *Challenge:* In a row of numbers, can two supercells be next to each other? Why or why not?
> (Think: if A and B are adjacent, A is a supercell means A > B, and B is a supercell means B > A — both can't be true at the same time!)

## Divisibility Rules

Instead of dividing to check, we can use quick **divisibility rules**:

| Divisible by | Rule | Example |
|-------------|------|---------|
| **2** | Last digit is 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8 (even) | 348 ÷ 2 ✓ (last digit 8) |
| **3** | Sum of digits is divisible by 3 | 375: 3+7+5=15, 15÷3=5 ✓ |
| **4** | Last two digits form a number divisible by 4 | 1,532: last two digits 32, 32÷4=8 ✓ |
| **5** | Last digit is 0 or 5 | 245 ÷ 5 ✓, 780 ÷ 5 ✓ |
| **6** | Divisible by both 2 AND 3 | 126: even ✓, 1+2+6=9 ÷3 ✓, so ÷6 ✓ |
| **8** | Last three digits divisible by 8 | 3,120: last three digits 120, 120÷8=15 ✓ |
| **9** | Sum of digits is divisible by 9 | 729: 7+2+9=18, 18÷9=2 ✓ |
| **10** | Last digit is 0 | 450 ÷ 10 ✓ |
| **11** | Difference of (sum of digits in odd positions) and (sum of digits in even positions) is 0 or divisible by 11 | 2,728: (2+2)−(7+8) = 4−15 = −11 → divisible by 11 ✓ |

**Worked Examples:**

*Is 4,572 divisible by 3?*
> Sum of digits = 4 + 5 + 7 + 2 = **18**. Is 18 divisible by 3? Yes (18 ÷ 3 = 6). So **yes**, 4,572 is divisible by 3.

*Is 7,836 divisible by 9?*
> Sum of digits = 7 + 8 + 3 + 6 = **24**. Is 24 divisible by 9? No (24 ÷ 9 = 2 remainder 6). So **no**.

*Is 5,280 divisible by 6?*
> Check divisible by 2: last digit 0 → **yes**.
> Check divisible by 3: 5+2+8+0=15, 15÷3=5 → **yes**.
> Divisible by both 2 and 3 → **yes**, 5,280 is divisible by 6.

## Playing with Digit Sums

The **digit sum** of a number is found by adding all its digits. If the result has more than one digit, add again — until you get a single digit.

*Example:* Digit sum of 4,679:
> 4 + 6 + 7 + 9 = 26 → 2 + 6 = **8**

*Example:* Digit sum of 999:
> 9 + 9 + 9 = 27 → 2 + 7 = **9**

**Pattern — The digit sum of multiples of 9 is always 9:**
- 9 → 9
- 18 → 1+8 = 9
- 27 → 2+7 = 9
- 36 → 3+6 = 9
- 99 → 9+9=18 → 1+8 = 9

This is why the **divisibility rule for 9** works — if the digit sum equals 9 (or a multiple of 9), the number is divisible by 9.

## Kaprekar's Constant — 6174

This is one of the most magical discoveries in recreational mathematics, made by Indian mathematician **D. R. Kaprekar** (1905–1986) from Devlali, Maharashtra.

**The trick:**
1. Take any **4-digit number** where not all digits are the same (e.g., 1234, 5678, 9081).
2. Arrange its digits in **descending order** (largest to smallest) → call this number A.
3. Arrange its digits in **ascending order** (smallest to largest) → call this number B.
4. Compute **A − B**.
5. Repeat with the result.
6. You will **always reach 6174** — usually within 7 steps!

**Example with 1234:**

| Step | Descending | Ascending | Difference |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | 4321 | 1234 | 4321 − 1234 = **3087** |
| 2 | 8730 | 0378 | 8730 − 0378 = **8352** |
| 3 | 8532 | 2358 | 8532 − 2358 = **6174** |

Done in 3 steps! Now check: 7641 − 1467 = **6174** — it loops back to itself!

**Example with 5678:**

| Step | Descending | Ascending | Difference |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | 8765 | 5678 | 8765 − 5678 = **3087** |
| 2 | 8730 | 0378 | 8730 − 0378 = **8352** |
| 3 | 8532 | 2358 | 8532 − 2358 = **6174** |

> **6174 is called Kaprekar's Constant** — every 4-digit number (with at least two different digits) leads to 6174!

*Try with your favourite 4-digit number. Count how many steps it takes to reach 6174!*

## The Collatz Conjecture — An Unsolved Mystery

Here is a simple rule that has puzzled mathematicians for nearly 100 years — and **nobody has proved it yet**.

**The rule:**
- Start with any positive whole number.
- If it is **even**: divide by 2.
- If it is **odd**: multiply by 3 and add 1.
- Repeat. The conjecture says you will **always eventually reach 1**.

**Example starting from 6:**

| Step | Number | Operation |
|------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | 6 | Even → 6 ÷ 2 = **3** |
| 2 | 3 | Odd → 3×3+1 = **10** |
| 3 | 10 | Even → 10 ÷ 2 = **5** |
| 4 | 5 | Odd → 5×3+1 = **16** |
| 5 | 16 | Even → 16 ÷ 2 = **8** |
| 6 | 8 | Even → 8 ÷ 2 = **4** |
| 7 | 4 | Even → 4 ÷ 2 = **2** |
| 8 | 2 | Even → 2 ÷ 2 = **1** ✓ |

**Example starting from 27:** (takes 111 steps to reach 1 — and goes as high as 9,232 along the way!)

**Example starting from 7:**
> 7 → 22 → 11 → 34 → 17 → 52 → 26 → 13 → 40 → 20 → 10 → 5 → 16 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1 ✓

Computers have checked the Collatz rule for all numbers up to 10²⁰ (that's 1 followed by 20 zeros!) — and it always reaches 1. But mathematicians have not been able to prove it will work for *every* number. This is called the **Collatz Conjecture** — a conjecture is something believed to be true but not yet proved.

> This is what makes mathematics exciting — there are simple questions that even the smartest people in the world haven't answered yet!

## Magic Squares

A **magic square** is a grid of numbers where the sum of every row, every column, and both diagonals is the **same number** (called the **magic sum**).

### 3×3 Magic Square

```
2  7  6
9  5  1
4  3  8
```

Check:
- Rows: 2+7+6=15, 9+5+1=15, 4+3+8=15 ✓
- Columns: 2+9+4=15, 7+5+3=15, 6+1+8=15 ✓
- Diagonals: 2+5+8=15, 6+5+4=15 ✓

Magic sum = **15**

This square uses the numbers 1 to 9, each exactly once!

> **Indian connection:** Magic squares have been studied in India for over 2,000 years. The ancient text *Garga Samhita* (1st century CE) contains magic squares. The 4×4 magic square in the famous **Parshvanath Jain temple** in Khajuraho (Madhya Pradesh) has a magic sum of **34** and was created around 900 CE — centuries before Europeans studied them!

### The Lo Shu Magic Square

The 3×3 magic square above is one of the oldest known — from ancient China (Lo Shu square, ~650 BCE). In India, similar squares appear in ancient Sanskrit texts.

## Pascal's Triangle

Arrange numbers in a triangle like this — each number is the **sum of the two numbers directly above it**:

```
           1
          1 1
         1 2 1
        1 3 3 1
       1 4 6 4 1
      1 5 10 10 5 1
```

**Hidden patterns in Pascal's Triangle:**

1. **Each row gives the powers of 11:**
   - Row 1: 1 → 11⁰ = 1
   - Row 2: 11 → 11¹ = 11
   - Row 3: 121 → 11² = 121
   - Row 4: 1331 → 11³ = 1331
   - Row 5: 14641 → 11⁴ = 14641

2. **Each row sums to a power of 2:**
   - Row 1: 1 = 2⁰
   - Row 2: 1+1 = 2 = 2¹
   - Row 3: 1+2+1 = 4 = 2²
   - Row 4: 1+3+3+1 = 8 = 2³

3. **The diagonal of 1s, then natural numbers, then triangular numbers!**
   - Outermost diagonal: 1, 1, 1, 1 … (all 1s)
   - Next diagonal: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 … (natural numbers)
   - Next diagonal: 1, 3, 6, 10, 15 … (triangular numbers!)

4. **The Virahanka (Fibonacci) sequence** appears — look at the shallow diagonals!

## Numbers in Different Bases

We normally use **base 10** — ten digits (0-9), and each position is a power of 10.

The number 345 means:
> 3 × 100 + 4 × 10 + 5 × 1 = 300 + 40 + 5

Computers use **base 2** (binary) — only two digits: 0 and 1.

*Example:* The number 13 in binary:
> 13 = 8 + 4 + 1 = 1×2³ + 1×2² + 0×2¹ + 1×2⁰ = **1101** in binary

| Decimal | Binary |
|---------|--------|
| 1 | 1 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 11 |
| 4 | 100 |
| 5 | 101 |
| 8 | 1000 |

## Estimation — The Art of Smart Guessing

Sometimes we do not need an exact answer — a good **estimate** is enough.

**Estimation strategies:**

**Rounding:** Replace numbers with nearby round numbers.
- 387 + 214 ≈ 400 + 200 = **600** (actual: 601 — very close!)

**Front-end estimation:** Use only the leading digits.
- 4,723 + 3,189 ≈ 4,000 + 3,000 = **7,000** (actual: 7,912)

*Indian examples of estimation:*
- A shopkeeper estimates if a customer has given enough money.
- A cook estimates the amount of dal needed for 20 guests.
- A civil engineer estimates the number of bricks needed for a wall.
- A cricket commentator estimates that a six cleared the boundary by 10 metres.

**Worked Example:** A school has 1,247 girls and 1,389 boys. About how many students in total?
> Round: 1,247 ≈ 1,250 and 1,389 ≈ 1,400
> Estimate: 1,250 + 1,400 = **2,650** (Actual: 2,636 — close enough for a quick estimate!)

## The Beauty of Number Patterns

### Palindromic numbers

A **palindrome** reads the same forwards and backwards.
- 121, 1331, 12321, 9009, 1001001

*The 1 × 1 pattern:*
```
  1 × 1 = 1
 11 × 11 = 121
111 × 111 = 12321
1111 × 1111 = 1234321
11111 × 11111 = 123454321
```

All palindromes! The pattern continues until 111,111,111 × 111,111,111.

### The 9 trick:
```
9 × 1 = 9
9 × 2 = 18   → 1+8 = 9
9 × 3 = 27   → 2+7 = 9
9 × 4 = 36   → 3+6 = 9
9 × 12 = 108 → 1+0+8 = 9
9 × 99 = 891 → 8+9+1 = 18 → 1+8 = 9
```

The digit sum of any multiple of 9 is always 9!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Divisibility rule** | A quick test to check if a number is divisible by another without doing full division |
| **Digit sum** | The sum of all digits of a number (repeated until a single digit) |
| **Kaprekar's Constant** | The number 6174 — every 4-digit number reaches it through the Kaprekar process |
| **Collatz Conjecture** | The unsolved puzzle: apply odd→3n+1, even→n÷2 repeatedly, you always reach 1 |
| **Magic square** | A grid where rows, columns, and diagonals all have the same sum |
| **Palindrome** | A number (or word) that reads the same forwards and backwards |
| **Estimation** | Finding an approximate answer, close enough to be useful |
| **Conjecture** | A statement believed to be true but not yet proved |
| **Pascal's Triangle** | A triangle of numbers where each number = sum of two above it |
| **Base 10** | Our number system using digits 0–9, with each position worth 10× the one to its right |
| **Binary (Base 2)** | Number system using only 0 and 1 — used by computers |
