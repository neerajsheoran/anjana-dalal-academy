## What Is Pressure?

Imagine carrying two bags of the same weight -- one with narrow straps and one with broad straps. The bag with narrow straps hurts your shoulders more. Why? Both bags weigh the same, but the narrow straps press the weight into a **smaller area**, while the broad straps spread it over a **larger area**.

This is the idea behind **pressure**. Pressure is the force acting per unit area.

**Pressure = Force / Area**

The **SI unit of pressure** is **newton per metre squared (N/m2)**, also called **pascal (Pa)**.

> **Key idea:** When the same force acts on a smaller area, the pressure is higher. When the same force acts on a larger area, the pressure is lower.

### Everyday Examples of Pressure

- A **nail** is driven by its pointed end because the small area creates high pressure
- A **knife** cuts better with its sharp edge because the force is concentrated on a tiny area
- People carrying heavy pots on their heads place a round cloth underneath to **increase the area** and reduce pressure
- A water bucket with a **broad handle** is easier to carry than one with a narrow handle

## Liquid Pressure

Liquids also exert pressure. If you fill two transparent pipes of different diameters with water to the **same height** and attach balloons at the bottom, both balloons bulge to the **same extent**. This proves that liquid pressure depends on the **height of the liquid column**, not the amount of liquid.

### Key Facts About Liquid Pressure

- The pressure exerted by a liquid increases with the **height of the liquid column**
- Liquids exert pressure not only at the **bottom** but also on the **sides** of a container
- In fact, liquids exert pressure in **all directions**

### Why Are Water Tanks Placed at a Height?

Overhead water tanks are placed on rooftops because a greater height of the water column creates more pressure at the taps below. This ensures a good stream of water flows from the taps. The higher the tank, the stronger the water pressure at ground level.

### Why Are Dams Broader at the Base?

The base of a dam is much broader than the top. This is because the water pressure near the bottom is very large (due to the tall column of water above it). A broad base helps the dam withstand this enormous horizontal pressure.

## Atmospheric Pressure

The envelope of air surrounding the Earth is called the **atmosphere**. This atmospheric air exerts pressure on all objects. The pressure exerted by air around us is called **atmospheric pressure**.

### How Large Is Atmospheric Pressure?

The force exerted by the atmospheric air column over an area of just 15 cm x 15 cm is nearly equal to the weight of a 225 kg object (about 2250 N)! That is enormous.

**Why are we not crushed?** Because the pressure **inside our bodies** is also equal to the atmospheric pressure. This internal pressure balances the external atmospheric pressure perfectly.

### The Sucker Experiment

When you press a rubber sucker against a smooth surface, most of the air between the sucker and the surface is pushed out. The higher atmospheric pressure surrounding the sucker holds it firmly in place. To pull it off, you must apply a force strong enough to overcome this pressure difference.

> **Key idea:** Air exerts pressure in all directions. When you inflate a balloon, the air inside pushes equally on all walls of the balloon, which is why balloons expand in all directions.

## Formation of Wind

### How Do Winds Form?

Air always moves from a region of **high pressure** to a region of **low pressure**. This movement of air is what we call **wind**.

The greater the difference in air pressure between two regions, the **faster** the wind blows.

### Sea Breeze and Land Breeze

**During the day (Sea Breeze):**
1. Land heats up faster than water
2. Warm air above the land rises, creating a **low-pressure area**
3. Cooler air from the sea (high pressure) blows towards the land
4. This is called a **sea breeze**

**During the night (Land Breeze):**
1. Land cools down faster than water
2. Water is now warmer, and warm air above the sea rises, creating a low-pressure area
3. Cooler air from the land blows towards the sea
4. This is called a **land breeze**

> **Key idea:** All winds are caused by differences in air pressure. Warm air rises (creating low pressure), and cooler air from surrounding high-pressure areas rushes in to take its place.

## High-Speed Winds and Low Pressure

Here is a surprising fact: **high-speed winds are accompanied by reduced air pressure**.

If you hang two inflated balloons side by side and blow air between them, the balloons move **towards each other** instead of apart. Blowing between the balloons creates a low-pressure area between them. The higher pressure on the outer sides pushes them together.

### Why Do Roofs Blow Off During Storms?

When high-speed winds blow over a house:
- A **low-pressure area** is created above the roof
- The air pressure **below the roof** (inside the house) remains higher
- This pressure difference pushes the roof **upward** and can blow it off

**Safety tip:** During storms, it is safer to **keep doors and windows open**. This allows wind to flow through the house, reducing the pressure difference between inside and outside, and helps prevent the roof from being blown off.

## Storms, Thunderstorms, and Lightning

### How Storms Form

1. **Land gets heated** and warm, moist air rises
2. This creates a **low-pressure area**
3. Cooler air from surrounding areas rushes in
4. This air also gets heated and rises
5. Rising air **expands and cools**, causing moisture to condense into water droplets (clouds)
6. Water droplets merge to form heavier drops that fall as **rain, hail, or snow**
7. Strong winds accompanied by rain form a **storm**

### How Lightning Occurs

1. Strong winds blow **upwards and downwards** inside clouds
2. This causes rubbing between water droplets and ice particles
3. This rubbing creates **static electric charges** (just like rubbing a plastic scale on cloth)
4. **Positively charged** lighter ice particles move to the upper part of clouds
5. **Negatively charged** heavier water droplets settle in the lower part
6. When the build-up of charges becomes very large, the insulating property of air breaks down
7. A sudden flow of charges produces a bright flash of light -- this is **lightning**
8. Lightning rapidly heats the surrounding air, causing it to expand and produce a loud sound -- this is **thunder**

A storm accompanied by lightning and thunder is called a **thunderstorm**.

### Regional Thunderstorms in India

- **Kalboishakhi** -- in West Bengal, Bihar, and Jharkhand
- **Bordoisila** -- in Assam
- **Mango showers** -- in Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu (help ripen mangoes)

### Safety During Lightning

- Stay away from tall objects like trees and poles
- Find a low-lying open area and **crouch down** (do not lie flat)
- **Do not use** an umbrella with a metallic rod
- If you are in water, **get out immediately**
- If inside a bus or car, you are comparatively safer

### Lightning Conductors

A **lightning conductor** is a metallic rod installed along the walls of buildings. One end (pointed) is kept higher than the building, and the other end is buried deep in the ground. It provides an easy path for electric charges to safely flow into the ground, protecting the building from lightning strikes.

## Cyclones

**Cyclones** are large, powerful storms that form over warm ocean waters.

### How Cyclones Form

1. Ocean water gets heated, and warm, moist air rises
2. Water vapour in the rising air **condenses** into raindrops, releasing heat
3. This released heat causes further warming, making the air rise even more
4. This creates an area of **very low pressure**
5. Air from surrounding high-pressure regions rushes in
6. The **Earth's rotation** causes this moving air to **spin**
7. This cycle repeats, creating a spinning system of clouds, winds, and rain -- a **cyclone**

### Structure of a Cyclone

- The centre of a cyclone is called the **eye of the cyclone**
- At the eye, the wind is **calm**
- But the surrounding region experiences **extremely strong winds** and heavy rainfall

### Destruction Caused by Cyclones

Cyclones can be extremely destructive:
- **Wind speeds** can reach 270 km/h or more (e.g., Cyclone Amphan, 2020)
- **Storm surges** -- strong winds push ocean water towards the shore, creating walls of water 3 to 12 metres high that flood coastal areas
- Heavy rainfall can cause rivers to overflow and trigger **landslides**
- Seawater can contaminate **drinking water sources** and damage farmland
- The salt in seawater makes soil **less fertile**, affecting crops
- Roads get blocked by fallen trees, and power outages can last for days

### Protection During Cyclones

- Stay updated on weather reports from the **India Meteorological Department (IMD)**
- Weather monitoring satellites help track cyclones and predict their paths
- If you live in a cyclone-prone area, keep an **emergency kit** ready
- Move to a nearby designated **cyclone shelter** during a cyclone

> **Key idea:** Once a cyclone reaches land, the source of moist air is cut off, and it gradually loses strength. However, it still leaves behind a trail of destruction that can take months or years to repair.

## Key Takeaways

- **Pressure** is force per unit area. Its SI unit is N/m2 or pascal (Pa).
- Liquids exert pressure in **all directions**, and this pressure increases with the height of the liquid column.
- **Atmospheric pressure** is the pressure exerted by air around us. It acts in all directions.
- Air flows from regions of **high pressure** to regions of **low pressure**, creating wind.
- **High-speed winds** are accompanied by **reduced air pressure**.
- **Thunderstorms** form when warm, moist air rises, condenses into clouds, and creates strong winds.
- **Lightning** occurs when static charges build up in clouds and discharge as a bright flash.
- **Cyclones** are large spinning storms that form over warm ocean waters due to continuous rising of warm, moist air and the spinning effect of Earth's rotation.
- The **India Meteorological Department (IMD)** monitors cyclones and issues warnings to protect lives and property.
