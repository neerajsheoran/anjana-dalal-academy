## Matter Is Made of Tiny Particles

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook says if you keep breaking chalk, you eventually reach particles that cannot be broken further. These are the constituent particles. But how small are they?

**Ravi:** Incredibly small. A single grain of sugar contains about 10 to the power 17 molecules — that is 100 quadrillion molecules. You cannot see individual molecules even with the most powerful optical microscope. This connects to the powers of 10 you studied in Maths Chapter 2.

**Kiran:** Acharya Kanad proposed the concept of Parmanu — the smallest indivisible particle — in his Vaisheshika Sutras. That is essentially the concept of an atom, proposed in India thousands of years ago.

**Ravi:** Kanad's insight was remarkable. He reasoned that if you keep dividing matter, you must eventually reach a point where further division is impossible. The Greeks had a similar concept — Democritus called these particles "atomos" (indivisible). But Kanad was likely earlier.

**Kiran:** When sugar dissolves in water, it seems to disappear, but the water tastes sweet. So the sugar particles fit into the spaces between water particles.

**Ravi:** These interparticle spaces are key. In solids, the spaces are minimal. In liquids, they are slightly larger. In gases, the spaces are enormous compared to the particles themselves. This explains why gases can be compressed but liquids and solids cannot.

## Three States of Matter

**Kiran:** Solids have fixed shape and volume because particles are tightly packed with strong interparticle forces. Liquids have fixed volume but take the shape of their container. Gases have neither fixed shape nor fixed volume.

**Ravi:** Think of it in terms of a classroom analogy. In a solid, students are sitting in fixed seats — they can fidget (vibrate) but not move around. In a liquid, students can walk around the classroom but cannot leave it. In a gas, students can run freely through the entire school.

**Kiran:** That is a great way to remember it! And the transition between states depends on temperature. Heating a solid increases vibration until particles break free — that is melting. Heating a liquid further gives particles enough energy to escape completely — that is boiling.

**Ravi:** The melting point and boiling point depend on the strength of interparticle forces. Ice melts at 0 degrees Celsius because water's interparticle forces are moderate. Iron melts at 1538 degrees Celsius because metallic bonds are very strong.

**Kiran:** ISRO's rockets deal with extreme temperatures — the rocket nozzle must withstand temperatures above 3000 degrees Celsius without melting. They must use materials with extremely high melting points.

**Ravi:** Rocket nozzles are made of special alloys and ceramic composites designed to maintain their structure at temperatures that would melt most metals. Understanding the particulate nature of matter is essential for designing such materials.

## Interparticle Spacing and Compression

**Kiran:** The syringe experiment shows that air can be compressed — pushing the plunger reduces the volume — but water is practically incompressible. This makes sense because gas particles are far apart while liquid particles are already close together.

**Ravi:** This has huge practical implications. Hydraulic brakes in cars use the incompressibility of brake fluid. When you press the brake pedal, the force is transmitted instantly through the fluid to all four wheels. If the fluid were compressible like air, pressing the brake pedal would just compress the fluid instead of applying force to the brakes.

**Kiran:** And the sugar dissolution experiment proves interparticle spaces exist even in liquids — when sugar dissolves, the water level drops slightly because sugar particles fill the spaces between water molecules.

**Ravi:** LPG — the cooking gas used in millions of Indian homes — is actually a gas that has been compressed into liquid form under high pressure. The cylinder contains liquid LPG. When you open the valve, the pressure drops and it turns back into gas. This is only possible because gas particles can be pushed much closer together.

**Kiran:** So compression, dissolution, and changes of state — they all depend on interparticle spacing and forces.

**Ravi:** Exactly. One simple model — particles with forces between them and spaces around them — explains an enormous range of phenomena.

## Particle Movement and Temperature

**Kiran:** The potassium permanganate experiment shows that particles in a liquid are in constant motion. In hot water, the colour spreads fastest; in cold water, slowest. So temperature directly affects particle speed.

**Ravi:** Temperature is essentially a measure of how fast particles are moving. Higher temperature means faster movement, which means particles spread more quickly, reactions happen faster, and substances dissolve more readily.

**Kiran:** That is why cooking happens faster at higher temperatures! The particles in food and spices are moving and reacting more quickly.

**Ravi:** And it is why pressure cookers work. By increasing pressure, the boiling point of water rises above 100 degrees Celsius. At about 120 degrees, food particles interact more vigorously, reducing cooking time by up to 70 percent. Every Indian kitchen with a pressure cooker is demonstrating particle physics daily.

**Kiran:** Incense smoke spreading through a room demonstrates particle movement in gases. The fragrance particles are bumped around by constantly moving air particles.

**Ravi:** This random motion is called Brownian motion, first observed by the botanist Robert Brown in 1827. Einstein provided the mathematical explanation in 1905 — the same year he published his theory of special relativity. Understanding particle motion at a fundamental level led to some of the greatest breakthroughs in physics.

## How Soap Works

**Kiran:** The textbook explains how soap cleans clothes using the particulate model. Soap particles have one end that attaches to oil and another end that mixes with water. They surround oil droplets and lift them off the fabric.

**Ravi:** Each soap molecule is like a tiny matchstick — the head is hydrophilic (water-loving) and the tail is hydrophobic (water-fearing but oil-loving). When soap molecules surround a grease particle, their tails embed in the grease while their heads face outward into the water. This creates a tiny sphere called a micelle that can be washed away.

**Kiran:** So understanding particles at the molecular level explains something as everyday as washing clothes!

**Ravi:** India is one of the world's largest soap markets, and companies like Hindustan Unilever formulate different soaps for different purposes — all based on this same particle-level understanding. Chemistry, physics, and everyday life are one continuous story.
