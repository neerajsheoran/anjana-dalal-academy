## What Is Matter Composed Of?

Take a stick of chalk and break it into two pieces. Keep breaking the pieces until they become too small to break by hand. Then grind the small pieces using a mortar and pestle. Even the finest powder you get is still chalk -- grinding does not change it into a new substance. This is a **physical change** where only the size of each piece has reduced.

If you could keep grinding endlessly, you would eventually reach a stage where the chalk particles **cannot be broken down any further**. These tiny units are the basic building blocks of chalk. They are called the **constituent particles** of chalk.

> **Key idea:** Matter is composed of a large number of extremely small particles called constituent particles. These particles are so small that they cannot be seen even through an ordinary microscope.

### Where Does the Sugar Go?

When you dissolve sugar in water, the sugar seems to disappear. But if you taste the water, it is sweet! The sugar has not vanished. When sugar dissolves, it breaks up into its constituent particles, which are too tiny to see. These tiny sugar particles separate and occupy the available spaces between the water particles. These spaces are called **interparticle spaces**.

### Ancient Indian Wisdom

**Acharya Kanad**, an ancient Indian philosopher, first spoke about the idea of a **Parmanu** (atom). He believed that matter is made up of tiny, indivisible, eternal particles called Parmanu. This idea was written in his work called the *Vaisheshika Sutras*.

## What Decides Different States of Matter?

The constituent particles of matter are held together through attractive forces called **interparticle attractions**. The strength of these attractions depends on:
- The **nature of the substance**
- The **distance between particles** -- even a slight increase in distance decreases the forces drastically

The strength of these interparticle forces ultimately decides whether a substance is a **solid**, **liquid**, or **gas**.

---

## The Solid State

In solids, the particles are **tightly packed** and the interparticle attractions are **very strong**. These strong forces hold the particles in fixed positions, preventing them from moving freely.

**Properties of solids:**
- Have a **definite shape** and **definite volume**
- Particles can only **vibrate** (move to and fro) about their fixed positions
- Cannot be compressed easily
- Cannot flow

**Examples:** Iron nail, rock salt, stone, wooden block, aluminium piece

![Diagram showing change of state from solid to liquid (melting) and liquid to gas (boiling) with increasing temperature](change-of-state-diagram.png)

### Melting: Solid to Liquid

When solids are heated, their particles vibrate more vigorously. At a certain temperature, the vibrations become so strong that particles start leaving their fixed positions. The interparticle forces weaken, and the solid converts into a liquid.

The **melting point** is the minimum temperature at which a solid melts to become a liquid at atmospheric pressure.

| Substance | Melting Point |
|-----------|--------------|
| Ice | 0 degrees C |
| Urea | 133 degrees C |
| Iron | 1538 degrees C |

Substances with **weak interparticle forces** have low melting points. Those with **strong forces** have high melting points.

---

## The Liquid State

In liquids, particles are somewhat farther apart than in solids. The interparticle attractions are **slightly weaker** than in solids, but still strong enough to keep particles close together.

**Properties of liquids:**
- Have a **definite volume** but **no fixed shape**
- Take the shape of the container they are kept in
- Particles can **move freely**, but only within a limited space
- Cannot be compressed easily

If you pour 200 mL of water into containers of different shapes, the water takes the shape of each container, but the volume remains 200 mL.

### Boiling: Liquid to Gas

When a liquid is heated, the particles move faster and faster. At a certain temperature, the movement becomes so vigorous that particles start escaping from the liquid.

The **boiling point** is the temperature at which a liquid boils and turns into vapour at atmospheric pressure. At the boiling point, bubble formation occurs throughout the liquid.

**Evaporation** is different from boiling. Evaporation occurs at **any temperature** (even below the boiling point), but it happens slowly and only at the surface of the liquid.

> **Key idea:** Both liquids and gases can flow and do not retain a fixed shape. These properties classify them as **fluids**.

---

## The Gaseous State

In gases, the interparticle attractions are **negligible** (almost zero). The particles have enough energy to overcome the forces of attraction and move **freely in all directions**.

**Properties of gases:**
- Have **no fixed shape** and **no fixed volume**
- Occupy the **entire available space**
- Particles move freely and randomly
- Can be **compressed** easily

### Demonstrating Gas Behaviour

If you burn an incense stick in one corner of a room, within a few minutes you can smell the fragrance throughout the room. This happens because the particles of the fragrance spread out, helped by the constantly moving air particles. This shows that gas particles are always in motion.

## Interparticle Spacing in the Three States

### Can Gases Be Compressed?

Take a syringe without a needle. Pull the plunger out, seal the open end with your thumb, and push the plunger inward. You will notice that the volume of air inside decreases. This shows that gas particles have **a lot of space** between them, which can be reduced by applying pressure.

If you try the same experiment with water, you will find that water is **practically incompressible**. This is because liquid particles are already close together with much less space between them.

### The Sugar Dissolution Experiment

When sugar is dissolved in water:
1. Add sugar to water and mark the new water level
2. After the sugar dissolves completely, the water level drops slightly below the mark
3. This proves that there are **spaces between water particles** where sugar particles fit in

### Summary of Interparticle Spacing

![Arrangement of particles in solids (tightly packed), liquids (slightly spread out), and gases (far apart and freely moving)](particle-arrangement-states-of-matter.png)

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|----------|-------|--------|-----|
| **Interparticle spacing** | Minimum | Slightly more than solids | Maximum |
| **Packing of particles** | Closely packed | A little loosely packed | Free and far apart |
| **Interparticle attraction** | Maximum (very strong) | Slightly weaker than solids | Minimum (negligible) |
| **Movement of particles** | Only vibrations | Restricted to limited space | Free movement in all directions |
| **Shape** | Fixed | Takes shape of container | No fixed shape |
| **Volume** | Fixed | Fixed | No fixed volume |

> **Key idea:** The interparticle spaces in solids contain nothing at all -- not even air. They are simply empty spaces between the tightly packed particles.

## How Particles Move in Different States

### Movement in Liquids

Drop a few grains of **potassium permanganate** into a glass of water (without stirring). Initially, you will see streaks of pink colour spreading out from the grain. With time, the entire water turns uniformly pink.

This happens because water particles are in **constant motion**. They pull out the potassium permanganate particles and spread them throughout the liquid.

**Temperature affects particle movement:**
- In **hot water**, potassium permanganate spreads the fastest
- In **room temperature water**, it spreads at a moderate speed
- In **ice-cold water**, it spreads the slowest

This proves that the movement of particles **increases when heat is provided**.

### Movement in Gases

When you light an incense stick in one corner of a room, the fragrance reaches all corners within minutes. The constantly moving air particles hit the fragrance particles and help them spread throughout the room. This is also why the smell of cooking in the kitchen reaches other rooms.

## The Role of Thermal Energy

It is the **thermal energy** (heat energy) of particles that determines the physical state of matter:

- **In solids:** Thermal energy is low, so particles remain close and experience strong attractive forces. They can only vibrate.
- **At the melting point:** Thermal energy is used to overcome the attractive forces, allowing the solid to change into a liquid.
- **In liquids:** Particles have enough energy to move around, but still within a limited space.
- **In gases:** Particles have enough energy to completely overcome the forces of attraction and move freely in all directions.

### Everyday Application: How Soap Works

The particulate nature of matter explains how soap cleans oily clothes. Numerous soap particles surround the oil particles on the fabric. One end of each soap particle attaches to the oil, and the other end mixes with water. This helps lift the oil off the fabric and wash it away.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Matter is composed of **extremely small constituent particles**.
- Particles are held together by **interparticle forces of attraction**.
- **Solids** have the strongest interparticle attractions, minimum spacing, and fixed shape and volume. Particles can only vibrate.
- **Liquids** have slightly weaker attractions, a little more spacing, and definite volume but no fixed shape. Particles move within a limited space.
- **Gases** have negligible interparticle attractions, maximum spacing, and no fixed shape or volume. Particles move freely in all directions.
- The **melting point** is the temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid.
- The **boiling point** is the temperature at which a liquid turns into vapour.
- **Thermal energy** of particles determines the physical state of matter.
- The tiny particles that make up all matter are **atoms** and **molecules**.
