## What Are Spherical Mirrors?

You already know about plane mirrors -- flat mirrors that form an erect image of the same size as the object. But have you ever looked at yourself in a shiny metallic spoon? The inner (curved inward) side shows a different kind of image, and the outer (curved outward) side shows yet another!

**Spherical mirrors** are mirrors whose reflecting surfaces are shaped like a part of a hollow sphere. There are two types:

### Concave Mirror

A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface **curves inwards** is called a **concave mirror**. Think of the inner surface of a spoon.

### Convex Mirror

A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface **curves outwards** is called a **convex mirror**. Think of the outer surface of a spoon.

> **Key idea:** Unlike plane mirrors that always show you an image of the same size, spherical mirrors can make images appear larger, smaller, or even inverted depending on how far the object is from the mirror.

---

## Images Formed by Spherical Mirrors

### Concave Mirror Images

When you place an object in front of a concave mirror:
- **Close to the mirror** -- the image is **erect and enlarged** (bigger than the object)
- **Far from the mirror** -- the image becomes **inverted** (upside down). Initially it is enlarged, then it keeps getting smaller as you move the object farther away

### Convex Mirror Images

When you place an object in front of a convex mirror:
- The image is **always erect** and **always smaller** (diminished) than the object
- As the object moves farther, the image gets only slightly smaller

### Uses of Concave Mirrors

- **Torch and car headlight reflectors** -- the curved shape helps concentrate light into a beam
- **Dental mirrors** -- dentists use small concave mirrors to get an enlarged view of teeth
- **Telescopes** -- most modern reflecting telescopes use large concave mirrors
- **Solar concentrators** -- used to concentrate sunlight to produce heat

### Uses of Convex Mirrors

- **Side-view mirrors on vehicles** -- provide a wider view of the road behind. The warning "Objects in mirror are closer than they appear" is written because the convex mirror makes things look smaller and farther away
- **Road safety mirrors** -- installed at sharp bends and intersections so drivers can see traffic from the other side
- **Surveillance mirrors** -- used in shops to monitor a large area

## Laws of Reflection

When a ray of light falls on a mirror and bounces back, this is called **reflection**. There are two important laws that govern how light reflects:

### Key Terms

- **Incident ray** -- the ray of light that falls on the mirror
- **Reflected ray** -- the ray of light that bounces back from the mirror
- **Normal** -- a line drawn at 90 degrees to the mirror surface at the point where the light hits
- **Angle of incidence (i)** -- the angle between the incident ray and the normal
- **Angle of reflection (r)** -- the angle between the reflected ray and the normal

![Diagram showing incident ray, reflected ray, normal line, angle of incidence, and angle of reflection on a mirror surface](laws-of-reflection-diagram.png)

### The Two Laws of Reflection

**Law 1:** The angle of incidence is **equal** to the angle of reflection.

**Law 2:** The incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray all lie in the **same plane**.

> **Key idea:** The laws of reflection are valid for ALL kinds of mirrors -- plane, concave, and convex. Every ray of light follows these laws when it reflects off any mirror surface.

---

## How Spherical Mirrors Handle Parallel Light

When multiple parallel beams of light fall on different types of mirrors:

![Comparison of light reflection from plane mirror (parallel rays), concave mirror (converging rays), and convex mirror (diverging rays)](concave-convex-mirror-reflection.png)

- **Plane mirror** -- the reflected beams remain **parallel**
- **Concave mirror** -- the reflected beams **converge** (come closer together and meet at a point)
- **Convex mirror** -- the reflected beams **diverge** (spread apart)

### Burning Paper with a Concave Mirror

Because a concave mirror converges light, it can concentrate sunlight into a small, bright spot. If you hold a concave mirror facing the Sun and adjust a piece of paper at the right distance, the concentrated sunlight produces enough heat to burn the paper!

This principle is used in **solar concentrators** that concentrate sunlight to generate electricity, cook food on a large scale, or even melt steel in solar furnaces.

## What Is a Lens?

A **lens** is a piece of transparent material (usually glass or plastic) that has curved surfaces. Unlike mirrors, lenses allow light to **pass through** them.

### Convex Lens

![Convex lens (thicker in middle) converging parallel light rays and concave lens (thinner in middle) diverging them](convex-concave-lens-comparison.png)

A lens that is **thicker in the middle** and thinner at the edges is called a **convex lens**. A magnifying glass is a convex lens.

A drop of water on a glass surface also acts like a tiny convex lens -- text underneath appears enlarged!

### Concave Lens

A lens that is **thicker at the edges** and thinner in the middle is called a **concave lens**.

---

## Images Formed by Lenses

### Convex Lens Images

- **Object close to the lens** -- the image is **erect and enlarged**
- **Object far from the lens** -- the image becomes **inverted**. Initially enlarged, then it diminishes as the object moves farther

### Concave Lens Images

- The image is **always erect** and **always diminished** (smaller than the object)

---

## How Lenses Handle Parallel Light

- **Thin glass plate** -- light passes through as it is (no change)
- **Convex lens** -- **converges** the light (brings beams together). A convex lens is also called a **converging lens**
- **Concave lens** -- **diverges** the light (spreads beams apart). A concave lens is also called a **diverging lens**

### Burning Paper with a Convex Lens

Just like a concave mirror, a convex lens can also concentrate sunlight to burn paper. The concentrated sunlight at the focal point produces enough heat to ignite the paper.

---

## Where Are Lenses Used?

Lenses are used everywhere around us:

- **Eyeglasses** -- convex or concave lenses help people see clearly
- **Cameras** -- in smartphones and professional cameras
- **Telescopes** -- to see distant objects like stars and planets
- **Microscopes** -- to magnify tiny objects
- **Our eyes** -- the human eye has a convex lens inside it that can change its shape, allowing us to read a book up close or see something far away

> **Key idea:** Some reading glasses have a curved line across them. This is because they use different lens powers in different parts -- the upper part for seeing far and the lower part for reading.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Spherical mirrors** are curved mirrors shaped like part of a hollow sphere -- either concave (curves inward) or convex (curves outward).
- A **concave mirror** can form enlarged, diminished, erect, or inverted images depending on the object's distance.
- A **convex mirror** always forms an erect, diminished image.
- **Laws of reflection:** The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, and the incident ray, normal, and reflected ray all lie in the same plane.
- A **concave mirror converges** light; a **convex mirror diverges** it.
- A **convex lens** is thicker in the middle and converges light. A **concave lens** is thicker at the edges and diverges light.
- A **convex lens** can form enlarged or diminished, erect or inverted images. A **concave lens** always forms an erect, diminished image.
- Lenses are used in eyeglasses, cameras, telescopes, microscopes, and our own eyes.
