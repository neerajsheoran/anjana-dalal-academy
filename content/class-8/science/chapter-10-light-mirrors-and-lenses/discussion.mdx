## Spherical Mirrors

**Kiran:** Papa, when I look at the inside of a steel spoon, I see my face enlarged and upside down. But the outside of the spoon shows a small, right-side-up image. Why?

**Ravi:** The inside of the spoon acts like a concave mirror — it curves inward. The outside acts like a convex mirror — it curves outward. Each type handles light differently, creating different kinds of images.

**Kiran:** A concave mirror can show an enlarged erect image when the object is close, or an inverted image when the object is far. A convex mirror always shows a smaller, erect image.

**Ravi:** That is precisely why convex mirrors are used as side-view mirrors on cars and two-wheelers. They provide a wide field of view, so you can see more of the road behind you. The warning "Objects in mirror are closer than they appear" is printed on every convex side-view mirror because the image makes things look smaller and farther.

**Kiran:** Concave mirrors are used in torches and car headlights. How do they create a beam of light?

**Ravi:** A bulb placed at the focus of a concave mirror produces parallel reflected rays — a concentrated beam. This is why torches can illuminate objects far away. The same principle is used in solar concentrators that focus sunlight to generate heat for cooking or electricity.

## Laws of Reflection

**Kiran:** The two laws of reflection — angle of incidence equals angle of reflection, and the incident ray, normal, and reflected ray all lie in the same plane — apply to all mirrors, not just plane mirrors?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. These laws are universal. On a curved mirror, you draw the normal at the exact point where the light hits. Since a curved surface is locally flat at any point, the laws apply at that specific point. This is why light hitting different parts of a concave mirror converges — each point follows the law, but the normals point in different directions.

**Kiran:** So a concave mirror converges parallel light to a point, and a convex mirror diverges it. That is why concave mirrors can burn paper by concentrating sunlight.

**Ravi:** Solar cookers designed at IIT Bombay and other Indian institutions use large concave reflectors to concentrate sunlight for cooking. In Rajasthan, solar concentrators are used in the world's largest solar kitchen at the Brahma Kumaris headquarters in Mount Abu, which serves thousands of meals daily using only solar energy.

**Kiran:** That is amazing — using the physics of reflection to solve a practical problem.

**Ravi:** And dental mirrors are small concave mirrors that give dentists an enlarged view of teeth. The same physics serves both cooking and dentistry.

## Lenses and Refraction

**Kiran:** Lenses are different from mirrors — light passes through them instead of bouncing off. A convex lens is thick in the middle and converges light. A concave lens is thick at the edges and diverges light.

**Ravi:** The convex lens is the more important one in daily life. Your eye contains a convex lens that focuses light onto the retina to form images. A magnifying glass is a convex lens. Camera lenses in your phone are combinations of convex and concave lenses designed to produce sharp images.

**Kiran:** A drop of water on a glass surface acts like a tiny convex lens — text underneath appears enlarged. That is such a simple demonstration!

**Ravi:** And it shows that lensing is a fundamental property of curved transparent surfaces, not just manufactured glass. Early microscopes used simple glass beads as lenses. Leeuwenhoek, who discovered bacteria, used tiny hand-ground lenses that were essentially glass spheres.

**Kiran:** The textbook says convex lenses can burn paper too, just like concave mirrors. Both work by concentrating sunlight.

**Ravi:** The principle is focusing energy. Whether by reflection (concave mirror) or refraction (convex lens), concentrating parallel sunlight onto a small area raises the temperature enough to ignite paper. This is a dramatic demonstration of energy conservation.

## Lenses in Vision Correction

**Kiran:** Spectacles use convex or concave lenses to correct vision. How does that work?

**Ravi:** If someone is farsighted (can see far but not near), their eye's lens is too weak — it cannot bend light enough to focus on nearby objects. A convex lens in their glasses provides extra bending power. If someone is nearsighted (can see near but not far), their eye's lens is too strong. A concave lens diverges the light slightly before it enters the eye, correcting the over-convergence.

**Kiran:** India has a huge vision correction challenge — millions of people need glasses but do not have access to eye care.

**Ravi:** Organisations like LV Prasad Eye Institute in Hyderabad and Aravind Eye Hospital in Madurai have pioneered low-cost eye care, performing millions of surgeries and distributing affordable glasses. Understanding optics is literally helping people see.

**Kiran:** Some reading glasses have a visible line across the lens — bifocals. The upper part for distance and lower for reading.

**Ravi:** Benjamin Franklin invented bifocals because he was tired of switching between two pairs of glasses. Modern progressive lenses provide a smooth transition with no visible line. The physics is the same — different lens powers for different viewing distances — but the manufacturing precision is remarkable.

## The Eye as an Optical Instrument

**Kiran:** Our eye has a convex lens that can change its shape to focus on objects at different distances. That is more flexible than any camera lens!

**Ravi:** This ability is called accommodation. Muscles around the lens can make it thicker (for near objects) or thinner (for distant objects). A camera lens must physically move forward and back, but your eye lens changes its curvature. It is a marvel of biological engineering.

**Kiran:** Telescopes use large mirrors or lenses to gather light from distant stars. India has the Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope near Pune — does that use mirrors or lenses?

**Ravi:** The GMRT uses large dish antennas to collect radio waves, not visible light. But the principle is the same as a concave mirror — the parabolic dish shape focuses radio waves to a point where the receiver is placed. India's AstroSat satellite uses optical telescopes in space to observe stars and galaxies without atmospheric distortion.

**Kiran:** So mirrors and lenses — in our eyes, in spectacles, in telescopes, in satellites — are all applications of the same basic principles of reflection and refraction.

**Ravi:** That is the power of understanding fundamentals. Master the laws of reflection and the behaviour of converging and diverging lenses, and you can understand everything from a magnifying glass to a space telescope.
