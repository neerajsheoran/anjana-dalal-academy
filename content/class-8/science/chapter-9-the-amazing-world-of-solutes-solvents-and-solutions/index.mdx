## What Are Solute, Solvent, and Solution?

Have you ever taken an **Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)** when you were unwell? Every sip of ORS tastes the same -- it does not taste salty in one sip and sweet in another. This is because when sugar and salt are dissolved in water, they form a **uniform mixture** where the components are evenly distributed throughout.

A uniform mixture is called a **solution**. When a solid is mixed with a liquid to form a solution:
- The **solid component** is called the **solute** (the substance that dissolves)
- The **liquid component** is called the **solvent** (the substance that does the dissolving)

When two liquids are mixed, the substance present in **smaller amount** is the solute and the one in **larger amount** is the solvent.

> **Key idea:** Air is a gaseous solution! Since nitrogen is present in the largest amount, it is the solvent. Oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases are the solutes.

### A Fun Fact

The **chashni** (sugar syrup) of gulab jamun contains a large amount of sugar dissolved in a small amount of water. Even though there is more sugar than water, the water is still considered the solvent and sugar the solute!

## How Much Solute Can a Solvent Dissolve?

If you keep adding salt to a glass of water and stir, initially the salt dissolves completely. But after adding several spoons, a stage comes when the salt **stops dissolving** and settles at the bottom. This means the water has reached its limit.

### Saturated and Unsaturated Solutions

- **Unsaturated solution** -- A solution in which **more solute can still be dissolved** at a given temperature
- **Saturated solution** -- A solution in which the **maximum amount of solute has been dissolved** and no more can dissolve at that temperature

### Concentration

The **amount of solute** present in a fixed quantity of solution is called its **concentration**.

- **Dilute solution** -- contains less solute
- **Concentrated solution** -- contains more solute

These are relative terms. A solution with 2 spoons of salt in 100 mL of water is dilute compared to one with 4 spoons in 50 mL of water.

### Solubility

The **maximum amount of solute** that dissolves in a fixed quantity of solvent at a particular temperature is called **solubility**.

---

## How Does Temperature Affect Solubility?

### Solubility of Solids in Liquids

For most substances, **solubility increases with temperature**. If you dissolve baking soda in water:
- At 20 degrees C, only a small amount dissolves
- At 50 degrees C, more baking soda dissolves
- At 70 degrees C, even more dissolves

A saturated solution at a lower temperature can become **unsaturated** at a higher temperature because it can now hold more solute.

### Solubility of Gases in Liquids

The opposite is true for gases: the **solubility of gases generally decreases as temperature increases**.

- More **oxygen** dissolves in cold water, which is good for aquatic life
- When water warms up, less oxygen dissolves, which can be harmful for fish

> **Key idea:** Cold water holds more dissolved oxygen than warm water. This is why aquatic organisms often thrive better in cooler waters.

## Why Do Objects Float or Sink?

While washing rice, husk particles float on water while rice sinks. If you add oil to water, the oil floats. Some objects float because they are **less dense** than water, and some sink because they are **denser** than water.

## What Is Density?

Imagine a crowded bus where many people are packed together -- that is like high density. The same bus with only a few people has low density. Similarly, a **dense forest** has trees growing close together, while a less dense forest has trees far apart.

**Density** is defined as the mass present in a unit volume of a substance:

**Density = Mass / Volume**

![Comparison of equal volumes of three materials with different densities, showing the heavier material sinks and lighter one floats in water](density-float-sink-comparison.png)

### Units of Density

- **SI unit:** kilogram per cubic metre (kg/m3)
- Other commonly used units: **g/mL** or **g/cm3**

### Key Facts About Density

- The density of a substance is **independent of its shape or size** -- a ball of iron and a flat sheet of iron have the same density
- Density depends on **temperature** and **pressure**
- The mass of 1 mL of water is approximately 1 g at room temperature

### Relative Density

**Relative density** of a substance is its density compared to the density of water. It is a number without units.

For example, if aluminium has a density of 2.7 g/cm3 and water has a density of 1 g/cm3, the relative density of aluminium is 2.7. This means aluminium is 2.7 times denser than water.

## How to Measure Mass and Volume

### Measuring Mass

Mass is measured using a **balance** (like a digital weighing balance). Steps:
1. Switch ON the digital balance
2. Make sure it shows zero (press tare/reset if needed)
3. Place a watch glass on the pan and press tare to reset to zero
4. Place the object on the watch glass
5. Read the mass displayed

### Measuring Volume of Liquids

Volume of liquids is measured using a **measuring cylinder** -- a narrow, transparent cylindrical container with markings.

**Important tips:**
- The liquid surface curves inside the cylinder, forming a **meniscus**
- For colourless liquids (like water), read the volume at the **bottom** of the meniscus
- For coloured liquids, read at the **top** of the meniscus
- Keep your eyes at the **same level** as the meniscus for an accurate reading

### Measuring Volume of Regular Solids

For objects with regular shapes (like a cuboid):

**Volume = Length x Width x Height**

### Measuring Volume of Irregular Solids

For objects with irregular shapes (like a stone), use the **water displacement method**:
1. Fill a measuring cylinder with water to a known level (e.g., 50 mL)
2. Gently lower the object into the water
3. Note the new water level (e.g., 55 mL)
4. Volume of the object = Final level - Initial level = 55 - 50 = 5 cm3

## Effect of Temperature on Density

Generally, the density of a substance **decreases with heating** and **increases with cooling**.

When temperature increases:
- Particles move away from each other and spread out
- Volume increases, but mass stays the same
- Since Density = Mass / Volume, density decreases

This explains:
- Why **hot air rises** (it is less dense than cool air)
- How **hot air balloons** work -- heating the air inside the balloon makes it less dense, causing it to float upward

### Why Does Ice Float on Water?

Water has a special property -- its density is **highest at 4 degrees C**. When water cools further and turns into ice at 0 degrees C, the particles arrange themselves in a way that takes up **more space**. This means ice has a **lower density** than liquid water.

This is critically important for aquatic life: ice forms a layer on top of lakes, keeping the water underneath warm enough for fish and other creatures to survive during winter.

---

## Effect of Pressure on Density

- **Gases:** Increasing pressure pushes particles closer together, decreasing volume and **increasing density**
- **Liquids:** Pressure has only a small effect (liquids are nearly incompressible)
- **Solids:** Pressure has negligible effect (particles are already very close)

---

## Layers of the Earth

Our planet Earth is composed of several layers, each with a different density:
- **Crust** -- the lightest outermost layer
- **Upper mantle**
- **Lower mantle**
- **Outer core** (liquid)
- **Inner core** (solid) -- the densest layer

As you move deeper into the Earth, both pressure and temperature rise, making materials heavier and more compact.

## Key Takeaways

- A **solution** is a uniform mixture. The **solute** dissolves in the **solvent**.
- A **saturated solution** contains the maximum amount of solute at a given temperature. An **unsaturated solution** can dissolve more.
- **Solubility** is the maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a fixed quantity of solvent at a given temperature.
- For most solids, solubility **increases** with temperature. For gases, solubility **decreases** with temperature.
- **Density = Mass / Volume**. Its SI unit is kg/m3.
- Density is independent of shape or size but depends on temperature and pressure.
- Objects **float** when they are less dense than the liquid; they **sink** when they are denser.
- Generally, density **decreases** with increasing temperature (ice floating on water is a notable exception to the general trend of solids being denser than their liquids).
