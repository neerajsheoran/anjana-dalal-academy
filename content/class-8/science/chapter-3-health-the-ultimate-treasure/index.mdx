## Health: Is It More Than Not Falling Sick?

Is being healthy just about not having diseases? The answer is no. Health also includes feeling good physically, staying positive, and having strong relationships with people around you.

As per the **World Health Organization (WHO)**, health is defined as a "state of complete **physical**, **mental**, and **social** well-being, and not merely the absence of disease."

A healthy person:
- Can perform various tasks efficiently
- Copes well in different and difficult situations
- Adjusts well with friends, family, and other members of society

> **Key idea:** Health has three aspects -- physical health (your body), mental health (your mind and emotions), and social health (your relationships). All three are equally important.

### Ayurveda and Traditional Wisdom

Indian traditional medicine system **Ayurveda** teaches that true health is a balance of body, mind, and surroundings. Following **dinacharya** (daily routine) and **ritucharya** (seasonal routine) helps maintain this balance. Eating fresh, wholesome food suited to one's **prakriti** (body constitution), along with regular exercise, cleanliness, restful sleep, and a calm mind supports overall well-being.

## How Can We Stay Healthy?

Staying healthy means taking care of both your body and mind. Here are the key areas:

### Maintain a Healthy Lifestyle

**Good habits to follow:**
- Eat a **balanced diet** with plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Avoid processed, fatty, or sugary food and drinks
- Stay **physically active** by playing outdoors, walking, running, cycling, or exercising
- **Limit screen time** and spend more time in nature
- Get **enough sleep** to help your body and mind rest and recover
- Practise **yoga** or simple breathing exercises like **pranayama** regularly
- Say "NO" to harmful substances like tobacco, alcohol, and addictive drugs

**Habits to avoid:**
- Spending too much time on mobile phones or other digital screens
- Eating fast food and junk food every day
- Sleeping very late or not getting enough sleep
- Skipping meals, especially breakfast

### Keep the Environment Clean

A clean environment is essential for good health:
- Clean air and water are important for our body
- Air pollution from vehicles and factories can cause coughing, asthma, and other problems
- The **Air Quality Index (AQI)** helps us know how clean the air around us is
- A cleaner environment helps us stay healthy and feel better

> **Key idea:** Our health depends on two main factors -- our lifestyle (how we live) and our environment (our surroundings). Both must be taken care of.

## How Do We Know That We Are Unwell?

When we feel unwell, it means something inside us may not be working as it should. Our body gives us signals in two ways:

- **Symptoms** -- What we feel (like pain, tiredness, or dizziness). These are subjective and only the person experiencing them can describe them.
- **Signs** -- What can be seen or measured (like fever, rash, high blood pressure, or swelling). A doctor can observe and measure these.

Together, symptoms and signs help doctors understand what might be making us unwell.

## Diseases: What Are the Causes and Types?

A **disease** is a condition that affects the normal working of the body or mind. It can happen when one or more organs or organ systems stop functioning properly.

Diseases can be grouped into two major types:

### 1. Communicable Diseases

These are diseases caused by **pathogens** (disease-causing organisms like bacteria, viruses, fungi, worms, or protozoa). They can **spread from one person to another**.

**How do communicable diseases spread?**
- **Through air** -- When an infected person coughs or sneezes (e.g., common cold, TB, chickenpox)
- **Through contaminated food and water** -- Drinking dirty water or eating infected food (e.g., cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A)
- **Through direct contact** -- Shaking hands, sharing personal items (e.g., skin infections)
- **Through vectors** -- Insects like mosquitoes and houseflies that carry pathogens (e.g., malaria, dengue)

### 2. Non-Communicable Diseases

These diseases are **not caused by pathogens** and **do not spread** from one person to another. They are usually linked to lifestyle, diet, and/or environment.

Examples: cancer, diabetes, asthma, heart disease, obesity, high blood pressure.

> **Key idea:** In recent years, non-communicable diseases (NCDs) like diabetes, heart disease, and cancer have become more common in India. Most deaths in India are now caused by NCDs, largely due to changes in diet, less exercise, and longer life spans.

## Common Communicable Diseases

### Diseases Spread Through Air

| Disease | Caused By | Symptoms | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Common Cold and Flu** | Virus | Nasal congestion, sore throat, fever, cough, body ache | Wash hands frequently, do not share personal items, cover mouth and nose |
| **Chickenpox** | Virus | Mild fever, itchy skin, rashes, blisters | Isolation of patient, vaccination |
| **Measles** | Virus | Fever, sore throat, reddish rashes on skin | Isolation of patient, good hygiene, vaccination |
| **Tuberculosis (TB)** | Bacteria | Cough, fever, fatigue, loss of appetite, night sweats | Avoid close contact with TB patients, good hygiene, vaccination |

### Diseases Spread Through Contaminated Water and Food

| Disease | Caused By | Symptoms | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Hepatitis A** | Virus | Fatigue, fever, loss of appetite, jaundice | Drinking boiled water, vaccination |
| **Cholera** | Bacteria | Diarrhoea and dehydration | Personal hygiene, properly cooked food, boiled water, vaccination |
| **Typhoid** | Bacteria | Headache, abdominal discomfort, fever, diarrhoea | Personal hygiene, properly cooked food, boiled water, vaccination |
| **Ascariasis** | Worms (roundworms) | Worms in stool, loss of appetite, weight loss, anaemia | Personal hygiene, properly cooked food, boiled water |

### Diseases Transmitted by Insects (Vectors)

| Disease | Caused By | Vector | Symptoms | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|--------|----------|------------|
| **Malaria** | Protozoa | Mosquito | High fever, sweating, periodic chills | Mosquito nets, repellents, long-sleeved clothes, prevent mosquito breeding |
| **Dengue** | Virus | Mosquito | Fever, headache, muscle and joint pain, nausea | Mosquito nets, repellents, avoid areas with still water |

### Simple Precautions to Prevent Communicable Diseases

1. Keep yourself and your surroundings clean
2. Practise basic hygiene every day
3. Wash hands with soap and water regularly
4. Cover your mouth and nose while coughing or sneezing
5. Wear a mask in crowded places
6. Do not share personal items like towels and handkerchiefs
7. Keep your home, food, and water clean
8. Stay at home and rest when you are unwell

## Non-Communicable Diseases

### Deficiency Diseases

These are caused by a lack of specific nutrients in the diet:
- **Scurvy** -- caused by lack of Vitamin C
- **Anaemia** -- caused by lack of iron
- **Goitre** -- caused by lack of iodine

### Lifestyle-Related Diseases

These often develop due to unhealthy habits:

- **Diabetes** -- India has one of the highest numbers of people with diabetes in the world. It develops due to hormonal imbalances, unhealthy eating, lack of physical activity, and being overweight.
- **Obesity** -- Caused by overeating and lack of exercise
- **High blood pressure** -- Linked to stress, poor diet, and lack of activity
- **Heart disease** -- Connected to poor diet, smoking, and sedentary lifestyle

> **Key idea:** Many non-communicable diseases can be prevented with healthy habits, regular exercise, and a balanced diet. Prevention is always better than cure.

## How to Prevent and Control Diseases

### The Immune System

Our body has a special system called the **immune system** that helps fight against diseases. The natural ability of our body to fight diseases is known as **immunity**.

Some people get sick more frequently than others, even when living in similar environments. This is because their immune system may be weaker.

### Vaccines: Training the Immune System

A **vaccine** helps our body fight certain diseases by training the immune system to recognise and attack harmful germs. This provides what is known as **acquired immunity** -- protection developed after exposure to a pathogen or a vaccine.

**How vaccines work:**
1. A vaccine contains dead, weakened, or harmless parts of a germ
2. When injected, it does not cause the disease
3. But it trains the immune system to recognise and fight that germ
4. If the real germ enters the body later, the immune system is ready to fight it quickly

**Vaccines are preventive, not curative** -- they help prevent diseases before they happen, but do not treat them once someone is already sick.

### The Story of the First Vaccine

**Smallpox** was a deadly disease that killed millions. In the late 1700s, English doctor **Edward Jenner** observed that milkmaids who had caught **cowpox** (a mild disease from cows) did not get smallpox. He tested this by injecting cowpox material into a boy, who later showed no illness when exposed to smallpox. This led to the invention of the first **vaccine**, and eventually smallpox was **completely eradicated** from the world by 1979.

### India's Tradition of Variolation

Long before modern vaccines, India had a traditional method called **variolation** to protect against smallpox. It involved using material from a smallpox sore to scratch the skin and create a mild infection that built immunity. People who performed this practice were known as **teekedaars**.

### India's Role in Vaccine Production

India is one of the world's largest vaccine producers. Indian vaccine companies played a key role during the **COVID-19 pandemic** and continue to support global health efforts.

## Treatment of Diseases

### Antibiotics

When our immune system fails to protect us against a bacterial infection, doctors may give medicines called **antibiotics**. These medicines kill the bacteria causing the disease.

**Important facts about antibiotics:**
- Antibiotics work **only against bacterial infections**
- They target parts of bacterial cells that are different from human cells
- They **do NOT work** against viruses, fungi, or protozoa
- You should never take antibiotics without a doctor's prescription

### The Discovery of Penicillin

**Penicillin** was the first antibiotic, discovered in 1928 by **Alexander Fleming**. While studying bacteria, he noticed that a mould on a discarded petri dish stopped bacteria from growing. The mould was releasing a substance that killed bacteria. This chance discovery led to the development of penicillin.

### The Problem of Antibiotic Resistance

**Antibiotic resistance** is a serious and growing problem. It happens when bacteria that were earlier killed by a given antibiotic learn to survive and multiply despite treatment.

**How does antibiotic resistance develop?**
1. Taking antibiotics when they are not required
2. Not completing the full course of antibiotics
3. Using antibiotics on animals unnecessarily

**How does it spread?**
- Resistant bacteria can spread from person to person
- Through contaminated food and water
- Through soil and animal products

> **Key idea:** To tackle antibiotic resistance, we must use antibiotics wisely -- only when prescribed by a doctor, in the correct dose, and for the right duration. Avoiding unnecessary use helps keep antibiotics effective for future generations.

### Traditional Medicine Systems

India has several traditional medicine systems:
- **Ayurveda**
- **Siddha**
- **Unani**

These systems use natural substances like herbs, oils, and minerals to manage illnesses and promote recovery. They focus on a healthy lifestyle and balanced diet. While useful for everyday well-being and some conditions, they may not be effective for all diseases at all stages.

## Key Takeaways

- **Health** means complete physical, mental, and social well-being -- not just the absence of disease.
- A **disease** affects the normal working of the body or mind.
- **Symptoms** are what we feel (like pain); **signs** are what can be seen or measured (like fever).
- **Communicable diseases** are caused by pathogens and can spread from person to person.
- **Non-communicable diseases** like diabetes and heart disease are linked to lifestyle and environment. They cannot spread from person to person.
- Our **immune system** helps protect us from harmful pathogens.
- **Vaccines** train the immune system to prevent diseases. They are preventive, not curative.
- **Antibiotics** work only against bacterial infections, not viral infections.
- **Antibiotic resistance** is a growing problem caused by misuse of antibiotics.
- Maintaining good hygiene, eating a balanced diet, exercising regularly, getting enough sleep, and staying positive are the keys to good health.
