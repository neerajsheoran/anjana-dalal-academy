## Ecosystems and Their Components

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook says elephants entering farms in India happens because their forests are shrinking. So deforestation does not just affect trees — it creates a chain reaction?

**Ravi:** Exactly. A habitat has biotic components (living things) and abiotic components (non-living things like air, water, soil, sunlight). When forests are cut, elephants lose their food sources and water holes. They move into villages, creating human-wildlife conflict. This is a cascade of consequences from disturbing one part of the ecosystem.

**Kiran:** An ecosystem is the interaction between biotic and abiotic components. So a pond, a forest, or even a single tree with all its organisms can be an ecosystem.

**Ravi:** Indian ecosystems are incredibly diverse. The Western Ghats, one of the world's biodiversity hotspots, contains thousands of species of plants, amphibians, and insects found nowhere else. The Sundarbans mangrove forest, the Thar Desert, the coral reefs of Lakshadweep — each is a unique ecosystem with its own web of interactions.

**Kiran:** The fish-dragonfly-pollination example shows how even organisms that seem unrelated are connected. Fish eating dragonfly larvae indirectly helps plant pollination.

**Ravi:** In ecology, these are called indirect effects. Removing one species can have unexpected consequences throughout the ecosystem. This is why conservation biologists say every species matters.

## Food Chains and Food Webs

**Kiran:** A food chain shows who eats whom — grass is eaten by grasshoppers, which are eaten by frogs, then snakes, then eagles. Each step is a trophic level.

**Ravi:** But real ecosystems are more complex. A grasshopper might also be eaten by a bird. The frog might eat other insects too. When you connect all these feeding relationships, you get a food web — which is a more accurate representation of how energy flows through an ecosystem.

**Kiran:** Producers — plants — are at the base. They convert solar energy into food through photosynthesis. Without them, no animal could survive.

**Ravi:** In India, agriculture is the foundation of food production. About 60 percent of India's population depends directly on farming. Protecting our agricultural ecosystems — maintaining soil health, protecting pollinators, ensuring water availability — is essential for food security.

**Kiran:** Decomposers — bacteria and fungi — break down dead organisms and return nutrients to the soil. They complete the cycle. Without them, nutrients would be locked in dead matter forever.

**Ravi:** When your grandmother makes compost from kitchen waste, she is harnessing decomposers. The bacteria and fungi in the compost pit break down vegetable peels, tea leaves, and other organic waste into rich, dark humus that feeds plants. It is nature's recycling system in your own garden.

## Types of Interactions Between Organisms

**Kiran:** Beyond feeding, organisms interact in other ways — mutualism, commensalism, parasitism, and competition. Can you give Indian examples?

**Ravi:** Mutualism — where both benefit — is beautifully illustrated by honeybees and flowers. India is the world's eighth-largest honey producer. Bees get nectar while pollinating the flowers. Without bees, many crops including mustard, sunflower, and fruits would produce far fewer seeds.

**Kiran:** Commensalism — one benefits, the other is unaffected — like orchids growing on trees. The orchid gets support and sunlight, and the tree is neither helped nor harmed.

**Ravi:** Parasitism — one benefits while the other is harmed — includes ticks on dogs and the malarial parasite in mosquitoes and humans. India has made tremendous progress against malaria, reducing cases by over 80 percent since 2015, partly by understanding the parasite's life cycle.

**Kiran:** Competition for resources like food and water helps control population sizes. If one species grows too large, competition increases until the population stabilises.

**Ravi:** The ban on frog leg exports in the 1980s is a dramatic example of what happens when this balance is disrupted. Exporting frogs led to more insects, which required more pesticides, which harmed the environment further.

## Threats to Ecosystems

**Kiran:** Deforestation, pollution, overuse of resources, invasive species, monoculture — these all threaten ecosystems. India faces all of these challenges.

**Ravi:** India lost significant forest cover during British colonial rule and continued to lose forests post-independence due to development. But awareness is growing. India's forest cover has actually increased slightly in recent years, partly due to afforestation programmes and community forest management.

**Kiran:** The Green Revolution increased food production but overuse of chemical fertilisers and pesticides has degraded soil in Punjab and Haryana. The ancient text Vrikshayurveda mentions organic methods like Kunapa Jala.

**Ravi:** Many Indian farmers are returning to organic and natural farming methods. Sikkim became India's first fully organic state in 2016. States like Andhra Pradesh have launched large-scale natural farming programmes. These approaches maintain soil health, protect beneficial organisms, and produce nutritious food without the harmful side effects of synthetic chemicals.

**Kiran:** Protected areas like Jim Corbett National Park and Manas National Park help conserve wildlife. But are they enough?

**Ravi:** Protected areas cover about 5 percent of India's land area. While they are crucial for conserving endangered species like tigers, elephants, and one-horned rhinoceroses, true conservation requires protecting ecosystems beyond park boundaries — in farmlands, wetlands, and urban areas too.

## Balance in Ecosystems

**Kiran:** The textbook says ecosystem balance is dynamic — always adjusting, not fixed. What does that mean?

**Ravi:** Populations naturally fluctuate. If there are many rabbits, fox populations increase because there is plenty of food. But more foxes mean more rabbits are eaten, so the rabbit population decreases. Then foxes have less food and their population drops too. This creates natural cycles of rising and falling populations.

**Kiran:** So the balance is like a seesaw that keeps tilting back and forth but stays roughly centred?

**Ravi:** That is a good analogy. Problems arise when human activities push the seesaw so far that it cannot recover — like clearing an entire forest or polluting a river beyond its capacity to self-clean.

**Kiran:** The Sundarbans — the world's largest mangrove forest — protects the coast from storms and is home to the Bengal tiger. But it faces deforestation, pollution, and rising sea levels.

**Ravi:** The Sundarbans was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987. Protecting it is vital not just for biodiversity but for the millions of people living in coastal Bengal and Bangladesh who depend on it for storm protection, fishing, and clean water. Conservation is not just about nature — it is about human survival too.
