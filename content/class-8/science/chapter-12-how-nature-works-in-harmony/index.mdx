## How Do We Experience Our Surroundings?

In several parts of India, elephants often enter farms and villages when vegetation is scarce and waterholes dry up. This happens because cutting down trees and changes in rainfall shrink their natural habitat. When forests cannot support wildlife, animals move into human settlements. This chain of events shows how closely **nature's elements are connected**.

A **habitat** is a place where an organism lives. It could be a pond, a forest, a farm, or even the bark of a tree. Every habitat has two types of components:

- **Biotic components** -- the living beings (plants, animals, microorganisms)
- **Abiotic components** -- the non-living things (air, water, soil, sunlight, temperature)

Every organism needs specific conditions to survive. Different habitats offer different living conditions.

> **Key idea:** The biotic and abiotic components of a habitat constantly interact with each other. These interactions are essential for the survival of all organisms.

---

## Population and Community

### Population

A **population** is a group of the same type of organisms living together in a habitat at a given time. For example, all the fish of one kind in a pond form a population.

### Community

A **community** comprises different populations sharing the same habitat. The plants, animals, and microorganisms living together in a habitat form the community. These organisms interact with and depend on one another for survival.

---

## Does Every Organism in a Community Matter?

Yes! Every organism plays an important role. Here is an example:

- **Fish** in a pond eat **dragonfly larvae**
- Fewer dragonflies means more **bees and butterflies** survive
- More bees and butterflies means better **pollination** of nearby plants
- Better pollination means plants produce **more seeds and fruits**

So, the presence of fish in a pond indirectly helps plants produce more seeds! This shows how every organism in an ecosystem is connected.

> **Key idea:** Even small changes in one population can have cascading effects on the entire ecosystem.

## Ecosystems

The **interaction between biotic components and abiotic components** in an area forms an **ecosystem**.

### Types of Ecosystems

- **Terrestrial ecosystems** -- forests, grasslands, deserts, farmlands
- **Aquatic ecosystems** -- ponds, rivers, lakes, oceans

Ecosystems can be large (like a forest) or small (like a single tree with all the organisms living on it).

### Human-Made Ecosystems

Humans have created artificial ecosystems like fish ponds, farms, parks, and gardens. Unlike natural ecosystems, these need human care and management.

---

## Feeding Relationships

Organisms in an ecosystem can be classified based on how they get their food:

### Producers
**Plants** make their own food through photosynthesis using sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water. They are the **producers** of an ecosystem.

### Consumers
Animals that eat plants or other animals are called **consumers**:
- **Herbivores** -- eat only plants (e.g., deer, cow, rabbit)
- **Carnivores** -- eat only animals (e.g., tiger, eagle, snake)
- **Omnivores** -- eat both plants and animals (e.g., bear, crow, fox)

### Decomposers
**Bacteria** and **fungi** break down dead plants and animals into simpler substances. They return nutrients to the soil, making them available for plants to use again. They are nature's recyclers.

> **Key idea:** Without decomposers, dead organisms would pile up, and nutrients would never return to the soil. The ecosystem would collapse.

## Food Chains and Food Webs

### Food Chain

A **food chain** shows who eats whom in an ecosystem. Each step in a food chain is called a **trophic level**.

![Food chain showing grass, grasshopper, frog, snake, and eagle with arrows indicating energy flow](food-chain-example.png)

Example: **Grass** (producer) --> **Grasshopper** (herbivore) --> **Frog** (carnivore) --> **Snake** (carnivore) --> **Eagle** (top carnivore)

### Food Web

![Food web diagram showing interconnected food chains with multiple organisms linked by feeding relationships](food-web-ecosystem.png)

In reality, organisms eat many different things, and many organisms are eaten by different predators. When multiple food chains are interconnected, they form a **food web**. Food webs show the complex feeding relationships in an ecosystem.

---

## Types of Interactions Between Organisms

Besides feeding relationships, organisms interact in many other ways:

### Competition
Organisms compete for common resources like food, water, space, or sunlight. Competition helps **control population size** and keeps the ecosystem balanced.

### Mutualism
Both organisms **benefit** from the relationship.
- Example: **Honeybees and flowers** -- bees get nectar, flowers get pollinated

### Commensalism
One organism **benefits** while the other is **not affected**.
- Example: **Orchids growing on trees** -- the orchid gets support and nutrition, the tree is unaffected

### Parasitism
One organism **benefits** while the other is **harmed**.
- Example: **Ticks on dogs** -- the tick feeds on dog's blood, the dog gets skin irritation

---

## Balance in Ecosystems

An ecosystem stays in balance when interactions among organisms and their environment keep populations and resources stable. This balance is **dynamic** (always adjusting), not fixed.

### What Happens When Balance Is Disturbed?

In the 1980s, India exported large quantities of frog legs. This led to a decline in frog populations. Since frogs eat insects, fewer frogs meant a **rise in agricultural pests**. Farmers were forced to use more **synthetic pesticides**, which harmed the environment, soil, water quality, and human health. The Government of India eventually **banned** the export of frog legs.

## Benefits of Ecosystems

Ecosystems provide many benefits to humans:
- **Forests** provide fresh air, fertile soil, food, fibres, timber, and medicines
- **Aquatic ecosystems** provide water and food
- **Mangrove forests** (like the Sundarbans) protect coastlines from storms and floods
- Ecosystems absorb **carbon dioxide** and release **oxygen**
- They offer aesthetic and recreational value

### The Sundarbans

The **Sundarbans**, the world's largest mangrove forest, is located where the Ganges and Brahmaputra rivers meet. It is home to many endangered species and was declared a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** in 1987. However, it faces threats from deforestation, illegal hunting, and pollution.

---

## Threats to Ecosystems

- **Deforestation** -- cutting down forests
- **Pollution** -- industrial waste, sewage, and chemicals
- **Overuse of natural resources** -- overfishing, excessive groundwater extraction
- **Invasive species** -- non-native organisms that disrupt ecosystems
- **Monoculture** -- growing the same crop repeatedly, reducing biodiversity
- **Overuse of synthetic fertilisers and pesticides** -- harms soil health and beneficial organisms

### Protected Areas in India

India has many protected areas to conserve wildlife:
- **Jim Corbett National Park** (Uttarakhand)
- **Manas National Park** (Assam)
- **Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve** (Western Ghats)
- **Chilika Lake** (Odisha)
- **Hemis National Park** (Leh)
- **Keibul Lamjao National Park** (Manipur)

---

## Sustainable Farming

The **Green Revolution** (mid-20th century) increased food production in India through machines, synthetic fertilisers, and pesticides. However, overuse of these has led to:
- **Soil degradation** and loss of fertility
- Reduction in beneficial soil microorganisms
- Loss of **organic matter (humus)**
- Soil erosion
- Pest resistance to pesticides

To make farming more sustainable, many farmers are exploring **organic and natural farming methods** that reduce the use of synthetic chemicals and support natural ecosystems.

The ancient Indian text **Vrikshayurveda** emphasises soil health through organic manure like **Kunapa Jala** (a liquid fertiliser made from animal and plant waste through fermentation).

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Habitats** have biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components.
- The interaction between biotic and abiotic components forms an **ecosystem**.
- Organisms are classified as **producers** (plants), **consumers** (herbivores, carnivores, omnivores), and **decomposers** (bacteria, fungi).
- **Food chains** show who eats whom; **food webs** show interconnected food chains.
- Organisms interact through **competition**, **mutualism**, **commensalism**, and **parasitism**.
- Ecosystems provide essential benefits: clean air, water, food, medicines, and climate regulation.
- Human activities like pollution, deforestation, and overuse of resources threaten ecosystems.
- **Protected areas** like national parks and wildlife sanctuaries help conserve India's biodiversity.
