## What Are Mixtures?

Look around you -- the air you breathe, the food in your lunch box, the water in your bottle. Most things around us are not made of just one substance. They are made of **two or more substances mixed together**.

When two or more substances are mixed, where each substance **retains its own properties**, the result is called a **mixture**. The individual substances that make up a mixture are called its **components**. The components of a mixture do not react chemically with each other.

### Types of Mixtures

**Non-uniform mixtures:** The different components are generally visible with the naked eye or a magnifying glass. For example, a sprout salad where you can see green gram, chickpeas, onion, and tomato separately.

**Uniform mixtures:** The components are evenly distributed and cannot be distinguished, even with a microscope. For example, sugar dissolved in water looks the same throughout.

> **Key idea:** In a mixture, each substance keeps its own properties. The components do not react chemically with each other and can usually be separated by physical methods.

### Alloys: Special Uniform Mixtures

**Alloys** are uniform mixtures of metals (or metals with non-metals). Examples:

- **Stainless steel** -- iron, nickel, chromium, and a small amount of carbon
- **Brass** -- copper and zinc
- **Bronze** -- copper and tin

In ancient India, alloys were called **Mishraloha**. Ancient texts like the *Charaka Samhita* and *Susruta Samhita* mention the use of alloys for medicinal purposes.

## Is Air a Mixture?

Yes! Air is a **uniform mixture** of mainly:
- **Nitrogen** (about 78%)
- **Oxygen** (needed for breathing and combustion)
- **Argon**
- **Carbon dioxide**
- **Water vapour**

### Confirming Carbon Dioxide in Air

When lime water (a solution of calcium hydroxide) is left exposed to air, it turns milky. This happens because carbon dioxide in the air reacts with calcium hydroxide to form tiny white particles of **calcium carbonate**. This confirms that air contains carbon dioxide.

### Dust in Air

If you place a black sheet of paper near an open window for a few hours, you will find tiny particles settled on it. These **dust particles** are suspended in the air but are not an integral part of it -- they are considered **pollutants**.

## Types of Mixtures by Physical State

Mixtures can be formed from components in different physical states:

| Mixture Type | Examples |
|-------------|----------|
| Gas + Gas | Air |
| Gas + Liquid | Soda water, oxygen dissolved in water |
| Solid + Gas | Carbon particles in air |
| Liquid + Liquid | Vinegar (acetic acid in water), oil and water |
| Solid + Liquid | Seawater, sand and water |
| Solid + Solid | Baking powder, alloys |

## What Are Pure Substances?

In everyday language, "pure" means unadulterated (not mixed with cheaper or poor-quality substances). But in **science**, a pure substance has a very specific meaning.

A **pure substance** is a kind of matter that:
- Consists of the **same type of particles** throughout
- **Cannot be separated** into other kinds of matter by any physical process

Even products labelled "pure" in shops (like milk or ghee) may not be pure in the scientific sense, because they contain more than one substance.

## Types of Pure Substances

![Flowchart classifying matter into mixtures and pure substances, with pure substances further divided into elements and compounds](matter-classification-flowchart.png)

Pure substances can be divided into two types: **elements** and **compounds**.

### Elements

**Elements** are substances that **cannot be further broken down** into simpler substances. They are the building blocks of all matter.

Each element is made up of identical particles called **atoms**. The atoms of one element are different from the atoms of any other element.

**Examples of elements:**
- **Metals:** Gold, silver, iron, aluminium, copper, magnesium
- **Non-metals:** Carbon, sulfur, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen
- **Metalloids:** Silicon, boron (have properties between metals and non-metals)

**Interesting facts about elements:**
- There are **118 known elements** at present
- Most exist in a solid state
- **11 elements** are gaseous at room temperature (all non-metals)
- Only **2 elements** are liquid at room temperature -- mercury (a metal) and bromine (a non-metal)

> **Key idea:** The atoms of most elements cannot exist independently. Two or more atoms combine to form a stable particle called a **molecule**. For example, two oxygen atoms combine to form one molecule of oxygen.

### Compounds

**Compounds** are substances formed when two or more elements combine **chemically** in a **fixed ratio**. The properties of a compound are **completely different** from the properties of the elements that form it.

**Key properties of compounds:**
- Made of two or more elements combined in a fixed ratio
- Have properties different from their constituent elements
- Cannot be separated into their elements by physical methods
- Can only be broken down by chemical reactions

**Examples:**
- **Water** -- made of hydrogen and oxygen (ratio 2:1). Hydrogen is a fuel, oxygen supports combustion, but water extinguishes fire!
- **Sodium chloride (common salt)** -- made of sodium (a soft, reactive metal) and chlorine (a hazardous gas), combined in a 1:1 ratio
- **Sugar** -- made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen

### Breaking Down Water

![Electrolysis of water apparatus showing hydrogen gas collecting at one electrode and oxygen gas at the other](electrolysis-of-water.png)

When electricity is passed through water (with a few drops of dilute sulfuric acid), it breaks down into two gases:
- **Hydrogen** -- burns with a "pop" sound
- **Oxygen** -- makes a burning candle glow brighter

The volume of hydrogen collected is **twice** the volume of oxygen, confirming that water has hydrogen and oxygen in a 2:1 ratio.

### Breaking Down Sugar

When sugar is heated:
1. It first turns **brown**
2. Then it turns **black** (chars)
3. Water droplets appear inside the tube
4. **Charcoal (carbon)** is left behind

This shows that sugar is a compound made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen.

## Iron and Sulfur: Mixture vs Compound

This is a classic experiment that shows the difference between a mixture and a compound.

### Sample A: Mixture of Iron and Sulfur
- Iron filings and sulfur powder are simply mixed
- Both substances can be seen separately
- Iron can be separated using a **magnet**
- Adding dilute hydrochloric acid produces hydrogen gas (colourless, odourless, burns with a pop sound)

### Sample B: Iron Sulfide (Compound)
- The mixture is heated until a black mass forms
- The black mass looks the **same throughout** (uniform)
- It is **not attracted** by a magnet
- Adding dilute hydrochloric acid produces **hydrogen sulfide** gas (colourless, smells like rotten eggs)

> **Key idea:** In a mixture, each component retains its own properties and can be separated physically. In a compound, the elements combine chemically to form a new substance with entirely different properties.

## Uses of Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

- **Air** (mixture) -- we breathe it every day
- **Water** (compound) -- essential for all life
- **Iron and aluminium** (elements) -- used in construction of buildings, bridges, and vehicles
- **Stainless steel** (mixture/alloy) -- stronger and more durable than pure iron
- **Medicines and vaccines** -- chemists study how elements combine to create life-saving compounds
- **Fertilisers** -- compounds that enhance crop production

## What Are Minerals?

**Minerals** are natural, solid substances found on Earth with a fixed chemical composition. Most rocks are a mixture of minerals.

- **Native minerals** are pure elements found in nature (e.g., gold, silver, sulfur, carbon)
- Most minerals are **compounds** made of more than one element

**Common minerals:** Quartz, calcite, mica, pyroxene, olivine, talc

**Uses of minerals:**
- **Cement** is made from calcite, quartz, alumina, and iron oxide
- **Talcum powder** is made from the mineral talc

### Indian Art and Metals

The **Dhokra art** from Bihar and Odisha uses brass or bronze to create beautiful figures. A beeswax model is covered with clay, the wax is melted out, and the hollow space is filled with molten metal. This ancient craft reflects tribal creativity and tradition.

## What Is NOT Matter?

Elements and compounds are the building blocks of **matter** -- everything that has mass and takes up space. However, not everything around us is matter. **Light, heat, electricity, thoughts, and emotions** are important parts of our world but are not made of matter.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A **mixture** consists of two or more substances mixed together. The components retain their individual properties and can be separated by physical methods.
- A **pure substance** consists of the same type of particles throughout.
- **Elements** are the simplest substances that cannot be broken down further. They are the building blocks of all matter.
- **Compounds** are formed when two or more elements combine chemically in a fixed ratio. Their properties are different from those of their constituent elements.
- **Alloys** are uniform mixtures of metals (e.g., stainless steel, brass, bronze).
- **Air** is a uniform mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapour.
- **Minerals** are natural, solid substances found on Earth with a fixed chemical composition. They are usually compounds but can also be pure elements.
