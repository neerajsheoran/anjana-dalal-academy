## The Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

**Kiran:** Papa, Oersted discovered that a current-carrying wire deflects a compass needle. So electricity and magnetism are connected? They seem like completely different things.

**Ravi:** That was the surprise of the century — literally. In 1820, Oersted was giving a demonstration and noticed a compass needle moving when he switched a circuit on and off. Before this, everyone thought electricity and magnetism were separate forces. His discovery opened an entirely new field — electromagnetism.

**Kiran:** So when current flows through a wire, it creates a magnetic field around the wire. And when the current stops, the magnetic field disappears.

**Ravi:** Exactly. This is different from a permanent magnet, which always has a magnetic field. The beauty of an electromagnet is that you can control it — turn it on and off, make it stronger or weaker, even reverse the poles.

**Kiran:** You make it stronger by increasing the current or the number of coil turns. And putting an iron core inside the coil makes it much stronger. That is why electromagnets use iron nails.

**Ravi:** Lifting electromagnets in scrapyards can pick up tonnes of steel when switched on, then release it by switching off. Indian Steel Authority of India Limited uses these in their facilities. The control aspect makes electromagnets incredibly useful.

## Applications of Electromagnets

**Kiran:** Electric bells, motors, loudspeakers — they all use electromagnets. How does a motor work?

**Ravi:** A motor uses the interaction between a permanent magnet and an electromagnet. When current flows through a coil placed in a magnetic field, a force acts on the coil and makes it rotate. This is the principle behind every fan, mixer, washing machine, and electric vehicle motor.

**Kiran:** India is pushing for electric vehicles. The motors in EVs use the same electromagnetic principle?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Companies like Tata Motors and Ola Electric are building vehicles with electric motors that are more efficient than combustion engines. The physics you are learning right now is the foundation of this technology revolution.

**Kiran:** And Earth itself is a giant magnet because of electric currents in its liquid iron core. Migratory birds use this magnetic field to navigate — like Siberian cranes that fly to Bharatpur in Rajasthan every winter.

**Ravi:** The bar-tailed godwit flies non-stop from Alaska to New Zealand — over 11,000 km — using Earth's magnetic field for navigation. It is remarkable that both human technology and animal biology exploit the same physical phenomenon.

## The Heating Effect of Electric Current

**Kiran:** When current flows through a wire, the wire heats up. That is why electric heaters, kettles, and irons work. But why does current cause heating?

**Ravi:** When electrons flow through a conductor, they collide with the atoms of the material. These collisions transfer energy to the atoms, making them vibrate faster — which is heat. Materials that resist current flow more — like nichrome — produce more heat because there are more collisions.

**Kiran:** So resistance determines how much heat is produced. Nichrome has high resistance, which is why it is used in heating elements. Copper has low resistance, which is why it is used in wiring.

**Ravi:** Precisely. You want heat in a heater but not in the wires leading to it. Using the right material for the right purpose is engineering design based on physics.

**Kiran:** The textbook warns about safety — overheating can melt plastic and cause fires. Is that why we have fuses and circuit breakers?

**Ravi:** Yes. A fuse is a thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit if too much current flows, preventing overheating of the main wires. Modern homes use MCBs — miniature circuit breakers — that trip automatically. The Bureau of Indian Standards specifies safety ratings for all electrical appliances sold in India.

## How Batteries Work

**Kiran:** The Voltaic cell uses two different metals and an electrolyte to produce electricity through a chemical reaction. Is that how my phone battery works too?

**Ravi:** The principle is the same — chemical energy is converted to electrical energy. But modern lithium-ion batteries are far more advanced than Volta's original design. They can store more energy, be recharged hundreds of times, and are compact enough for phones and laptops.

**Kiran:** The lemon battery experiment is fun — you can power an LED using lemons, copper wires, and iron nails. The lemon juice is the electrolyte!

**Ravi:** That experiment demonstrates the fundamental principle. The chemical reaction between different metals in an acidic solution generates a voltage. Volta proved that electricity comes from metals and chemicals, not from animals as Galvani believed.

**Kiran:** Rechargeable batteries do not last forever. After many charge-discharge cycles, they degrade. My phone battery lasts much less now than when it was new.

**Ravi:** Lithium-ion batteries typically maintain good performance for 500 to 1000 charge cycles. After that, their capacity diminishes. Scientists are working on solid-state batteries that could last much longer, charge faster, and be safer. India has set up the National Mission on Transformative Mobility to advance battery technology.

## Responsible Battery Disposal

**Kiran:** The textbook says used batteries should never go in regular garbage. Why?

**Ravi:** Batteries contain toxic materials — lead, cadmium, lithium, and acids — that can leach into soil and groundwater, contaminating drinking water sources and harming ecosystems. India generates thousands of tonnes of e-waste annually, and improper disposal is a growing environmental concern.

**Kiran:** So we should take old batteries to e-waste recycling centres?

**Ravi:** Yes. Many electronics stores and municipal bodies have collection points. India's E-Waste Management Rules mandate that producers take back old batteries and electronics for proper recycling. As citizens, we should use these facilities.

**Kiran:** It connects to the ecosystem chapter — pollution from batteries can enter food chains and affect wildlife.

**Ravi:** Everything in science is connected. Physics gives us batteries, chemistry explains the reactions inside them, and biology shows us the consequences of irresponsible disposal. Being scientifically literate means seeing these connections and acting responsibly.
