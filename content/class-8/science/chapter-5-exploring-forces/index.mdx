## What Is a Force?

Think about all the ways you interact with objects every day -- pushing a door open, pulling a drawer, lifting your school bag, or kicking a football. In all these actions, you are applying a **push** or a **pull** on an object.

In science, the push or pull applied on an object is called a **force**.

The **SI unit of force** is **newton** (written with a small 'n') and its symbol is **N**.

> **Key idea:** A force is a push or pull on an object resulting from the object's interaction with another object. At least two objects must interact for a force to come into play.

---

## What Can a Force Do?

A force can produce many different effects on an object:

1. **Make a stationary object move** -- Pushing a box makes it slide across the floor
2. **Change the speed of a moving object** -- A fielder stops a cricket ball by catching it
3. **Change the direction of motion** -- Hitting a moving ball with a bat changes its direction
4. **Change the shape of an object** -- Pressing an inflated balloon changes its shape
5. **Cause a combination of these effects**

> **Key idea:** Whenever there is a change in speed, direction of motion, or shape, a force must be acting on the object. None of these changes can take place without the action of a force.

### What If an Object Is at Rest?

If an object is at rest, it does not mean that no force is acting on it. It means that all the forces acting on the object are **balancing one another**. These are called balanced forces.

---

## Types of Forces

Forces can be divided into two main categories based on whether the objects need to touch each other or not.

### Contact Forces

These are forces that act **only when there is physical contact** between the objects. The contact can be direct (using hands) or indirect (using a stick or rope).

### Non-Contact Forces

These are forces whose effect can be experienced **even if the objects are not in contact**.

---

## Contact Forces

### 1. Muscular Force

When we perform any physical activity -- walking, running, lifting, pushing, jumping, or stretching -- the force comes from the action of **muscles** in our body. This is called **muscular force**.

Muscular force occurs when muscles contract and elongate during any activity.

- Animals, birds, fish, and insects all use muscular forces for movement and survival
- Humans have used the muscular force of animals like horses, bullocks, and camels to carry out tasks for thousands of years
- Inside our body, muscular force helps us chew food, push food through the digestive system, and keeps our heart pumping blood

### 2. Friction

Have you noticed that a ball rolling on flat ground stops on its own after some time? Or that a bicycle slows down and stops if you stop pedalling? Something is bringing these moving objects to rest. That something is **friction**.

**Friction** is the force that comes into play when an object moves or tries to move over another surface. Friction always acts in a direction **opposite** to the direction of motion.

**Why does friction occur?**

Even surfaces that appear smooth have a large number of tiny irregularities. When two surfaces are placed in contact, the irregularities of the two surfaces lock into each other and oppose any effort to move one surface over the other.

**Key facts about friction:**
- Friction is **greater on rough surfaces** and less on smooth surfaces
- The force of friction depends on the **nature of the surfaces** in contact
- Friction is a **contact force** since it arises due to two surfaces being in contact
- Air and water also exert friction on objects moving through them -- this is why aeroplanes, ships, and high-speed trains are designed with streamlined shapes

> **Key idea:** Friction is both helpful and necessary. Without friction, you could not walk, write, or even hold anything. But sometimes friction is unwanted, like in machine parts where it causes wear and heat.

## Non-Contact Forces

### 1. Magnetic Force

A magnet can exert a force on another magnet or a magnetic material **without being in contact**. This is called **magnetic force**.

- **Like poles repel** (North-North or South-South push each other away)
- **Unlike poles attract** (North-South pull towards each other)

If you place two ring magnets on a vertical stick with like poles facing each other, the upper magnet will float above the lower one! This demonstrates that magnetic force acts without contact.

### 2. Electrostatic Force

When certain materials are rubbed together, they build up **static charges** on their surfaces. A **charged object** can attract uncharged objects made of certain materials.

**Try this:** Rub a plastic scale vigorously with a piece of polythene. Bring it close to small pieces of paper (without touching them). The paper pieces get pulled towards the scale and stick to it!

**Two kinds of charges exist:**
- **Positive** charge
- **Negative** charge

**Rules of charges:**
- **Like charges repel** each other (two similarly charged balloons push apart)
- **Unlike charges attract** each other (a charged balloon is attracted to the cloth used to rub it)

The force exerted by a charged body on another charged or uncharged body is called **electrostatic force**. It is a non-contact force.

> **Key idea:** When charges move through a conductor, they form an electric current -- the same current that makes a lamp glow or creates heating and magnetic effects!

### 3. Gravitational Force

Throw a ball vertically upwards. No matter how hard you throw it, it always comes back down. Why? Because the **Earth attracts (pulls)** every object towards itself.

The force with which the Earth attracts objects towards itself is called **gravitational force** (also called the force of gravity or simply gravity).

**Key properties of gravitational force:**
- It is a **non-contact force**
- It is always an **attractive force** (it only pulls, never pushes)
- It acts on every object, regardless of what it is made of

**What happens when you throw a ball upward?**
1. While going **up**, the speed decreases because gravity pulls it down
2. At the **topmost point**, the ball stops momentarily
3. While coming **down**, the speed increases because gravity accelerates it

## Weight and Its Measurement

The force with which the Earth pulls an object towards itself is called the **weight** of the object. Since weight is a force, it is measured in **newton (N)**.

### How Is Weight Measured?

Weight is measured using a **spring balance**. When an object is hung from a spring balance:
- The spring stretches due to the gravitational force
- Different objects cause different amounts of stretching
- This shows that the Earth pulls different objects with different forces

### Weight vs Mass

| Property | Mass | Weight |
|----------|------|--------|
| **What is it?** | Amount of matter in an object | Gravitational force pulling the object |
| **Unit** | kilogram (kg) or gram (g) | newton (N) |
| **Does it change?** | Remains the same everywhere | Changes from place to place |
| **On the Moon** | Same as on Earth | About 1/6th of weight on Earth |

**Weight on different planets (for a 1 kg object):**
- Earth: 10 N
- Moon: 1.6 N
- Mars: 3.8 N
- Venus: 9 N
- Jupiter: 25.4 N

> **Key idea:** Mass never changes, but weight depends on the gravitational force of the planet or moon you are on. A 1 kg object that weighs 10 N on Earth would weigh only 1.6 N on the Moon!

## Floating and Sinking

When objects are placed in water, some float while others sink. If the Earth's gravity acts on all objects, why don't all objects sink?

### Buoyant Force (Upthrust)

When an object is placed in a liquid, the liquid pushes it **upwards**. This upward force applied by a liquid on an object is called **upthrust** or **buoyant force**.

**What determines whether an object floats or sinks?**
- If the **gravitational force is greater** than the buoyant force, the object **sinks**
- If the gravitational force **equals** the buoyant force, the object **floats**

The buoyant force depends on the **density** of the liquid (you will learn about density in a later chapter).

### Archimedes' Principle

The famous Greek scientist **Archimedes** discovered that when an object is fully or partially immersed in a liquid, it experiences an upward force equal to the weight of the liquid it displaces.

### Floating Rocks!

**Pumice** is a rock formed during volcanic eruptions. When lava with lots of gas cools quickly, it traps tiny bubbles of gas inside. This makes pumice so light that it can actually **float on water**!

---

## Key Takeaways

- A **force** is a push or pull resulting from interaction between objects. Its SI unit is **newton (N)**.
- Force can change an object's speed, direction of motion, shape, or all of these.
- **Contact forces** act only when objects touch -- examples include muscular force and friction.
- **Non-contact forces** act without contact -- examples include magnetic force, electrostatic force, and gravitational force.
- **Friction** opposes motion and depends on the nature of surfaces in contact.
- **Gravitational force** always attracts objects towards Earth. It is never repulsive.
- **Weight** is the gravitational force on an object (measured in N). **Mass** is the amount of matter (measured in kg).
- Mass stays the same everywhere, but weight can change from place to place.
- **Buoyant force** (upthrust) is the upward force applied by a liquid on an immersed object. It determines whether an object floats or sinks.
