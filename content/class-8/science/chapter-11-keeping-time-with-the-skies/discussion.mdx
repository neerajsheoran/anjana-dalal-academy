## Phases of the Moon

**Kiran:** Papa, the Moon does not produce its own light — it reflects sunlight. The phases happen because we see different portions of the illuminated half as the Moon orbits Earth. But many people think phases are caused by Earth's shadow.

**Ravi:** That is a very common misconception. Earth's shadow on the Moon causes a lunar eclipse, which is a rare event — it happens only a few times a year. Moon phases happen every month because the angle between the Sun, Moon, and Earth changes continuously as the Moon revolves around Earth.

**Kiran:** On a full Moon night — Purnima — the entire illuminated side faces us. On a new Moon night — Amavasya — the illuminated side faces away from us. The whole cycle takes about 29.5 days.

**Ravi:** And between these extremes, we see the waxing phase (Shukla Paksha) where the bright portion increases from new Moon to full Moon, and the waning phase (Krishna Paksha) where it decreases from full Moon to new Moon.

**Kiran:** A waxing Moon is easiest to spot at sunset, and a waning Moon at sunrise. That is useful for actual observation.

**Ravi:** Try it tonight. Check the Moon's phase and predict what it will look like in a week. Ancient Indian astronomers did exactly this — they tracked the Moon so precisely that they created calendars, predicted eclipses, and planned agricultural seasons. Varahamihira's Pancha Siddhantika from the 6th century CE describes sophisticated lunar calculations.

## Calendars and Timekeeping

**Kiran:** Three natural cycles gave us units of time — the day from Earth's rotation, the month from the Moon's phases, and the year from Earth's revolution around the Sun. But they do not fit together neatly, right?

**Ravi:** That is the fundamental challenge of calendar-making. A lunar month is about 29.5 days, so 12 lunar months give 354 days — 11 days short of a solar year. This means a pure lunar calendar drifts out of sync with seasons over time.

**Kiran:** The Gregorian calendar fixes this by making months 30 or 31 days with February at 28 or 29. It is a solar calendar — it stays in sync with seasons but ignores the Moon.

**Ravi:** Indian luni-solar calendars are more complex and arguably more elegant. They use the Moon for months but add an extra month — Adhika Maasa — every 2 to 3 years to stay synchronized with the solar year and seasons. This is why Indian festivals linked to Moon phases — like Diwali on the new Moon of Kartika and Holi on the full Moon of Phalguna — shift dates in the Gregorian calendar each year.

**Kiran:** The Indian National Calendar starts on 22nd March — the day after the spring equinox. It has 365 days with month names from traditional calendars.

**Ravi:** This was adopted in 1957 to standardise the diverse regional calendars across India. Each Indian state had its own calendar tradition. The National Calendar provides a common reference while respecting traditional month names.

## Ancient Indian Astronomical Knowledge

**Kiran:** The concepts of Uttarayan and Dakshinayan — the Sun's apparent northward and southward movement — show that ancient Indians were careful astronomical observers.

**Ravi:** Makar Sankranti marks the transition from Dakshinayan to Uttarayan — when the Sun begins its apparent northward journey. This astronomical event is celebrated across India under different names — Pongal in Tamil Nadu, Lohri in Punjab, Bihu in Assam. The festival dates are tied to actual celestial events.

**Kiran:** Aryabhata proposed that the Earth rotates on its axis — explaining why the Sun appears to rise and set. This was correct, and he stated it over 1500 years ago!

**Ravi:** Aryabhata also calculated the length of a year as 365 days, 6 hours, 12 minutes, and 30 seconds — remarkably close to the modern value of 365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, and 46 seconds. His accuracy with the instruments of his time is extraordinary.

**Kiran:** Did Indian astronomers predict eclipses?

**Ravi:** Yes. Indian astronomical texts like the Surya Siddhanta contain methods for predicting both solar and lunar eclipses. The Jantar Mantar observatories built by Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II in Jaipur, Delhi, and other cities in the 18th century are remarkable instruments for tracking celestial positions.

## Artificial Satellites

**Kiran:** ISRO has launched many satellites — for communication, weather monitoring, navigation, and scientific research. Chandrayaan missions study the Moon, Mangalyaan studied Mars, and Aditya L1 is studying the Sun.

**Ravi:** India's space programme is remarkable for its cost-effectiveness. Mangalyaan — the Mars Orbiter Mission — cost about 450 crore rupees, less than the budget of many Hollywood movies. It made India the first Asian country to reach Mars orbit successfully.

**Kiran:** Cartosat satellites capture high-resolution images of Earth for mapping. Are these used for urban planning?

**Ravi:** Yes — for urban planning, disaster management, agricultural monitoring, and even border surveillance. During the Kerala floods of 2018, satellite imagery was crucial for planning rescue operations. ISRO's INSAT series provides weather data that IMD uses for cyclone tracking and monsoon prediction.

**Kiran:** The textbook mentions space debris as a growing problem. Old satellites and rocket parts orbiting Earth could collide with active satellites.

**Ravi:** There are currently over 30,000 pieces of tracked space debris larger than 10 cm, travelling at speeds of about 28,000 km per hour. Even a paint flake at that speed can damage a satellite. ISRO and other space agencies are developing strategies to remove debris and design satellites that deorbit safely after their mission ends.

## Moon Observation Activity

**Kiran:** The textbook encourages us to observe the Moon over a month and record its phases. Can I do this from our terrace?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. You do not need any special equipment. Just note the date, time, and the shape and position of the Moon each evening or morning. You will see the crescent grow to half Moon to gibbous to full Moon, then shrink back. After a full month, you will have personally verified what the textbook describes.

**Kiran:** The Moon rises about 50 minutes later each day. So if I see it at 7 PM today, it will be at about 7:50 PM tomorrow.

**Ravi:** And a full Moon rises roughly at sunset and sets at sunrise. A new Moon rises and sets with the Sun, which is why it is invisible — it is lost in the Sun's glare. Observing these patterns yourself makes the astronomy real and memorable.

**Kiran:** This is the investigative approach from Chapter 1 — observing, recording, and finding patterns. Science comes full circle.

**Ravi:** Beautifully put. The sky is the oldest laboratory, and the Moon is perhaps humanity's first scientific subject. Every civilisation studied it, and you can join that tradition simply by looking up.
