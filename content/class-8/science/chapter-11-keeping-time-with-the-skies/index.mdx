## How Does the Moon's Appearance Change?

Have you ever noticed the Moon during the day? The Moon does not appear only at night -- it can sometimes be seen in the daytime too! Also, the shape of its bright portion changes from day to day. But the Moon itself does not change shape. It is always a sphere. What changes is the portion of the illuminated Moon that we can see from Earth.

### Why Does the Moon Shine?

The Moon does not emit its own light. It shines because it **reflects sunlight**. The half of the Moon that faces the Sun receives sunlight and becomes illuminated. The other half, facing away from the Sun, remains dark.

## Phases of the Moon

As the Moon revolves around the Earth, we see different portions of its illuminated side. These changing shapes of the bright portion are called the **phases of the Moon**.

### The Waning Period (Krishna Paksha)

After the **full Moon day (Purnima)**, the bright portion of the Moon starts decreasing:
1. **Full Moon** -- the entire illuminated side faces Earth
2. **Gibbous** -- more than half is visible (about a week later)
3. **Half Moon** -- exactly half is visible (about a week after full Moon)
4. **Crescent** -- less than half is visible
5. **New Moon (Amavasya)** -- the illuminated side faces away from Earth, so the Moon is not visible

This two-week period of decreasing brightness is called the **waning period** or **Krishna Paksha**.

### The Waxing Period (Shukla Paksha)

After the new Moon, the bright portion starts increasing again:
1. **Crescent** -- a thin sliver becomes visible
2. **Half Moon** -- half the Moon is visible
3. **Gibbous** -- more than half is visible
4. **Full Moon** -- the entire illuminated side faces Earth again

This two-week period of increasing brightness is called the **waxing period** or **Shukla Paksha**.

> **Key idea:** The complete cycle from one full Moon to the next takes about 29.5 days (nearly a month). The phases are NOT caused by Earth's shadow -- that would be a lunar eclipse, which is rare. Phases happen because we see different parts of the illuminated Moon as it revolves around the Earth.

### Locating the Moon

- A **waning Moon** is easiest to spot at **sunrise**
- A **waxing Moon** is easiest to spot at **sunset**
- On a full Moon day, the Moon is nearly **opposite** the Sun -- it rises when the Sun sets
- The Moon rises about **50 minutes later** each day

## How Did Calendars Come into Existence?

Ancient people observed three natural periodic events that helped them measure time:

1. **The Day** -- The Sun appears to rise and set every day due to Earth's rotation. The average time from one noon to the next is **24 hours** (one mean solar day).

2. **The Month** -- The Moon completes one cycle of phases in about **29.5 days**. This cycle became the basis of a month.

3. **The Year** -- Earth takes about **365 and a quarter days** to complete one revolution around the Sun. During this time, one complete cycle of seasons occurs.

### Lunar Calendars

**Lunar calendars** use the Moon's phases to track time. A lunar month is about 29.5 days, and a lunar year has 12 lunar months (about 354 days).

**Problem:** The lunar year is 11 days shorter than the solar year, so seasons do not remain synchronised with the same months over time.

### Solar Calendars

**Solar calendars** are designed to keep the calendar synchronised with the seasons. The **Gregorian calendar** (used worldwide today) is a solar calendar:
- Months are adjusted to add up to **365 days** (some have 30 days, some 31, February has 28)
- Every **4 years**, a **leap day** is added (February 29) to account for the extra quarter day

### Luni-Solar Calendars

**Luni-solar calendars** use the Moon's phases for counting months but make adjustments to stay in sync with seasons. Every 2-3 years, an extra month called **Adhika Maasa** (intercalary month) is added. Many Indian calendars follow this system.

Indian luni-solar calendar months: Chaitra, Vaisakha, Jyeshtha, Ashadha, Shravana, Bhadrapada, Ashwin, Kartika, Margashirsha, Pausha, Magha, and Phalguna.

> **Key idea:** Indian festivals like Diwali (new Moon of Kartika), Holi (full Moon of Phalguna), and Eid-ul-Fitr (after sighting the crescent Moon) are tied to Moon phases, which is why their dates change in the Gregorian calendar each year.

## The Indian National Calendar

The **Indian National Calendar** is a solar calendar adopted by the Government of India in 1957:
- It has **365 days** in a regular year
- The year begins on **22 March** (the day after the spring equinox)
- Month names come from traditional Indian calendars (Chaitra, Vaisakha, etc.)
- Months have either **30 or 31 days**
- Leap years match the Gregorian calendar

### Uttarayan and Dakshinayan

Ancient Indians observed that the Sun does not always rise exactly in the East:
- In summer, it rises a little **northward** of East
- In winter, it rises a little **southward** of East
- The Sun's apparent northward movement (December to June) is called **Uttarayan**
- The apparent southward movement (June to December) is called **Dakshinayan**

## Artificial Satellites

Besides the Moon (Earth's natural satellite), many **artificial satellites** orbit the Earth. These are launched by various countries for:
- **Communication** (TV, phone, internet)
- **Navigation** (GPS, maps)
- **Weather monitoring** (predicting cyclones, rainfall)
- **Disaster management**
- **Scientific research**

The **Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO)** has launched many satellites:
- **Cartosat series** -- capture high-quality images of Earth for mapping
- **AstroSat** -- makes scientific observations of stars
- **Chandrayaan** missions -- to study the Moon
- **Aditya L1** -- to study the Sun
- **Mangalyaan** -- India's Mars mission

> **Key idea:** Space debris (space junk) from old satellites and rocket parts is a growing problem. Countries are working together to remove dangerous debris from space.

## Key Takeaways

- The **phases of the Moon** happen because we see different parts of the illuminated portion as the Moon revolves around Earth.
- A full cycle of Moon phases takes about **29.5 days** (nearly a month).
- The waning period (decreasing bright portion) is called **Krishna Paksha**; the waxing period (increasing bright portion) is called **Shukla Paksha**.
- **Lunar calendars** follow the Moon's cycle; **solar calendars** follow the cycle of seasons; **luni-solar calendars** combine both.
- The **Gregorian calendar** is a solar calendar. Many Indian calendars are luni-solar.
- The **Indian National Calendar** begins on 22 March and is a solar calendar.
- Many Indian festivals are linked to Moon phases, which is why their dates change each year in the Gregorian calendar.
- **Artificial satellites** launched by ISRO and other agencies help in communication, navigation, weather monitoring, and scientific research.
