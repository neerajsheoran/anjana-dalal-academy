## Cells as Building Blocks of Life

**Kiran:** Papa, Robert Hooke saw cells in cork in 1665, but he was seeing dead, empty cells. How did we learn about living cells?

**Ravi:** Antonie van Leeuwenhoek built better microscopes and was the first to observe living cells — bacteria, blood cells, and single-celled organisms swimming in pond water. He called them "animalcules." Modern electron microscopes can magnify up to 10 lakh times, revealing structures inside cells that were invisible to Hooke and Leeuwenhoek.

**Kiran:** The three basic parts of a cell are the cell membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleus. The membrane controls what enters and exits, the cytoplasm is where most processes happen, and the nucleus controls everything.

**Ravi:** Think of a cell like a factory. The cell membrane is the security gate — it decides what comes in and what goes out. The cytoplasm is the factory floor where production happens. The nucleus is the manager's office — it contains the instructions and directs all operations.

**Kiran:** Plant cells have extra parts — a cell wall for rigidity, chloroplasts for photosynthesis, and large vacuoles for storage. That is why plants can stand upright without bones.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The rigid cell wall is like a brick around each cell. When millions of brick-encased cells stack together, they form a strong structure — from the stems of rice plants in Punjab's fields to the trunks of banyan trees.

## Microorganisms and Their Types

**Kiran:** Microorganisms include bacteria, protozoa, fungi, and algae. But viruses are different — they are not even cells. How do they work?

**Ravi:** Viruses are packets of genetic material wrapped in a protein coat. They cannot reproduce on their own — they must hijack a living cell and use its machinery to make copies. That is why they are at the boundary between living and non-living.

**Kiran:** The COVID-19 virus was a recent example. It spread so rapidly because each infected cell produced thousands of new virus copies.

**Ravi:** And understanding viral structure helped Indian scientists and companies develop vaccines quickly. The Serum Institute of India became the world's largest vaccine manufacturer. This is applied microbiology at a national scale.

**Kiran:** Bacteria are different from other cells because they do not have a proper nucleus — just a nucleoid region. Is that a big difference?

**Ravi:** It is fundamental. Cells with a proper nucleus are called eukaryotic — plants, animals, fungi, and protozoa. Cells without one are prokaryotic — bacteria. This distinction is one of the most important in biology. Bacterial cells are simpler and smaller, which is why they can multiply so rapidly.

## Useful Microorganisms

**Kiran:** I knew curd is made using bacteria, but I did not realise the same principle applies to idli, dosa, and bhatura batter. Fermentation is everywhere in Indian cooking!

**Ravi:** Lactobacillus bacteria ferment milk into curd by producing lactic acid. Yeast ferments sugar in bread dough, producing carbon dioxide gas that makes the dough fluffy. In South India, the fermentation of rice and urad dal batter for idli and dosa involves both bacteria and yeast working together.

**Kiran:** The textbook mentions Rhizobium bacteria in legume roots that fix nitrogen from the air. Is that why farmers rotate crops with pulses?

**Ravi:** Exactly. After growing wheat or rice, which depletes soil nitrogen, farmers plant chickpeas, lentils, or soybeans. The Rhizobium in their root nodules converts atmospheric nitrogen into forms plants can use, naturally replenishing the soil. This is ancient Indian agricultural wisdom supported by modern microbiology.

**Kiran:** Spirulina is called a superfood — more than 60 percent protein and rich in Vitamin B12. Could it help solve malnutrition?

**Ravi:** India has several Spirulina farms, especially in Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh. It grows quickly, needs minimal resources, and is extremely nutritious. Some mid-day meal programmes have explored adding Spirulina to school food. Microalgae also produce more than half of Earth's oxygen — they are more important to our atmosphere than all the forests combined.

## Harmful Microorganisms

**Kiran:** If microorganisms are so useful, why do some cause diseases?

**Ravi:** From the microbe's perspective, it is simply surviving and reproducing. Some bacteria and viruses happen to do this inside human bodies, causing illness as a side effect. The balance between beneficial and harmful microbes is what matters.

**Kiran:** The textbook explains why pickles and murabbas do not spoil — high concentrations of salt or sugar prevent microbial growth. So food preservation is applied microbiology!

**Ravi:** Indian methods of food preservation are sophisticated science. Sun-drying papads reduces moisture, inhibiting bacterial growth. Adding turmeric to food provides antibacterial properties. Fermenting milk into curd actually preserves it longer than raw milk while adding beneficial bacteria.

**Kiran:** Decomposition by microbes is also crucial — they recycle dead matter back into nutrients. Without them, fallen leaves and dead animals would pile up forever.

**Ravi:** And biogas plants use anaerobic bacteria to decompose waste and produce methane for cooking. India has millions of biogas plants, especially in rural areas, converting cow dung and agricultural waste into clean fuel. It is a perfect example of using microbiology for sustainable energy.

## The Cell as the Basic Unit of Life

**Kiran:** The cell is called the basic unit of life because all living organisms are made of cells. But cells themselves are incredibly complex — they have membranes, organelles, genetic material. Are they really "basic"?

**Ravi:** "Basic" here means the smallest functional unit. You can have a living cell — like a bacterium or an amoeba — that performs all life functions. But if you break a cell into its components, none of them are alive on their own. The cell is the threshold of life.

**Kiran:** The yolk of an ostrich egg is the largest single cell — up to 170 mm in diameter. That is huge compared to bacteria, which are a few micrometres!

**Ravi:** The range of cell sizes spans several orders of magnitude — connecting to the powers of 10 you studied in Maths Chapter 2. From bacteria at 10 to the power minus 6 metres to ostrich eggs at 10 to the power minus 1 metres, that is five orders of magnitude within cells alone.

**Kiran:** Different cell shapes match their functions — spindle-shaped muscle cells for contraction, long branching nerve cells for transmitting signals, flat cheek cells for protection. Form follows function.

**Ravi:** That principle — form follows function — is central to both biology and engineering. Just as an aeroplane wing is shaped for lift and a cricket bat is shaped for hitting, cells are shaped for their specific tasks.
