## Introduction: A Hidden World

The human eye can only see objects that are above a certain size. For a long time, many tiny things around us remained unknown. Long ago, people discovered that a curved piece of glass could make small things look bigger. This piece of glass was shaped like a lentil seed -- thick in the middle and thin at the edge -- and was called a **lens**.

Over time, lenses were improved to become more powerful. Each new tool, from simple magnifying glasses to microscopes, helped humans see what their eyes could not. The invention of the **microscope** opened a fascinating hidden world filled with tiny living creatures.

> **Key idea:** All living beings, whether plants or animals, are called organisms. Some organisms are so small that they cannot be seen with the naked eye. They can only be seen with the help of a microscope.

---

## The Discovery of Cells

### Robert Hooke and the Microscope

In 1665, a scientist named **Robert Hooke** published a book called *Micrographia*. Using a microscope that made things look 200 to 300 times bigger, he examined a thin slice of cork and saw many small, empty spaces. These compartments reminded him of a honeycomb. He drew what he saw and called each small space a **cell**.

This was the first time the word "cell" was used in science to describe the basic unit of life.

### Antonie van Leeuwenhoek

Around the same time in the 1660s, **Antonie van Leeuwenhoek**, a Dutch scientist, made better lenses that allowed him to build more useful microscopes. He was the first person to clearly see and describe tiny living things like **bacteria** and **blood cells**. Because of this, he is known as the **Father of Microbiology**.

## What Is a Cell?

All living beings are made up of **cells**. A cell is the basic building block of life, just like a brick is the basic building block of a wall. Let us look at the basic structure of a cell.

### Observing an Onion Peel Cell

When you place a thin, transparent layer of onion peel under a microscope, you can observe nearly rectangular structures closely arranged without any space between them. These are the cells of the onion peel.

### Observing a Human Cheek Cell

If you gently scrape the inside of your cheek with a clean toothpick and observe the material under a microscope, you will see polygon-shaped structures. These are **cheek cells**, which form the inner lining of your mouth.

### Three Main Parts of a Cell

Every cell has three basic parts:

1. **Cell Membrane** -- The thin outer lining that encloses the cell. It separates one cell from another. It is porous and allows essential materials to enter and waste materials to exit.

2. **Cytoplasm** -- The jelly-like substance that fills the space between the cell membrane and nucleus. It contains important compounds like carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and mineral salts. Most life processes take place within the cytoplasm.

3. **Nucleus** -- The small round structure inside the cell, covered by a thin membrane. It regulates all activities within the cell, including growth.

> **Key idea:** The cell membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleus are the three basic parts of every cell.

---

## Plant Cells vs Animal Cells

Plant cells have some extra features that animal cells do not have:

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Cell Wall** | Present (provides rigidity and strength) | Absent |
| **Chloroplasts** | Present in green parts (contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis) | Absent |
| **Vacuole** | Large (stores substances, removes waste, maintains shape) | Usually absent or very small |
| **Cell Membrane** | Present | Present |
| **Cytoplasm** | Present | Present |
| **Nucleus** | Present | Present |

The **cell wall** in plant cells provides rigidity and strength to plants. This is why plant cells look firm in structure.

**Plastids** are tiny rod-shaped structures found in plant cells. Some plastids, like **chloroplasts**, contain chlorophyll, which makes them green and helps in photosynthesis. In non-green parts of the plant, plastids help in the storage of substances.

---

## Variation in Shape and Structure of Cells

Not all cells look the same. Different cells have different shapes depending on the function they perform.

- **Muscle cells** are shaped like a spindle (narrow at both ends, wide in the middle). This shape helps them contract and relax.
- **Nerve cells** (neurons) are very long and have branches. This elongated shape and branched structure help them reach different parts of the body and pass on messages quickly.
- **Cheek cells** are thin and flat. They form a protective lining on the inner surface of the cheek.
- **Plant cells** may be rectangular, elongated, oval, or even tube-like. Some plant cells form long tubes that help carry water throughout the plant.

> **Key idea:** The unique shape, size, and structure of cells help them carry out their specific functions. A cell's shape is related to the job it does.

---

## Levels of Organisation in Living Organisms

The body of a living organism is organised in an amazing way, from simple building blocks to complex systems:

1. **Cell** -- The basic unit of life
2. **Tissue** -- A group of similar cells working together
3. **Organ** -- Different tissues organised together (like the stomach)
4. **Organ System** -- Several organs working together to perform a major body function (like the digestive system)
5. **Organism** -- All organ systems together make a complete living being

### Unicellular and Multicellular Organisms

- **Multicellular organisms** are made up of many cells. The life of complex living organisms begins with a single cell (the egg), which divides repeatedly to form the complete organism. Animals, humans, and plants are all multicellular.
- **Unicellular organisms** are made up of just one cell. They carry out all life functions in that single cell.

> **Key idea:** The yolk (yellow part) of an ostrich egg is the largest known cell in the living world, measuring about 130 mm to 170 mm in diameter!

## What Are Microorganisms?

Some living organisms are so small that they cannot be seen with the naked eye. These tiny living beings are called **microorganisms** or **microbes** (micro means very small).

Microorganisms can be:
- **Unicellular** -- Made of just one cell (e.g., bacteria, Amoeba, Paramecium)
- **Multicellular** -- Made of many cells (e.g., some fungi, some algae)

They are found **everywhere** -- in water, soil, air, and even inside our bodies!

### Types of Microorganisms

| Type | Examples | Key Features |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Protozoa** | Amoeba, Paramecium | Single-celled, can move around |
| **Bacteria** | Lactobacillus, Rhizobium | Very tiny, single-celled, various shapes (spherical, rod, spiral) |
| **Fungi** | Yeast, bread mould | Can be unicellular or multicellular, cannot photosynthesise |
| **Algae** | Spirulina, Chlorella | Contains green pigment (chlorophyll), can make their own food |

### Viruses

**Viruses** are microscopic and acellular (not made of cells). They can only multiply when they enter a living cell. Viruses may infect plants, animals, or bacteria and can cause diseases.

## How Are We Connected to Microbes?

Have you ever seen a lemon, tomato, or orange rot after being left outside for some time? You may have noticed a powdery or cotton-like growth on them. This happens because microorganisms from the air, water, or soil have infected the food.

But why do pickles (achar) and murabbas not get infected? This is because salt or sugar is added in high concentrations, which does not allow microorganisms to grow.

### Key Players in Cleaning the Environment

Microorganisms play a crucial role in keeping our environment clean:

- **Fungi** and **bacteria** in the soil act on plant and animal waste and slowly break it down into simpler, nutrient-rich **manure**.
- Decaying plants, fallen leaves, and dead animals are all broken down by microorganisms.
- The nutrients released go back into the soil and help new plants grow.
- This is nature's own **recycling system**.

This is why gardeners collect dry leaves and plant waste and put them into pits -- to make natural compost!

> **Key idea:** If microorganisms did not exist on Earth, dead plants and animals would keep piling up, and nutrients would never return to the soil.

### Microbes as a Source of Biogas

Some bacteria and fungi can live without oxygen. They can decompose plant and animal waste and release a mixture of gases including **methane**. This gas, called **biogas**, can be used as fuel for cooking, heating, generating electricity, and even running vehicles.

## Microorganisms and Food

Microorganisms play a big role in our kitchen!

### Making Bread with Yeast

**Yeast** is a type of fungus. When yeast is added to dough along with sugar and warm water:
1. Yeast feeds on the sugar and breaks it down
2. During this process, **carbon dioxide** gas is released
3. The CO2 forms bubbles that make the dough soft and fluffy
4. Yeast also produces a small amount of alcohol, giving the dough a slightly different smell

This property of yeast is used in making **breads, cakes, and pastries**.

### Making Curd with Lactobacillus

When a small spoonful of curd is added to lukewarm milk and kept in a warm place:
1. The bacterium **Lactobacillus** (present in curd) feeds on the sugar in milk (lactose)
2. It multiplies and **ferments** the milk
3. It produces **lactic acid**, which makes the curd sour
4. The milk thickens and turns into curd

Lactobacillus grows well in warm conditions. That is why curd sets better when kept in a warm place, not in the refrigerator.

### Other Fermented Foods

Bacteria like Lactobacillus also help in fermentation of batter for making **idli, dosa**, and dough for making **bhatura**. Fermented foods like fermented soybeans and fermented bamboo shoots are consumed as traditional food in parts of India.

---

## Nitrogen-Fixing Bacteria

Some bacteria, such as **Rhizobium**, live in the roots of legume plants (beans, peas, lentils, chickpeas). They form swollen regions called **root nodules**.

These bacteria:
- Trap **nitrogen** from the air
- Convert it into a form useful for plants
- Help plants grow better **without chemical fertilisers**

This is why farmers practise **crop rotation** -- they grow legumes in rotation with other crops. The legumes naturally increase the nitrogen content in the soil and keep it healthy for the next crop.

---

## Amazing Microalgae: Tiny Helpers in Water

**Microalgae** are microscopic, plant-like organisms that live in water, soil, air, and even on trees. They are incredibly important:

- They make their own food using sunlight
- They produce **more than half of the Earth's oxygen supply**
- They are rich in nutrients and serve as food for many aquatic animals
- Some, like **Spirulina**, **Chlorella**, and **Diatoms**, are used as health supplements

**Spirulina** is called a "superfood" because:
- It has more than 60% protein by weight
- It contains only a small amount of fat and sugar
- It is a good source of **Vitamin B12**

> **Key idea:** Microalgae produce more than half of the oxygen on Earth. Protecting them is essential for maintaining the oxygen balance of our planet.

## Why Is the Cell the Basic Unit of Life?

The cell is considered the basic unit of life because:
- The body of **all living organisms** is made up of cells
- A single cell contains various components that help organisms perform their functions
- Even unicellular organisms (like bacteria and Amoeba) can carry out all life processes in just one cell
- Multicellular organisms start life as a single cell that divides to form the entire body

### Bacterial Cells Are Different

Bacterial cells differ from plant and animal cells in an important way:
- They do **not** have a well-defined nucleus or nuclear membrane
- Instead, they have a region called the **nucleoid** where their genetic material is found
- This feature distinguishes bacteria from yeast, protozoa, algae, fungi, plants, and animals

### Electron Microscopes

For observing very tiny cell structures, scientists use **electron microscopes** that can magnify a cell about **10,00,000 times** (ten lakh times). This allows them to see structures inside the cell that are invisible even under ordinary microscopes.

## Key Takeaways

- **Microorganisms** (microbes) are very small organisms not visible to the naked eye. They can be seen using a microscope.
- They live in all kinds of environments -- water, soil, air, and even inside the bodies of plants and animals.
- The **cell** is the basic unit of life. All living organisms are made up of cells.
- A typical cell has a **cell membrane**, **cytoplasm**, and **nucleus**. Plant cells also have a **cell wall**, **chloroplasts**, and large **vacuoles**.
- Bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and algae are different kinds of microorganisms.
- **Viruses** are different from other microorganisms -- they can only reproduce inside a host organism.
- Some microorganisms are **beneficial** (decompose waste, make food, fix nitrogen, produce oxygen).
- Some microorganisms are **harmful** and cause diseases.
- **Yeast** is used in making bread, cakes, idlis, dosas, and bhaturas.
- **Lactobacillus** bacteria convert milk into curd through fermentation.
- **Rhizobium** bacteria in root nodules of legumes fix nitrogen from the air and enrich the soil.
