## 1.1 Fractions as Percentages

You have probably seen signs like "Mega Sale -- up to 50% off!" or heard "She scored 83% in her exams." But what does the symbol **%** actually mean?

The word **per cent** comes from the Latin phrase **per centum**, meaning "by the hundred" or "out of hundred."

So **25%** means 25 out of every 100 -- like 25 rupees out of 100 rupees, or 25 marks out of 100 marks.

**Percentages are simply fractions where the denominator is 100.**

- 20% = 20/100 = 1/5
- 33% = 33/100
- 50% = 50/100 = 1/2
- 75% = 75/100 = 3/4

---

### Expressing Fractions as Percentages

**Example 1:** Express 3/4 as a percentage.

**Method 1:** Find an equivalent fraction with denominator 100.
- 3/4 = (3 x 25)/(4 x 25) = 75/100 = **75%**

**Method 2:** Multiply the fraction by 100.
- 3/4 x 100 = 75. So 3/4 = **75%**

**Example 2:** Express 2/5 as a percentage.
- 2/5 = 40/100 = **40%**
- Or: 2/5 x 100 = 40. So 2/5 = **40%**

> **Key idea:** To express a fraction as a percentage, multiply the fraction by 100. To express a percentage as a fraction, write it over 100 and simplify.

**Example 3:** Express 24% as a fraction.
- 24% = 24/100 = 12/50 = **6/25**

---

### Why Use Percentages?

Suppose a factory makes two varieties of biscuits. Sugar makes up 9/34 of Variety 1 and 13/45 of Variety 2. Which is more sugary?

With fractions, it is hard to compare at a glance. But as percentages:
- Variety 1: 9/34 x 100 = **26.47%**
- Variety 2: 13/45 x 100 = **28.88%**

Now it is immediately clear that Variety 2 is more sugary!

**Percentages are useful because:**
- They provide a **common base** (100) for easy comparison
- They work naturally with our **base-10 number system**: 31% = 0.31
- 100 is a sweet spot -- large enough for detail, yet simple enough to understand

### Historical Note

The idea of "per hundred" appears as early as the 4th century BCE in **Kautilya's Arthashastra**, where interest rates were described "per cent." The Romans also used taxes of 1/100 in trade. The modern **%** symbol evolved from Italian manuscripts of the 15th century.

---

### Percentages Around Us

- The human body is about **60% water** by weight
- Ice cream is about **30-50% air** by volume
- About **99.86%** of the Solar System's mass is in the Sun
- 45% of the world's population watched part of the 2022 FIFA World Cup

## 1.2 Percentage of Some Quantity

**Example:** Madhu ate 120 g of biscuits with 25% sugar. How much sugar did he eat?

25% sugar means 25 g sugar per 100 g biscuits.

**Using proportion:** 25 : 100 :: x : 120
- x = (25 x 120) / 100 = **30 g**

**Using the unitary method:**
- 100 g has 25 g sugar
- 1 g has 25/100 g sugar
- 120 g has (25/100) x 120 = **30 g sugar**

**Quick formula:** To find z% of a quantity Q:

**z% of Q = (z/100) x Q**

**Example:** 35% of 95 g = (35/100) x 95 = **33.25 g**

> **Key idea:** Be careful when comparing percentages! 25% of 120 g (= 30 g) can be more than 35% of 80 g (= 28 g). Percentages represent proportions, so the **whole** matters.

## 1.3 Profit and Loss

When you buy something for a certain price and sell it for a different price:

- **Cost Price (CP):** The price at which you buy an item
- **Selling Price (SP):** The price at which you sell it
- **Profit:** SP - CP (when SP > CP)
- **Loss:** CP - SP (when CP > SP)

### Profit and Loss Percentages

Profit and loss are expressed as percentages of the **cost price**:

- **Profit %** = (Profit / CP) x 100
- **Loss %** = (Loss / CP) x 100

**Example:** Ramu bought a bicycle for Rs 3000 and sold it for Rs 3450.
- Profit = 3450 - 3000 = Rs 450
- Profit % = (450/3000) x 100 = **15%**

**Example:** A shopkeeper bought a toy for Rs 500 and sold it for Rs 425.
- Loss = 500 - 425 = Rs 75
- Loss % = (75/500) x 100 = **15%**

### Finding SP from CP and Profit/Loss %

- **SP = CP + Profit** = CP + (Profit% / 100) x CP = CP x (1 + Profit%/100)
- **SP = CP - Loss** = CP - (Loss% / 100) x CP = CP x (1 - Loss%/100)

**Example:** CP = Rs 800, Profit = 20%.
- SP = 800 x (1 + 20/100) = 800 x 1.20 = **Rs 960**

## 1.4 Simple Interest

When you borrow money from a bank or lend money to someone, **interest** is charged for the use of that money.

- **Principal (P):** The original amount borrowed or deposited
- **Rate of interest (R):** The percentage charged per year
- **Time (T):** The number of years
- **Interest (I):** The extra amount earned or paid

### Simple Interest Formula

In **simple interest**, the interest is calculated only on the original principal:

**Simple Interest (SI) = (P x R x T) / 100**

**Total Amount = Principal + Interest = P + (P x R x T) / 100**

**Example:** Rani deposits Rs 5000 in a bank at 8% per annum for 3 years.
- SI = (5000 x 8 x 3) / 100 = **Rs 1200**
- Total amount after 3 years = 5000 + 1200 = **Rs 6200**

**Example:** Raju borrows Rs 12,000 at 10% per annum for 2 years.
- SI = (12000 x 10 x 2) / 100 = **Rs 2400**
- He must pay back 12000 + 2400 = **Rs 14,400**

> **Key idea:** In simple interest, the interest each year is the same (it does not compound). The formula SI = PRT/100 is a direct application of proportional reasoning.

## 1.5 Discount

A **discount** is a reduction in the marked price of an item.

- **Marked Price (MP):** The price written on the item (also called MRP or list price)
- **Discount:** The reduction offered
- **Selling Price:** MP - Discount

**Discount % = (Discount / MP) x 100**

**Example:** A shirt has a marked price of Rs 800. The shop offers a 15% discount.
- Discount = 15% of 800 = (15/100) x 800 = Rs 120
- Selling Price = 800 - 120 = **Rs 680**

---

## 1.6 Tax (GST)

**GST (Goods and Services Tax)** is a tax added to the price of goods and services.

- **Bill amount = Selling Price + GST**
- **GST amount = (GST% / 100) x Selling Price**

**Example:** A phone costs Rs 15,000 and has 18% GST.
- GST = (18/100) x 15000 = Rs 2700
- Total bill = 15000 + 2700 = **Rs 17,700**

---

## Summary

- **Percentage** means "per hundred." z% = z/100.
- To convert a fraction to a percentage, multiply by 100.
- To find z% of a quantity Q: (z/100) x Q.
- **Profit %** = (Profit/CP) x 100; **Loss %** = (Loss/CP) x 100.
- **Simple Interest** = (P x R x T) / 100.
- **Discount** = (Discount%/100) x Marked Price.
- **GST** is added to the selling price to get the final bill amount.
- Percentages are fractions in disguise -- they make comparison easier by using a common denominator of 100.
