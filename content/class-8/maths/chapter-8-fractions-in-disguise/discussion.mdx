## Understanding Percentages

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter title says fractions in disguise. So percentages are really just fractions with denominator 100?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The word "per cent" comes from the Latin "per centum" — by the hundred. Saying 25 percent is the same as saying 25 out of 100, which is the fraction 1/4. The disguise is just a more convenient way to compare fractions.

**Kiran:** Why is 100 such a good denominator?

**Ravi:** Because it is large enough to express detail — you can distinguish between 37 percent and 38 percent — but small enough to understand intuitively. Also, since we use a base-10 number system, 100 connects naturally to decimals. 25 percent equals 0.25. This dual representation makes percentages incredibly versatile.

**Kiran:** Kautilya's Arthashastra from the 4th century BCE used "per hundred" for interest rates. So India has been using percentages for over two thousand years!

**Ravi:** And the concept is everywhere today — exam scores, GST rates, batting averages, inflation rates, nutritional labels. Understanding percentages is essential for financial literacy.

## Profit, Loss, and Percentage

**Kiran:** Profit and loss percentages are always calculated on the cost price. Why not on the selling price?

**Ravi:** Because the cost price is the baseline — it is what you invested. A profit of 450 rupees on a 3000-rupee bicycle is 15 percent. If you calculated on the selling price of 3450, you would get about 13 percent, which misrepresents the return on your investment.

**Kiran:** So if I buy something for 800 rupees and want a 20 percent profit, my selling price should be 800 times 1.20, which is 960 rupees.

**Ravi:** Right. The formula SP equals CP times (1 plus profit percent over 100) works for profit, and SP equals CP times (1 minus loss percent over 100) works for loss. These are just the distributive property in action — multiplying by (1 plus something) distributes into the original plus the percentage.

**Kiran:** That connects directly to Chapter 6 on distributivity!

**Ravi:** Maths is deeply interconnected. Every chapter builds on others. Indian traders and merchants have been calculating profit margins mentally for centuries using these exact methods — especially in markets like Chandni Chowk in Delhi or Crawford Market in Mumbai.

## Simple Interest

**Kiran:** The simple interest formula SI equals PRT over 100 — is that just proportional reasoning?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Interest is directly proportional to the principal, to the rate, and to the time. If you double any one of them while keeping the others constant, the interest doubles. This is a three-variable direct proportion.

**Kiran:** So if Rani deposits 5000 rupees at 8 percent for 3 years, the interest is 5000 times 8 times 3 divided by 100, which is 1200 rupees. Total amount is 6200 rupees.

**Ravi:** In simple interest, the interest each year is identical — 400 rupees per year in this case. That is what makes it "simple." In real banking, compound interest is more common, where you earn interest on the interest too. You will study that in higher classes.

**Kiran:** Is there a quick way to estimate interest in my head?

**Ravi:** Yes. For 10 percent interest, the calculation is trivial — just move the decimal point. For other rates, use 10 percent as a reference. Eight percent is 10 percent minus 2 percent. So for 5000 rupees: 10 percent is 500, 2 percent is 100, so 8 percent is 400 per year. Times 3 years gives 1200. Quick and accurate.

## Discounts and GST

**Kiran:** When a shop says "Flat 30 percent off," the discount is calculated on the marked price, not the cost price. So the shopkeeper might still make a profit even after the discount?

**Ravi:** Always. Shops mark up prices before offering discounts. If a shirt costs the shopkeeper 500 rupees and he marks it at 800 rupees, a 15 percent discount gives a selling price of 680 rupees. His profit is still 180 rupees, which is a 36 percent profit on cost price.

**Kiran:** So the "percentage off" in sales is not the same as the shopkeeper's loss. It is calculated on a different base — the marked price.

**Ravi:** Exactly. This is why understanding the base of a percentage is critical. When you see "50 percent off" during a sale, the question should be: 50 percent of what? The marked price might have been inflated.

**Kiran:** And GST is added on top of the selling price, right? So a phone costing 15,000 rupees with 18 percent GST becomes 17,700 rupees.

**Ravi:** GST — Goods and Services Tax — replaced multiple older taxes in India in 2017. Understanding how it adds to prices is financial literacy that every Indian citizen needs. The bill amount equals the selling price times (1 plus GST percent over 100).

## Comparing Percentages Carefully

**Kiran:** The textbook warns that 25 percent of 120 grams can be more than 35 percent of 80 grams. That feels counterintuitive.

**Ravi:** Let us verify. 25 percent of 120 is 30 grams. 35 percent of 80 is 28 grams. So even though 25 percent sounds smaller than 35 percent, the actual quantities depend on the whole.

**Kiran:** So percentages are relative measures. You cannot compare percentages meaningfully without knowing the wholes they refer to.

**Ravi:** This is a common trap in media reporting. A headline might say "City A has a 20 percent crime increase" and "City B has a 10 percent increase." But if City A started with 100 incidents and City B started with 1000, the absolute increases are 20 and 100 — City B is far worse despite the lower percentage.

**Kiran:** The human body is about 60 percent water by weight. Does that mean a 50-kg person has about 30 kg of water?

**Ravi:** Exactly. And ice cream is 30 to 50 percent air by volume — which is why it melts into a surprisingly small amount of liquid. Percentages help you see through the disguise of everyday things.
