## Properties of Rectangles

**Kiran:** Papa, the carpenter's problem in the textbook is interesting. If she joins two equal strips at their midpoints, she always gets a rectangle regardless of the angle between them. Why?

**Ravi:** Because the diagonals of a rectangle are equal and bisect each other. When the strips are equal and joined at their midpoints, they form the two diagonals. The resulting quadrilateral automatically has all angles equal to 90 degrees — the maths works out beautifully using triangle congruence.

**Kiran:** The textbook proves this using SAS and AAS congruence conditions. So the proof depends on things we learned last year about triangles?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Geometry builds on itself. Every new theorem uses previously established results. This is what makes geometry so logical — it is like building a house where each floor rests on the one below. In engineering, we use these properties constantly. When constructing buildings, carpenters check if a frame is rectangular by measuring the diagonals — if they are equal, the frame is a rectangle.

**Kiran:** That is a practical application! So workers on construction sites are using the diagonal property without even knowing the formal proof.

**Ravi:** Indeed. And in Indian temple architecture, like the Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur, precise rectangular and square geometries were essential. The architects used these properties over a thousand years ago.

## Squares as Special Rectangles

**Kiran:** A square is a special rectangle where all sides are also equal. So every square is a rectangle, but not every rectangle is a square. This hierarchical relationship confuses some of my classmates.

**Ravi:** Think of it like categories. All tigers are cats, but not all cats are tigers. Similarly, squares are a subset of rectangles. The additional constraint — all sides equal — gives squares extra properties that rectangles do not have.

**Kiran:** Like the diagonals of a square bisect each other at 90 degrees, while rectangle diagonals do not necessarily meet at 90 degrees.

**Ravi:** Correct. And square diagonals bisect the vertex angles into 45 degrees each. This makes the square the most symmetric quadrilateral. For the carpenter to make a square instead of a rectangle, she must place the two strips perpendicular to each other.

**Kiran:** So the angle between the diagonals determines whether we get a rectangle or a square — 90 degrees gives a square, any other angle gives a non-square rectangle.

**Ravi:** That is a sharp observation. For exams, you should be able to quickly identify which properties are exclusive to squares and which are shared with rectangles.

## Parallelograms and Their Properties

**Kiran:** How is a parallelogram different from a rectangle?

**Ravi:** A parallelogram only requires that opposite sides are parallel. A rectangle adds the constraint that all angles are 90 degrees. So a parallelogram can be "tilted" — its angles need not be right angles.

**Kiran:** The textbook says adjacent angles of a parallelogram add up to 180 degrees, and opposite angles are equal. So if one angle is 30 degrees, the four angles are 30, 150, 30, 150.

**Ravi:** Right. This follows from the co-interior angles property of parallel lines. When a transversal cuts two parallel lines, the co-interior angles add up to 180 degrees. In a parallelogram, each side acts as a transversal for the pair of parallel opposite sides.

**Kiran:** And the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other but are NOT necessarily equal. That is different from a rectangle.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The diagonals of a parallelogram are equal only when all angles are 90 degrees — making it a rectangle. This distinction is a favourite in exam questions. They might give you a parallelogram with some conditions and ask whether it must be a rectangle.

## The Rhombus

**Kiran:** A rhombus has all four sides equal, like a square, but its angles can be anything. And it is always a parallelogram?

**Ravi:** Yes. Since all sides are equal, you can prove using triangle congruence (SSS) that opposite sides are parallel. So every rhombus is a parallelogram. A rhombus has some special properties — its diagonals bisect each other at right angles and they bisect the vertex angles.

**Kiran:** Those are the same extra properties that a square has! So a square is a rhombus with all angles 90 degrees.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The Venn diagram relationship is: the set of squares is the intersection of the set of rectangles and the set of rhombuses, and all three are subsets of parallelograms.

**Kiran:** This is like a family tree of quadrilaterals. Understanding it helps me answer questions like "Is every rectangle a rhombus?" — No, only if all sides are also equal, making it a square.

**Ravi:** That kind of logical reasoning scores well in exams. Practice stating the relationships clearly and giving counterexamples when a statement is false.

## Trapeziums and Kites

**Kiran:** Beyond the parallelogram family, there are trapeziums and kites. A trapezium has only one pair of parallel sides, right?

**Ravi:** Correct. And an isosceles trapezium — where the non-parallel sides are equal — has some nice properties, like equal base angles. Trapeziums appear frequently in real life. The cross-section of many bridges, the shape of certain dams, and even the profile of a bucket are all trapezoids.

**Kiran:** And a kite has two pairs of consecutive sides that are equal. Its diagonals are perpendicular, like a rhombus.

**Ravi:** But unlike a rhombus, only one diagonal bisects the other, and only one pair of opposite angles is equal. If you have ever flown a patang during Makar Sankranti, you have held a kite-shaped quadrilateral.

**Kiran:** The angle sum property — all quadrilateral angles adding to 360 degrees — applies to all of these: rectangles, squares, parallelograms, rhombuses, trapeziums, and kites.

**Ravi:** Yes. And the proof is simple — any diagonal divides the quadrilateral into two triangles, each with angle sum 180 degrees. So the total is 360 degrees. This is one of those fundamental results you must know cold for any exam.
