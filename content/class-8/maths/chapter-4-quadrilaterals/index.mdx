## What is a Quadrilateral?

A **quadrilateral** is a closed figure with exactly four sides. The word comes from Latin: **quadri** (four) + **latus** (side).

The **angles** of a quadrilateral are the angles formed between its sides. In this chapter, we will study different types of quadrilaterals -- rectangles, squares, parallelograms, and rhombuses -- and discover their properties.

---

## 4.1 Rectangles and Squares

### Definition of a Rectangle

A **rectangle** is a quadrilateral in which:
- All angles are **right angles (90 degrees)**
- Opposite sides are of **equal length**

### The Carpenter's Problem

Imagine a carpenter who has two thin wooden strips. She wants to connect them so that when a thread is passed through their endpoints, it forms a rectangle.

If one strip is 8 cm long, what should the other strip's length be? Where should they be joined?

The strips become the **diagonals** of the rectangle. Through geometric reasoning, we can discover:

### Properties of a Rectangle

**Property 1:** All angles of a rectangle are **90 degrees**.

**Property 2:** Opposite sides of a rectangle are **equal** in length.

**Property 3:** Opposite sides of a rectangle are **parallel** to each other.

**Property 4:** The diagonals of a rectangle are **equal in length** and they **bisect each other** (cross at their midpoints).

> **Key idea:** Two strips of wood will form a rectangle if and only if they are **equal in length** and are **joined at their midpoints**. The angle between them does not matter -- any angle works!

### Proving the Diagonals are Equal (Deduction 1)

In rectangle ABCD:
- AB = CD (opposite sides)
- Angle BAD = Angle CDA = 90 degrees
- AD is common to both triangles ADC and DAB

By the **SAS congruence condition**, triangle ADC is congruent to triangle DAB. Therefore, diagonal AC = diagonal BD.

### Proving the Diagonals Bisect Each Other (Deduction 2)

Using the **AAS congruence condition**, we can show that triangle AOB is congruent to triangle COD (where O is the intersection point). This gives us OA = OC and OB = OD, meaning O is the midpoint of both diagonals.

### Why All Angles Must Be Equal

If two equal diagonals bisect each other at any angle x, the four angles of the resulting quadrilateral always work out to be:

90 - x/2 + x/2 = **90 degrees** each

So no matter what angle the diagonals make, the quadrilateral is always a rectangle!

## Squares: A Special Rectangle

A **square** is a rectangle in which all four sides are also equal in length.

**Square:** A quadrilateral in which all angles are 90 degrees and all sides are equal.

Since every square satisfies the definition of a rectangle, **every square is a rectangle**, but not every rectangle is a square.

### Properties of a Square

A square has all the properties of a rectangle, plus some special ones:

**Property 1:** All sides of a square are **equal**.

**Property 2:** Opposite sides are **parallel**.

**Property 3:** All angles are **90 degrees**.

**Property 4:** Diagonals are **equal in length** and **bisect each other at 90 degrees**.

**Property 5:** The diagonals **bisect the angles** of the square (each angle is split into two 45-degree angles).

> **Key idea:** For the carpenter to make a square, the two strips must be equal in length, joined at their midpoints, and placed **perpendicular** to each other (at 90 degrees).

## 4.2 Angles in a Quadrilateral

### The Angle Sum Property

Draw any quadrilateral and one of its diagonals. The diagonal splits the quadrilateral into **two triangles**. Since the sum of angles in each triangle is 180 degrees:

**The sum of all angles in any quadrilateral = 180 + 180 = 360 degrees.**

> **Key idea:** The sum of all four angles in any quadrilateral is always **360 degrees**. This explains why if three angles of a quadrilateral are 90 degrees each, the fourth must also be 90 degrees (since 90 + 90 + 90 + fourth = 360, so fourth = 90).

## 4.3 Parallelograms

### What is a Parallelogram?

A **parallelogram** is a quadrilateral in which both pairs of opposite sides are **parallel**.

Since a rectangle has parallel opposite sides, every rectangle is a parallelogram. But not every parallelogram is a rectangle -- a parallelogram can have angles other than 90 degrees.

### Constructing a Parallelogram

**Example:** Draw a parallelogram with sides 4 cm and 5 cm, and an angle of 30 degrees between them.

1. Draw AB = 4 cm and AD = 5 cm with a 30-degree angle at A.
2. Draw a line through D parallel to AB.
3. Draw a line through B parallel to AD.
4. Mark the intersection point as C. ABCD is the parallelogram.

### Properties of a Parallelogram

**Property 1:** Opposite sides are **equal** (AB = CD, AD = BC).

**Property 2:** Opposite sides are **parallel**.

**Property 3:** Adjacent angles add up to **180 degrees**, and opposite angles are **equal**.

**Property 4:** The diagonals **bisect each other** (but they are NOT necessarily equal in length).

### How to Find the Angles

If one angle of a parallelogram is x degrees:
- The **adjacent** angle = 180 - x degrees
- The **opposite** angle = x degrees

**Example:** If one angle is 30 degrees, the four angles are: 30, 150, 30, 150.

> **Key idea:** In a parallelogram, opposite angles are equal, and each pair of adjacent angles adds up to 180 degrees. This follows from the property that co-interior angles of parallel lines add up to 180 degrees.

### Proving Opposite Sides are Equal (Deduction 7)

In parallelogram ABCD, draw diagonal BD:
- The angles marked by alternate angles are equal (since AD is parallel to BC)
- AE = YS (opposite sides)
- BD is common

By **AAS congruence**, triangle ABD is congruent to triangle CDB. Therefore AB = CD and AD = CB.

### Proving Diagonals Bisect Each Other (Deduction 8)

Using alternate angles and opposite sides, we show that triangle AOE is congruent to triangle YOS (where O is the intersection). This gives OA = OY and OE = OS, proving the diagonals bisect each other.

## 4.4 Rhombus: Quadrilaterals with Equal Sides

### What is a Rhombus?

A **rhombus** is a quadrilateral in which **all four sides are equal** in length.

A square is a special rhombus (with all angles 90 degrees), but a rhombus can have any angle.

### Constructing a Rhombus

1. Draw two sides AD and AB of equal length, with any angle (say 50 degrees) between them.
2. Using a compass, mark point C at the same distance from both B and D.
3. Join BC and DC to complete the rhombus.

### Finding the Angles

If one angle of a rhombus is 50 degrees, then:
- Since a rhombus is also a parallelogram (opposite sides are parallel), adjacent angles add up to 180 degrees.
- The four angles are: **50, 130, 50, 130 degrees**.

### A Rhombus is a Parallelogram

Since all sides of a rhombus are equal, the diagonal divides it into two congruent triangles with equal alternate angles. This proves the opposite sides are parallel, making every rhombus a parallelogram.

### Properties of a Rhombus

**Property 1:** All sides are **equal**.

**Property 2:** Opposite sides are **parallel**.

**Property 3:** Adjacent angles add up to **180 degrees**; opposite angles are **equal**.

**Property 4:** Diagonals **bisect each other**.

**Property 5:** Diagonals **bisect the angles** of the rhombus.

**Property 6:** Diagonals intersect at **90 degrees** (right angles).

> **Key idea:** The diagonals of a rhombus are special -- they bisect each other **at right angles** and they bisect the angles of the rhombus. This makes the rhombus very useful in geometric constructions.

## Relationships Between Quadrilaterals

The different types of quadrilaterals are related to each other like a family tree:

- Every **square** is a **rectangle** (all angles 90 degrees)
- Every **square** is a **rhombus** (all sides equal)
- Every **rectangle** is a **parallelogram** (opposite sides parallel)
- Every **rhombus** is a **parallelogram** (opposite sides parallel)

This can be shown as a Venn diagram:
- **Parallelogram** is the outermost set
- Inside it: **Rectangle** and **Rhombus** overlap
- Their overlap region contains **Square**

## 4.5 Playing with Quadrilaterals

### Joining Triangles to Make Quadrilaterals

**Activity 1:** Take two equilateral triangles with side 8 cm each. Join them along one side. You get a **rhombus** (all sides 8 cm).

**Activity 2:** Take two isosceles triangles (sides 8, 8, 6 cm). They can be joined in different ways:
- Joining along the **equal sides** (8 cm) gives a parallelogram
- Joining along the **unequal side** (6 cm) gives a different quadrilateral

### Trapezium

A **trapezium** is a quadrilateral with exactly **one pair of parallel sides**. The parallel sides are called **bases**, and the non-parallel sides are called **legs**.

A trapezium where the non-parallel sides are equal is called an **isosceles trapezium**.

### Kite

A **kite** is a quadrilateral with **two pairs of consecutive sides** that are equal, but opposite sides are not equal.

Properties of a kite:
- The diagonals are **perpendicular** (meet at 90 degrees)
- One diagonal bisects the other
- One pair of opposite angles is equal

## Summary of Properties

| Property | Rectangle | Square | Parallelogram | Rhombus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| All angles 90 degrees | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| Opposite sides equal | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| All sides equal | No | Yes | No | Yes |
| Opposite sides parallel | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Diagonals equal | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| Diagonals bisect each other | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Diagonals perpendicular | No | Yes | No | Yes |
| Diagonals bisect angles | No | Yes | No | Yes |

> **Key idea:** The sum of angles in any quadrilateral is **360 degrees**. Different types of quadrilaterals have different special properties related to their sides, angles, and diagonals. Understanding these properties helps us construct figures and solve geometric problems.
