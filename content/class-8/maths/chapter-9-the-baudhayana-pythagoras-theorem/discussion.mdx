## Baudhyana's Elegant Discovery

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook calls it the Baudhayana-Pythagoras Theorem and says Baudhyana stated it around 800 BCE — almost 300 years before Pythagoras. Why is it usually called just the Pythagorean theorem?

**Ravi:** Historical naming in mathematics often reflects who popularised a result in Europe, not who discovered it first. Baudhyana's Shulba-Sutra, verse 1.12, clearly states that the square on the diagonal equals the sum of the squares on the two sides. This was written for constructing vedic altars with precise geometric proportions.

**Kiran:** So Baudhyana was solving practical problems — altar construction — and discovered a fundamental mathematical truth in the process.

**Ravi:** Exactly. The Shulba-Sutras are among the oldest mathematical texts in the world. Baudhyana was not just stating a formula — he was providing construction methods. How to double the area of a square, how to combine two squares into one — these are engineering problems solved with pure geometry.

**Kiran:** And the solution for doubling a square's area is so elegant — just draw a square on the diagonal of the original. The new square has exactly double the area.

**Ravi:** This follows because the diagonal square is made of 4 right triangles, while the original square is made of 2 of the same triangles. The ratio is 4 to 2, which is 2. Simple, visual, and profound.

## The Theorem and Its Proof

**Kiran:** So in any right triangle with legs a and b and hypotenuse c, a squared plus b squared equals c squared. Can you walk me through why this works geometrically?

**Ravi:** Consider a right triangle with legs 3 and 4. Build squares on each side — areas 9, 16, and the one on the hypotenuse. If you cut and rearrange the squares on the legs, they exactly fill the square on the hypotenuse. 9 plus 16 equals 25, which is 5 squared.

**Kiran:** And (3, 4, 5) is a Pythagorean triple — all whole numbers. The textbook lists others: (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), (7, 24, 25).

**Ravi:** Here is a useful pattern. If (a, b, c) is a triple, then (ka, kb, kc) is also a triple for any positive k. So from (3, 4, 5) you get (6, 8, 10), (9, 12, 15), (15, 20, 25), and infinitely many more. Babylonian clay tablets from 1800 BCE list many such triples — this knowledge is ancient.

**Kiran:** For exams, should I memorise some triples?

**Ravi:** Knowing (3, 4, 5), (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), and (7, 24, 25) is very useful. Many exam problems are built around these, and recognising them saves time.

## Irrational Numbers and the Square Root of 2

**Kiran:** The chapter introduces irrational numbers through the square root of 2. If an isosceles right triangle has legs of length 1, the hypotenuse is the square root of 2, which is approximately 1.41421356 and never terminates or repeats.

**Ravi:** The proof of irrationality is beautiful. If the square root of 2 were rational, say m over n in lowest terms, then m squared equals 2n squared. In m squared, the prime factor 2 appears an even number of times. But in 2n squared, it appears an odd number of times. Even cannot equal odd — contradiction.

**Kiran:** This is the proof by contradiction method. We assume something is true and show it leads to an impossibility.

**Ravi:** Exactly. This method was formalised by Euclid, but the concept was understood by Indian mathematicians too. The discovery that the square root of 2 is irrational was a philosophical shock in ancient Greece — the Pythagoreans believed all numbers were rational.

**Kiran:** Baudhyana gave an approximation for the square root of 2 in the Shulba-Sutra: 1 plus 1/3 plus 1/(3 times 4) minus 1/(3 times 4 times 34), which equals approximately 1.4142156. That is accurate to five decimal places!

**Ravi:** Remarkable precision for 800 BCE. This shows that Indian mathematicians were not just stating theorems — they were computing practical approximations for construction.

## Applications of the Theorem

**Kiran:** The ladder problem is classic — a 5-metre ladder against a wall, with the foot 3 metres away. The height is the square root of (25 minus 9), which is the square root of 16, which is 4 metres.

**Ravi:** Civil engineers and architects use this theorem constantly. When designing ramps for buildings — which is mandatory under disability access laws in India — the height, horizontal distance, and ramp length form a right triangle.

**Kiran:** Two friends walking east and north from the same point are 10 km apart after going 6 km and 8 km respectively, because the square root of (36 plus 64) equals 10. It is the (6, 8, 10) triple — just double the (3, 4, 5).

**Ravi:** Navigation, whether by ships, aeroplanes, or even Google Maps, relies on this theorem to calculate straight-line distances. The formula for distance between two points on a coordinate plane is a direct application.

**Kiran:** Does the converse work too — if a squared plus b squared equals c squared, is it definitely a right triangle?

**Ravi:** Yes. The converse is also true. Builders use this to check if corners are right angles — they measure 3 metres along one wall, 4 metres along the adjacent wall, and if the diagonal is exactly 5 metres, the corner is a perfect right angle.

## Connecting to Other Chapters

**Kiran:** This theorem connects to so many other topics. The squares from Chapter 1, the powers from Chapter 2, the irrational numbers here, and even the area formulas in Chapter 14.

**Ravi:** Mathematics is not a collection of isolated topics — it is a web of connected ideas. The Baudhayana-Pythagoras theorem is perhaps the single most connected theorem in all of mathematics. It bridges geometry and algebra, appears in trigonometry and calculus, and underpins physics from optics to relativity.

**Kiran:** Einstein used it in his theory of relativity?

**Ravi:** A generalised version, yes. The space-time metric in special relativity is essentially the Pythagorean theorem extended to four dimensions. So when you master this theorem, you are building a foundation for the most advanced physics.

**Kiran:** That gives me extra motivation to understand it deeply rather than just memorising the formula!

**Ravi:** That is the right attitude. Understand the why, and the what becomes unforgettable.
