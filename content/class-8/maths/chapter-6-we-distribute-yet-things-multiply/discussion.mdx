## The Distributive Property

**Kiran:** Papa, the chapter title is poetic — "We Distribute, Yet Things Multiply." What does distribution mean in maths?

**Ravi:** It means breaking a multiplication into simpler parts. If you need to compute 7 times 98, you can write 98 as 100 minus 2. Then 7 times 98 becomes 7 times 100 minus 7 times 2, which is 700 minus 14, which equals 686. You distributed the multiplication of 7 over the subtraction of 100 and 2.

**Kiran:** So a times (b plus c) equals ab plus ac. It connects multiplication and addition.

**Ravi:** And this is not just a modern idea. Brahmagupta described this technique in 628 CE as "khanda-gunanam" — multiplication by parts. He said: break the multiplier into parts, multiply separately, and add the results. Indian mathematicians used this for mental calculation long before calculators existed.

**Kiran:** The visual proof using rectangles is helpful too — a rectangle of a rows and (b plus c) columns can be split into two rectangles of areas ab and ac.

**Ravi:** That geometric interpretation is exactly how Euclid understood it too. Algebra and geometry are two languages describing the same truth.

## Important Identities

**Kiran:** The three main identities — (a plus b) squared, (a minus b) squared, and (a plus b)(a minus b) — keep appearing everywhere. Are these really that important?

**Ravi:** They are among the most frequently tested concepts in all of Indian mathematics education, from Class 8 through competitive exams like JEE. Let me show you why they are so powerful.

**Kiran:** Okay, let me try. To find 104 squared, I write it as (100 plus 4) squared, which is 10000 plus 800 plus 16, which equals 10816. That was much faster than multiplying 104 by 104!

**Ravi:** Now try 99 squared using the difference identity.

**Kiran:** (100 minus 1) squared equals 10000 minus 200 plus 1 equals 9801. And for 63 times 57, which is (60 plus 3)(60 minus 3), I get 3600 minus 9 equals 3591.

**Ravi:** Brilliant. Sridharacharya and Bhaskaracharya used these identities extensively in their algebraic works. Bhaskaracharya's Lilavati from 1150 CE is filled with elegant applications of these identities in verse form.

## Fast Multiplication Tricks

**Kiran:** The trick for multiplying by 11 is amazing. For 3874 times 11, you just add adjacent digits: the answer is 42614. How does this work algebraically?

**Ravi:** When you multiply a number by 11, you multiply by 10 plus 1. So each digit gets added to its right neighbour. For a number like dcba, 11 times dcba equals dcba times 10 plus dcba. When you add these, each position becomes the sum of two adjacent digits.

**Kiran:** And multiplying by 99 is even simpler — just multiply by 100 and subtract the number. So 37 times 99 equals 3700 minus 37 equals 3663.

**Ravi:** These mental maths techniques are invaluable. In competitive exams with time pressure, shaving seconds off each calculation can make the difference between finishing and not finishing. Indian shopkeepers have used similar mental calculation tricks for centuries.

**Kiran:** The textbook mentions multiplying by 101 too. So 34 times 101 equals 3400 plus 34 equals 3434. The digits just repeat!

**Ravi:** For two-digit numbers, yes — the result is the number written twice. This is because 101 times a two-digit number ab gives 100 times ab plus ab, which places the number in both the hundreds-tens position and the units-ones position.

## Difference of Squares Pattern

**Kiran:** The pattern n times (n plus 2) equals (n plus 1) squared minus 1 is really useful. So 19 times 21 equals 20 squared minus 1 equals 399.

**Ravi:** This comes directly from (a minus b)(a plus b) equals a squared minus b squared. With a equals 20 and b equals 1, you get 19 times 21 equals 400 minus 1. You can extend this: 18 times 22 equals 20 squared minus 4 equals 396.

**Kiran:** And (a plus b) squared plus (a minus b) squared equals 2(a squared plus b squared). What is that useful for?

**Ravi:** Suppose you know that 47 squared plus 53 squared equals some value. Since 47 equals 50 minus 3 and 53 equals 50 plus 3, this equals 2 times (2500 plus 9) equals 5018. Much faster than squaring both numbers separately.

**Kiran:** These identities feel like shortcuts that save enormous amounts of computation.

**Ravi:** They are not just shortcuts — they reveal the structure of numbers. Understanding why (a plus b) squared is not equal to a squared plus b squared is one of the most important conceptual leaps in algebra. The 2ab term is what many students forget, leading to errors in exams.

## Working with Algebraic Expressions

**Kiran:** When expanding (a plus b)(a squared plus 2ab plus b squared), I have to multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second, then combine like terms. It gets messy.

**Ravi:** Organisation is key. Write out all six products systematically, then group like terms. For this particular expansion, the result is a cubed plus 3a squared b plus 3ab squared plus b cubed — the cube of a sum.

**Kiran:** So (a plus b) cubed equals a cubed plus 3a squared b plus 3ab squared plus b cubed. The coefficients are 1, 3, 3, 1 — those look familiar from Pascal's Triangle!

**Ravi:** Excellent connection. In India, this triangle was known to Pingala and later to Varahamihira — centuries before Pascal. The coefficients of (a plus b) to the power n are always the nth row of this triangle.

**Kiran:** For exams, should I memorise the cube identity or derive it each time?

**Ravi:** Memorise it, but also know how to derive it. If you forget the formula under pressure, you can always expand (a plus b) times (a plus b) squared. Understand the process, and the formula becomes unforgettable.
