## Map Scales and Real Distances

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook says map scales like 1 to 60,00,000 mean 1 cm on the map is 60 km in reality. How do surveyors decide on the scale?

**Ravi:** The scale depends on the purpose. A map of all India might use 1 to 60,00,000, but a city map of Bengaluru would use something like 1 to 50,000 — because you need more detail. Indian railways and ISRO use very precise maps for different purposes.

**Kiran:** So if Bengaluru and Chennai are 5.5 cm apart on a 1 to 60,00,000 map, the actual distance is 5.5 times 60, which is 330 km. That matches what Google Maps shows!

**Ravi:** Map reading is proportional reasoning in action. Architects use it too — a floor plan at 1 to 50 scale means 1 cm on paper represents 50 cm in the actual building. If a room measures 8 cm by 6 cm on the plan, the real room is 4 metres by 3 metres.

**Kiran:** We could make a scale drawing of our classroom! If the classroom is 10 metres by 8 metres, at 1 to 50 scale that would be 20 cm by 16 cm on paper.

**Ravi:** Exactly. And you could mark furniture positions, windows, doors — all maintaining the same scale. This is precisely what architects do when designing homes and offices.

## Multi-Term Ratios

**Kiran:** Ratios can have more than two terms. The spice mix example has coriander, red chillies, toor dal, and fenugreek in the ratio 8 to 4 to 2 to 1. How do I scale this?

**Ravi:** The same way as a two-term ratio — multiply or divide all terms by the same factor. If Puneet has only 2 red chillies instead of 4, the factor is 1/2. So he needs 4 coriander, 2 chillies, 1 toor dal, and 0.5 fenugreek.

**Kiran:** The cement concrete mix of 1 to 1.5 to 3 for cement, sand, and gravel is interesting. Civil engineers use this for building pillars and beams. With 3 bags of cement, I multiply everything by 3 to get 3 to 4.5 to 9.

**Ravi:** Multi-term ratios are essential in chemistry too — chemical equations specify the ratio of reactants and products. When you study chemistry, the concept of stoichiometry is exactly this — scaling multi-term ratios.

**Kiran:** For the purple paint mix of red to blue to white as 2 to 3 to 5, if I have 10 litres of white paint, I need 4 litres of red and 6 litres of blue. Total is 20 litres.

**Ravi:** You found that 1 part equals 2 litres, then scaled up each component. This "find the unit part" method is the unitary method applied to multi-term ratios.

## Dividing a Quantity in a Given Ratio

**Kiran:** To divide 180 degrees among angles in the ratio 1 to 3 to 5, the total parts are 9, and each part is 20 degrees. So the angles are 20, 60, and 100 degrees.

**Ravi:** This technique appears everywhere — dividing inheritance, splitting costs, allocating resources. If three friends invest in a business in the ratio 2 to 3 to 5 and earn a profit of 50,000 rupees, the shares are 10,000, 15,000, and 25,000 respectively.

**Kiran:** The general formula is: each share equals the total multiplied by that term divided by the sum of all terms. So the first share is 50,000 times 2 over 10, which is 10,000.

**Ravi:** In Indian partnership law, profits and losses are divided exactly this way — in the ratio of investment. Understanding this has direct practical value.

**Kiran:** Can the terms of a ratio be fractions or decimals?

**Ravi:** Yes. The cement ratio 1 to 1.5 to 3 has a decimal. You can convert it to whole numbers by multiplying all terms by 2, getting 2 to 3 to 6. Either form works — use whichever is more convenient.

## Pie Charts

**Kiran:** Pie charts convert proportions into angles of a circle. The full circle is 360 degrees, representing 100 percent. So 25 percent is 90 degrees?

**Ravi:** Exactly. Each percentage point is 3.6 degrees. To draw a pie chart, convert each quantity to a fraction of the total, multiply by 360, and draw the corresponding angle with a protractor.

**Kiran:** For a class of 40 students with grade A having 12 students, the angle is 12 over 40 times 360, which is 108 degrees.

**Ravi:** Pie charts are used in budget presentations, company reports, and election analysis. When the Finance Minister presents the Union Budget, pie charts show how tax revenue is distributed — defence, education, healthcare, infrastructure. Understanding them is civic literacy.

**Kiran:** What if the percentages do not add up to 100 or the angles do not add up to 360?

**Ravi:** Then there is an error somewhere. The total must always be 100 percent or 360 degrees. This serves as a built-in verification — if your angles sum to 358, you know something went wrong.

## Direct and Inverse Proportion

**Kiran:** Direct proportion means both quantities increase together, while inverse proportion means one increases as the other decreases. How do I tell which is which in a word problem?

**Ravi:** Ask yourself: if I double the first quantity, does the second also double, or does it get halved? If doubling one doubles the other, it is direct. If doubling one halves the other, it is inverse.

**Kiran:** More workers means less time to finish a job — that is inverse. 6 workers take 10 days, so 15 workers take 60 divided by 15, which is 4 days. The product — workers times days — stays constant at 60.

**Ravi:** And more distance at the same speed means more time — that is direct. The ratio — distance over time — stays constant.

**Kiran:** The formulas are neat: for direct proportion, x over y equals constant. For inverse proportion, x times y equals constant.

**Ravi:** Indian railways optimise train schedules using inverse proportion — faster trains on congested routes reduce travel time. ISRO uses direct proportion to calculate fuel needs — more payload requires proportionally more fuel. These concepts are the foundation of engineering calculations.
