## The Locker Puzzle and Perfect Squares

**Kiran:** Papa, our textbook starts with a locker puzzle where 100 people toggle lockers. Only the perfect square lockers stay open. But why do square numbers have an odd number of factors?

**Ravi:** Think about it this way. For most numbers, factors come in pairs. Take 12 — its factor pairs are (1, 12), (2, 6), (3, 4). Six factors, three pairs. But for a number like 9, one pair is (3, 3) — the same number paired with itself. So 9 has factors 1, 3, 9 — an odd count.

**Kiran:** So the repeated factor in a perfect square is what makes the count odd! That is a neat connection between multiplication and counting.

**Ravi:** Exactly. And here is an exam tip — if a question asks you to quickly identify whether a number is a perfect square, first check its units digit. Perfect squares can only end in 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9. If a number ends in 2, 3, 7, or 8, you can immediately rule it out.

**Kiran:** But ending in those digits does not guarantee it is a perfect square, right? Like 26 ends in 6 but is not a square.

**Ravi:** Correct. It is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one. For certainty, you need prime factorisation — every prime factor must appear an even number of times.

## Square Roots and Estimation

**Kiran:** Finding square roots by prime factorisation makes sense, but what about estimating square roots of numbers that are not perfect squares, like 250?

**Ravi:** Engineers do this all the time. First, bracket the number between two consecutive perfect squares. Since 15 squared is 225 and 16 squared is 256, the square root of 250 lies between 15 and 16.

**Kiran:** And since 250 is much closer to 256 than to 225, the answer is closer to 16?

**Ravi:** Precisely. In competitive exams, this estimation technique saves time. Baudhyana and Aryabhata used similar approximation methods centuries ago. Aryabhata even gave a remarkably accurate value of the square root of 2 as 1.4142, which is correct to four decimal places.

**Kiran:** Wait, we also learned that the square root of 2 is irrational — it never terminates or repeats. That is covered in the Baudhayana chapter too, right?

**Ravi:** Yes, those chapters connect beautifully. The concept of irrationality was understood by Indian mathematicians long before the Greeks formalised it.

## Cubes and the Ramanujan Story

**Kiran:** The story of 1729 and Ramanujan is fascinating. He instantly saw that 1729 equals both 1 cubed plus 12 cubed and 9 cubed plus 10 cubed. How could anyone think that fast?

**Ravi:** Ramanujan had an extraordinary intuition for numbers. He grew up in Kumbakonam, Tamil Nadu, and taught himself mathematics from borrowed books. His ability to see patterns in numbers was almost supernatural. The Hardy-Ramanujan number 1729 is now famous worldwide.

**Kiran:** The textbook also says cubes can be written as sums of consecutive odd numbers, just like squares. Is there a pattern to which odd numbers?

**Ravi:** There is. The cube of n uses exactly n consecutive odd numbers. For example, 4 cubed uses four consecutive odd numbers: 13 plus 15 plus 17 plus 19 equals 64. The starting odd number follows a pattern too — it connects to triangular numbers.

**Kiran:** So squares and cubes are both connected to odd numbers but in different ways. Squares use consecutive odd numbers starting from 1, while cubes use groups of consecutive odd numbers starting from different points.

**Ravi:** That is an excellent comparison. This kind of cross-topic analysis is exactly what helps in exams.

## Perfect Cubes and Prime Factorisation

**Kiran:** For perfect squares, every prime factor appears an even number of times. For perfect cubes, every prime factor appears a multiple of three times. Is there a general rule?

**Ravi:** Yes. For a number to be a perfect nth power, every prime factor in its factorisation must appear a multiple of n times. So for a perfect fourth power, every prime factor appears a multiple of 4 times, and so on.

**Kiran:** That makes sense. Let me try one — is 3375 a perfect cube? Its prime factorisation is 3 times 3 times 3 times 5 times 5 times 5. Both 3 and 5 appear three times each. So the cube root is 3 times 5, which is 15.

**Ravi:** Perfect. And here is something useful — the successive differences method. For squares, the second level of differences gives a constant 2. For cubes, the third level gives a constant 6. This is a quick verification tool.

**Kiran:** So if I am unsure whether a sequence represents cubes, I can check if the third-level differences are all 6?

**Ravi:** Exactly. The Sanskrit terms are beautiful too — varga for square, ghana for cube, and varga-varga for the fourth power. Aryabhata used these terms in his astronomical calculations over 1500 years ago.

## Historical Connections

**Kiran:** It is amazing that the Babylonians had lists of squares and cubes on clay tablets 3700 years ago. Were Indian mathematicians working on these at the same time?

**Ravi:** The Indian tradition has deep roots too. The Vedic texts contain references to large numbers and their powers. The word "root" in mathematics — as in square root — comes from the Sanskrit word mula, meaning the root of a plant. Varga-mula meant square root, and ghana-mula meant cube root.

**Kiran:** And these Sanskrit terms were later adopted into Arabic as jidhr and into Latin as radix, both meaning root of a plant. So our mathematical vocabulary has Indian origins!

**Ravi:** Indeed. When you study maths, you are not just learning formulas — you are connecting with a global intellectual heritage that India helped shape. For your exams, remember the key formulas, but also understand the reasoning behind them. That understanding is what stays with you beyond the exam hall.
