## The Locker Puzzle

Imagine 100 lockers in a row, all closed. 100 people walk past them one by one:

- **Person 1** opens every locker.
- **Person 2** toggles every 2nd locker (closes it if open, opens it if closed).
- **Person 3** toggles every 3rd locker.
- This continues until all 100 people have had their turn.

Which lockers remain open at the end?

A locker is toggled once for each **factor** of its number. For example, locker 6 is toggled by persons 1, 2, 3, and 6 (the factors of 6). Since 6 has an **even** number of factors, it ends up closed.

> **Key idea:** Most numbers have factors that come in pairs (like 1 and 6, 2 and 3 for the number 6). But **square numbers** have one factor that pairs with itself (like 3 and 3 for the number 9). This gives them an **odd** number of factors.

The lockers that remain open are: **1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100** -- all perfect squares!

## 1.1 Square Numbers

A **square number** (or **perfect square**) is a number obtained by multiplying a number by itself.

| Side length | Area of the square |
|---|---|
| 1 | 1 x 1 = **1** |
| 2 | 2 x 2 = **4** |
| 3 | 3 x 3 = **9** |
| 4 | 4 x 4 = **16** |
| 5 | 5 x 5 = **25** |
| 10 | 10 x 10 = **100** |

We write n x n as **n²**, read as "n squared."

For example:
- 5² = 5 x 5 = 25
- 12² = 12 x 12 = 144

We can also square fractions and decimals:
- (3/5)² = (3/5) x (3/5) = 9/25
- (2.5)² = 2.5 x 2.5 = 6.25

> **Key idea:** The squares of natural numbers (1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ...) are called **perfect squares**.

## Patterns and Properties of Perfect Squares

Here are the squares of the first 20 natural numbers:

| n | n² | n | n² |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | 11 | 121 |
| 2 | 4 | 12 | 144 |
| 3 | 9 | 13 | 169 |
| 4 | 16 | 14 | 196 |
| 5 | 25 | 15 | 225 |
| 6 | 36 | 16 | 256 |
| 7 | 49 | 17 | 289 |
| 8 | 64 | 18 | 324 |
| 9 | 81 | 19 | 361 |
| 10 | 100 | 20 | 400 |

### Units digit pattern

Look at the units digit of perfect squares. They can only end in **0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9**. No perfect square ever ends in 2, 3, 7, or 8.

- If a number ends in **1 or 9**, its square ends in **1**.
- If a number ends in **2 or 8**, its square ends in **4**.
- If a number ends in **3 or 7**, its square ends in **9**.
- If a number ends in **4 or 6**, its square ends in **6**.
- If a number ends in **5**, its square ends in **5**.
- If a number ends in **0**, its square ends in **0**.

> **Key idea:** If a number ends in 2, 3, 7, or 8, it is definitely **not** a perfect square. But ending in 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9 does not guarantee it is a perfect square (for example, 26 ends in 6 but is not a perfect square).

### Zeros at the end

- 10² = 100 (one zero in 10, two zeros in 100)
- 100² = 10000 (two zeros in 100, four zeros in 10000)
- 200² = 40000 (two zeros in 200, four zeros in the square)

**The square of a number always has double the number of trailing zeros.** This means perfect squares can only have an **even** number of zeros at the end.

### Even and odd squares

- The square of an **even** number is always **even**.
- The square of an **odd** number is always **odd**.

## Perfect Squares and Odd Numbers

Look at the differences between consecutive squares:

- 4 - 1 = **3**
- 9 - 4 = **5**
- 16 - 9 = **7**
- 25 - 16 = **9**

The differences are consecutive odd numbers! This gives us a beautiful pattern:

- 1 = **1**
- 1 + 3 = **4**
- 1 + 3 + 5 = **9**
- 1 + 3 + 5 + 7 = **16**
- 1 + 3 + 5 + 7 + 9 = **25**
- 1 + 3 + 5 + 7 + 9 + 11 = **36**

> **Key idea:** The sum of the first n odd numbers is always n². Every perfect square can be written as a sum of consecutive odd numbers starting from 1.

### Using this to check if a number is a perfect square

Take a number like 25 and keep subtracting consecutive odd numbers starting from 1:

- 25 - 1 = 24
- 24 - 3 = 21
- 21 - 5 = 16
- 16 - 7 = 9
- 9 - 9 = **0**

We reached 0 at the 5th step, so 25 = 5² (a perfect square).

Now try 38:

- 38 - 1 = 37
- 37 - 3 = 34
- 34 - 5 = 29
- 29 - 7 = 22
- 22 - 9 = 13
- 13 - 11 = 2
- 2 - 13 = -11

We crossed zero without hitting it, so 38 is **not** a perfect square.

### Finding the next square from the previous one

If you know that 35² = 1225, you can find 36²:
- The 36th odd number is 2(36) - 1 = 71
- So 36² = 1225 + 71 = **1296**

In general, the **nth odd number** is **2n - 1**.

## Perfect Squares and Triangular Numbers

**Triangular numbers** are: 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, ...

There is a beautiful connection between triangular numbers and square numbers:

- 1 + 3 = **4** = 2²
- 3 + 6 = **9** = 3²
- 6 + 10 = **16** = 4²
- 10 + 15 = **25** = 5²

> **Key idea:** The sum of two consecutive triangular numbers is always a perfect square.

## Square Roots

If the area of a square is 49 sq. cm, what is its side length? Since 7 x 7 = 49, the side is **7 cm**. We call 7 the **square root** of 49.

In general, if y = x², then x is the square root of y.

The square root is written using the symbol **√**:
- √64 = ±8 (since both 8² = 64 and (-8)² = 64)
- √100 = ±10
- √9 = ±3

> **Key idea:** Every perfect square has two integer square roots -- one positive and one negative. In this chapter, we usually consider only the positive square root.

### Methods to Find Square Roots

**Method 1: Listing squares**

To check if 576 is a perfect square, list squares starting from a nearby known square:
- 20² = 400, 21² = 441, 22² = 484, 23² = 529, 24² = 576

So √576 = 24. This method works but becomes slow for large numbers.

**Method 2: Subtracting consecutive odd numbers**

Subtract 1, 3, 5, 7, ... from the number until you reach 0. If you reach 0 at the nth step, the square root is n.

For example, √81: subtract 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15, 17 from 81 and you reach 0 at the 9th step. So √81 = 9.

**Method 3: Prime factorisation**

This is the most efficient method.

**Example:** Is 324 a perfect square?
- 324 = 2 x 2 x 3 x 3 x 3 x 3
- Pair up the factors: (2 x 2) x (3 x 3) x (3 x 3)
- All factors pair up, so 324 is a perfect square.
- √324 = 2 x 3 x 3 = **18**

**Example:** Is 156 a perfect square?
- 156 = 2 x 2 x 3 x 13
- The factors 3 and 13 cannot be paired up.
- So 156 is **not** a perfect square.

> **Key idea:** A number is a perfect square if every prime factor in its factorisation appears an **even** number of times. The square root is found by taking one factor from each pair.

### Estimating Square Roots

**Example:** Find √1936.

1. 1936 lies between 1600 (40²) and 2500 (50²), so 40 &lt; √1936 &lt; 50.
2. The last digit is 6, so the square root must end in 4 or 6. Possible answers: 44 or 46.
3. Check: 45² = 2025 > 1936, so 40 &lt; √1936 &lt; 45.
4. The answer must be **44**. Verify: 44² = 1936. Correct!

**Example:** Estimate √250.
- 15² = 225 and 16² = 256
- So 15 &lt; √250 &lt; 16
- Since 250 is much closer to 256 than to 225, √250 is approximately 16 (but slightly less).

## 1.2 Cubic Numbers

A **cube** is a solid with all sides equal that meet at right angles.

- A cube of side 2 cm is made of **2 x 2 x 2 = 8** unit cubes.
- A cube of side 3 cm is made of **3 x 3 x 3 = 27** unit cubes.
- A cube of side 4 cm is made of **4 x 4 x 4 = 64** unit cubes.

We write n x n x n as **n³**, read as "n cubed."

The numbers 1, 8, 27, 64, 125, ... are called **perfect cubes**.

### Table of Cubes

| n | n³ | n | n³ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | 11 | 1331 |
| 2 | 8 | 12 | 1728 |
| 3 | 27 | 13 | 2197 |
| 4 | 64 | 14 | 2744 |
| 5 | 125 | 15 | 3375 |
| 6 | 216 | 16 | 4096 |
| 7 | 343 | 17 | 4913 |
| 8 | 512 | 18 | 5832 |
| 9 | 729 | 19 | 6859 |
| 10 | 1000 | 20 | 8000 |

### Properties of Cubes

- Unlike squares, cubes can end in **any digit** (0 through 9).
- Cubes **cannot** end with exactly two zeros. If a number ends in zeros, its cube has triple the number of trailing zeros (e.g., 10³ = 1000 has three zeros, 100³ = 1000000 has six zeros).
- We can also cube fractions and decimals:
  - (4/6)³ = (4/6) x (4/6) x (4/6) = 64/216
  - (−6)³ = −6 x −6 x −6 = −216

> **Key idea:** The cube of a negative number is negative, unlike squares which are always positive.

## Taxicab Numbers: A Ramanujan Story

The great Indian mathematician **Srinivasa Ramanujan** was visiting with G. H. Hardy at Cambridge. Hardy arrived in a taxi numbered **1729** and remarked it was "rather a dull number."

Ramanujan immediately replied: "No, it is a very interesting number. It is the smallest number that can be expressed as the sum of two cubes in two different ways."

- 1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 1 + 1728
- 1729 = 9³ + 10³ = 729 + 1000

Because of this story, 1729 is called the **Hardy-Ramanujan Number**. Numbers that can be expressed as the sum of two cubes in two different ways are called **taxicab numbers**.

The next two taxicab numbers are **4104** and **13832**.

## Perfect Cubes and Consecutive Odd Numbers

Just like squares, cubes also have a connection with odd numbers:

- 1 = **1** = 1³
- 3 + 5 = **8** = 2³
- 7 + 9 + 11 = **27** = 3³
- 13 + 15 + 17 + 19 = **64** = 4³
- 21 + 23 + 25 + 27 + 29 = **125** = 5³
- 31 + 33 + 35 + 37 + 39 + 41 = **216** = 6³

Each perfect cube can be written as a sum of consecutive odd numbers!

## Cube Roots

If y = x³, then x is the **cube root** of y, written as **∛y**.

- ∛8 = 2 (because 2³ = 8)
- ∛27 = 3 (because 3³ = 27)
- ∛1000 = 10 (because 10³ = 1000)

### Finding Cube Roots Using Prime Factorisation

**Example:** Is 3375 a perfect cube?
- 3375 = 3 x 3 x 3 x 5 x 5 x 5
- Group the factors into triplets: (3 x 3 x 3) x (5 x 5 x 5)
- All factors form complete triplets, so 3375 is a perfect cube.
- ∛3375 = 3 x 5 = **15**

**Example:** Is 500 a perfect cube?
- 500 = 2 x 2 x 5 x 5 x 5
- The factor 2 appears only twice (not a complete triplet).
- So 500 is **not** a perfect cube.

> **Key idea:** A number is a perfect cube if every prime factor in its factorisation appears exactly **three times** (or a multiple of three times). The cube root is found by taking one factor from each triplet.

### Successive Differences of Cubes

For perfect squares, the second level of successive differences gives a constant (2). For perfect cubes, the **third level** of successive differences gives a constant:

| Perfect cubes | 1 | 8 | 27 | 64 | 125 | 216 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | 7 | 19 | 37 | 61 | 91 | |
| Level 2 | 12 | 18 | 24 | 30 | | |
| Level 3 | 6 | 6 | 6 | | | |

At the third level, all differences equal **6**.

## 1.3 A Pinch of History

- The first known list of perfect squares and cubes was compiled by the **Babylonians** around **1700 BCE** on clay tablets.
- In ancient **Sanskrit** works, the term **varga** was used for square (both the shape and the number), and **ghana** was used for cube. The fourth power was called **varga-varga**.
- **Aryabhata** (499 CE) described varga as "a square figure of four equal sides and the number representing its area."
- The word **"root"** in mathematics (square root, cube root) comes from the Sanskrit word **mula**, meaning root of a plant, basis, or origin. The term **varga-mula** meant square root and **ghana-mula** meant cube root.
- This Sanskrit usage was later adopted in **Arabic** (jidhr) and **Latin** (radix), both meaning "root of a plant."

## Summary

- A **square number** is obtained by multiplying a number by itself: n² = n x n.
- Perfect squares end in **0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9** and have an even number of trailing zeros.
- The **square root** (√) is the inverse of squaring. Every perfect square has two integer square roots (positive and negative).
- A **cube number** is obtained by multiplying a number by itself three times: n³ = n x n x n.
- A number is a **perfect square** if every prime factor appears an even number of times.
- A number is a **perfect cube** if every prime factor appears a multiple of three times.
- The **cube root** (∛) is the inverse of cubing.
- The sum of the first n odd numbers equals n².
- Perfect cubes can also be expressed as sums of consecutive odd numbers.
