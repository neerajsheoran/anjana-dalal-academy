## 4.1 Fractals

A **fractal** is a shape that exhibits the same or similar pattern at smaller and smaller scales. This property is called **self-similarity**.

### Fractals in Nature

One of the most beautiful natural fractals is the **fern**. A fern leaf has smaller copies of itself as its leaflets, and these leaflets have even smaller copies, and so on!

Self-similarity also occurs in:
- **Trees** -- trunks have limbs, limbs have branches, branches have branchlets
- **Clouds** and **mountains** -- similar bumpy patterns at every scale
- **Coastlines** -- a jagged line that looks jagged no matter how much you zoom in
- **Lightning** -- branches into smaller branches

---

### Sierpinski Carpet

The Polish mathematician **Sierpinski** discovered a fractal made by:
1. **Step 0:** Start with a square.
2. **Step 1:** Divide it into 9 equal smaller squares and remove the central one.
3. **Step 2:** Repeat the process on each of the 8 remaining squares.
4. Continue forever...

![Steps 0 to 3 of the Sierpinski Carpet fractal showing progressive removal of central squares](sierpinski-carpet-steps.png)

**Pattern in the numbers:**
- Remaining squares at step n: **R(n) = 8^n**
- R(0) = 1, R(1) = 8, R(2) = 64, R(3) = 512, ...
- Holes at step n: H(n) = H(n-1) + R(n-1)
- H(0) = 0, H(1) = 1, H(2) = 9, H(3) = 73, ...

---

### Sierpinski Triangle (Gasket)

Another Sierpinski fractal starts with an **equilateral triangle**:
1. Join the midpoints of the sides to divide it into 4 smaller equilateral triangles.
2. Remove the central triangle.
3. Repeat on the 3 remaining triangles.

At each step, the number of remaining triangles is **3^n** and each is (1/4)^n of the original area.

---

### Koch Snowflake

![Steps 0 to 3 of the Koch Snowflake fractal showing the boundary growing more intricate at each step](koch-snowflake-steps.png)

Named after the Swedish mathematician **Von Koch** (1904):
1. Start with an equilateral triangle.
2. Divide each side into 3 equal parts.
3. Raise an equilateral triangle over the middle part and remove the base.
4. Each side becomes a "bump" shape. Repeat on all new sides.

At step n:
- Number of sides = **3 x 4^n**
- The perimeter keeps increasing with each step, but the area stays bounded!

> **Key idea:** Fractals demonstrate **exponential growth** in complexity at every step. The number of pieces, holes, or sides grows as powers of a number.

---

### Fractals in Art

Fractal-like patterns have been used in art for centuries:
- **Indian temples** -- The Kandariya Mahadev Temple in Khajuraho (c. 1025 CE) shows a tall structure made of smaller copies of itself, with even smaller copies on those.
- **African art** -- Nigerian Fulani wedding blankets feature diamond patterns within diamond patterns.
- **M.C. Escher** -- The Dutch artist created prints like "Smaller and Smaller" showing identical patterns of lizards at smaller and smaller scales.

## 4.2 Visualising Solids

### Profiles of Solids

When we look at a 3D object from different viewpoints, we see different 2D **profiles** (outlines).

**Examples:**
- A **cube** can show a square profile (from the front) or a hexagonal profile (from a corner)
- A **cylinder** can show a rectangle (from the side) or a circle (from the top)
- A **cone** can show a triangle (from the side) or a circle (from below)

**Challenge:** Can you think of a solid that has:
- A rectangular profile from one view and circular from another? (A cylinder!)
- A circular profile from one view and triangular from another? (A cone!)
- A trapezium profile from one view and circular from another? (A frustum or bucket shape!)

---

### Faces, Edges, and Vertices

Solid shapes with flat surfaces have:
- **Faces:** The flat surfaces forming the boundary
- **Edges:** The line segments where two faces meet
- **Vertices:** The points where edges meet

| Solid | Faces | Edges | Vertices |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cube/Cuboid | 6 | 12 | 8 |
| Triangular prism | 5 | 9 | 6 |
| Triangular pyramid (tetrahedron) | 4 | 6 | 4 |
| Square pyramid | 5 | 8 | 5 |
| Pentagonal prism | 7 | 15 | 10 |
| Octahedron | 8 | 12 | 6 |

### General Formulas

For a **prism** with an n-sided polygon base:
- Faces = n + 2
- Edges = 3n
- Vertices = 2n

For a **pyramid** with an n-sided polygon base:
- Faces = n + 1
- Edges = 2n
- Vertices = n + 1

> **Key idea:** For any convex polyhedron (solid with flat faces), **Euler's formula** holds: **V - E + F = 2**, where V = vertices, E = edges, F = faces. Check this with any solid above!

---

### Nets of Solids

A **net** is a flat shape that can be folded to form a solid. It is obtained by "unfolding" a solid onto a flat surface.

![All 11 possible nets of a cube shown as flat arrangements of 6 connected squares](nets-of-a-cube.png)

**Net of a cube:** A cross-shaped arrangement of 6 squares (and other arrangements). A cube has **11 different possible nets**.

**Net of a cylinder:** Two circles plus a rectangle (whose width equals the circumference of the circle).

**Net of a cone:** A circle (base) plus a sector of a larger circle (the curved surface).

**Net of a regular tetrahedron:** 4 equilateral triangles arranged in a specific pattern. It has only **2 different nets**.

**Net of an octahedron:** 8 equilateral triangles. An octahedron has **11 different nets**.

**Net of a dodecahedron** (12 pentagonal faces): Has an amazing **43,380 different nets**!

**Can you make a net of a sphere?** Try wrapping paper around a ball perfectly -- you will find it is impossible without wrinkles or gaps! A sphere cannot have a flat net.

---

### Shortest Paths on a Cube

An interesting application of nets: finding the **shortest path** between two points on the surface of a cuboid.

**Method:** Unfold the cuboid into its net. On the flat net, the shortest path between two points is a straight line. This straight line, when folded back, gives the shortest path on the surface.

**Example:** An ant is at the centre of one face and needs to reach a laddu at the centre of the top face.
- Draw the net of the cuboid.
- Mark the ant's position and the laddu's position on the net.
- The straight line between them on the net gives the shortest surface path.

Sometimes you may need to try different ways of unfolding to find which gives the truly shortest path.

## Summary

- **Fractals** are self-similar shapes that repeat the same pattern at smaller and smaller scales.
- Famous fractals include the **Sierpinski Carpet**, **Sierpinski Triangle**, and **Koch Snowflake**.
- Fractals appear in nature (ferns, trees, coastlines) and in art (Indian temples, African blankets).
- 3D solids have **faces** (flat surfaces), **edges** (line segments), and **vertices** (corner points).
- **Euler's formula:** V - E + F = 2 for any convex polyhedron.
- A **net** is a flat shape that folds into a solid. Different solids have different numbers of possible nets.
- **Shortest paths** on a cuboid surface can be found by unfolding the cuboid into a net and drawing a straight line.
