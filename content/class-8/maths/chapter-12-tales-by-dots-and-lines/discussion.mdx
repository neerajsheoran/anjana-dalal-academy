## The Mean as a Balancing Point

**Kiran:** Papa, the textbook says the mean is like a balancing point — the total distance below equals the total distance above. Can you explain with an example?

**Ravi:** Take the data 10, 10, 11, 17. The mean is 48 divided by 4, which is 12. Now check the distances: 10 is 2 below, 10 is 2 below, 11 is 1 below — total below is 5. And 17 is 5 above — total above is 5. They balance perfectly.

**Kiran:** So the mean is the only point where this balance happens?

**Ravi:** Yes, and that is what makes it special. If you try any other point, say 11, the distances below would be 1 plus 1 plus 0 equals 2, and above would be 6. Not balanced. The mean is unique in this property.

**Kiran:** This reminds me of the fulcrum of a seesaw — the point where weights on both sides are in equilibrium.

**Ravi:** That is exactly the right analogy. Physicists call it the centre of mass. In ISRO's satellite design, the centre of mass must be calculated precisely — an unbalanced satellite would tumble in space. The mathematical mean is the one-dimensional version of this concept.

## How the Mean Responds to Changes

**Kiran:** If I add a value greater than the mean, the mean increases. If I add a value less than the mean, it decreases. If I add a value equal to the mean, it stays the same. These rules make sense intuitively.

**Ravi:** And there are two elegant algebraic properties. If you add a constant k to every value, the mean increases by k. If you multiply every value by k, the mean gets multiplied by k.

**Kiran:** So if everyone in a class gets 5 bonus marks, the class average increases by exactly 5. And if a teacher doubles all marks, the average doubles.

**Ravi:** These properties are used in data normalisation — when scientists compare data from different instruments or scales, they shift and scale the data using these properties to make fair comparisons.

**Kiran:** Can I add or remove values without changing the mean?

**Ravi:** Yes — add two values equidistant from the mean, one above and one below. For example, if the mean is 12, adding 7 and 17 keeps the mean at 12 because their average is 12.

## The Median

**Kiran:** The median is the middle value when data is sorted. How is it different from the mean in practice?

**Ravi:** The median is more robust against extreme values. Consider incomes in a village: 10,000, 12,000, 15,000, 18,000, and 5,00,000. The mean is 1,11,000 — which makes it sound like everyone is well-off. But the median is 15,000, which better represents the typical villager's income.

**Kiran:** The one rich person distorts the mean but not the median. So for data with outliers, the median is more representative.

**Ravi:** India's economic data often reports both mean and median incomes for this reason. The average per-capita income can be misleading in a country with high income inequality.

**Kiran:** Is there a rule for when to use mean versus median?

**Ravi:** Use the mean when data is roughly symmetric with no extreme outliers — like exam scores. Use the median when data is skewed — like incomes, house prices, or ages in a population.

## Line Graphs and Trends

**Kiran:** Line graphs connect data points with lines to show trends. The temperature graph in the textbook shows temperature rising from 6 AM to 3 PM and then falling.

**Ravi:** The steepness of the line tells you how fast the change is happening. A steep upward line from 9 AM to noon means rapid warming. A gentle downward line from 6 PM to 9 PM means slow cooling. This is an intuitive introduction to the concept of rate of change, which becomes central in calculus.

**Kiran:** Can I predict future values from a line graph?

**Ravi:** To some extent. If the trend is consistent, you can extend the line. But real-world data often has surprises — a sudden cloud cover could change the temperature pattern. Prediction from data is the basis of weather forecasting and economic analysis.

**Kiran:** The India Meteorological Department must use much more sophisticated graphs.

**Ravi:** They use line graphs, bar charts, heat maps, and complex statistical models. But the fundamental skill — reading trends from visual data — starts right here with these simple line graphs.

## Using Frequency Tables

**Kiran:** When data has repeated values, the frequency table is more efficient. For family sizes of 3, 4, 5, 6 with frequencies 3, 11, 9, 7, the mean is (9 plus 44 plus 45 plus 42) divided by 30, which is 4.67.

**Ravi:** Notice that 4.67 is not one of the actual data values. The mean does not have to be a value that appears in the data. A family cannot have 4.67 members, but that is the average family size.

**Kiran:** This is like saying the average Indian family has 2.2 children — no family literally has 2.2 children, but it is a useful summary statistic.

**Ravi:** Frequency tables are used throughout census data. The Census of India collects data on family size, income, education, and more, and presents it in frequency tables. Understanding how to compute and interpret the mean from these tables is essential for reading and understanding such data.

**Kiran:** For exams, I should practise the formula: mean equals the sum of (each value times its frequency) divided by total frequency.

**Ravi:** Yes, and watch for common mistakes — forgetting to multiply by frequency, or dividing by the number of distinct values instead of total frequency. Thirty families with four different sizes means you divide by 30, not by 4.
