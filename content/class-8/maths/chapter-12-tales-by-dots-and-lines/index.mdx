## 5.1 The Balancing Act

### Mean as the Centre

The **mean** (or arithmetic mean/average) of a set of numbers is the sum of all values divided by the number of values.

**Example:** The mean of 3 and 7 is (3 + 7)/2 = **5**.

When you plot numbers on a number line, the mean sits at the **centre** -- like a balancing point. The total distances from the mean to all values below it equals the total distances to all values above it.

**Example:** For the data 10, 10, 11, 17:
- Mean = (10 + 10 + 11 + 17)/4 = 48/4 = **12**
- Distances below: (12-10) + (12-10) + (12-11) = 2 + 2 + 1 = 5
- Distances above: (17-12) = 5
- Both sides balance at 5!

> **Key idea:** The mean is the unique point where the total distance on the left equals the total distance on the right. There is no other point with this property. This is why the mean is called a measure of **central tendency**.

---

### How the Mean Changes

**Adding a value greater than the mean:** The mean **increases** to maintain balance.

**Adding a value less than the mean:** The mean **decreases**.

**Adding a value equal to the mean:** The mean **stays the same**.

**Removing a value greater than the mean:** The mean **decreases**.

**Removing a value less than the mean:** The mean **increases**.

---

### Unchanging Mean

Can you add or remove values without changing the mean?

**Yes!** If you add two values -- one above and one below the mean -- such that their distances from the mean are equal, the mean stays unchanged.

You can also add 2 values less than the mean and 1 value greater than the mean, as long as the total distances balance out.

---

### Relatively Unchanged

**What happens if you add a fixed number to every value?**

If every value increases by k, the mean also increases by k.

**Proof using algebra:** If the original mean is a = (x1 + x2 + ... + xn)/n, then after adding k to each value:

New mean = ((x1+k) + (x2+k) + ... + (xn+k))/n = a + k

**What happens if you multiply every value by a fixed number?**

If every value is multiplied by k, the mean also gets multiplied by k.

**Proof:** New mean = (kx1 + kx2 + ... + kxn)/n = k(x1 + x2 + ... + xn)/n = ka

> **Key idea:** The mean responds proportionally to changes in the data. Adding a constant shifts the mean by that constant. Multiplying by a constant scales the mean by that constant.

## 5.2 Median

The **median** is the middle value when data is sorted in order. If there is an even number of values, the median is the average of the two middle values.

### How the Median Changes

- Adding a value **greater** than the median causes the median to **increase** (or stay the same).
- Adding a value **less** than the median causes it to **decrease** (or stay the same).
- The median is less affected by extreme values compared to the mean.

## Finding the Unknown

### Example 1: Missing Value

A coach notes the weights of 10 wrestlers with a mean of 39.2 kg. The known weights are 42, 40, 39, 33, 48, 38, 42, 35, 32, and one unknown weight w.

Sum of all weights = Mean x Number of players
- 349 + w = 39.2 x 10 = 392
- w = 392 - 349 = **43 kg**

### Example 2: Incorrect Value

A farmer calculates the average coconut harvest per tree as 25.6, with 15 trees. One tree's count was recorded 3 more than the actual.

- Total from incorrect data = 25.6 x 15 = 384
- Correct total = 384 - 3 = 381
- Correct average = 381/15 = **25.4**

## Mean and Median with Frequencies

When data has repeated values, we use a **frequency table**.

**Example:** Family size data:

| Family Size | Frequency |
|---|---|
| 3 | 3 |
| 4 | 11 |
| 5 | 9 |
| 6 | 7 |

**Mean = (3x3 + 4x11 + 5x9 + 6x7) / (3 + 11 + 9 + 7)**
= (9 + 44 + 45 + 42) / 30
= 140/30 = **4.67**

The mean does NOT have to be one of the values in the data. Here, 4.67 is between 4 and 5.

## 5.3 Line Graphs

A **line graph** uses points connected by lines to show how data changes over time or another variable.

### Reading Line Graphs

- The **horizontal axis (x-axis)** usually shows time or categories.
- The **vertical axis (y-axis)** shows the measured quantity.
- Points are plotted for each data pair and connected by straight lines.
- An **upward slope** means the value is increasing; a **downward slope** means it is decreasing.

### Example: Temperature Over a Day

| Time | 6 AM | 9 AM | 12 PM | 3 PM | 6 PM | 9 PM |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Temperature (degrees C) | 18 | 22 | 28 | 32 | 26 | 20 |

Plot these points on a graph with time on the x-axis and temperature on the y-axis. Connect with lines. You can see the temperature rising in the morning, peaking at 3 PM, and dropping in the evening.

### Drawing Line Graphs

**Steps:**
1. Draw the axes and label them.
2. Choose an appropriate scale for each axis.
3. Plot each data point.
4. Connect the points with straight lines.
5. Give the graph a title.

> **Key idea:** Line graphs are excellent for showing **trends** -- how a quantity changes over time. The steepness of the line shows how fast the change is happening.

---

## 5.4 Interpreting Data

When looking at data and graphs, ask yourself:
- What **trend** does the data show? (increasing, decreasing, stable)
- Are there any **outliers** (unusual values)?
- What might be the **reason** behind the pattern?
- Can we **predict** future values based on the trend?

### Comparing Data Sets

When comparing two data sets, look at both the **mean** (or median) and the **spread** of the data. Two sets can have the same mean but very different distributions.

**Example:** Two cricket players both average 40 runs per match. But Player A scores between 30 and 50 consistently, while Player B sometimes scores 0 and sometimes 80. Their means are equal, but Player A is more consistent.

---

## Summary

- The **mean** is the balancing point of data -- total distances are equal on both sides.
- Adding a constant k to all values increases the mean by k. Multiplying all values by k multiplies the mean by k.
- The **median** is the middle value in sorted data and is less sensitive to extreme values than the mean.
- **Frequency tables** help calculate the mean when values repeat: multiply each value by its frequency, add up, and divide by total frequency.
- **Line graphs** show how data changes over time using connected points.
- When interpreting data, look for trends, outliers, and possible explanations.
