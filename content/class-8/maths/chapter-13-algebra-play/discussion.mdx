## Think of a Number Tricks

**Kiran:** Papa, the "think of a number" trick in the textbook always gives the answer 2, no matter what number you start with. How does the unknown cancel out?

**Ravi:** Let us trace it algebraically. Start with x. Double it to get 2x. Add 4 to get 2x plus 4. Divide by 2 to get x plus 2. Subtract the original number x, and you are left with just 2. The x cancels out completely.

**Kiran:** So the trick works because the algebra is designed to eliminate the variable. If I wanted the answer to be 5 instead of 2, I would add 10 in step 3 instead of 4, because 10 divided by 2 is 5.

**Ravi:** Exactly. You can design your own tricks by working backwards from the desired answer. This is a powerful algebraic skill — reverse engineering. Engineers do this when they know the desired output and need to find the right input parameters.

**Kiran:** The date trick is more complex — it encodes the month and day into a single number using 100M plus D plus 165. You subtract 165 to decode it.

**Ravi:** The key is that 100M separates the month into the hundreds position, while D stays in the units and tens positions. This is place value in action — the same concept from Chapter 3. Algebra, place value, and number tricks all connect.

## Number Pyramids

**Kiran:** In a number pyramid, each number is the sum of the two below it. If the bottom row is a, b, c, the top is a plus 2b plus c. Why does the middle number count twice?

**Ravi:** Because it contributes to both the left and right sums in the second row. The second row has (a plus b) and (b plus c). Adding these gives a plus 2b plus c. The middle number b appears in both paths to the top.

**Kiran:** For a 4-row pyramid with bottom row a, b, c, d, the top is a plus 3b plus 3c plus d. The coefficients 1, 3, 3, 1 are from Pascal's Triangle!

**Ravi:** This is a deep connection. Pascal's Triangle — known in India as Meru Prastara, described by Pingala and later by Varahamihira and Halayudha — appears in binomial expansions, number pyramids, probability, and combinatorics.

**Kiran:** The textbook also connects pyramids to Virahanka-Fibonacci numbers. If the bottom row has 1, 2, 3, 5, all numbers in the pyramid are Virahanka-Fibonacci numbers.

**Ravi:** Mathematical structures are interconnected in surprising ways. The more mathematics you learn, the more these connections reveal themselves. For exams, practise solving pyramids both forward (bottom to top) and backward (given the top, find missing bottom values).

## Calendar and Grid Puzzles

**Kiran:** The calendar 2 by 2 grid trick is neat. If the top-left date is a, the grid contains a, a plus 1, a plus 7, a plus 8. The sum is 4a plus 16. So given the sum, a equals (sum minus 16) divided by 4.

**Ravi:** This is setting up and solving a linear equation — the core skill of algebra. The calendar's structure provides the relationships between the numbers (consecutive dates in the same week are 7 apart), and algebra turns that structure into a formula.

**Kiran:** For a 3 by 3 grid, the sum would involve more terms. Would the formula be different?

**Ravi:** Yes. In a 3 by 3 grid starting at a, the nine values are a, a plus 1, a plus 2, a plus 7, a plus 8, a plus 9, a plus 14, a plus 15, a plus 16. The sum is 9a plus 72. So a equals (sum minus 72) divided by 9.

**Kiran:** The algebra grid puzzles with shapes representing unknowns are like systems of equations. Triangle plus Triangle plus Triangle equals 27 means Triangle equals 9.

**Ravi:** Then substituting into the next equation: Circle plus Circle plus 9 equals 19 gives Circle equals 5. This method of substitution is exactly how engineers solve systems of equations — find one variable, substitute into other equations, and repeat.

## Divisibility Tricks Decoded

**Kiran:** The trick about reversing a 2-digit number and taking the difference always gives a multiple of 9. The textbook proves it using 10a plus b minus (10b plus a) equals 9(a minus b). That is so clean!

**Ravi:** Place value notation turns digits into algebra. A 2-digit number with tens digit a and units digit b is simply 10a plus b. Once you write it this way, the algebra does the rest.

**Kiran:** For a 3-digit number, the difference between the number and its reverse is 99(a minus c), always a multiple of 99. And the middle digit b cancels out completely!

**Ravi:** And the sum of a 2-digit number and its reverse is 11(a plus b), always a multiple of 11. These are not magic — they are algebra applied to the structure of our number system.

**Kiran:** Can I create my own tricks using this method?

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Try this: take any 3-digit number, write it twice to make a 6-digit number (like 425425). This number is always divisible by 7, 11, and 13. Can you prove why?

**Kiran:** The 6-digit number is the 3-digit number times 1001. And 1001 equals 7 times 11 times 13! So the 6-digit number is always divisible by all three.

**Ravi:** Excellent algebraic reasoning. That is the power of this chapter — turning mysterious tricks into clear, logical explanations.

## Solving Equations with Algebra

**Kiran:** The age problem — Ravi is 3 times his son's age, and 5 years ago he was 4 times the son's age — uses one equation with one unknown.

**Ravi:** Let the son's age be x, so Ravi's age is 3x. Five years ago: 3x minus 5 equals 4 times (x minus 5). Expanding: 3x minus 5 equals 4x minus 20. Rearranging: 15 equals x. So the son is 15 and Ravi is 45.

**Kiran:** The check: 5 years ago they were 10 and 40. And 40 is indeed 4 times 10. It works!

**Ravi:** Always verify your answer by substituting back. For the two-number problem — sum is 48 and difference is 14 — adding the equations gives 2x equals 62, so x equals 31. And y equals 17. Check: 31 plus 17 is 48, and 31 minus 17 is 14.

**Kiran:** These techniques will be important for higher classes where equations get more complex.

**Ravi:** They are the foundation. Quadratic equations, simultaneous equations, polynomial equations — they all build on the linear equation-solving skills you develop here. Master this, and the rest follows.
