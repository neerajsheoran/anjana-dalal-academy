## Area vs Perimeter

**Kiran:** Papa, I used to think that a larger perimeter means a larger area. But the textbook shows that two shapes can have the same perimeter and completely different areas. Why?

**Ravi:** Perimeter measures the boundary length, while area measures the space inside. They are independent measurements. A rectangle of 6 by 2 has perimeter 16 and area 12. A square of 4 by 4 also has perimeter 16, but area 16. Same boundary, more space.

**Kiran:** So the square has more area for the same perimeter. Is that always true?

**Ravi:** Among all rectangles with a given perimeter, the square has the maximum area. And among all shapes with a given perimeter, the circle has the maximum area. These are fundamental optimisation results used in engineering — why pipes and wells are circular, for instance.

**Kiran:** A farmer fencing a field should make it as close to a circle as possible to get the most area per metre of fencing.

**Ravi:** Exactly. In agricultural planning across Indian states, understanding the relationship between boundary and area directly affects yields and costs.

## Triangle Area Formula

**Kiran:** The formula for the area of a triangle is half times base times height. The textbook proves this by showing that a triangle is half of a rectangle. But what about obtuse triangles where the height falls outside?

**Ravi:** Even then, the formula works. The proof for obtuse triangles uses a subtraction approach. You construct two right triangles and subtract one from the other. The algebra shows the answer is still half times base times height, regardless of the triangle type.

**Kiran:** The base can be any side, and the height is the perpendicular from the opposite vertex to that base line. So one triangle has three different base-height pairs, but the area is always the same.

**Ravi:** Yes. This is a powerful verification tool. If you calculate the area using two different base-height pairs and get different answers, you have made an error somewhere.

**Kiran:** The Shulba-Sutras describe transforming a rectangle into a triangle of equal area — making the triangle's base equal to the rectangle's length and height equal to twice the rectangle's width. Since half times l times 2w equals l times w, the areas match!

**Ravi:** Ancient Indian mathematicians were essentially doing area-preserving transformations — a concept central to modern geometry and physics.

## Triangles Between Parallel Lines

**Kiran:** If I fix the base BC and slide the third vertex along a parallel line, all triangles have the same area. That is because the height — the distance between the parallel lines — stays constant.

**Ravi:** This is a beautiful result with practical applications. When surveyors need to mark out a field with a specific area, they can adjust the position of one boundary along a parallel line while maintaining the same area.

**Kiran:** The minimum perimeter problem is clever. Among all these equal-area triangles, the one with the shortest perimeter is found using the reflection principle.

**Ravi:** You reflect one vertex across the parallel line and draw a straight line to find the optimal position. This reflection technique appears in optics too — light always takes the shortest path, which is why the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Physics and geometry are deeply connected.

**Kiran:** Also, a line from any vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side divides the triangle into two triangles of equal area. This is the median property.

**Ravi:** Correct. And the three medians of a triangle meet at a single point — the centroid — which divides each median in the ratio 2 to 1. This connects to the concept of the mean as a balancing point from Chapter 12.

## Parallelograms and Trapeziums

**Kiran:** The area of a parallelogram is base times height — the same as a rectangle. The height is the perpendicular distance between the parallel sides, not the slant side.

**Ravi:** This is a common exam mistake — students use the slant side length instead of the height. A parallelogram with base 8 and slant side 5 does not have area 40 unless the angle is 90 degrees. You need the perpendicular height.

**Kiran:** For a trapezium with parallel sides a and b and height h, the area is half times (a plus b) times h. This is like the average of the two parallel sides times the height.

**Ravi:** That is an excellent way to remember it. The formula comes from joining two identical trapeziums to form a parallelogram with base (a plus b) and height h. The trapezium is half of that.

**Kiran:** In Indian irrigation canals, the cross-section is often a trapezium. Engineers calculate the water flow capacity using the trapezoidal area formula and the canal's length.

**Ravi:** And the Mumbai-Pune Expressway has many trapezoidal cross-sections in its drainage channels and retaining walls. The formula is not abstract — it shapes the infrastructure you see every day.

## Area of Any Polygon

**Kiran:** Any polygon can be split into triangles, and the total area is the sum of the triangle areas. So the triangle formula is the foundation of all area calculations.

**Ravi:** Precisely. For a quadrilateral with diagonal d and perpendicular distances h1 and h2 from the other two vertices, the area is half times d times (h1 plus h2).

**Kiran:** And a rhombus with diagonals d1 and d2 has area half times d1 times d2, because the diagonals divide it into four right triangles.

**Ravi:** For exams, have the summary table memorised: rectangle is l times w, square is s squared, triangle is half b times h, parallelogram is b times h, trapezium is half (a plus b) times h, rhombus is half d1 times d2. But more importantly, understand that all of these derive from the triangle formula.

**Kiran:** So one formula to rule them all — half times base times height!

**Ravi:** That is the beauty of mathematics. Complex formulas reduce to simple principles. The Shulba-Sutra authors and Euclid both understood this — area is fundamentally about counting unit squares, and the triangle is the simplest polygon that tiles any shape.
