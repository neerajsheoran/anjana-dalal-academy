## Understanding Proportionality

**Kiran:** Papa, the image resizing example is very relatable. When I stretch a photo on my phone, sometimes it looks normal and sometimes distorted. The textbook says it is about factors versus differences.

**Ravi:** Exactly. If both width and height change by the same factor — say both are halved or both are doubled — the image looks proportional. But if you add or subtract the same number of pixels from both dimensions, the image gets distorted because the ratio changes.

**Kiran:** So proportionality is about multiplicative change, not additive change. That is why ages are not proportional — when Neelima was 3 and her mother was 30, the ratio was 1 to 10. Nine years later, they are 12 and 39, with ratio 4 to 13. The ages changed by addition, not multiplication.

**Ravi:** This is one of the most important distinctions in mathematics. Proportional reasoning appears everywhere — in cooking recipes, engineering scale models, map reading, financial calculations, and even in medicine where drug dosages are proportional to body weight.

**Kiran:** In the lemonade example, if 6 glasses need 10 spoons of sugar, then 18 glasses need 30 spoons — because the factor of change is 3 for both.

**Ravi:** And the key test is cross-multiplication. If a over b equals c over d, then a times d equals b times c.

## Ratios and Simplest Form

**Kiran:** Why do we reduce ratios to their simplest form?

**Ravi:** To make comparison easy. The ratios 60 to 40, 30 to 20, and 90 to 60 all look different, but they are all 3 to 2 in simplest form. Without simplifying, you would have to do mental gymnastics to check if they are proportional.

**Kiran:** You simplify by dividing both terms by their HCF. So for 60 and 40, the HCF is 20. Dividing both by 20 gives 3 to 2.

**Ravi:** In the cement wall problem, Nitin's ratio of 60 feet to 3 bags and Hari's ratio of 40 feet to 2 bags both simplify to 20 to 1. So both walls are equally strong, even though the absolute numbers are different.

**Kiran:** That is like normalisation — bringing everything to the same scale for fair comparison.

**Ravi:** Precisely. Indian textile manufacturers use this when comparing fabric quality — the thread count per square centimetre is a ratio that must be compared in simplest terms.

## The Rule of Three (Trairasika)

**Kiran:** Aryabhata called the proportion method Trairasika — the Rule of Three. It uses three known quantities to find a fourth unknown. Why was this so important historically?

**Ravi:** Because it solved virtually every practical problem in ancient commerce. How much grain for a different number of workers? How much land tax for a different area? How long for a journey at a different speed? Aryabhata described it as pramana, phala, and ichchha — the known measure, the known result, and the desired measure.

**Kiran:** So if 5 kg of rice costs 200 rupees, and I want 8 kg: pramana is 5, phala is 200, ichchha is 8. The ichchha-phala is 200 times 8 divided by 5, which is 320 rupees.

**Ravi:** This is the same as the cross-multiplication formula. If 5 to 200 is proportional to 8 to x, then 5x equals 200 times 8, giving x equals 320.

**Kiran:** The unitary method — finding the value per unit first — seems simpler for some problems. Cost of 1 kg is 40 rupees, so 8 kg costs 320 rupees.

**Ravi:** Both methods are equivalent. The unitary method is often easier to explain, while cross-multiplication is faster for computation. Use whichever feels more natural for the specific problem.

## Filter Coffee Ratios

**Kiran:** The filter coffee example is fun. Regular coffee has decoction to milk in ratio 3 to 7. Stronger coffee has ratio 2 to 3. How do I compare these ratios?

**Ravi:** Convert them to the same denominator or compare their values as fractions. The ratio 3 to 7 means 3 parts decoction out of 10 total, which is 30 percent decoction. The ratio 2 to 3 means 2 parts out of 5 total, which is 40 percent decoction. So the second coffee is stronger.

**Kiran:** So higher proportion of decoction means stronger coffee. This connects to the percentage chapter too.

**Ravi:** Absolutely. Ratios and percentages are two ways of expressing the same idea. In South Indian restaurants, the coffee master adjusts the decoction-to-milk ratio based on customer preference. That skill is pure proportional reasoning.

**Kiran:** My grandmother says her filter coffee is the best because she gets the ratio just right!

**Ravi:** She is a mathematician without knowing it. Getting the proportion right — in cooking, in engineering, in art — is the essence of quality.

## When Proportionality Fails

**Kiran:** The ages example shows that adding the same number to both terms of a ratio changes the ratio. Are there other situations where proportionality does not work?

**Ravi:** Many. If doubling the speed does not halve the travel time because of traffic, the relationship is not proportional. If adding more workers to a software project does not proportionally reduce time because of coordination overhead — that is called Brooks's Law in software engineering.

**Kiran:** So real life is more complex than simple proportions?

**Ravi:** Often, yes. But proportional reasoning gives you a baseline. You first calculate what the answer would be if things were proportional, then adjust for real-world complexities. Engineers designing bridges first use proportional scaling, then add safety factors.

**Kiran:** For exams, I should clearly identify whether a problem involves direct proportion, inverse proportion, or neither.

**Ravi:** Exactly. Ask yourself: if I double one quantity, does the other also double (direct proportion), get halved (inverse proportion), or do something else? That question is the key to choosing the right method.
