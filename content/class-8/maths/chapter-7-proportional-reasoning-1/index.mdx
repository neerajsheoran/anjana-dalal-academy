## 7.1 Observing Similarity in Change

Look at digital images on your phone or computer. When you resize an image, sometimes it looks fine, and sometimes it looks stretched or squished. Why?

Consider five images of a tiger, all different sizes:

| Image | Width (mm) | Height (mm) |
|---|---|---|
| A | 60 | 40 |
| B | 40 | 20 |
| C | 30 | 20 |
| D | 90 | 60 |
| E | 60 | 60 |

Images A, C, and D look similar (the tiger looks natural). But B looks elongated and E looks compressed. Why?

- Compare A and C: Width of C is half of A (30 = 60/2). Height of C is also half of A (20 = 40/2). **Both changed by the same factor (1/2)**, so the image looks similar.
- Compare A and B: Width decreased by 20 mm. Height also decreased by 20 mm. Same **difference**, but the width is NOT half of A's width. Since both dimensions did not change by the **same factor**, B looks distorted.

> **Key idea:** When two quantities change by the **same factor** (through multiplication), the relationship is **proportional** and things look similar. When quantities change by the same difference (through subtraction), they may NOT be proportional.

## 7.2 Ratios

A **ratio** compares two quantities using multiplication. We write the ratio of width to height of image A as **60 : 40**.

The numbers 60 and 40 are called the **terms** of the ratio.

In the ratio a : b, we say "for every a units of the first quantity, there are b units of the second quantity."

The ratios of images A, C, and D are:
- A: 60 : 40
- C: 30 : 20
- D: 90 : 60

These ratios are all **proportional** because their terms change by the same factor.

---

## 7.3 Ratios in Their Simplest Form

We reduce a ratio to its **simplest form** by dividing both terms by their **HCF** (Highest Common Factor).

**Image A:** 60 : 40. HCF of 60 and 40 is 20. Simplest form = **3 : 2**

**Image C:** 30 : 20. HCF is 10. Simplest form = **3 : 2**

**Image D:** 90 : 60. HCF is 30. Simplest form = **3 : 2**

**Image B:** 40 : 20. HCF is 20. Simplest form = **2 : 1** (not the same as 3 : 2)

**Image E:** 60 : 60. HCF is 60. Simplest form = **1 : 1** (not the same as 3 : 2)

> **Key idea:** Two ratios are **proportional** (or "in proportion") if their simplest forms are the same. We write this as a : b :: c : d, which means a : b is proportional to c : d. So 60 : 40 :: 30 : 20 :: 90 : 60, because they all simplify to 3 : 2.

## 7.4 Problem Solving with Proportional Reasoning

### Example 1: Are 3 : 4 and 72 : 96 proportional?

- 3 : 4 is already in simplest form.
- HCF of 72 and 96 is 24. So 72 : 96 = 3 : 4 in simplest form.
- **Yes**, they are proportional. 3 : 4 :: 72 : 96.

---

### Example 2: Lemonade Problem

Kesang made 6 glasses of lemonade with 10 spoons of sugar. She needs to make 18 more glasses with the same sweetness. How many spoons of sugar?

To maintain the same sweetness, the ratios must be proportional:
- 6 : 10 :: 18 : ?
- Factor of change in the first term: 18 / 6 = **3**
- Apply the same factor to the second term: 10 x 3 = **30**

She needs **30 spoons of sugar** for 18 glasses.

---

### Example 3: Cement for a Wall

Nitin built a 60-foot wall using 3 bags of cement. Hari built a 40-foot wall using 2 bags. Is Hari's wall weaker?

- Nitin's ratio: 60 : 3 = **20 : 1** (simplest form)
- Hari's ratio: 40 : 2 = **20 : 1** (simplest form)

The ratios are proportional! **Both walls are equally strong.**

---

### Example 4: Ages and Proportions

When Neelima was 3 years old, her mother was 30. Ratio = 3 : 30 = **1 : 10**.

When Neelima is 12, her mother will be 39 (9 years later). Ratio = 12 : 39 = **4 : 13**.

The ratio has **changed**! This is because ages change by addition, not multiplication.

> **Key idea:** When we **add** (or subtract) the same number to both terms of a ratio, the ratio changes and is NOT necessarily proportional to the original. Proportionality requires change by the same **factor** (multiplication), not the same **difference** (addition).

---

### Example 5: Filter Coffee

Manjunath makes regular filter coffee by mixing 15 mL decoction with 35 mL milk (ratio 15 : 35 = **3 : 7**).

- **Stronger coffee:** 20 mL decoction + 30 mL milk (ratio 20 : 30 = **2 : 3**) -- more decoction per unit milk
- **Lighter coffee:** 10 mL decoction + 40 mL milk (ratio 10 : 40 = **1 : 4**) -- less decoction per unit milk

The coffee is stronger when the ratio of decoction to milk is **higher**, and lighter when it is **lower**.

### Finding Missing Terms

**Example:** Fill in the missing numbers for ratios proportional to 14 : 21.

**? : 42**
- 42 is 2 times 21. So multiply 14 by 2 = **28**. Answer: **28 : 42**

**6 : ?**
- What factor converts 14 to 6? That is 6/14 = 3/7
- Multiply 21 by 3/7 = **9**. Answer: **6 : 9**

**2 : ?**
- 14 / 7 = 2 (dividing by HCF 7)
- 21 / 7 = 3. Answer: **2 : 3** (simplest form)

## The Rule of Three (Trairasika)

Many real-world problems involve finding a fourth unknown quantity when three quantities are known and the ratios are proportional.

### The Cross-Multiplication Method

If **a : b :: c : d**, then:

**ad = bc** (cross multiplication)

This means: **d = (b x c) / a**

### Example: Mid-Day Meal

A school makes 15 kg of rice for 120 students. On a rainy day, only 80 students come. How much rice is needed?

- 120 : 15 :: 80 : x
- Cross multiply: 120 x = 15 x 80 = 1200
- x = 1200 / 120 = **10 kg**

### Example: Car Travel

A car covers 90 km in 150 minutes. How far will it go in 4 hours (240 minutes)?

- 150 : 90 :: 240 : x
- Cross multiply: 150x = 90 x 240 = 21600
- x = 21600 / 150 = **144 km**

### Historical Note

In ancient India, **Aryabhata** (499 CE) described this method as the **Rule of Three** (Trairasika). He used three given quantities:
- **Pramana** (measure) -- the known reference quantity
- **Phala** (fruit) -- the known related quantity
- **Ichchha** (requisition) -- the desired reference quantity

To find the **Ichchha-phala** (yield -- the unknown answer):

**Ichchha-phala = (Phala x Ichchha) / Pramana**

> **Key idea:** The Rule of Three has been used for thousands of years to solve proportion problems. If a : b :: c : d, then d = bc/a. This is called **cross multiplication** and is the key tool for solving proportion problems.

---

## Unitary Method

Another approach is to first find the value for **one unit**, then multiply.

**Example:** If 5 kg of rice costs Rs 200, what is the cost of 8 kg?

1. Cost of 1 kg = 200 / 5 = Rs 40
2. Cost of 8 kg = 40 x 8 = **Rs 320**

This is called the **unitary method** -- find the value per unit first, then scale up.

---

## Summary

- A **ratio** a : b compares two quantities by division.
- Ratios are written in **simplest form** by dividing both terms by their HCF.
- Two ratios are **proportional** if their simplest forms are the same.
- Proportionality means change by the same **factor** (multiplication), not the same difference (addition/subtraction).
- If a : b :: c : d, then **ad = bc** (cross multiplication), and the unknown fourth quantity is d = bc/a.
- This method was called **Trairasika** (Rule of Three) by ancient Indian mathematicians like Aryabhata.
- The **unitary method** finds the value for one unit first, then scales to the desired quantity.
