## What is Data?

When we collect information about things, that information is called **data**. For example, if we ask all the children in a class what their favourite colour is, the answers we get are data.

Data helps us answer questions like:
- Which colour do **most** children like?
- Which colour do the **fewest** children like?
- Do more children like red or blue?

## Collecting Data

To collect data, we ask people a simple question and write down their answers.

**Example:** Priya asks her classmates, "What is your favourite colour?"

She writes down each answer:
- Red, Blue, Green, Red, Yellow, Blue, Red, Green, Blue, Red, Blue, Yellow, Red, Green, Blue, Red

Now she has the data. But it is hard to understand when it is just a long list! She needs to **organise** it.

## Organising Data in a Table

The best way to organise data is to make a **table**. We count how many times each answer appears.

| Colour | Number of Children |
|--------|-------------------|
| Red | 6 |
| Blue | 5 |
| Green | 3 |
| Yellow | 2 |

Now we can see clearly:
- **Red** is the most liked colour (6 children).
- **Yellow** is the least liked colour (2 children).
- More children like red than green.
- Blue is liked more than yellow.

## Favourite Fruits

At a fruit party, children are eating their favourite fruits. Let us organise the data:

| Fruit | Number of Children |
|-------|-------------------|
| Apple | 4 |
| Banana | 6 |
| Orange | 3 |
| Guava | 5 |
| Kiwi | 2 |

From the table, we can see:
- The **most liked** fruit is **banana** (6 children).
- The **least liked** fruit is **kiwi** (2 children).
- More children like guava than apple.
- Fewer children like orange than guava.

## How We Come to School

Data is not just about favourite things. We can collect data about many things! For example, how do children come to school?

| Mode of Travel | Number of Students |
|---------------|-------------------|
| On foot | 8 |
| Autorickshaw | 4 |
| Bus | 6 |
| Bike | 3 |
| Bicycle | 5 |

From this table:
- **Most** students come to school **on foot**.
- **Fewest** students come by **bike**.
- More students come by bus than by autorickshaw.
- Fewer students come by bike than by bicycle.

## Pictographs -- Pictures That Show Data

A **pictograph** uses small **pictures** or **symbols** to show data. Each picture stands for **one thing** (or sometimes more).

Instead of writing numbers in a table, we draw small faces, stars, or icons to show the count.

### Example: How We Come to School

We can show the same data using faces. Each face means 1 student:

```
On foot:       faces faces faces faces faces faces faces faces  (8)
Autorickshaw:  faces faces faces faces                          (4)
Bus:           faces faces faces faces faces faces              (6)
Bike:          faces faces faces                                (3)
Bicycle:       faces faces faces faces faces                    (5)
```

By looking at the pictograph, we can quickly see which row is the **longest** (most students) and which is the **shortest** (fewest students).

> **Tip:** In a pictograph, the longer the row of pictures, the bigger the number!

## Asking Your Own Questions

You can collect your own data! Here is how:

1. **Choose a question.** For example: "What is your favourite vegetable?"
2. **Ask your friends.** Write down each answer.
3. **Make a table.** Count how many times each answer appears.
4. **Read the table.** Find which vegetable is liked the most and the least.

### Example: Favourite Vegetables

| Vegetable | Number of Children |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Potato | 7 |
| Carrot | 3 |
| Beans | 5 |
| Peas | 4 |
| Capsicum | 2 |

- Most liked: **Potato** (7 children)
- Least liked: **Capsicum** (2 children)

## Comparing Data

When we read data from a table or a pictograph, we can compare groups using these words:

| Word | What It Means |
|------|--------------|
| **More than** | A bigger number |
| **Less than** | A smaller number |
| **Equal to** | The same number |
| **Most** | The biggest number in the group |
| **Least** | The smallest number in the group |

### Example:
If 8 children like cricket and 5 like football:
- Cricket is liked by **more** children **than** football.
- Football is liked by **fewer** children **than** cricket.

## Family Data

You can also collect data about families! For example, how many people live in each family?

| People in a Home | Number of Families |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| 2 people | 1 |
| 3 people | 4 |
| 4 people | 8 |
| 5 people | 5 |
| 6 or more people | 3 |

From this data:
- **Most** families have **4 people** in their homes.
- The **least** number of families have only **2 people**.

> **Activity idea:** Visit families in your neighbourhood and find out how many families live with their grandparents. Make a table to show your data!

## Key Words to Remember

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Data** | Information collected by asking questions or observing things |
| **Table** | A chart with rows and columns used to organise data |
| **Pictograph** | A chart that uses pictures or symbols to show data |
| **Count** | Finding how many of each type there are |
| **Most** | The largest number in a group |
| **Least** | The smallest number in a group |
| **Compare** | Looking at two or more numbers to see which is bigger, smaller, or equal |
| **Organise** | Arranging data in a neat and clear way |
